Co-evolution of Global Urban Planning: Civilizational Dialogue and Institutional Innovation at the Interface of Conflict

Urban planning in the context of globalization has evolved into a complex trans-civilizational practice field, where institutional dialogue between different governance paradigms in spatial production forms its essence. This paper reveals how urban planning transforms cultural tensions into innovative momentum through technological-political mechanisms using a reconstructed three-dimensional analytical framework of "Conflict-Transformation-Co-evolution." It proposes "institutional translation capacity" as a new indicator for evaluating the effectiveness of transnational planning cooperation.

I. Translation Mechanism of Cultural Conflict: From Symbolic Confrontation to Meta-rule Symbiosis

The cultural conflict in urban planning fundamentally results from the paradigm incommensurability in spatial production logics. Take NEOM City as an example: the collision between the Japanese team's "Hyper-Fluid City" concept (maximizing spatial efficiency) and Islamic spatial norms of gender separation (prioritizing social order) exposes deep contradictions between modern planning paradigms and non-Western spatial cognition. Through developing culturally adaptive intelligent material systems (e.g., nano-coatings with programmable light transmittance), the project achieved Dynamic Sharia Compliance for spatial functions. Its breakthrough lies in establishing a "technological mediation layer" to dissolve direct value conflicts.

Singapore's Gardens by the Bay exemplifies a deeper translation logic through its "Controlled Wildness" paradigm: by constructing "meta-rule mapping" between Eastern and Western ecological concepts, it transforms Daoist "non-interference governance" into modular ecological units, while materializing Western scientific management as parametric maintenance systems. This translation doesn't seek value unification but creates a "transcendent spatial syntax" that simultaneously satisfies different cultural expectations.

Theoretical Contribution: Proposes a "Cultural Translation Gradient" model to quantitatively evaluate adaptation costs of planning solutions across different cultural contexts.

II. Techno-Political Game Theory: Institutional Design of Data Sovereignty

The core conflict in smart city development lies in the power restructuring between technological standardization and local knowledge. Barcelona's resistance against Amazon's "City of Tomorrow" demonstrates that when a technology solution's "Institutional Package" contains elements of data colonialism, it triggers civil society's immune response. The failure stems from the lack of "sovereignty-compatible design" — failing to translate the EU GDPR's data localization requirements into the foundational architecture of spatial governance.

In contrast, the Shenzhen-Singapore Smart City Corridor achieved breakthroughs through "Blockchain Federalism": adopting common protocols (e.g., IEEE P2874 standard) at the infrastructure layer, maintaining sovereignty isolation at the data layer, while allowing cross-domain interoperability at the application layer. This "Onion Model" architecture proves that the solution to techno-political conflicts lies in building "techno-legal complexes" with institutional resilience.

Methodological Innovation: Introduces institutional economics' "Modular Separation" theory to establish a sovereignty risk assessment matrix for technology solutions.

III. Mechanism Design for Planning Diplomacy: Spatial Algorithms for Peacebuilding

Urban planning in post-conflict areas essentially transforms political contradictions into computable spatial variables. The success key of the Croatia-Serbia Danube River Project lies in establishing a "depoliticized" planning interface: reaching technical consensus on river ecological restoration (e.g., sediment flow control algorithms) bypasses politically sensitive territorial disputes. Its innovation is developing a "Spatial Equivalent Substitution" mechanism — substituting military functions with economic ones without altering legal status.

The "Resilient Border" research on the Korean DMZ goes further, reconfiguring the military buffer zone into a dual ecological-economic system. Using space syntax analysis, it demonstrates how specific topological structures (e.g., star-shaped networks) can simultaneously satisfy security needs and economic connectivity, providing political entities with "risk-controlled contact interfaces."

Policy Tool: Proposes a "Planning Confidence-Building Measures" (PCBM) framework to gradually reduce conflict intensity through incremental spatial reciprocity.

IV. Co-evolution Paradigm: Building Institutional Translation Capacity

The core competency for future transnational planning cooperation lies in cultivating "institutional translation capacity," which requires:

  1. Establishing a meta-rule repository for cultural cognition to transform value differences into actionable design parameters
  2. Developing sovereignty-sensitive technology architecture to achieve dynamic balance between technical efficiency and institutional compatibility
  3. Designing politically resilient spatial algorithms to make the physical environment serve as a conflict transformation valve

Research Prospects:

  • Using complex adaptive systems theory to simulate evolutionary paths of different cultural translation strategies
  • Developing computational models for measuring institutional friction coefficients of planning schemes
  • Building blockchain-based transnational planning credential trading systems
