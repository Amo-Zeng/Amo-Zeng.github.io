Redefining Humanity: Philosophical Inquiry into the Ethical Foundations of "Natural Humans" in the Age of Designer Babies

With the groundbreaking advancements in CRISPR gene-editing technology, the team led by Harvard geneticist George Church has successfully achieved targeted modifications of genes related to embryonic intelligence. This technological leap suddenly makes the "Philosopher-King Breeding Program" from Plato's "Republic" technically feasible, but it also fundamentally shakes the two cornerstones of Western ethics: the sanctity of life emphasized by the Christian "Image of God" doctrine, and the reverence for natural randomness since the Enlightenment. When the gender, IQ, and even empathy of embryos become adjustable parameters, the traditional ethical proposition that "life is a sacred gift" is being replaced by the perception that "life is an optimizable product."

I. The Dissolution of the Sanctity of Life: When Randomness Becomes a Programmable Variable

The existentialist philosopher Sartre's proposition that "existence precedes essence" is fundamentally challenged here—when genetic predetermination makes "essence" precede existence, are humans using technological means to completely resist what Heidegger called the "thrownness" of existence? A 2023 report by the Nordic Bioethics Association shows that 67% of surveyed parents believe that eliminating depression genes in their offspring is a "moral obligation." This shift in collective perception suggests that human tolerance for life's randomness is systematically diminishing in the wave of technological optimism.

II. The Ethical Dilemma of Agency: The Paradox of Genetic Selection and Identity

According to Article 7 of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, children have the fundamental right to "know their ancestry." But when parents predetermine their children's hair color, athletic talent, or musical ability through gene editing, we face a sharp philosophical question: Does this intergenerational choice constitute a colonization of future subjectivity? The "asymmetrical agency dilemma" described by German philosopher Habermas reaches its extreme here—the edited individual can never exercise veto power over their own genetic blueprint.

A 2024 tracking study by the University of Tokyo on gene-edited children reveals that adolescents with artificially enhanced genes commonly experience "double alienation": they struggle to identify with the biological essence of natural humans while also failing to internalize technological interventions as part of their self-identity. This dilemma confirms Foucault's prophecy about "technological power shaping subjectivity." When genes become a field for the expression of parental will, Simone de Beauvoir's existentialist declaration that "one is not born a woman, but becomes one" may need to be rewritten as "one does not become oneself, but is programmed to be oneself."

III. The Philosophical Paradox of Posthumanism: From Nietzsche's Übermensch to the Genetic Übermensch

The "Übermensch" that Nietzsche called for in "Thus Spoke Zarathustra" is essentially a poetic imagination of the possibility of human self-transcendence. But when this transcendence is achieved through genetic scissors rather than spiritual cultivation, there is a fundamental rupture between technological means and philosophical ends. The Neuroethics Research Group at Caltech found that individuals with memory-enhancing gene modifications experienced a significant reduction in Nietzschean "eternal recurrence"—technological enhancement actually weakened the core existential experience.

This paradox continually reappears in human enhancement technologies: When we can edit out all pain genes, do we also eliminate the biological basis for empathy? French philosopher Stiegler's philosophy of technology reminds us that while humans achieve "prosthetic evolution" through technology, it may also lead to the disappearance of the philosophically significant trait of "defective existence." On the path to pursuing the perfect posthuman, we may be eradicating the fundamental characteristics that make us human.

The Ultimate Question in the Existential Framework: Does Technology End the Possibility of "Becoming"?

Placing Heidegger's concept of "Dasein" in the context of gene editing raises a startling philosophical question: When technological intervention predetermines the range of existential possibilities, do humans lose the authenticity of "being-toward-death"? Empirical research by the Norwegian Institute of Life Philosophy shows that adolescents who are aware of their gene editing experience a 37% reduction in the sense of autonomy in their life projects. This state of "determined existence" directly challenges the core of existentialist freedom.

For Sartre, human freedom is manifested in the absolute responsibility of "having to be free." But when parents choose an optimistic personality for their child through gene editing, are they essentially constructing a new form of "biological determinism"? This dilemma may require us to revisit Hans Jonas's principle of responsibility ethics: Technological capability must be matched with a metaphysical responsibility toward "future others." In the age of gene editing, the true ethical challenge lies not in what we can do, but in what we should preserve for those who do not yet exist.
