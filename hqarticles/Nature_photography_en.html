The Three Realms of Nature Photography:

From Visual Hegemony to Ecological Symbiosis – A Triple Leap

When the shutter clicks, the world is not 'recorded'—it is rewritten. The lens is the inscription of power, and the crack through which awakening emerges.

We raise our cameras, believing we are gazing at nature, yet we are in fact performing a ritual of visual colonization—drawing boundaries with composition, naming with light, filtering through the standard of "beauty." The lens is never a transparent window; it is a cognitive cage, framing the visible while concealing the invisible; glorifying the sublime while banishing scars; staring at landscapes while turning a blind eye to wounds.

True nature, meanwhile, grows beyond the lens—like a vine, winding out of concrete cracks, climbing forgotten riverbanks, piercing through AI-generated illusions. It does not shout or please; it slowly, firmly, entwines the ruins of human civilization, writing a silent ecological manifesto between ruins and data.

This vine is the visual metaphor that runs through the three realms:
It sprouts from the dark side of the lens, traverses the fog of algorithms, and finally climbs to the top of the ruins—
From concealment, to counteraction, to rebirth.
It does not 'conquer' humanity, nor 'return' to the primitive, but reconfigures a new order of symbiosis through the cycle of matter.

Realm One: The Dark Side of the Lens – Are We Recording Nature, or Defining It?

The Ethics of Negative Space: Who Has the Right to Be Seen?

Hiroshi Sugimoto's Seascapes are the most silent "emptiness" in the history of photography. Long exposures erase waves, ships, birds—leaving only the horizon and a gradient of gray. This "emptiness" is not void, but a reverse gaze—it forces the viewer to step back and realize: beyond the lens, there are countless natures unnamed, unacknowledged, denied the "right to exist."

We photograph reflections on snow-capped peaks, deer in morning mist, snowfields under auroras—these are "worthy of being seen."
Dry riverbeds, cut stumps, seabirds tangled in plastic, stray cats on city fringes, bees killed by pesticides—these are "unworthy of being seen."
This selectivity is not accidental, but visual violence: we use aesthetics as a blade, slicing nature into "displayable" and "hidden," reducing ecological wounds to "ugly" or "unnatural."

More deeply: Does nature have the right to refuse being gazed upon?
When photographers disturb snow leopards for "perfect composition," when drones hover over nests during breeding season, when flashlights blind nocturnal animals—have we reduced nature to a passive object of observation, rather than a being with its own agency?

Donna Haraway's warning echoes: "To gaze is to wield power, and power is violence."
The lens is the embodiment of this power. It is not merely a tool, but a machine of cognitive colonization, objectifying, aestheticizing, and commodifying the non-human world.

Ecological philosopher Timothy Morton introduces the concept of hyperobjects—entities that transcend space and time, are nearly impossible to perceive directly, yet are everywhere: climate crisis, nuclear radiation, microplastics, biosphere degradation. They refuse to be framed, named, or turned into "landscapes." They remind us: nature is not "scenery," but a networked existence that resists full perception yet remains ever-present.

True nature photography begins with acknowledging the "negative space"—the ecological details filtered out by the lens: rotting leaves, polluted streams, roots wrapped in plastic, forgotten mosses. They exist silently, yet have never been "seen."
*The dark side of the lens is ecological conscience—it reveals the wounds we refuse to acknowledge.*

Realm Two: The Illusion of Algorithms – "Ideal Nature" as Humanity's Final Fortress

The Birth of the Cyborg Landscape: When AI Takes Over Vision

While the lens's concealment can still be reflected upon, algorithmic intervention completely reconstructs the logic of how "nature" is generated. A DALL·E-generated "aurora snow leopard," a MidJourney-painted "rainbow desert"—perfect pixels, "ideal" composition—yet entirely detached from ecological reality. These are not "captured" nature, but calculated illusions.

We are entering a post-human visual era: nature no longer needs to be "recorded"—it can be "generated." Algorithms are trained on the "classic compositions" of photographic history: Ansel Adams' tonal contrasts, Sebastião Salgado's grand narratives, National Geographic's "iconic moments." These become internalized as the visual grammar of "how nature should look." It won't generate a chaotic tundra or a turbid stream unless you input "pollution" or "ruins"—because in its database, "nature" has already been purified, romanticized, and aestheticized.

This is Heidegger's enframing (Gestell): technology is no longer a mere tool, but a system that "sets upon" the world, transforming it into visual resources—calculable, controllable, consumable. In AI's eyes, nature is not the emergence of being, but a "composable object," reduced to data material, waiting to be "revealed" as "beauty."

Even more dangerous: this "ideal nature" is shaping our perception in reverse. When social media floods with AI-generated "perfect forests" and "pristine lakes," real-world pollution, degradation, and extinction appear "unreal"—as if those are the anomalies, and the AI's illusions are the norm. We are falling into a collective visual hallucination: the more we see "beauty," the less we see "truth."

Yet the post-human perspective also opens new possibilities: if we train AI on ecological databases—satellite imagery, biodiversity monitoring, pollution maps, species migration patterns—teaching it "ecological truth" instead of "visual perfection," we might generate critical "post-nature images": images that acknowledge human gaze while exposing its limits; that show "beauty" while revealing its constructedness; that even actively generate "wound imagery"—like "seabirds entangled in plastic," "plants in radiation zones," "the breath of urban heat islands."

True algorithmic photography should not aim to "generate prettier pictures," but to "generate deeper questions." It is not an answer, but a continuous interrogation of visual hegemony.
*When AI becomes our eyes, we must teach it to see truth, not illusion; to question, not to replicate.*

Realm Three: Rebirth from the Ruins – When Nature Rewrites Civilization's Epitaph

The Cycle of Matter and Justice: How Vines Write History

While the lens conceals and algorithms fabricate, nature quietly unfolds a silent resistance in the ruins. Chernobyl's forests, Fukushima's abandoned villages, islands swallowed by rising seas, skyscrapers consumed by vines—here, human civilization retreats, and nature reclaims dominance.

But this "nature" is no longer romantic pastoralism. It is a traumatized, actively reconstructing, material existence. In Fukushima, wild boar populations surge in radiation zones, carrying radioactive isotopes—they are both victims and carriers, belonging to both nature and the ruins of human civilization. Their existence forces us to acknowledge: humans and non-humans are already deeply entangled, inseparable.

Vines climb abandoned hospitals, moss covers nuclear reactor walls, trees grow from concrete cracks—this is not a simple story of "nature defeating humans," but a cycle of matter: calcium from concrete, iron from steel, carbon from plastic—absorbed by plants, metabolized by animals, decomposed by microbes—re-entering the ecological loop. The ruins of human civilization become the nutrients for nature's rebirth.

Morton's hyperobjects emerge here: climate crisis, nuclear radiation, plastic pollution—existences that span space and time—force us to admit: we are already "symbiotic companions" (Haraway). We, animals, plants, microbes, even algorithms, are all co-weavers of Earth's living network. We are not "masters of nature," but co-bearers of ecological justice.

The vines in the ruins are the symbol of this symbiosis—they do not "conquer" human structures, but coexist with them: roots entwine steel, leaves cover glass, life is reborn in death. They are not victors, but witnesses, the pen with which nature rewrites civilization.
*Ruins are not an end, but a beginning—where nature rewrites civilization. What vines write is interspecies justice.*

Ecological Awakening: The Ethical Shift from "Capturing" to "Symbiosis"

The three realms, a layered evolution:
- Realm One reveals the violence of "seeing"—the lens as power;
- Realm Two reflects on the illusion of "truth"—algorithms as narrative;
- Realm Three witnesses the cycle of "existence"—ruins as rebirth.

Ultimately, they point to a deeper proposition: the ethical transformation of nature photography.

We must no longer aim to "capture," but to "witness";
no longer measure by "aesthetics," but by "symbiosis";
no longer center "humanity," but center interspecies responsibility as our ethical core.

Photography should become an ecological practice:
it documents wounds, and witnesses recovery;
it exposes illusions, and acknowledges limits;
it gazes at humans, and listens to nature;
it is not merely visual record, but ethical participation.

Practical Ethical Guidelines for Photography: Toward "Slow Ecological Photography"

  1. Slow Photography
    Reject the "perfect moment." Use long exposures, continuous recording, multi-temporal comparisons to capture nature's flow, uncertainty, and ecological rhythm. Let time become part of the lens, not a compressed instant.
  2. Negative Space Practice
    Actively photograph "unbeautiful" ecological details: rotting leaves, polluted streams, roots wrapped in plastic, urban "non-scenery." Challenge visual hegemony, and bring wounds into the legitimate domain of imagery.
  3. Critical AI Training
    Integrate ecological databases into AI models to generate "wound imagery": e.g., "seabirds entangled in plastic," "plants in radiation zones," "the breath of urban heat islands." Let AI become a tool of ecological critique, not a machine of illusion.
  4. Interspecies Responsibility Protocol
    Assess impact on non-human subjects before shooting. Establish an "ecological ethics review": Does it disturb animals? Damage habitats? Reinforce stereotypes? Make photography a responsible intervention, not a predatory gaze.
  5. Cultural Comparative Perspective
    Western "wilderness" narratives emphasize "uninhabited" landscapes, romanticizing nature as an "escape." Eastern "unity of heaven and humanity" emphasizes symbiosis and cycles—e.g., Chinese gardens' "borrowed scenery," Japanese mono no aware (beauty of impermanence), Indian animism's recognition of non-human agency. Future nature photography may integrate both, moving toward a decentered, de-instrumentalized, anti-anthropocentric ecological aesthetics.
"We are not photographing nature," an ecological photographer once said. "We are learning how to be photographed by nature."
—And this, is where ecological awakening begins.

When vines climb the lens, when algorithms learn silence, when ruins regain life, we finally understand:
Nature has never been absent. It has only been waiting for us to see it anew.

And true seeing is not when we see nature,
but when nature sees itself through us—
in the dark side of the lens,
in the cracks of algorithms,
at the top of the ruins,
in the breath of a vine,
in the silence of symbiosis.

*True nature photography is not when we see nature, but when nature sees itself through us—with scars as eyes, ruins as mirrors, vines as pens, rewriting Earth's poem.*
