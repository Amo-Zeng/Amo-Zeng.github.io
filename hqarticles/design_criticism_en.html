Design: Between Power, Emotion, and Virtual Mirrors

The Multifaceted Dimensions of Design: From Physical to Virtual Social Practices

Design, as an omnipresent social practice, has transcended mere material construction, becoming a key force shaping human cognition, emotion, and virtual experiences. In today's society, design profoundly influences our relationship with nature, manipulates or liberates our emotional experiences through the mechanisms of emotional labor, and participates in shaping new power structures within the metaverse, a virtual mirror of reality. This article attempts to explore how design operates within the complex social practices between power, emotion, and the virtual world from three dimensions: ecocriticism, emotional labor critique, and metaverse power dynamics, reflecting on its future possibilities.

Design and Ecology: The Interplay of Power and Responsibility

From an ecocritical perspective, design is both a creator of ecological crises and an enabler of ecological awareness. Through the symbolization of nature, spatial division, and product form creation, design reinforces anthropocentrism, transforming nature into objects of consumption and control. However, design also quietly nurtures a new ecological consciousness, attempting to establish a more harmonious relationship between humans and nature. Pioneering practices such as ecological architecture, circular design, and interactive installation art demonstrate that design can be a crucial medium for reflecting on and reconstructing the human-nature relationship.

The relationship between design and nature is fundamentally a projection of human cognition about their relationship with the world. By symbolizing nature, dividing space, and shaping product forms, design reinforces humanity's dominant position over nature, masking the fragility of ecosystems. However, this alienation and exploitation of nature ultimately lead to existential crises for humanity. Ecocriticism calls for design to transcend the limitations of anthropocentrism, moving beyond mere visual pleasure and functional satisfaction to focus on ecological sustainability, taking on the historical mission of guiding humanity to re-understand and respect nature. Only then can design truly become a bridge connecting humans and nature, rather than a blade that separates them.

Design and Emotion: The Game of Exploitation and Liberation

Design not only shapes our physical environment but also profoundly influences our emotional experiences. From the perspective of emotional labor critique, design exploits or liberates users' emotions through visual symbols and emotional manipulation. The concept of emotional labor was initially used to describe the emotional management process required by service industry workers, but its scope has expanded to encompass emotional expression and regulation in daily life. Design guides users' emotional reactions through the use of color, shape, material, and other elements, creating experiences ranging from joy and tranquility to excitement. This process of shaping emotional experiences is itself a form of emotional labor, with users becoming providers of emotional labor.

In a commercial society, design often becomes a tool for emotional manipulation, stimulating users' desires, anxieties, and fears to promote consumption and brand loyalty. More concerningly, emotional manipulation in design is increasingly being commodified, with users' emotional experiences quantified and converted into commercial value. However, design is not destined to exploit emotions; critical design practices explore how to use design to liberate users' emotions and empower self-expression. For example, interactive installation art and social issue design evoke public awareness of vulnerable groups and environmental protection through emotional expression.

Design in the Metaverse: Virtual Mirrors and Power Reconfiguration

The metaverse, as a digital utopia blending virtual and reality, is reshaping human society at an astonishing pace. The openness and decentralization of the metaverse offer the potential to challenge the power structures of the real world. However, the construction and operation of the virtual world rely on the resources and technology of tech giants, making them the dominant players in the virtual world and controlling platform rules, data resources, and user behavior. The power structures within the virtual world, through economic, cultural, and social dimensions, exert a reciprocal influence on the real world, exacerbating power concentration and the digital divide.

Design plays a crucial role in the metaverse, shaping not only the visual and functional experiences of virtual spaces but also participating in power dynamics within the virtual world. Platform monopolies and data control make the power structures of the virtual world as centralized as those in the real world. However, the metaverse also provides new opportunities for ethical reflection in design, with technological ethics, diverse participation, and legal regulation becoming key dimensions for building a fair virtual world. By emphasizing technological ethics, promoting diverse participation, and strengthening legal regulation, design critique can push for the decentralization of power in the metaverse and create a more equitable, free, and diverse virtual world.

The Multifaceted Dimensions of Design: From Physical to Virtual Social Practices

As a multifaceted social practice, design shapes our perception of nature, manipulates or liberates our emotional experiences, and participates in reshaping power structures in the metaverse. This multidimensional practice makes design not just a creator of physical environments but also a shaper of human cognition, emotion, and virtual experiences. The awakening of ecological awareness, the liberation of emotional labor, and the power dynamics in the metaverse together constitute the complex role of design in contemporary society.

Future design must transcend mere visual pleasure and functional satisfaction, taking on the historical mission of guiding humanity to re-understand and respect nature. At the same time, design should reflect on the value of emotional labor, avoiding emotional manipulation and commodification, instead empowering individuals for self-expression and creating healthier and more authentic emotional experiences. In the metaverse, design needs to promote the fair construction of virtual worlds through technological ethics, diverse participation, and legal regulation, ensuring it becomes a force for human civilization advancement rather than reinforcing power structures in the real world.

Conclusion

In an era fraught with ecological crises, emotional alienation, and virtual power struggles, design critique must move beyond aesthetics and technology, delving into how design shapes our perceptions and attitudes toward nature, emotion, and the virtual world. Only then can design truly become a force driving human civilization forward, rather than continuing to play the role of an accomplice to environmental destruction, a tool for emotional exploitation, and a booster for virtual power.
