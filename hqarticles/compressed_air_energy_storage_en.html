Underground Space Utilization and Technological Innovation: The Future of CAES Energy Storage Systems

The Key to Energy Storage Technology: The Future of CAES Energy Storage Systems

With the continuous growth of global energy demand and the rapid adoption of renewable energy, energy storage technology has become a critical driver of energy transition. Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES) is a highly efficient energy storage method, receiving widespread attention due to its long-term energy storage capabilities and high energy conversion efficiency. However, challenges such as site selection, construction, and operational costs have limited its widespread application. This article will explore the future development path of CAES energy storage systems from three perspectives: underground space utilization, the integration of multi-energy complementarity and energy internet, and technological and material innovations. We will also analyze the potential and challenges of CAES in the energy transition.

I. Underground Space Utilization: Exploring Abandoned Mines, Bunkers, and Urban Underground Energy Storage

1.1 The Potential of Transforming Abandoned Mines and Idle Bunkers

Abandoned mines and idle bunkers are ideal candidates for converting underground spaces into CAES gas storage facilities. Mines typically possess large underground spaces, stable rock formations, and good sealing properties, providing natural advantages for gas storage. Globally, many mines are abandoned after resource depletion, and utilizing these spaces could not only reduce land resource waste but also offer new solutions for energy storage.

The feasibility of transforming abandoned mines depends largely on their geological conditions. An ideal gas storage facility should feature stable rock layers, low permeability, sufficient storage capacity, and good sealing properties. Sedimentary rocks (e.g., sandstone, shale) are often considered ideal due to their low permeability and good sealing properties, while metamorphic and volcanic rocks may require additional sealing treatments. Additionally, the depth and scale of the mine influence the construction of the CAES gas storage facility. Mines with depths between 500 meters to 1000 meters generally have favorable temperature and pressure conditions, suitable for compressed air storage and release.

1.2 Feasibility Studies on Urban Underground Energy Storage

With the acceleration of urbanization, urban energy demand continues to increase, and the utilization of underground space has become a critical direction in urban planning. Urban underground energy storage can address the variability of urban energy supply and provide clean, efficient energy reserve solutions.

The selection of urban underground space requires a comprehensive consideration of geological conditions, space scale, and location factors. Ideal underground storage spaces should have large capacity, good sealing properties, and low permeability. Common underground spaces include subway tunnels, underground parking lots, and underground malls, though these spaces typically lack the necessary sealing and stability for gas storage and may require special treatments. In urban underground space planning, the construction of CAES facilities needs to be closely integrated with urban infrastructure to ensure the coordinated operation of the energy storage system with the power grid.

II. Multi-Energy Complementarity and the Energy Internet: Deep Integration of CAES with Renewable Energy

2.1 Complementarity of CAES and Renewable Energy: Addressing Intermittency Challenges

Renewable energy sources such as wind and solar power offer clean, low-carbon advantages, but their intermittency and variability are major obstacles to large-scale adoption. Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES), as a large-scale energy storage technology, can effectively address the variability of renewable energy. Its basic principle involves converting electrical energy into the potential energy of compressed air, which is then released during peak electricity demand to drive turbines for power generation.

2.2 CAES Applications in Virtual Power Plants and the Energy Internet

A Virtual Power Plant (VPP) integrates and manages distributed energy resources (e.g., wind, solar, energy storage systems) through a smart platform. By coordinating the operation of various energy resources, VPPs provide stable support to the grid while participating in electricity market transactions, enhancing the flexibility and economic efficiency of the energy system.

III. Technological and Material Innovations: Driving Revolution in CAES System Efficiency and Longevity

3.1 New Compression and Expansion Technologies: Dual Breakthroughs in Efficiency and Longevity

The compression and expansion processes are the core of the CAES system, directly impacting its overall efficiency and operational lifespan. Recent advancements in compressor and expander technologies have brought revolutionary changes to CAES systems.

3.2 High-Temperature Heat Storage and Solid-State Heat Storage Materials: Enhancing System Efficiency

In addition to innovations in compression and expansion technologies, the introduction of high-temperature heat storage and solid-state heat storage materials offers new pathways to improve CAES system efficiency. By utilizing waste or residual heat, these technologies not only enhance the system's overall energy utilization but also broaden the application scenarios for CAES.

IV. Conclusion

Underground space utilization, the integration of multi-energy complementarity and the energy internet, as well as technological and material innovations, provide multi-dimensional support for the future development of CAES energy storage systems. By effectively utilizing abandoned mines, bunkers, and urban underground spaces, the construction costs of CAES facilities can be significantly reduced, enhancing the efficiency and security of energy storage. Meanwhile, the deep integration of CAES with renewable energy, particularly through the application of virtual power plants and energy internet platforms, can further improve grid flexibility and economic efficiency.
