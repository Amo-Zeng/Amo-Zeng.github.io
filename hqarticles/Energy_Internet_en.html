Paradigm Reconstruction of Energy Internet and the Path to Carbon Neutrality

Paradigm Reconstruction of Energy Internet and the Path to Carbon Neutrality: Moving Towards an Intelligent, Green, and Sustainable Future

With the continuous growth of global energy demand and the increasing pressure of climate change, the Energy Internet, as an important means to drive energy system transformation, is facing unprecedented development opportunities and challenges. The traditional Energy Internet primarily focuses on the integration of the physical and information layers, optimizing energy production, transmission, and consumption through advanced sensors, communication technologies, and data analysis. However, this paradigm is insufficient in dealing with complex and ever-changing social factors. Changes in user behavior, socio-economic fluctuations, and adjustments in policies and regulations have profound impacts on the stability and efficiency of the energy system. Furthermore, the goals of climate change and carbon neutrality require a deep reconstruction of the Energy Internet in terms of value orientation, functional systems, and technical support, to meet the needs of low-carbon, environmentally friendly, and sustainable development.

1. Limitations and Challenges of Traditional Energy Internet

1.1 Limitations of the Physical-Information Binary Structure

The core architecture of the traditional Energy Internet is the binary integration of the physical and information layers. The physical layer includes energy production, transmission, and storage devices, while the information layer enables real-time monitoring and optimal scheduling of these devices through IoT, big data, and AI technologies. However, this binary structure overlooks the importance of the social layer. Factors such as user behavior, socio-economic factors, and policies and regulations have a significant impact on the operation of the energy system. For example, minor changes in user behavior can lead to dramatic fluctuations in electricity demand, while policy adjustments can affect the supply and demand balance in the energy market. Traditional energy systems often lack flexibility and adaptability when dealing with these social factors, leading to imbalances in energy supply and demand, reduced system efficiency, and even safety issues.

1.2 Lack of Real-Time Accurate Perception and Prediction Capabilities

Existing Energy Internet systems lack real-time accurate perception and prediction capabilities in the face of complex and ever-changing energy supply and demand environments. This is manifested in delayed data collection and processing, difficulties in fault prediction and proactive maintenance, and insufficient optimization control capabilities. These issues not only affect the operational efficiency of the energy system but also increase the probability of faults and the difficulty of maintenance.

1.3 Single Value Orientation Under the Carbon Neutrality Goal

The value system of the traditional Energy Internet is economically oriented, focusing primarily on improving energy efficiency and reducing costs, lacking a systematic and top-level design for low-carbon, environmentally friendly, and sustainable development. This single value orientation is difficult to meet the new requirements of the carbon neutrality goal for the energy system. Additionally, traditional Energy Internet platforms lack effective market mechanisms, making it difficult to guide users to actively participate in carbon reduction actions and to foster a low-carbon development pattern that involves the entire society.

2. Paradigm Reconstruction of the Energy Internet for the Future

2.1 The New Paradigm of Physical-Information-Social Integration in the Energy Internet

To address the above challenges, constructing a new paradigm of the Energy Internet based on physical-information-social integration becomes an inevitable choice. This paradigm incorporates user behavior, socio-economic factors, and policies and regulations into the modeling and optimization process of the energy system, enabling fine-grained regulation of energy supply and demand and intelligent upgrading of user interaction.

  • Modeling and Prediction of User Behavior: By integrating research findings from behavioral economics, psychology, and other fields, dynamic models of user electricity consumption behavior are constructed. For example, using the "prospect theory" from behavioral economics to predict changes in user behavior under different electricity pricing policies, and dynamically adjusting grid scheduling strategies to achieve supply and demand balance.
  • Incorporation of Socio-Economic Factors: In energy system modeling, socio-economic factors such as employment rate, economic growth rate, and energy prices are introduced to more accurately predict future energy demand and develop corresponding supply strategies.
  • Dynamic Response to Policies and Regulations: Policies and regulations are incorporated into the energy system model to achieve dynamic response to policy adjustments. For instance, when a new renewable energy subsidy policy is introduced by the government, the system can automatically adjust the proportion of renewable energy generation to maximize policy benefits.

2.2 Deep Integration of Digital Twin Technology and the Energy Internet

To solve the problem of insufficient real-time accurate perception and prediction capabilities, the deep integration of digital twin technology and the Energy Internet becomes a highly promising innovation direction. Digital twin technology, by constructing virtual models of physical systems, enables real-time monitoring, state prediction, fault diagnosis, and optimization control of the system, thereby enhancing the intelligence level and operational efficiency of the energy system.

  • Construction of Full-Factor Digital Twins: By applying digital twin technology to the Energy Internet, a digital twin of the energy system covering equipment, grid, and users can be constructed. This virtual model can reflect the operational state of the physical system in real time, providing data support for fine-grained management of the system.
  • Real-Time Monitoring and State Prediction: Digital twins can monitor all aspects of the energy system in real time and, through big data analysis and machine learning algorithms, predict the future state of the system and provide early warnings of potential faults.
  • Fault Diagnosis and Proactive Maintenance: Based on the fault diagnosis function of digital twins, precise localization and root cause analysis of system faults can be achieved, allowing for the development of effective maintenance strategies and proactive maintenance.

2.3 Value Reconstruction Towards the Carbon Neutrality Goal

Towards the carbon neutrality goal, the Energy Internet needs to redefine its value system, integrating low-carbon, environmentally friendly, and sustainable development concepts into the entire lifecycle of energy system planning, construction, and operation, building a green and low-carbon energy Internet ecosystem.

  • Value Orientation Reconstruction: Integrate low-carbon, environmentally friendly, and sustainable development concepts into the entire process of Energy Internet construction, transforming from a traditional single economic efficiency orientation to a multi-dimensional value orientation that balances economic, environmental, and social benefits.
  • Functional System Reconstruction: On the basis of traditional energy scheduling and trading functions, add carbon emission quota allocation, trading, and regulatory modules to build a complete carbon emission trading system, guiding users to actively participate in carbon reduction actions.

3. Case Analysis: Combination of Blockchain Technology, Digital Twin, and Carbon Emission Trading

3.1 Distributed Energy Trading Platform and Blockchain Technology

Blockchain technology provides technical support for the decentralization and transparency of energy trading. Through smart contracts, users can directly trade energy on the platform without relying on traditional power companies. This decentralized trading model not only reduces transaction costs but also enhances the transparency of transactions, ensuring security and traceability.

3.2 Virtual Power Plant Based on Digital Twin Technology

Virtual power plants build virtual models through digital twin technology to monitor the operational status of various distributed energy devices in real time, predict power generation and load changes, and dynamically adjust device output based on grid demand. This model not only enhances the flexibility and reliability of the power system but also promotes the consumption of renewable energy.

3.3 Construction of Carbon Emission Trading Platform

The Energy Internet platform based on carbon emission trading can deeply integrate carbon emission quota allocation, trading, and regulatory functions with energy scheduling and trading functions. By scientifically and reasonably allocating carbon emission quotas, establishing a carbon emission trading market, and using IoT and big data technologies to monitor corporate carbon emissions in real time, the fairness and justice of the carbon emission trading market can be ensured.

4. Conclusion

Towards the carbon neutrality goal, the paradigm reconstruction and value system reshaping of the Energy Internet become key to driving the energy system towards an intelligent, green, and sustainable direction. Through the new paradigm of a physical-information-social integrated Energy Internet, combined with digital twin technology and blockchain technology, the Energy Internet can not only achieve fine-grained regulation in response to complex social factors but also realize green and low-carbon transformation under the carbon neutrality goal. In this process, the synergy of technological innovation, policy guidance, and social participation is crucial, collectively propelling the Energy Internet towards an intelligent, green, and sustainable future.
