Multidimensional Analysis of Relative Deprivation: Interplay of Psychology and Economics

The phenomenon of relative deprivation is widespread in modern society, influencing not only the field of psychology but also deeply affecting various aspects of economics. This article explores the mechanisms, impacts, and coping strategies of relative deprivation from both psychological and economic perspectives.

Psychological Perspective: Cognitive Bias, Emotional Responses, and Self-Concept

Cognitive Bias: Self-Underestimation in Social Comparison

Social comparison is a fundamental way humans assess their living conditions. According to Social Comparison Theory (Festinger, 1954), people determine their social status and self-worth by comparing themselves with others. However, this comparison often involves cognitive biases, especially in today's social media-driven world. Individuals tend to see the carefully curated "best moments" of others, like travel, lavish lifestyles, or career achievements (Krasnova et al., 2013). This "upward comparison" leads individuals to overestimate others' happiness, resulting in self-underestimation and feelings of relative deprivation. For instance, studies have found that college students who frequently use social media are more likely to feel relatively deprived and dissatisfied with life than those who do not (Chou & Edge, 2012).

Emotional Responses: Negative Emotions Triggered by Deprivation

Relative deprivation triggers negative emotions such as jealousy, anger, disappointment, and helplessness. These emotions not only affect personal mental health but can also lead to changes in social behavior. For example, the "Occupy Wall Street" movement can be seen as a collective expression of relative deprivation (Jost et al., 2012). These emotions reflect not just individual feelings but also broader societal discontent and a desire for fairness.

Self-Concept: The Impact of Deprivation on Self-Perception

When individuals feel disadvantaged in social comparisons, their self-concept and self-esteem are significantly affected. Research indicates that this impact not only influences self-esteem but might also undermine one's sense of self-efficacy in facing life's challenges (Crocker & Karpinski, 1993). Over time, this can decrease life satisfaction and mental health.

Economic Perspective: Income Inequality, Consumerism, and the Labor Market

Income Inequality: The Economic Roots of Relative Deprivation

Income inequality is one of the economic roots of relative deprivation. Piketty (2014) noted that widening income gaps lead to social unrest and political instability, as seen during the Great Depression in the early 20th century in America, where feelings of relative deprivation spurred the implementation of the New Deal. Such policies not only increased social welfare but also reduced income disparities through progressive taxation and labor law reforms. Income inequality impacts consumer confidence and spending, potentially slowing economic growth.

Consumerism: The Economic Drive Behind Relative Deprivation

Consumerism is a significant driver of relative deprivation, with individuals attempting to elevate their social status and happiness through purchasing. For example, the widespread occurrence of credit card debt reflects people's overconsumption to maintain or improve their social status (Rucker & Galinsky, 2009). This consumer behavior not only increases personal and household debt levels but can also lead to economic bubbles and financial instability. Moreover, consumerism exacerbates environmental issues.

Labor Market: Dynamic Effects of Relative Deprivation

In the labor market, employees might become dissatisfied when they see colleagues promoted or earning higher wages, pushing companies to adjust their HR strategies. Akerlof and Kranton's (2000) "Economics of Identity" theory suggests that employees' motivations are influenced not only by economic incentives but also by social identity and status. When employees feel relatively deprived, they might demand better working conditions or higher wages.

Integrated Strategies: Psychological and Economic Interventions to Address Relative Deprivation

  1. Cognitive Reconstruction and Psychological Education: Educate and train individuals to understand the limitations of social comparison and recognize the unreality of social media portrayals. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) can help individuals reconstruct their perceptions of self and society, reducing unnecessary self-underestimation.
  2. Emotion Management and Support: Provide psychological support and counseling to help manage negative emotions. Emotional intelligence training can enhance individuals' ability to understand and regulate their emotions.
  3. Economic Policies and Social Welfare: Implement progressive taxation, raise minimum wages, and provide social security to reduce income inequality and alleviate feelings of relative deprivation.
  4. Enhancing Self-Efficacy and Economic Education: Encourage setting personal goals, community service, or learning new skills to boost self-efficacy. Economic education can help individuals consume more rationally, reducing debt burdens.

Conclusion

By integrating psychological and economic perspectives, we gain a deeper understanding of the complexity of relative deprivation and can develop comprehensive strategies to address it, fostering overall health and harmony in individuals and society. The analysis of relative deprivation not only reveals its underlying mechanisms but also points to the potential pathways for improvement through multidimensional interventions.
