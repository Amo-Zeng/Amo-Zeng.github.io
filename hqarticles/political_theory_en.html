Post-Truth, Algorithms, and Ecology: Three Dimensions of Reconstructing Political Theory

We are living in an unprecedented era where post-truth, algorithms, and ecological crises are reshaping political theory in ways that are both profound and far-reaching. These three forces are not isolated; they intertwine and collectively drive political theory from rational debate to emotional resonance, from centralized power to pluralistic governance, and from human-centric to ecological interdependence.

I. The Post-Truth Era: The Rise of Emotion, Narratives, and Identity

The advent of the post-truth era signals the waning dominance of traditional political theories based on rational debate and fact-checking. The proliferation of misinformation and deepfakes has made fact-verification exceedingly difficult. Social media algorithms trap people in "information cocoons," reinforcing existing biases and stances. The space for rational debate is shrinking, replaced by emotional partisanship and polarization, exacerbating political divides.

  • Emotional Politics: Exploring the role of emotion in political mobilization, decision-making, and governance, and how to guide and harness emotions for public benefit rather than leveraging them to create conflict.
  • Narrative Politics: Examining how narratives shape political realities and constructing more inclusive and just political narratives to counter misinformation and extremist narratives.
  • Identity Politics: Investigating the relationship between identity and political identity, and exploring how to build communities that transcend identity differences, fostering dialogue and understanding among diverse groups to mitigate societal divides.

II. The Algorithmic Era: The Reconfiguration of Power and the Construction of Digital Political Order

The rise of the algorithmic era is quietly reshaping the distribution, operation, and balance of power. Algorithmic recommendation systems act as invisible "information curators," shaping public information cocoons through personalized content filtering. This precise information targeting reduces opportunities for citizens to engage with diverse viewpoints, fragmenting and polarizing public discourse, affecting policy discussions and formulation.

  • Synergistic Governance of Technology and Institutions: Building a framework for the collaborative governance of technology and institutions, leveraging the benefits of algorithms while mitigating their risks.
  • Pluralistic Participation in Governance: Constructing a digital political order requires the joint participation of governments, enterprises, civil society, and the public.
  • International Cooperation to Address Global Challenges: Issues such as cross-border data flow and algorithmic applications are transnational, necessitating global cooperation to establish rules and address the political, economic, and social risks of the algorithmic era.

III. Ecological Politics: The Paradigm Shift from Human-Centric to Ecological Interdependence

Traditional political theories have long centered around state-centric and human-centric paradigms, focusing on issues such as inter-state relations, class struggle, and economic growth. However, with the escalating global ecological crises like climate change and biodiversity loss, this traditional paradigm is facing unprecedented challenges, demanding a profound theoretical revolution.

  • Ecological Justice: Emphasizing equal environmental rights for both present and future generations, as well as between humans and non-human species.
  • Intergenerational Equity: Focusing on the principle that development today should not compromise the living environment of future generations.
  • Rights Among Species: Advocating for the recognition of non-human species' agency and opposing their reduction to mere tools for human use.

Conclusion: Constructing a Pluralistic and Collaborative New Paradigm for Political Theory

The forces of post-truth, algorithms, and ecological crises are profoundly reshaping political theory. We need to adopt a more open and inclusive approach, exploring the roles of emotion, narratives, and identity in politics to build a more pluralistic and inclusive political theory to address post-truth challenges. Simultaneously, we need to construct a fair and just digital political order, maximizing the benefits of algorithms while effectively mitigating their risks. Finally, we must transcend the limitations of traditional state-centric and human-centric paradigms, fostering ecological politics that promote harmony between humans and nature.
