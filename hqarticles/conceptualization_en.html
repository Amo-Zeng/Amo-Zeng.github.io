The Reconstruction of Concepts: From Snackification to Memeification and Embodied Cognition

In this era of information explosion, our thinking is undergoing a transformation unlike any before. The fragmented intake of vast amounts of information, instant interactions on social media, and the sensory reconfiguration brought by new technologies are collectively driving a profound shift in how concepts are formed and disseminated. This shift not only changes the way we perceive the world but also redefines the essence and value of concepts themselves. This article explores this cognitive revolution from three dimensions: snackification, memeification, and embodied cognition.

1. Concept Snackification: The Crisis of Thought in the Fragmented Era

Information fragmentation is one of the most significant characteristics of today's society. We consume vast amounts of information fragments every day, much like snacking, quickly acquiring and quickly forgetting. This way of consuming information quietly changes how we perceive the world and reshapes the paths we take to form concepts. As we become accustomed to finding answers in fragments, summarizing complexity with brief sentences, and staying on the surface without delving into the essence, a profound crisis is emerging: our thoughts are being mercilessly snackified.

The fragmented reading method is the direct cause of concept snackification. In an era that emphasizes instant gratification, deep reading has become a luxury. People prefer to browse headlines, summaries, and short videos, obtaining information in a few seconds and then quickly moving on to the next. This reading method leads to fragmented knowledge accumulation; we can acquire a lot of information but struggle to connect it into a coherent cognitive framework. In fields requiring deep thinking, such as philosophy, art, and science, this fragmented thinking pattern makes it difficult for us to form systematic concepts, leaving us wandering at a shallow cognitive level.

The instant interactivity of social media further exacerbates this trend. On social media, the expression of views tends to be short, direct, and emotional. People are accustomed to expressing complex ideas with tag-like language and simple slogans instead of deep thinking. This phenomenon leads to concept crystallization, reducing diverse possibilities to a few fixed labels. For example, when discussing environmental protection, the label "environmentalism" may encompass different levels from personal lifestyle to policy-making, but we often stay at the label itself, ignoring the rich differences in its underlying connotations.

Concept snackification is not just a change in information acquisition methods but a fundamental transformation in thinking patterns. When we simplify complex problems into a few keywords and satisfy ourselves with surface-level understanding while abandoning deeper thought, we are losing our ability to handle complex issues. This transformation in thinking makes it difficult for us to propose innovative solutions when faced with increasingly complex real-world problems, often trapped within existing labels and frameworks.

To address the crisis of concept snackification, we need to rebuild our ability for deep thinking. This requires conscious systematic reading and cultivating critical thinking, but more importantly, we need to reevaluate the value of concepts. Concepts are not simple labels but tools to help us understand the world. In this era of information overload, we need to learn to find clues in fragments, construct order in chaos, and grasp deep patterns amidst surface fluctuations. Only in this way can we maintain intellectual independence and creativity in the information age, avoiding becoming victims of snackified thinking.

2. Concept Memeification: Cultural Genes and Cognitive Evolution

In the information age, the formation and dissemination of concepts are no longer isolated processes of individual cognition but are deeply embedded in social and cultural networks. Drawing on memetics, we can view concepts as cultural genes, exploring how they replicate, mutate, and evolve among people. This perspective not only reveals how concepts gain vitality through cultural dissemination but also how they adapt and evolve in different social contexts.

Meme Propagation and the Vitality of Concepts

As cultural genes, memes spread through imitation, learning, and dissemination among people. Concepts, as memes, have propagation mechanisms similar to biological genes but are more complex and rely on multi-level social interactions. The propagation of concepts is not just a process of information transmission but a process of cognitive reconstruction and meaning attribution. In this process, concepts undergo mutation, expansion, or simplification to adapt to different cultural contexts and understanding frameworks.

For example, the concept of "democracy" mutates as it spreads in different countries due to cultural backgrounds and social structures. In Western democratic countries, democracy is often closely related to individual freedom, power separation, and electoral systems; in some Asian countries, its meaning may lean more toward collective interests and social stability. This mutation is not random but driven by specific cultural values and social needs.

Popular Culture and Concept Reshaping

Popular culture and internet memes are important carriers of concept dissemination in modern society. Films, music, social media, and other cultural products not only transmit information but also redefine and reshape concepts through emotional resonance and visual impact. For instance, futuristic technological concepts like artificial intelligence and virtual reality in science fiction films profoundly influence the audience's cognition and imagination through visual storytelling and plot design. These cultural products are not just mediums of concept transmission but also significant shapers of concept内涵.

Internet memes, through simple and humorous forms, quickly spread a viewpoint or idea, becoming an essential means of concept simplification and popularization. For example, the concept of "lying flat" became popular on Chinese social media, reflecting both the younger generation's satire of social pressure and a redefinition of traditional success. The speed and reach of internet memes enable concepts to quickly cross geographical and cultural boundaries, becoming global cultural phenomena.

Concept Mutation and Social Change

Concept mutation is not only a reflection of cultural differences but also a response to social changes and group psychology. In a rapidly changing society, concept内涵 and extension continuously adjust with technological and institutional changes. For example, the concept of "work" was primarily associated with physical labor and factory production during the industrial age, while in the information age, it is more linked to knowledge creation, information processing, and personal creativity.

This change is not only a result of technological progress but also a reflection of social structure and value changes. The advent of the information age has made knowledge and creativity crucial production factors, replacing traditional production methods and occupational structures with new models. This shift not only changes how people understand work but also generates new conceptual systems, such as "remote work" and "freelancers."

Concept Evolution in Cross-Cultural Transmission

Concept memeification is particularly evident in cross-cultural transmission. Under globalization, many concepts originating in one culture spread to others, undergoing localization and reinterpretation. For example, the concept of "globalization" may emphasize economic integration and free trade in Western contexts but carry additional connotations of cultural conflict and identity in some developing countries.

This evolution in cross-cultural transmission not only reflects cultural differences but also reveals different social needs and understandings of concepts. Through this process, originally singular concepts gain diverse内涵, becoming bridges connecting different cultures. For instance, the concept of "sustainable development" has been widely disseminated worldwide, but its内涵 varies across regions due to resource conditions, economic development stages, and cultural values.

3. Concept Embodiment: The Power of Experience and Perception

Embodied cognition emphasizes the significant role of the body and perception in the cognitive process. Traditional cognitive science often focuses on logical reasoning and abstract thinking while overlooking the importance of bodily experience and perception. From the perspective of embodied cognition, we can explore how the body participates in concept formation.

For example, how does kinesthetic experience help us form spatial and movement-related concepts? How do sensory experiences like taste and smell influence our understanding of concepts related to food and emotions? How do new technologies like virtual reality reshape our perceptual experiences, thereby affecting concept formation?

Through these questions, we can gain a deeper understanding of the role of the body in concept formation. For instance, dance and movement are not just physical activities but accumulations of kinesthetic experiences. Through this experience, we can better understand concepts related to space, movement, and force. Taste and smell, through direct sensory stimulation, influence our understanding of concepts related to food, emotions, and pleasure. Virtual reality technology, by simulating real perceptual experiences, redefines our cognitive boundaries, enabling new concept experiences in virtual worlds.

Diversity in Embodied Cognition

Embodied cognition is not just the simple accumulation of bodily experiences but a comprehensive effect of multiple perceptual experiences. For example, vision and hearing play crucial roles in concept formation. Vision transmits information through images and symbols, helping us form abstract concepts; hearing transmits information through sound and language, helping us understand complex social relationships and cultural backgrounds. These perceptual experiences work together to form our cognitive system.

For instance, in learning a new language, the combined effect of vision and hearing is indispensable. By integrating visual symbols (such as words and images) with auditory symbols (such as pronunciation and tone), we can better understand and master the concepts and structures of the new language. Similarly, in understanding complex scientific concepts, the combined effect of vision and hearing helps us grasp abstract theories and models more effectively.

Applications of Embodied Cognition

The theory of embodied cognition not only provides new perspectives on understanding concept formation but also has extensive applications in education, healthcare, and technology. In education, designing embodied learning environments allows students to better understand abstract concepts through bodily experiences and perceptual activities. The application of virtual reality technology enables students to perform actual operations and experiences in virtual worlds, improving learning outcomes.

In healthcare, the theory of embodied cognition helps us better understand patients' pain and disease experiences, enabling the design of more personalized treatment plans. For example, by using virtual reality technology to simulate patients' pain experiences, doctors can better understand patients' feelings, providing more accurate treatment.

In technology, the theory of embodied cognition helps us design more human-centric intelligent systems. For example, by simulating human perceptual experiences, intelligent robots can better understand and respond to human needs, enhancing the effectiveness of human-computer interaction.

Conclusion: The Cognitive Revolution from a Meme Perspective

Concept memeification reveals a profound phenomenon: concepts are not just products of individual cognition but crystallizations of social and cultural interactions. By viewing concepts as cultural genes, we can better understand how they spread, mutate, and evolve among people. This perspective not only provides a window into cultural dissemination and social change but also offers possibilities for building a more adaptive and innovative cognitive system in the information age.

In future social development, concept memeification will continue to drive the cognitive revolution, helping us better understand and address the challenges of globalization and multiculturalism. Through this perspective, we can not only capture the dynamic trajectory of concept evolution but also open new paths for future cultural and knowledge innovation.

Conclusion: The Reconstruction of Concepts and Future Visions

In this era of information explosion, our thinking is undergoing an unprecedented transformation. From snackified information intake to memeified cultural dissemination and embodied perceptual experiences, the ways concepts are formed and disseminated are profoundly changing. This transformation not only changes how we perceive the world but also redefines the essence and value of concepts.

By rebuilding our ability for deep thinking, we can find true wisdom in the ocean of information, avoiding becoming victims of snackified thinking. By understanding concept memeification, we can better grasp the dynamics of cultural dissemination, promoting cognitive innovation and evolution. Through the perspective of embodied cognition, we can gain a deeper understanding of the interaction between the body and perception, opening new directions for future cognitive science.

On the road ahead, we not only need technological progress but also intellectual innovation. Only through continuous reflection and reconstruction can we find our own cognitive path in this complex and ever-changing world.
