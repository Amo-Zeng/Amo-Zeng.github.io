The Deep Dilemma of Anti-Corruption: Systemic Reconstruction and Civilizational Leap in the Triple Dimensions of Institution, Culture, and Technology

— A Civilizational Revolution on Justice, Cognition, and Technology

In the evolution of human civilization, corruption is not merely an accidental "abuse of power," but a systemic pathological phenomenon arising from the interplay of weak institutional resilience, distorted cultural cognition, and technological alienation. It is not only a distortion of power, but also a litmus test for governance civilization. When we treat anti-corruption as a profound campaign of civilizational advancement, we must move beyond the superficial narrative of "cracking down on tigers and swatting flies" and confront the three structural dilemmas beneath: How do institutions self-corrupt? Why does culture condone corruption? How is technology weaponized against anti-corruption? These three dimensions are not isolated variables, but a dynamically nested, mutually reinforcing "anti-corruption ecosystem." This article analyzes the generative logic of corruption through the core dimensions of institutional resilience, cultural cognitive justice, and technological ethics, introduces cutting-edge concepts such as "institutional corruption," "epistemic justice," "technological alienation," and "reflexive governance," and constructs a systemic meta-governance framework for anti-corruption, proposing the civilizational value of the Chinese approach in global anti-corruption governance.

Chapter 1: Institutional Resilience — The Self-Corruption of Anti-Corruption Mechanisms and the Meta-Governance Dilemma

The Hidden "Blind Spot of Institutional Immunity"

Anti-corruption institutions are often likened to a nation's "immune system"—capable of identifying threats, eliminating pathogens, and restoring order. Yet, just as the immune system can suffer from autoimmune disease due to genetic flaws or chronic stress, anti-corruption mechanisms themselves can become breeding grounds for corruption. This phenomenon is termed institutional corruption (Institutional Corruption): the process by which an institution, due to structural flaws, distorted incentives, or centralized power, deviates from the public interest and begins serving private or group interests.

When supervisors lack checks, enforcers instrumentalize power, and incentives are hijacked by performance metrics, anti-corruption mechanisms transform from an "immune system" into a "cancer cell," silently spreading through the institutional body. Its core manifestation is corruption to fight corruption—using corrupt logic to execute anti-corruption, ultimately falling into a self-legitimizing vicious cycle.

Three Mechanisms of Alienation: From Function to Dysfunction

  1. Supervisory Absence: The Paradox of Supervisors and "Information-Monopoly Corruption"
    Anti-corruption agencies often consolidate investigation, prosecution, and adjudication powers, creating a "super-authority." But "who supervises the supervisor?" This meta-governance dilemma (Meta-governance Dilemma) has long been neglected. The Ming Dynasty's Eastern Depot and Jinyiwei, established to "eliminate corruption," rapidly evolved into tools of political terror due to the absence of judicial checks. In contemporary cases, discipline inspectors extort "consulting fees" from families of investigated officials using preliminary investigation clues—a classic case of secondary corruption: using anti-corruption as a cover for extortion. Its essence is information monopoly as rent-seeking.
  2. Implementation Alienation: Selective Justice and Politicization
    Anti-corruption is often embedded in factional struggles, with vague standards, "packaged" evidence, and selective enforcement, eroding its public legitimacy. A provincial official was charged with inflated "embezzlement amounts" to mask a political purge. This selective justice not only damages the rule of law but also undermines public trust, creating a crisis of anti-corruption legitimacy.
  3. Distorted Incentives: "Anti-Corruption for Anti-Corruption's Sake" under Performanceism
    When case numbers and recovered funds become KPIs, grassroots units resort to "padding cases" or "overcharging" minor violations. Some regions see officials investigated for petty misconduct to meet quotas, while real corruption goes unchecked. This number game deviates from the original purpose of prevention and institution-building, reducing anti-corruption to formalism and falling into the trap of performance alienation (Performance Alienation).

Case Synthesis: Mirrors of History and Reality

  • Ming Dynasty Eastern Depot: From anti-corruption agency to political terror machine, illustrating the iron law: "Power without oversight breeds corruption";
  • Contemporary "Hunting-Style Anti-Corruption": Discipline inspectors extorting investigated parties using information advantage, exposing "information monopoly as rent-seeking";
  • Singapore's CPIB: Tripartite oversight (executive, judicial, audit), parliamentary hearings, and independent evaluations have ensured 70 years without major internal corruption—a global model of institutional resilience built on external checks + transparency + independent assessment.

Theoretical Elevation: Institutional Resilience and Meta-Governance

Institutional resilience is not merely resistance to shocks, but the capacity for self-correction and immune renewal. When anti-corruption becomes a closed system—information monopolized, power centralized, feedback absent—it enters an institutional immunity blind spot, unable to detect and eliminate internal corrupt mutations. This leads to the meta-governance proposition: governors themselves must be governed. Anti-corruption cannot rely solely on "internal self-inspection," but requires a tripartite structure of external checks, transparent accountability, and independent evaluation.

Proposals: Building an "Anti-Corruption Mechanism for Anti-Corruption Institutions"

  • Separation of Powers: Separate investigation, adjudication, and appeal functions to avoid "referees who are also players";
  • Transparency: Implement "sunshine anti-corruption," publicly disclosing key stages, piloting blockchain-based evidence storage;
  • Independent Evaluation: Establish an "Anti-Corruption Effectiveness Evaluation Committee" composed of the People's Congress, Audit Office, and universities, conducting triennial assessments;
  • De-KPI-ization: Replace quantitative KPIs with qualitative metrics like "corruption prevention index," "public trust," and "institutional resilience score";
  • Cultural Embedding: Cultivate a culture of "institutional humility," making anti-corruption personnel recognize that "power itself is the object of supervision."

Yet, institutions do not operate in isolation. No matter how well-designed, if embedded in a cultural soil that de-moralizes corruption, institutions become like roots in saline soil—unable to draw nourishment from justice. The crisis of institution is, in essence, a crisis of culture—and the crisis of culture is ultimately the absence of epistemic justice (Epistemic Justice).

Chapter 2: Cultural Cognition — The "Moral Immunity" of Corruption and the Reconstruction of Epistemic Justice

The Cognitive Fog of Corruption: Why Is Corruption Not Seen as "Evil"?

In the grand narrative of anti-corruption, we often analyze "institutional flaws" or "rent-seeking," yet overlook the deepest question: Why do certain corrupt acts, in specific social contexts, go uncondemned, even normalized, glorified, or celebrated? When "accepting gifts to get things done" is called "social reciprocity," and "public fund banquets" are seen as "relationship-building," corruption completes its transformation from "moral evil" to "cultural norm." This moral immunity does not stem from individual depravity, but from a de-moralization mechanism deeply embedded in cultural scripts.

Three Mechanisms of De-Moralization

  1. Cultural Script Reconfiguration: Semantic Substitution and Moral Grammar Disruption
    In "relationship-based" societies, corruption is re-coded. "Giving gifts" becomes "maintaining relationships," "backdoor dealings" become "efficiency-first." A grassroots official admitted: "If you don't give, the project won't be approved; if you do, everyone understands—it's called 'lubricant.'" Semantic substitution removes the act from the domain of "evil," creating moral grammar disruption (Moral Grammar Disruption).
  2. Collective Cognitive Inertia: Complicit Silence and "Normalization"
    The "broken windows effect" emerges: when minor corruption becomes widespread, moral boundaries blur. A retired director recalled: "In the 1990s, giving cigarettes and alcohol during holidays was 'custom'—if you didn't, you lost out." Collective silence creates social pressure, making it difficult for individuals to resist, leading to epistemic complicity (Epistemic Complicity).
  3. Intergenerational Transmission: The Emergence of Adaptive Ethics
    Young civil servants grow up in an environment of unwritten rules, developing "adaptive ethics"—flexible interpretations of rules to survive. A newly hired official said: "At first I thought gift-giving was wrong, but when I saw others succeed by doing it, I had to convince myself: 'It's for the job.'" This reflects adaptive moral degradation.

Case Synthesis: Mirrors of Cultural Cognition

  • Field Research: "Spring Festival condolence funds" redefined as "team cohesion-building expenses," creating patron-client relationships;
  • Oral History: Retired officials describe gift-giving as "responsibility" and "serving the people," reframing corruption as morally justified;
  • Cross-National Comparison: Nordic societies emphasize "institutional trust," East Asia relies on "relational trust"—culture defines the public-private boundary;
  • New Media Discourse: Narratives like "corrupt officials have feelings" or "corruption for the public good" reflect public emotional ambivalence toward corruption, revealing epistemic justice fractures.

Theoretical Elevation: From Moral Immunity to Epistemic Justice

The "moral immunity" of corruption is essentially the re-coding of cultural cognition. When "relationships," "reciprocity," and "collective interest" dominate discourse, corruption shifts from "individual evil" to "systemic necessity." This reveals: anti-corruption cannot rely solely on institutional pressure, but must reconstruct society's moral grammar—establishing new linkages between "integrity and dignity," "integrity and trust." This points to the core of epistemic justice: Who has the authority to define what is "just behavior"? Whose voices are excluded from moral discourse?

Proposals: Pathways for Cultural Transformation

  • Reconstruct Public Discourse: Use media, education, and arts to reshape the narrative of integrity, breaking the cognitive binding of "corruption = efficiency" or "corruption = survival";
  • Institutionalize "De-Personalization": Implement "sunshine etiquette," establish gift registration systems, making "reciprocity" transparent;
  • Intergenerational Ethics Intervention: Introduce "ethical scenario simulations" in civil service training to cultivate integrity-based decision-making;
  • Localization of International Experience: Learn from Nordic "transparency culture," pilot "integrity credit systems," linking integrity to promotion;
  • Public Participation: Establish "community integrity observers" to break "complicit silence," achieving decentralized cognition.

Yet, as culture begins to transform, technology emerges as a new variable. In the era of digital governance, technology should be a "divine weapon" for anti-corruption, yet it may also become a tool for corrupt actors to "hijack legitimacy." Technology is reshaping the form and boundaries of corruption—we now face a deep crisis of technological alienation (Technological Alienation).

Chapter 3: Technological Ethics — Algorithmic Corruption and the Reflexive Dilemma of Technology

From "Empowerment" to "Backlash"

Big data, AI, and blockchain are widely hailed as the "smart eyes" of anti-corruption. Yet, technology is not value-neutral. When corrupt actors systematically "reverse-engineer" these tools, technology becomes a new weapon for evading oversight, entrenching interests, and legitimizing violations. We now face algorithmic corruption (Algorithmic Corruption)—not a failure of technology, but the inevitable result of technology being re-coded by power and profit.

Three Pathways: How Technology Is Weaponized

  1. Data Poisoning and Model Deception
    Corrupt actors falsify clean records (e.g., deleting suspicious transactions), training AI to learn that "corruption is compliance." A city's "integrity profiling" system was "poisoned by reverse training," misjudging officials' integrity.
  2. Algorithmic Collusion and Selective Transparency
    In inter-departmental data sharing, corrupt actors upload only harmless fields or encrypt critical logs. More subtly, they "label manipulation"—marking violations as "special approvals" or "emergencies" to bypass alerts, forming algorithmic collusion (Algorithmic Collusion).
  3. The Paradox of Technological Empowerment: Blockchain's "Legitimization Trap"
    Corrupt actors pre-upload fake contracts to the blockchain, gaining "technological endorsement." In 2022, a country used blockchain for subsidy distribution; local officials controlled nodes, prioritizing transactions for allies—making corruption "compliant." This is the epitome of technological alienation: technology created for oversight becomes a "cloak of legitimacy" for corruption.

Theoretical Elevation: The Reflexive Crisis of Technological Governance

"Algorithmic corruption" reveals the reflexive dilemma (Reflexive Dilemma) of technological governance: technology is created to overcome corruption, yet its black-box nature, complexity, and data dependence provide corrupt actors with new operational interfaces. This confirms Andrew Feenberg's insight: technology is not neutral, but shaped by social power structures. When technology becomes a symbol of governance authority, corrupt actors seek to "hijack" its legitimacy.

Proposals: Building "Algorithmic Ethics Review" and "Technological Counter-Capabilities"

  • Establish an "Algorithmic Ethics Review Committee": Conduct full-life-cycle evaluations of anti-corruption AI and blockchain, including data authenticity audits, mandatory algorithmic explainability, and minimal permission design;
  • Develop "Technological Counter-Capabilities": Deploy "anti-AI" detection tools, establish data integrity verification, and introduce adversarial training;
  • Enact "Sunshine Algorithm" Legislation: Mandate algorithmic transparency, define "security exceptions" to avoid full disclosure or complete black-boxing;
  • Train "Tech-Ethics" Interdisciplinary Supervisors: Equip disciplinary inspection agencies with professionals skilled in both algorithms and ethics.

Conclusion: Toward Systemic Anti-Corruption — Civilizational Advancement and Global Vision

Anti-corruption has never been a single-dimensional battle. Institution, culture, and technology form a dynamically nested "anti-corruption ecosystem": institutions are the skeleton, culture the soil, technology the nervous system. When these three dimensions are unbalanced, the system falls into a triple dilemma—

  • Institutional Dilemma: Anti-corruption mechanisms self-corrupt, falling into a "corrupt anti-corruption" cycle;
  • Cultural Dilemma: Corruption is de-moralized, forming "complicit silence";
  • Technological Dilemma: Anti-corruption tools are weaponized, breeding "algorithmic corruption".

This demands we transcend "band-aid" governance and propose a "triple-dimensional synergistic governance" meta-framework:

1. Institutional Dimension: Building a "Meta-Governance Chain"

Establish a tripartite structure: "supervision — being supervised — third-party evaluation," achieving self-balancing of anti-corruption power.

2. Cultural Dimension: Launching "Moral Grammar Reconstruction"

Break the cognitive binding of "corruption = survival" or "corruption = efficiency," establish new linkages between "integrity and dignity," "integrity and trust," achieving epistemic justice.

3. Technological Dimension: Implementing "Algorithmic Ethics Review"

Ensure technology serves the people, not power, resisting technological alienation.

Synergistic Mechanism: Dynamic Feedback and Systemic Immunity

These three dimensions should form a dynamic feedback loop: technological transparency → enhanced cultural cognition → strengthened institutional trust; cultural de-moralization → reduced institutional resistance → decreased technological evasion; institutional independent evaluation → forces technological compliance → reshapes cultural legitimacy.

The ultimate goal is to build an anti-corruption civilization with "systemic immunity": institutions capable of self-renewal, culture of self-purification, technology of self-defense.

Global Vision: The Civilizational Value of the Chinese Approach

In global anti-corruption governance, Western models often rely on "institutional checks" and "judicial independence," yet frequently fall into "selective enforcement" and "politicized anti-corruption." The Chinese approach offers a new civilizational path:
- Rooted in institutional resilience, emphasizing "self-revolution" over external checks;
- Founded on cultural reconstruction, advancing "epistemic justice" and "moral grammar" reform;
- Pioneering technological ethics, exploring "algorithmic justice" and "digital integrity";
- Centered on the people, integrating anti-corruption into "whole-process people's democracy," achieving unified governance legitimacy and social trust.

This is not merely institutional innovation, but a leap in governance civilization—from "fear-based integrity" to "voluntary integrity," from "supervision of power" to "civilizational consciousness."

Epilogue

Anti-corruption is not merely a contest of power, but of civilization. It tests whether a society can, across the triple dimensions of institution, culture, and technology, achieve a systemic reconstruction of corruption. Only then can we break the cycle of "more anti-corruption, more corruption," and enter a new civilizational realm where integrity becomes the norm, and corruption loses its soil. This is the ultimate meaning of anti-corruption—a civilizational revolution on justice, cognition, and technology.
