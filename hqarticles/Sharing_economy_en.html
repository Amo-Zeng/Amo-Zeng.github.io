The Evolution of the Sharing Economy: Triple Paradigm Shift in Institutions, Technology, and Psychology

Introduction: From Resource Optimization to Ecosystem Co-creation

Over the past decade, the sharing economy has rapidly risen, with its mainstream narrative long dominated by "monetizing idle resources" and "platform efficiency optimization." From Airbnb's home sharing to Uber's ride-hailing services, the sharing model has been seen as an efficiency innovation for traditional resource allocation systems. However, with the iteration of digital technology and the structural evolution of social needs, the core issues of the sharing economy are undergoing a profound transformation: the key challenge is no longer limited to "how to match resources more efficiently," but extends to "how to build a sustainable digital ecosystem featuring user co-governance, technology empowerment, and emotional connection".

This transformation requires us to go beyond the traditional logic of "usage right sharing" and the "platform-centric" structure, re-understanding the sharing economy from three dimensions: institutional design, technological integration, and social psychology. The institutional level requires reconstructing power and value distribution mechanisms; the technological level needs to break physical limitations and achieve intelligent construction of dynamic contexts; the psychological level needs to focus on the ethical foundation of emotional capital and non-monetized interactions. Together, they point to a higher-order form of the sharing economy: moving from resource optimization to ecosystem co-creation.

I. Institutional Innovation: From Platform Monopoly to Co-governance and Sharing — DAO and Decentralized Governance

Traditional sharing economy platforms (such as Uber, Airbnb), while achieving distributed resource supply, have strengthened the platform's centralized control. Resource providers are placed at the end of the value chain, while the platform obtains the vast majority of profits through algorithmic hegemony, data monopoly, and rule-making. This model not only causes unfair value distribution but also leads to issues like lack of trust and ecosystem fragility.

Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) provide a path to break through at the institutional level for the sharing economy. They rely on blockchain smart contracts to codify rules and return decision-making power and profit rights to users through governance token mechanisms, thereby achieving true co-construction, co-governance, and sharing.

Restructuring and Empowerment of Governance Structures

Users transform from passive service providers into collaborative builders and governors of the ecosystem. Taking the decentralized transportation network DIMO as an example, car owners earn governance tokens by sharing driving data and participate in voting on fee rules and data usage policies. According to DIMO's 2023 annual report, data contribution from governance-participating car owners was 42% higher than non-participants, and platform commission rates dropped from the traditional 20%~30% to 5%, with adjustments decided by community consensus.

Technologically Embedded Trust Mechanisms

Smart contracts enable automatic rule execution and full transaction traceability, reducing trust-building costs; staking mechanisms bind user credit to behavioral interests, creating a game environment that incentivizes trustworthiness and punishes dishonesty, significantly suppressing fraud. This mechanism corroborates the "polycentric governance" theory proposed by institutional scholar Elinor Ostrom: when users truly participate in rule-making and profit-sharing, the system is more likely to achieve self-discipline, mutual trust, and long-term stability.

The Tokenization Revolution in Value Distribution

DAOs incorporate data value, network effects, and governance contributions into the distribution system through token economic models, enabling users to share the multi-dimensional value they create. This not only improves economic efficiency but also enhances system resilience and participants' sense of belonging.

II. Technological Integration: From Physical Sharing to Context Reconstruction — The Spatial Revolution Driven by Sensing Technologies

Most traditional sharing economies rely on the static segmentation and timed use of physical space, such as shared offices or short-term rentals. While this improves space utilization, it is constrained by spatiotemporal rigidity and functional singularity.

The integrated development of spatial sensing technologies (including AR/VR, digital twins, and IoT) is pushing the sharing economy from "space reuse" towards "context sharing." It赋予 the same physical location with diverse meanings and functions through augmented reality overlays, real-time data mapping, and dynamic responses.

AR/VR: Context Switching Through Virtual-Real Fusion

Augmented and Virtual Reality technologies support the overlay of multiple virtual contexts within the same physical space. For example, in a shared studio, a designer can manipulate 3D models through an AR interface, while a meditator uses VR to enter an immersive relaxation environment, achieving multi-functional and emotional development of the space.

Digital Twin: Dynamic Optimization and Predictive Management

Using IoT sensors, the system collects real-time environmental data and constructs a high-fidelity digital mirror, enabling it to predict user needs in advance and automatically adjust resource allocation. An empirical study by the MIT Media Lab in 2022 showed that shared workspaces using digital twin models achieved a 28% improvement in energy efficiency and a 35% increase in user satisfaction.

The Rise of Situation-as-a-Service

Sharing platforms are gradually transforming from "space suppliers" to "context builders." Their pricing mechanisms will also move beyond单一的 area or duration dimensions towards dynamic pricing based on situational complexity, immersion depth, and real-time data dimensions. This转型 extends sociologist Manuel Castells's "space of flows" theory, redefining space as a programmable, interactive socio-technical interface.

III. Social Psychology: From Transaction Logic to Emotional Connection — Emotional Capital and Psychological Accounting Mechanisms

Sharing economy behavior is not only an economic exchange but also the construction and maintenance of social relationships. Traditional models overemphasize rationality and efficiency, neglecting the underlying social psychological dimension—trust, reputation, reciprocity, and other emotional elements are also key capital supporting the sharing ecosystem.

The Theoretical Connotation of Emotional Capital

This concept, further expanded by sociologist Pierre Bourdieu, is seen as an important form of social capital, including trust, goodwill, and reputation accumulated through持续 cooperation and善意 behavior. For example, a shared host's friendly interaction not only brings economic returns but also accumulates potential emotional assets and social credit.

Micro-social Debt and Psychological Accounts

Users often develop an implicit sense of obligation to reciprocate after receiving additional services or emotional support, i.e., "micro-social debt." Richard Thaler's "mental accounting" theory provides the basis for understanding this mechanism: users "keep accounts" for emotional input and social回报 in their minds and adjust their subsequent behavior accordingly.

Quantification of Emotional Capital and Mechanism Design

Future sharing systems need to develop more sophisticated emotional incentive and clearing tools:

  • Introduce "emotional points" and credit rating systems, allowing friendly behavior to be exchanged for priority or physical services;
  • Design debt clearing algorithms to recommend reciprocity opportunities to maintain a良性循环 of relationships;
  • Establish community consensus mechanisms to prevent emotional exploitation and credit fraud.

However, we must also be wary of the potential ethical crises of emotional capitalization: including exploitation of emotional labor, instrumentalization of interpersonal relationships, increased psychological burden, and algorithmic fairness issues. These require platforms to incorporate more ethical considerations and humanized design.

IV. Synergistic Resonance: The Triple Linkage of Institutions, Technology, and Psychology

Institutional innovation, technological integration, and social psychology do not develop independently but are coupled and co-evolve, forming a reinforcing loop for the sustainable development of the sharing ecosystem:

  1. Institutions set the governance framework for technology: The democratic governance mechanisms of DAOs can supervise the ethical boundaries of spatial sensing technologies. For example, deciding privacy protection levels in AR scenes through community voting, or jointly reviewing data usage policies for digital twins.
  2. Technology enhances institutional operation and psychological experience: Digital twins and VR technology can visualize policy impacts, lower decision-making thresholds, and enhance the sense of participation in governance; affective computing technology can provide real-time feedback on user emotional states, optimizing interaction design to增强 trust and satisfaction.
  3. Psychological capital provides the social foundation for institutions and technology: Emotional trust and micro-social debt are the "social glue" that maintains DAO activity and technology acceptance. Good psychological experiences can feed back into willingness to participate in governance and use technology, forming a positive feedback loop.
  4. The reinforcing loop of triple linkage: Users participate in governance through DAOs (Institution) → Receive tokens and emotional回报 (Psychology) → Obtain high-quality experiences in AR/VR contexts (Technology) → Further contribute and govern (Institution)... This cycle continuously pushes the system towards a higher-order ecosystem.

V. Future Landscape and Ethical Challenges

The future sharing economy will manifest as a three-dimensional integrated ecosystem of user co-governance, technology empowerment, and emotional connection. Its possible forms include:

  • Urban Governance DAOs: Communities use token democracy to decide on public resource usage;
  • Dynamic Context Sharing Platforms: High-level services like education and healthcare achieve平民化 access through AR/VR;
  • Emotional Credit Economies: Blockchain-based emotional point systems promote community cooperation and trust accumulation.

However, this evolution also comes with significant challenges:

  • Institutional Level: Need to address bottlenecks in DAOs regarding legal recognition, governance efficiency, and technological inclusivity;
  • Technological Level: Sensor monitoring and data capture raise privacy concerns; issues of algorithmic manipulation and the digital divide cannot be ignored;
  • Psychological Level: Emotional monetization may induce behavioral alienation and relational utilitarianism, requiring the protection of the "humanistic bottom line" of non-utilitarian social interactions.

Addressing these challenges requires promoting the implementation of the following principles:

  • Uphold user ownership of data and emotional information;
  • Advocate for algorithmic transparency and community co-governance;
  • Design hybrid incentive models, reserving autonomous space for emotional and non-monetized interactions;
  • Strengthen technological inclusivity to prevent digital exclusion.

Conclusion: Towards an Ecological Future of Co-governance, Co-existence, and Sharing

The future of the sharing economy is far more than just technological upgrades or model expansions; it is a deep-level协同进化 of institutional restructuring, technological integration, and psychological identification. DAOs redefine power and value distribution, sensing technologies reconstruct the usage of space and context, and emotional capital lays the psychological foundation for trust and cooperation.

These triple paradigm shifts collectively propel the sharing economy from the primary stage of "transaction efficiency" to the advanced form of "ecosystem co-creation." Its ultimate goal is not to pursue极致 resource allocation, but to build a human-centric, fair, resilient, and sustainable digital society—where technology empowers without controlling, institutions regulate without suppressing, and emotions connect without exploiting. We stand at such a historical juncture: only by transcending traditional sharing logic can we truly move towards a future of co-governance, sharing, and co-existence.
