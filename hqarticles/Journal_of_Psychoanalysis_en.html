The Unconscious Theatre of the Digital Age: Social Media, Algorithms, and the New Dimensions of Psychoanalysis in Remote Therapy

In the wave of the digital age, social media, algorithms, and remote therapy together construct a new unconscious theatre. This theatre not only profoundly changes our relationships with ourselves, others, and the world, but also reshapes the core concepts of psychoanalysis, such as self-identity, transference, and countertransference. This article attempts to explore the intersection of these three fields from a psychoanalytic perspective, revealing how they redefine the essence of the human mind and its expressions in the digital age.

Social Media: Self-Loss in Virtual Mirrors

Social media, with its immediacy and visibility, creates an unprecedented experimental field for virtual identities. Within this domain, individuals constantly construct and reshape their self-images through interactions such as likes, comments, and shares. However, this construction is not merely a technological application, but deeply touches on issues of self-identity.

From a psychoanalytic perspective, social media echoes the individual's deep pursuit of the "ideal self." By carefully curating content, individuals can project their most desired self-image in the virtual space. However, this self-presentation also brings deep crises. When self-identity becomes overly dependent on social media feedback, individuals may fall into the modern dilemma of Lacan's "mirror stage," where the boundaries between the "real self" and the "mirror self" blur, ultimately leading to self-alienation and identity loss.

The decontextualization and fragmentation characteristics of social media further exacerbate this problem. Individual life narratives are broken into countless moments, which are then given excessive value. This process of symbolizing value actually deconstructs Freudian "ego," as it reduces the complete individual existence to a series of quantifiable social data.

Algorithms: The Computational Model of the Unconscious

With the rapid development of artificial intelligence, algorithms have gradually permeated every aspect of our lives, from search engines to social media, from e-commerce to medical diagnosis. Although algorithms play an increasingly important role in daily life, their understanding is often limited to a utilitarian level.

From a psychoanalytic perspective, algorithms can be seen as an unconscious model. They simulate the unconscious psychological structure of humans and, through the processes of data input, model training, and decision output, demonstrate the driving forces and desire projections of the unconscious.

In the training data of algorithms, desires and needs that conflict with mainstream values and are suppressed by society and culture are often ignored or distorted by algorithms. For example, recommendation algorithms may marginalize content that does not conform to mainstream aesthetics, thereby exacerbating social inequality. Additionally, the unconscious driving forces in the model training process, such as the biases and cultural backgrounds of developers, may be internalized into the model through the decision-making process, affecting the behavior of the algorithm.

The unconscious nature of algorithms also brings many potential risks, including exacerbating social inequality, manipulating human thought, and challenging ethical norms. Understanding the unconscious mechanisms of algorithms not only helps us better recognize the complexity of algorithms themselves but also helps us reflect on the profound connections between algorithms and human psychology and social culture.

Remote Therapy: The Ghosts of Transference and Countertransference

The rise of remote therapy brings new challenges and opportunities to psychoanalysis. Traditional transference relationships are based on the face-to-face contact between therapists and patients, while remote therapy reshapes the essence of transference and countertransference through screen images, voice transmission, and text exchanges.

In remote therapy, the patient's transference object shifts from the "real" therapist to the "screen" therapist, making the transference relationship more complex and elusive. Meanwhile, the therapist's countertransference is also profoundly affected by remote therapy. Facing the screen, the therapist's emotional reactions may become more intense and direct, and the interference of technical issues and the enhancement of anonymity may also trigger new countertransference reactions.
