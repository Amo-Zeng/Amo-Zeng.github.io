From Quantum Micro to Classical Macro: Multidimensional Reconstruction of Solid Mechanics

Multidimensional Reconstruction of Solid Mechanics

Solid mechanics, as one of the important cornerstones of classical physics, has long been regarded as a static and mature discipline. However, with the rapid advancement of science and technology, the research paradigm of solid mechanics is undergoing a profound transformation. This transformation not only reveals new dimensions of solid mechanics at the quantum level but also introduces temporal dynamics and multiscale modeling, providing us with a fresh perspective to redefine and expand solid mechanics.

Quantum Mechanics: A New Dimension for Solid Mechanics

The birth and development of quantum mechanics have overturned our traditional understanding of the material world. Concepts such as particle wave-particle duality, quantum tunneling, and entangled states challenge the basic assumptions of classical physics. When we introduce the perspective of quantum mechanics into solid mechanics, a new research field—quantum solid mechanics—gradually emerges. This field not only re-examines the fundamental concepts of classical solid mechanics but also reveals complex behaviors hidden in the microscopic world.

  • Lattice Vibrations: From phonons to quantum field theory, the quantized manifestations of lattice vibrations deepen our understanding of material behavior.
  • Electron-Phonon Interaction: The interaction between electrons and phonons not only affects the electronic structure of materials but also has a profound impact on their electrical conductivity and thermal conductivity.
  • Defects and Phase Transitions: The power of quantum fluctuations plays a crucial role in defects and phase transitions, driving materials to exhibit new quantum states at low temperatures.

From Static to Dynamic: The Introduction of Temporal Dimensions

Traditional studies of solid mechanics often overlook the influence of time factors on material behavior, focusing mainly on stress, strain, and deformation in static states. However, materials and structures in the real world are constantly undergoing dynamic changes. Phenomena such as high-speed impacts, fatigue damage, viscoelastic behavior, and thermal effects require us to introduce temporal dimensions and re-examine the research methods of solid mechanics.

  1. Limitations of Static Solid Mechanics: Ignoring time factors makes it difficult to address issues like high-speed impacts and dynamic fractures.
  2. Development of Dynamic Solid Mechanics: Introducing temporal dimensions to study the dynamic responses of materials and structures across different time scales.
  3. Applications of Dynamic Solid Mechanics: Widely applied in aerospace, transportation, and materials science, such as analyzing dynamic loads on aircraft and satellites.

From Single to Multiscale: The Rise of Multiscale Modeling

Traditional studies of solid mechanics rely on single-scale models, such as molecular dynamics and finite element analysis, which have achieved significant success within their application scopes but also face obvious limitations. To overcome these limitations, multiscale modeling has gradually become a research focus in solid mechanics.

  • Multiscale Modeling Strategies: Establishing connections between different scales to link mechanisms at the microscopic level with behaviors at the macroscopic level.
  • Applications and Challenges of Multiscale Modeling: Showcasing tremendous potential in materials design and structural analysis but facing issues like scale bridging and computational efficiency.

Future Outlook: Multidimensional Reconstruction of Solid Mechanics

Re-examining solid mechanics from the perspective of quantum mechanics, introducing temporal dimensions, and multiscale modeling, we open a door to a new world. In this world, classic and quantum intertwine, micro and macro merge, and static and dynamic coexist, providing us with new ideas and methods to understand and manipulate solid materials. In the future, with continuous technological advancements, quantum solid mechanics, dynamic solid mechanics, and multiscale modeling will play increasingly important roles in materials science, engineering technology, and biomedical fields, redefining our understanding of solid mechanics.
