From "Dark Forest" to "The Light Era": Speculations on Civilizations During the Reionization Period

Introduction: The Memory and Future of the Universe

The evolution of the universe is like an epic with sweeping grandeur. Among its chapters, the reionization period stands out as one of the most critical. Dark Forest: The Cosmic Environment and Early Civilization Speculations During the Reionization Period

In Liu Cixin's "Dark Forest" theory, the universe is depicted as a vast forest where civilizations must cautiously hide themselves to survive, lest they become prey to other civilizations. This theory seems pessimistic, but based on real observations of the early universe, it offers a plausible explanation.

The reionization period occurred between 300 million to 1 billion years after the Big Bang. With the formation of the first stars and galaxies, hydrogen gas in the universe gradually became ionized, creating an environment filled with photons. However, the conditions of the universe during this period were extremely unfavorable for the emergence of life and the rise of civilizations. Extreme radiation, sparse primordial matter, and frequent supernovae posed significant challenges for early civilizations.

If early civilizations existed during the reionization period, they might have had to face the harsh rules of the "Dark Forest." Highly advanced technology could have made them aware of threats from other civilizations in the universe, leading them to choose hiding. For example, they might have built massive underground cities, using minimal radiation from stars to sustain life while avoiding exposure of their existence.

Cosmic Memory During the Reionization Period: Photon Escape and the Fate of Civilizations

The cosmic environment during the reionization period not only shaped the survival strategies of early civilizations but also provided us with rich "cosmic memory," preserved in various forms and waiting to be deciphered.

Polarization signals of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) and 21 cm hydrogen line radiation are important "fingerprints" of this era. During reionization, electrons scattered CMB photons, creating characteristic B-mode polarization signals, while neutral hydrogen, upon being irradiated, underwent spin state transitions, producing radiation at a 21 cm wavelength. These signals carry abundant cosmic information, including the spatio-temporal evolution of reionization.

Photon escape is a crucial microscopic mechanism during the reionization period. Photons interact with neutral hydrogen atoms through Thomson scattering and photoionization processes, altering the propagation paths and energy distributions of photons in the universe. This process not only affected the ionization state of the universe but also had profound implications for its macro-evolution.

Firstly, photon escape changed the anisotropy spectrum of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB), providing us with important clues to study the physical processes during the reionization period. Secondly, escaping photons influenced the cosmic radiation background, affecting the formation and evolution of galaxies. Escaping photons provided additional radiation pressure, suppressing the formation of low-mass galaxies and promoting the evolution of large-scale structures. Lastly, photon escape impacted the distribution of energy density in the universe, which in turn affected the properties of dark energy and the rate of cosmic expansion. Escaping photons reduced the radiation energy density in the universe, potentially enhancing dark energy and accelerating the rate of cosmic expansion.

Technological Empowerment: Future Predictions for Deciphering Cosmic Memory

Deciphering the "cosmic memory" of the reionization period requires the development of new observational technologies and data analysis methods. Next-generation radio telescopes, such as the "Square Kilometre Array (SKA)," will have unprecedented sensitivity and resolution, capable of capturing weaker 21 cm signals and revealing the details of the reionization period. Meanwhile, high-precision CMB experiments, like the "Simons Observatory," will enhance the accuracy of measuring CMB polarization signals, helping us reconstruct the history of reionization more precisely. Additionally, machine learning and artificial intelligence will process massive datasets, identify complex cosmic signal patterns, and build more accurate models of cosmic evolution.

By conducting in-depth research on the "cosmic memory" of the reionization period, we will be able to uncover the mysteries of the origin and evolution of the universe, understand the formation processes of the first stars and black holes, explore the nature of dark matter and dark energy, and ultimately construct the complete cosmological standard model. Furthermore, we can predict the future development of the universe, such as whether it will continue to expand forever or experience a Big Crunch or Big Rip.
