Triple Leap: A Paradigm Revolution in Eco-Poverty Alleviation — Synergistic Transitions in Technology, Culture, and Institutions

Abstract: Driven by the dual national strategies of ecological civilization and common prosperity, China's eco-poverty alleviation is undergoing a paradigm shift from "resource compensation" to "capital appreciation." Based on ecological capital theory (Costanza et al., 1997) and institutional change theory (North, 1990), this paper proposes a tripartite analytical framework of "technology-culture-institution" synergistic transition. By integrating cutting-edge practices such as Ganzi's "Ecological Bank" and Guangxi's "Carbon Sink + Poverty Alleviation," it reveals institutional innovation pathways in eco-poverty alleviation. The study finds that the essence of eco-poverty alleviation lies in the institutional reconfiguration of ecological capital, whose success depends on the systemic coupling of property rights clarification, cultural legitimacy, and market accessibility. Finally, the paper constructs an "ecological bank"-style governance paradigm and offers actionable policy recommendations, providing theoretical support and practical pathways for the synergistic governance of poverty reduction and carbon neutrality.

I. Theoretical Framework: Institutional Change and Capital Reconfiguration in Eco-Poverty Alleviation

The profound transformation of eco-poverty alleviation is not merely an iteration of policy tools, but a systemic project of institutionalization of ecological capital. This paper presents an integrative theoretical framework, grounded in ecological capital theory and institutional change theory, to uncover the internal mechanisms of the triple leap.

1. Ecological Capital Theory: The Foundation for Valuing Ecosystems

Costanza et al. (1997) in their seminal Nature study on global ecosystem service valuation estimated that the annual value of global ecosystem services reaches $33 trillion—surpassing global GDP. This discovery shattered the traditional notion of "nature as valueless," establishing the legitimacy of ecological capital as a factor of production. Ecological capital comprises four categories of services:

  • Provisioning services (e.g., water, medicinal plants)
  • Regulating services (e.g., carbon sequestration, flood mitigation)
  • Cultural services (e.g., ecotourism, spiritual value)
  • Supporting services (e.g., soil formation, biodiversity)

However, due to ambiguous property rights, non-excludability, and strong externalities, ecological capital has long remained in a state of "value submersion," leading to the "tragedy of the commons" and the "ecological poverty trap."

2. Institutional Change Theory: From Path Dependence to Institutional Innovation

North (1990) emphasized that institutions are rule systems that reduce transaction costs, define property rights, and shape incentives. The realization of ecological capital value must rely on institutional innovation to break path dependence:

  • Formal institutions (e.g., carbon trading rules, ecological compensation mechanisms)
  • Informal institutions (e.g., ecological beliefs, community norms)
  • Implementation mechanisms (e.g., digital platforms, cooperatives)

North's theory of the "institutional matrix" further posits that institutional change becomes possible when technological change (e.g., blockchain, remote sensing) reduces information costs, cultural cognition (e.g., rising ecological awareness) alters behavioral preferences, and institutional supply (e.g., carbon markets, ecological banks) provides incentive structures.

3. Theoretical Integration: A Tripartite Synergistic Transition Model

Dimension Function Theoretical Basis Practical Goal
Technological Leap Reduce measurement and transaction costs North's "technology-driven institutional change" Property rights clarification and visualization of ecological capital
Cultural Leap Endow ecological capital with cultural legitimacy Berkes (1999) on Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) Community identification and behavioral internalization
Institutional Leap Realize value conversion of ecological capital Costanza's capitalization of ecological value Market accessibility and revenue redistribution

Core Proposition: The success of eco-poverty alleviation hinges on whether these three dimensions can form a positive feedback loop—technology empowers institutions, institutions reinforce culture, and culture sustains technology—ultimately achieving the sustainable appreciation of ecological capital.

II. Technological Leap: Digital Technologies Drive Property Rights Clarification and Governance Reconfiguration

The technological leap is the critical breakthrough enabling ecological capital to transition from "invisible" to "measurable." Its essence lies in digital technologies reducing transaction costs, transforming ecological capital from public goods into quasi-private assets.

(1) Blockchain + IoT: The "Trinity Separation" of Ecological Asset Rights

The "Ecological Cloud Chain" pilot in Guizhou's Leigong Mountain employs blockchain + IoT + remote sensing technologies to convert forest, water, and wetland resources into digital asset packages, enabling:

  • Ownership (state/collective)
  • Usage rights (contracting households/cooperatives)
  • Benefit rights (carbon sink dividends, ecological compensation)

Through smart contracts, carbon sink revenues are automatically distributed to villagers' digital wallets, reducing transaction costs by 72% (2023 evaluation data), solving the traditional problem of "layer-by-layer siphoning" in ecological compensation.

(2) Ganzi, Sichuan: "Ecological Bank" — A Digital Operation Platform for Ecological Assets

The Ganzi Prefecture "Ecological Bank" Pilot (China's first provincial-level ecological capital operation platform), launched in 2022, further upgrades the model:

  • Establishes "Ecological Asset Digital Accounts", integrating grassland, wetland, and forest data;
  • Introduces AI valuation models for dynamic assessment of ecosystem service values;
  • Develops the "Ecological Bank" APP, enabling villagers to monitor asset returns and apply for carbon sink-backed loans.

2023 data shows:

  • Total ecological asset value in pilot areas increased by 35%;
  • Per capita ecological income reached 2,800 RMB/year;
  • Ecological capital-backed loans exceeded 120 million RMB.

(3) Governance Redistribution: From "Government-Led" to "Polycentric Co-Governance"

The technological leap reshapes governance structures. Under the "Ecological Bank" model, a "government-platform-community-third party" quadripartite governance structure emerges:

  • Government: Rule-setting and compliance oversight;
  • Platform (e.g., ecological bank): Asset valuation and transaction facilitation;
  • Community: Participation in decision-making and benefit distribution;
  • Third parties (NGOs, research institutions): Monitoring, evaluation, and cultural empowerment.

This model aligns with North's assertion that "institutional efficiency arises from decentralization and incentive compatibility," but also requires vigilance against digital exclusion that may marginalize vulnerable groups.

III. Cultural Leap: Modern Activation of Traditional Ecological Knowledge and Ecological Memory

Ecological capital carries not only economic value but also cultural legitimacy. The cultural leap centers on transforming Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) into the cultural foundation of ecological capital, achieving "technologizing culture, and capitalizing culture."

(1) The Triple Value of Traditional Ecological Knowledge

Berkes (1999) defines TEK as long-term adaptive knowledge systems with:

  • Ecological value: e.g., Hani terraced fields' "forest-village-terrace-water system" vertical structure, ensuring soil conservation and biodiversity;
  • Cultural value: e.g., Dong people's "sacred mountain taboos," forming informal conservation mechanisms;
  • Economic value: e.g., Guangxi Longji's "rice-fish-duck co-culture," yielding 40% higher output per unit area than monoculture.

(2) Guangxi's "Carbon Sink + Poverty Alleviation": Synergistic Value-Addition through Culture and Technology

The Longji Terraced Fields "Carbon Sink Cultural Cooperative" (2021) introduces an innovative model:

  • Integrates traditional water systems into carbon sink monitoring networks, enabling "quantification of cultural-system carbon sequestration";
  • Allocates 20% of carbon sink revenue to an "Ecological Ritual Fund" to support traditional ceremonies like "Water Worship Festival" and "Plowing Festival";
  • Youth engaged in carbon management receive "Ecological Cultural Inheritor" certification, increasing return-to-village rates by 35%.

2023 data: Carbon sink revenue increased by 40%, cultural identity index rose by 28%, forming a virtuous cycle of "carbon sinks nourish culture, culture protects ecology."

(3) "Cultural Alignment" Mechanism: Legitimizing Technology Through Cultural Embeddedness

In Hani terraces, solar water pumps require a "water worship ritual" before installation, embedding technology in cultural meaning. In Ganzi, the Ecological Bank APP integrates Tibetan script and Thangka art, enhancing user identification. This "cultural alignment" mechanism is crucial for technology adoption, echoing Costanza's warning that "ecological capital must be embedded within socio-cultural structures."

IV. Institutional Leap: Carbon Sink Trading Chains and Synergistic Governance of Poverty Reduction and Carbon Neutrality

The institutional leap is the final stage for value realization of ecological capital. Its core is building a tradable, redistributable, and sustainable carbon sink poverty alleviation mechanism.

(1) Institutional Innovation: From "Carbon Tickets" to "Carbon Sink Chains"

  • Sanming, Fujian's "Carbon Ticket System": Quantifies forest carbon sinks as "carbon tickets," tradable, pledgeable, and dividend-paying;
  • Puer, Yunnan's "Agroforestry Carbon Sinks": Integrates rubber-tea intercropping into CCER projects, enhancing carbon density;
  • Kubuqi, Inner Mongolia's "Carbon Sink-Backed Loans": Herders use grassland carbon sinks as collateral for low-interest loans for ecological restoration.

(2) Institutional Deepening of Ganzi's "Ecological Bank"

Building on technology and culture, Ganzi's pilot introduces three institutional innovations:

  1. Carbon Sink Revenue Insurance Fund: Co-established by local government and insurers to hedge against carbon price volatility, ensuring stable redistribution;
  2. Smallholder "Regional Bundled Registration" Mechanism: Bundles dispersed smallholder carbon projects for CCER registration, reducing costs by 60%;
  3. "Carbon Sink Ritual" System: Allocates 10% of annual carbon revenue to ecological rituals, reinforcing community participation.

2023 data: Carbon sink revenue stability improved by 60%, with 85% of ecological capital value realized.

V. Policy Recommendations: Building a New Governance Paradigm for Eco-Poverty Alleviation

Based on theoretical frameworks and practical experience, we propose the "Ecological Bank 2.0" governance paradigm to scale eco-poverty alleviation from pilot to institutionalization.

1. Technological Level: Build Digital Infrastructure for Ecological Capital

  • Promote a national standard for "Ecological Asset Digital Accounts" to enable cross-regional interoperability;
  • Construct a "space-air-ground integrated carbon sink monitoring network" covering all ecologically fragile regions;
  • Develop an open-source "Ecological Bank APP" platform supporting local customization.

2. Cultural Level: Activate the Institutional Role of Traditional Ecological Knowledge

  • Establish a "National Ecological Memory Database" to systematically document TEK;
  • Introduce an "Ecological Cultural Inheritor" system, integrating into rural revitalization talent programs;
  • Implement a "Cultural Alignment Assessment" mechanism, requiring cultural feasibility reviews for all ecological projects.

3. Institutional Level: Build a Market-Based System for Ecological Capital

  • Legislative Safeguards: Incorporate the "ecological capital revenue redistribution principle" into the Promotion of Rural Revitalization Act revision;
  • Market Innovation: Establish an "Ecological Carbon Sink Exchange" to pilot "carbon sink futures" products;
  • Financial Support: Develop "ecological capital-backed loans" and "carbon sink revenue ABS" (Asset-Backed Securities);
  • Risk Hedging: Create a "National Ecological Capital Insurance Fund" to cover price volatility and natural disasters.

4. Governance Mechanism: Institutionalize "Polycentric Co-Governance"

  • Establish an "Eco-Poverty Alleviation Multi-Stakeholder Council" including government, communities, enterprises, and NGOs;
  • Introduce an annual "Ecological Capital Governance White Paper" to enhance transparency;
  • Launch an "Ecological Capital Innovation Award" to incentivize grassroots institutional innovation.

VI. Conclusion: Toward the Institutional Reconfiguration of Ecological Capital

The paradigm revolution in eco-poverty alleviation is, at its core, an institutional reconfiguration of ecological capital. This reconfiguration is not merely technological stacking or policy patching, but a tripartite synergy grounded in ecological capital theory as the value foundation and institutional change theory as the pathway guide: technological leap enables property rights clarification, cultural leap confers legitimacy, and institutional leap realizes value.

In the future, eco-poverty alleviation will evolve into the "Ecological Bank 2.0" era—an integrated ecological capital operation platform combining digital property rights, cultural empowerment, market trading, risk hedging, and revenue redistribution. Under this paradigm:

  • Poverty reduction is no longer a cost of ecology, but the starting point of ecological appreciation;
  • Carbon neutrality is no longer an external constraint, but an internal incentive;
  • The tensions between tradition and modernity, individual and collective, human and nature, are transformed into a narrative of coexistence and shared prosperity.

Ultimate Goal: Let every forest, every wetland, every sacred mountain become measurable, tradable, and inheritable ecological capital, illuminating the path to common prosperity under the light of institutions.
