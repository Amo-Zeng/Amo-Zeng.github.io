The Evolution of Extremism and "Deradicalization": A Complex Co-Conspiracy from Social Psychology to Digital Media

In today's society, the proliferation and dissemination of extremism have become a global issue that cannot be ignored. This article will delve into the evolution of extremism and its complex social-psychological mechanisms from three dimensions: social psychology, digital media, and "deradicalization" strategies. It will also propose a new "deradicalization" mindset based on empathy.

Convergence and Conformity: The Psychological Soil of Extremism

Humans have an innate instinct for "convergence" and "conformity." These psychological mechanisms make individuals tend to align with others to gain social recognition and a sense of belonging. Extremism skillfully exploits these instincts by creating an "us versus them" dichotomy, gradually pushing marginal views into the mainstream. Extremist organizations use meticulously designed rituals, symbols, and discourse systems to strengthen members' identity and create a strong sense of belonging within the group, leading to an outward sense of rejection. This high level of identification makes members more susceptible to accepting and spreading extremist thoughts.

Social Turmoil and Uncertainty: The Fertile Soil for Extremism

When society is in turmoil, people feel confused and fearful about the future, more likely to question the existing order and seek simple and brutal solutions. Extremism takes advantage of this vulnerability, providing clear goals and action plans to attract helpless individuals. During social upheaval, extremism provides a simple explanatory framework, blaming complex social problems on specific external enemies, allowing individuals to find "certainty" in chaos. This "certainty" not only meets people's psychological needs but also stimulates their actions, enabling marginal views to spread rapidly in a turbulent social environment.
