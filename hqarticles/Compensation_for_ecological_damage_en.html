From "Environment with Value" to "Ecology with Value": Reconstructing a New Paradigm for Ecological Damage Compensation

Each leap in human civilization is accompanied by profound changes in our understanding of the relationship between humanity and nature. Since the Industrial Revolution, humans have gradually viewed nature as an object of conquest and transformation, simplifying the environment to a resource repository for exploitation. However, as we stand before the warning bell of ecological crises, we are compelled to re-examine this narrow mindset of "environment with value." The ecosystem is not merely a production facility for material wealth; it is also a habitat for life, a carrier of culture, and a sanctuary for the soul. Rebuilding the ecological damage compensation system is not merely a patchwork of existing institutions but a paradigm shift in human civilization towards ecological civilization, a profound transformation from "environment with value" to "ecology with value."

I. From "Environment with Value" to "Ecology with Value": The Leap in Value Paradigms

Traditional ecological damage compensation systems, akin to a meticulous accountant, simplify the value of the environment into addition and subtraction of "economic losses." Whether it's the reduction of fishery resources or the expenditure on pollution control, all are quantified into cold numbers. This "environment with value" mindset is like a shattered mirror, reflecting only fragmented parts of the ecosystem and failing to capture its overall grandeur. The ecosystem not only provides us with material resources but also constructs a sanctuary for life through intangible services such as climate regulation, air purification, and biodiversity maintenance. Biodiversity is not just a decoration of nature; it is the lifeline of the Earth's living network. Green mountains and clear waters are not just scenery; they are the spiritual home of humanity.

The introduction of the "ecology with value" concept is not just a revolution in thought but also a leap in value paradigms. It requires us to expand the value of the ecosystem from a single economic dimension to multiple dimensions such as ecology, culture, and spirit. This value reconstruction is not merely a technical improvement of traditional systems but a fundamental reflection on the development model of human civilization. In recent years, Zhejiang Province's "ecological damage compensation fund" system and the successful practice of environmental public interest litigation have demonstrated the vitality of the "ecology with value" concept. However, institutional reforms are just the starting point; the real challenge lies in internalizing this concept into collective social awareness. This requires establishing a scientific ecological value assessment system, improving legal safeguards, and nurturing a cultural environment that respects nature in society.

II. Multi-Stakeholder Governance: Reconstructing Social Mechanisms for Ecological Damage Compensation

Ecological damage often resembles an invisible epidemic, with effects that are far beyond the capacity of a single entity to address. While the government, as the main claimant in traditional ecological damage compensation, possesses resources and authority, it also faces limitations such as information asymmetry and rigid action mechanisms. The public good attributes of the ecosystem determine that its protection requires the participation of the entire society. Social organizations, with their professionalism and flexibility, can fill the gaps left by the government; the public, with their extensive supervisory power, can form powerful social constraints. Building a diversified ecological damage compensation mechanism is not a weakening of the government but an overall enhancement of social governance capabilities.

The successful practice of public interest litigation has provided valuable experience for a diversified claim mechanism. The environmental pollution case brought by the China Environmental Protection Federation against a chemical company in Jiangsu Province is not only a victory for rights protection but also a breakthrough in social governance models. By clearly defining the litigation subject qualifications of social organizations, simplifying the litigation procedures, and stimulating public participation enthusiasm, a governance pattern involving the government, society, and the public has been formed. However, the construction of a diversified claim mechanism should not stop at institutional improvement; it also requires cultivating social ecological responsibility. The repair of ecological damage is not just a legal issue but also an ethical one. Only when every social member regards ecological protection as their own responsibility can a truly socialized governance of ecological damage compensation be achieved.

III. Ecological Restoration: The Practical Shift from Compensation to Restoration

The limitations of monetary compensation have long been exposed in practice. The irreplaceability of the environment determines that economic compensation cannot restore the integrity of the ecosystem; the comprehensiveness of ecological services determines that simple compensation cannot repair the loss of ecological functions. The ecological restoration case of abandoned mines in Nanping City, Fujian Province, presents a new compensation model: ecological restoration is not just "paying the bill" but also "rebirth." Through measures such as soil improvement, vegetation restoration, and wetland reconstruction, the damaged ecosystem is rejuvenated. This restoration model is not only a compensation for environmental damage but also a profound reflection on the relationship between humans and nature.

The practical exploration of ecological restoration provides us with new ideas. Alternative restoration achieves the substitution of ecological service functions by rebuilding ecosystems in other areas; carbon sink subscription combines ecological restoration with carbon neutrality goals, forming a sustainable restoration mechanism. However, the real challenge lies in constructing a complete ecological restoration system. This requires the government to formulate scientific policy norms, enterprises to assume restoration responsibilities, the public to participate in supervision, and the formation of a joint force from multiple parties. At the same time, the evaluation of restoration effects needs to establish a scientific indicator system to ensure the long-term effectiveness of restoration measures. Most importantly, ecological restoration should not remain at the technical level but should become a cultural consciousness and a manifestation of reverence for nature.

IV. The Call of Ecological Civilization: Reconstructing the Relationship Between Humans and Nature

The transformation of the ecological damage compensation system is not just an adjustment of a legal institution but a profound shift in the development model of human civilization. It reminds us that humans are not the rulers of the Earth but a part of the ecosystem. Only when we abandon the arrogance of "anthropocentrism" and truly regard nature as an equal partner can we achieve harmonious coexistence between humans and nature. This transformation is not only a technical innovation but also a cultural awakening. The reform of the ecological damage compensation system should serve as an important lever for promoting ecological civilization construction and a significant platform for cultivating ecological culture.

Let us join hands to reconstruct the ecological damage compensation system and jointly protect our only home. This is not just an environmental repair but also a repair of human civilization; not just an ecological protection but also a nurture of the human spirit. Only by viewing the world with the perspective of "ecology with value," reconstructing mechanisms with a multi-stakeholder governance mindset, and safeguarding the future through practical ecological restoration can we leave a truly sustainable beautiful home for future generations.
