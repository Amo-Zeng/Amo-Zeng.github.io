Philosophy of Art and the Digital Future

The Contemporary Reconfiguration of Artworks: A Triple Variation of Subjectivity, Memory, and Cognition

When art no longer resides within a frame or pedestal, when creation is no longer the work of a single subject, and when memory no longer depends on stone tablets or archives, we are forced to ask: is it still an artwork? In today’s era, where digital technologies deeply infiltrate artistic practice, artworks have long surpassed the realm of static aesthetic objects, evolving into dynamic, participatory, and even self-evolving entities. They are no longer the pure crystallization of a "creator's will," nor passive vessels of cultural memory, nor mere objects of observation—they are increasingly becoming algorithmically co-produced entities, anti-monumental ephemera, and co-evolving cognitive interfaces. These three trajectories do not run in isolation but form a spiraling network of critical thought: from the algorithmicization of creation, to the destabilization of cultural meaning, and ultimately, to the neural reconstruction of cognition. Together, they point to a fundamental proposition—the contemporaneity of artworks is undergoing a triple reconfiguration.

I. The Transformation of Subjectivity: The "Post-Author" Ghost in Algorithmic Collaboration

In traditional art, "the death of the author" (Barthes) was once seen as a declaration of liberation. Today, the author is not only "dead," but has been parasitized by algorithmic collaboration. AI-generated art, such as Refik Anadol’s Machine Hallucinations, is not "created" by humans but "generated" by neural networks trained on massive datasets, producing visual dreams in latent space. Here, the author is no longer the creator, but the curator of data, the tuner of models, and the selector of outputs. Subjectivity does not vanish—it becomes distributed across the entanglement of humans and algorithms.

This resonates with the core insight of posthumanism: subjectivity is no longer the exclusive privilege of humans, but a co-constructed process across species, media, and intelligences. Donna Haraway’s "cyborg" is no longer merely a metaphor, but a reality in art production—humans and algorithms together form a "creative subject," a non-dualistic, non-centralized, non-intentional "post-author." This subjectivity is not lost, but diluted, reconfigured as a relational existence. As media philosopher Friedrich Kittler stated: "Media determine our situation." When algorithms become the medium of creation, subjectivity is no longer "I think" (cogito), but "I calculate" (calculo)—a techno-cognitive hybrid that evolves within data streams and feedback loops.

II. The Resistance of Memory: The "Politics of Ephemerality" in Anti-Monuments

If algorithms reconfigure the creative subject, then the ephemerality of digital art challenges the fixed logic of cultural memory. Traditional monuments—made of stone, permanent, and centralized in narrative—seek to freeze history into authoritative discourse. Contemporary art, however, employs "self-destruction" as a strategy, enacting a resistance to monumentality.

The sharpest example is artist Kevin Abosch's 2021 NFT work IAMA COIN, which was programmed with a "self-destruction protocol"—once sold, its digital file would be permanently erased from the blockchain. This is not a negation of value, but a provocation against the commodification of memory. NFTs are designed to affirm "ownership," yet this work completes its meaning through "destruction": only in disappearance does memory truly belong to the public sphere. It is no longer "possessed," but "experienced"; no longer "preserved," but "revisited."

This echoes Paul Ricoeur’s theory of "narrative memory": memory is not archival storage, but a continuous practice of retelling. The self-destruction of digital art liberates memory from the authority of monuments, transforming it into a fluid, participatory, and decentralized cultural practice. It refuses to be archived, deified, or capitalized upon. As Giorgio Agamben said: "True memory is that which is not remembered."—In the digital flood, ephemerality itself becomes a political stance against memory monopolization.

III. The Leap of Cognition: The "Cognitive Interface" in the Fracture of Perception

As subjectivity is diluted by algorithms and memory is restructured by ephemerality, art’s function no longer ends at "seeing" or "remembering," but ascends to a cognitive interface—a co-thinking apparatus connecting human perception with external intelligent systems.

The most revolutionary example is brain-computer interface (BCI) art. In 2023, artist Neri Oxman collaborated with neuroscientists to create Neural Canvas, using EEG devices to capture viewers’ brainwaves in real time, driving generative AI to produce dynamic images. Here, art is no longer "seen," but "thought"—the viewer’s neural activity directly becomes a creative parameter. Perception and cognition, inner and outer, neural and algorithmic, achieve real-time co-creation. This is no longer "human-machine interaction," but human-machine co-thinking.

The cognitive science theory of "extended mind" finds artistic validation here: the mind does not reside solely in the brain, but extends outward through technological media. When neural signals are transformed into images, art becomes an exosomatic organ of cognition—not reflecting thought, but participating in its generation. This overturns Kantian "transcendental cognition," pointing toward a new paradigm of cognition—embodied, technological, and environmentally coupled. Art thus shifts from "aesthetic object" to "cognitive infrastructure."

IV. The Philosophical Intertextuality of the Triple Variation: From Posthumanism to Cognitive Ecology

The three trajectories—algorithmic collaboration, anti-monumentality, and cognitive interface—are not parallel, but form a spiraling structure of critical thought. They share a common philosophical foundation: the thorough deconstruction of anthropocentrism.

  • In algorithmic collaboration, subjectivity is diluted by posthumanism—humans are no longer the sole intelligences;
  • In anti-monumentality, memory is restructured by media philosophy—history is no longer the monologue of stone;
  • In cognitive interface, cognition is expanded by cognitive science—thinking is no longer a closed theater within the skull.

Together, they point toward a vision of cognitive ecology: art is no longer isolated from society, but embedded in complex networks of technology, memory, and cognition. It is both a mediator of technology, a resister of memory, and a co-thinker of thought. This triple variation is the meta-paradigm of contemporary art—no longer asking "what is art?" but "how does art reconfigure our relationship with the world?"

Deeper still, these three form a paradoxical symbiosis:

  • Algorithmic collaboration dissolves the author, yet generates a more complex creative subject;
  • Anti-monumentality denies permanence, yet activates a more authentic politics of memory;
  • Cognitive interface externalizes thought, yet deepens the subject’s sense of existence.

This is the true power of contemporary art: it does not seek stability, but embraces uncertain generation; it does not cling to form, but explores revolutionary function.

Conclusion: The Ultimate Paradox—When Art Is No Longer "Seen," Can It Still Be "Thought"?

We stand at a cognitive singularity: art no longer needs to be "seen," but can be "thought"; no longer needs to be "preserved," but can be "co-created"; no longer needs to be "interpreted," but can be "participated in." Yet, in this process, a fundamental cognitive paradox emerges:

When art becomes a cognitive interface, when the artwork and thought co-generate in real time, can we still distinguish between "art" and "cognition"? When algorithms, neural signals, and blockchain together form the creative system, has art ceased to be a "work" and become a "process" itself?

Further: if art’s function is to reconfigure cognition, then when it succeeds, does it self-abolish?—Just as anti-monuments become new forms of memory through resistance, cognitive interfaces blur the boundaries of art through co-creation.

This may be the deepest fate of contemporary art: it does not seek eternity, but continuous self-negation through reconfiguration; it does not define itself, but through each technological intervention, each act of memory resistance, and each cognitive co-creation, re-asks "what is art?"

Thus, the ultimate question is no longer "what is art?" but:

When art no longer exists as an "object," does it not, in fact, more authentically reveal—who we are, and how we think?

Within the undercurrents of algorithms, in the ashes of memory, and in the tremors of neurons, contemporary art, through its triple variation, is sounding a cognitive revolution about existence, memory, and thought. This is its true mission in the digital age—not to provide answers, but to constantly open new questions.
