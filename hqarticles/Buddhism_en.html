Buddhism as a Meta-Cognitive and Ethical Operating System for the 21st Century

— Reconstructing Subjectivity, Understanding, and Ethical Agency Amid Digital Alienation, Post-Truth, and Systemic Crisis

In the triple convergence of technological acceleration, information explosion, and global crises, humanity faces an unprecedented modern cognitive and ethical crisis: we are defined by digital identities, manipulated by emotionalized narratives, and ensnared by invisible structural violence. Technology, meant to expand freedom, has become a new form of alienation; information, meant to lead to truth, has become a weapon in cognitive warfare; society, meant to uphold justice, is mired in invisible causal entanglements. Confronted with this "triple alienation," dominant Western paradigms—techno-optimism, liberal individualism, and rationalist epistemology—have all shown their limits. Yet in this context, Buddhist philosophy, with its profound insights into non-self (anattā), dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda), and karma (karma), is quietly emerging as a non-Western, non-techno-deterministic, non-individualistic meta-cognitive and ethical operating system. It not only diagnoses the deep pathologies of modernity but also offers a holistic response framework—from cognition to understanding to action.

This article argues: the core value of Buddhist philosophy in the 21st century lies precisely in its function as a "meta-operating system"—not providing direct technologies or policies, but reconstructing the foundational logic through which we understand the world, locate ourselves, and assume responsibility. This system consists of three interdependent modules: "non-self" to counter digital identity alienation and rebuild subjectivity; "dependent origination and emptiness" to dismantle post-truth cognitive paralysis and reconstruct understanding; and "systemic karma" to reframe causal ethics and rebuild moral agency. These three layers progress from cognitive awakening, to deepened understanding, to ethical practice, together forming a Buddhist critique of modernity—offering a non-alienated path of human existence in the digital age, ecological crisis, and cognitive disarray.

I. Non-Self: Rebuilding Subjectivity from the Digital Cage

We live in an era defined by data. Social media reduces "the self" to likes, followers, and algorithmic profiles; platform economies reify the self as consumer tags, attention commodities, and behavioral data streams. This digital identity alienation distorts self-perception and fundamentally undermines agency and dignity—we are no longer "actors," but "respondents shaped by algorithms."

Buddhism's doctrine of non-self (anattā) does not deny existence but reveals that the "self" is not a fixed, independent, or substantial entity, but a transient aggregate of the five aggregates (form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness) in constant flux through dependent conditions. In the digital age, this insight is revolutionary: when platforms claim "this is who you are," non-self reminds us: there is no 'you'—only 'how you are constructed'. Every click, every pause, every emotional fluctuation participates in the temporary generation of "self." Algorithms capture these moments, solidifying the fluid "non-self" into static labels, thus enabling control over human beings.

Non-self thus becomes a cognitive vaccine against digital alienation. It does not reject data but refuses to be defined by it; it does not flee platforms but resists internalizing their logic. Through mindfulness, we can recognize during video scrolling: "This is not me, only a temporary manifestation of desire"; in social anxiety: "This is not me, only a conditioned habit of comparison." This decentered awareness allows the subject to step back from algorithmic automation, restoring the freedom of choice—not by rejecting technology, but by refusing technological colonization.

More importantly, non-self provides the subjective foundation for the next module: "dependent origination and emptiness." Without deep recognition of the self's illusoriness, we cannot truly grasp "all phenomena are empty of self-nature"; without loosening attachment to the ego, we cannot release the fixation on "absolute truth." Non-self is the gateway to emptiness, the starting point of cognitive liberation. In the digital age, it is not a passive philosophy of escapism, but a wakeful resistance: in the flood of data, preserving the absence of "I" is precisely to protect the presence of "human."

II. Dependent Origination and Emptiness: Reconstructing Understanding in the Fog of Post-Truth

Having deconstructed the substantiality of the self, "dependent origination and emptiness" further deconstructs the substantiality of the world. The core crisis of the post-truth era is not a lack of information, but the collapse of cognitive frameworks: we no longer believe in "truth," only in "positions"; we no longer seek "understanding," only "resonance." Algorithmic feeds, emotionalized narratives, and echo chambers on social media swallow "facts" into "meaning," replacing "evidence" with "belief." We enter a state of cognitive paralysis: the more we know, the less we understand.

Buddhism's dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda) teaches that all phenomena arise from interdependent conditions and lack independent self-nature; emptiness (śūnyatā) further reveals that because of dependent origination, all things are empty of inherent existence. This is not nihilism, but a dynamic, relational ontology: the world is not composed of "substances" but of "relations"; events are not determined by "single causes" but by "multiple interwoven conditions."

- There are no "pure facts": every "fact" is embedded in specific contexts, perspectives, interests, and narrative structures—a temporary manifestation of dependent conditions;
- There is no "absolute truth": so-called "truth" is only a temporary consensus from a particular viewpoint, not an eternal, unchanging essence;
- There are no "independent events": every social event (war, pandemic, election) is the result of countless individual, institutional, historical, and cultural conditions—never reducible to "someone said," "a party did," or "a country is evil."

This "cognitive emptiness" liberates us from fixation on "who is right and who is wrong," shifting toward "how to understand"—understanding complexity, the mechanisms behind positions, and the construction of narratives. It encourages us to move beyond "either/or" binaries into "both/and" dialectical thinking. For example, in the Russia-Ukraine war, instead of choosing between "justice vs. aggression," we might ask: What historical conditions led to this conflict? What structural pressures trap both sides in zero-sum logic? What discursive mechanisms amplify polarization?

"Dependent origination and emptiness" is thus not just philosophy, but a cognitive practice. It requires:

  1. Suspending judgment: refrain from taking sides until conditions are fully understood;
  2. Tracing context: inquire into the multiple causes behind phenomena;
  3. Reflecting on perspective: examine the limitations and biases of one's own cognition.

This reconstruction of understanding depends precisely on the prior completion of "non-self"—only when the "I" no longer clings to its own view can we see the full picture of dependent origination. Without non-self, "dependent origination" is still distorted by egoic fixation into "the truth I see."

III. Systemic Karma: Reconstructing Ethical Responsibility Amid Structural Violence

When non-self liberates the subject, and dependent origination expands understanding, Buddhism's karma (karma) points toward ethical action. Traditional karma is often misunderstood as "individual moral retribution," but in the modern context, we must elevate it to "systemic karma"—the chain-like, delayed, and asymmetrical consequences of individual actions within complex social systems.

Ecological crisis, economic inequality, algorithmic bias, war violence—none of these are "someone's fault" alone, but the long-term accumulation of countless small choices, institutional inertia, and technological pathways within a web of conditions. Each of us participates in collective karma (sādhāraṇa-karma):

  • Consumers choosing fast fashion participate in global exploitation chains;
  • Users clicking on hate content reinforce algorithmic bias;
  • Citizens remaining silent in the face of injustice condone systemic violence.

"Systemic karma" reminds us: responsibility is not "who is to blame," but "how I participate". It does not deny structural power, but refuses to externalize responsibility entirely. Buddhist karma is not fate, but a dynamic, transformable process—every present choice reshapes future conditions. Deforestation is karma, but refusing to buy illegally harvested wood is also karma; sharing inflammatory speech is karma, but reporting misinformation is also karma.

"Systemic karma" thus offers a non-individualistic, non-deterministic ethics of action:

  1. Acknowledge structural constraints: we are not free, but still have agency;
  2. Recognize personal participation: even small, my actions affect the system;
  3. Actively transform conditions: through mindfulness, empathy, and institutional innovation, break vicious cycles.

For example, in the climate crisis, we need not wait for global treaties, but can act on specific conditions: "reduce carbon footprint," "support green policies," "join community initiatives"—gradually reshaping the system. This "micro-karma intervention" is the modern practice of Buddhist ethics.

The possibility of "systemic karma" rests precisely on the framework of "dependent origination": only by understanding the world as relational, dynamic, and non-substantial can we see how "small actions" might leverage "large systems". Without dependent origination, karma becomes mechanical retribution; without non-self, we cannot release the despair of "nothing I do matters," abandoning action.

IV. A Buddhist Critique of Modernity: A Non-Western Path for Civilization

The three principles—non-self, dependent origination and emptiness, systemic karma—are not isolated doctrines, but a coherent operating system:

  • Non-self is the subject module, deconstructing digital alienation and rebuilding cognitive freedom;
  • Dependent origination and emptiness is the understanding module, dismantling post-truth and expanding cognitive depth;
  • Systemic karma is the ethical module, reframing responsibility and guiding transformative action.

Together, they form a Buddhist critique of modernity:

  • It critiques techno-determinism, asserting technology is condition, not destiny;
  • It critiques individualism, asserting responsibility is collective karma, not sole blame;
  • It critiques Western rationalism, asserting understanding is relational, not substantial.

At a time when human civilization faces digital totalitarianism, ecological collapse, and cognitive fragmentation, Buddhist thought offers a non-Western, non-techno, non-anthropocentric path of reflection:

  • It does not rely on "more advanced algorithms" or "stronger regulation," but calls for a fundamental shift in cognitive paradigm;
  • It does not seek to "control nature" or "conquer others," but advocates wisdom of coexistence through dependent origination;
  • It does not place hope in "saviors" or "final solutions," but emphasizes each moment of awareness and choice.

This is a sustainable civilizational operating system: it does not promise utopia, but offers the capacity to remain wakeful in crisis, understanding in chaos, and active in despair. It teaches: true freedom is not escape from systems, but non-attachment within them; true responsibility is not changing the world, but not becoming a condition of harm.

In the flood of data, we may not stop algorithmic control, but we can choose not to be defined by it; in the fog of information, we may not find absolute truth, but we can choose not to be carried away by emotion; in systemic violence, we may not instantly change structures, but we can choose not to replicate them.

Buddhism, as an ancient wisdom, is re-emerging in a new form as a meta-cognitive and ethical operating system for the 21st century—not replacing science, but correcting its blind spots; not rejecting technology, but guarding its human boundaries; not fleeing modernity, but offering its redemption.

At the crossroads of human civilization, we do not need more technology, but deeper wisdom. And this path of wisdom may begin with an ancient reminder: non-self, dependent origination, karma can be transformed.
