Taste as Sovereignty: The Triple Abyss in Global Food Security Governance and Its Paradigm Breakthrough

— A Civilizational Reflection from Climate Rupture to Cultural Alienation and Systemic Failure

I. Abyss One: The Reconstruction of "Food Geography" Amid Climate Rupture — Nature's Retribution and the Fragility of Civilization

As glaciers melt, monsoons falter, and soils acidify as global norms, we must acknowledge: food is no longer a simple gift of nature, but the frontline battlefield of the climate crisis. According to the UN Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), about 34% of the world’s arable land has already degraded due to extreme weather, and for every 1°C rise in temperature, major cereal yields decline by an average of 5–10%. This is not a distant forecast—it is an unfolding "agricultural great migration": wheat belts shifting northward, coffee regions shrinking, and rice irrigation systems collapsing.

Behind this lies the complete deconstruction of traditional "food geography" by climate rupture. Once, Mediterranean sunlight nurtured olives, Andean highlands domesticated quinoa, and East Asian monsoons shaped rice civilizations—these "terroir codes" are now being relentlessly eroded by extreme weather. In 2022, Pakistan’s floods submerged 4 million hectares of farmland; in 2023, Europe’s heatwave drove olive oil prices up 150%; in 2024, Amazonian droughts cut coffee production by 30%… Climate is no longer background—it is the direct arbiter of food sovereignty.

The deeper question is: do we still have the right to choose what we eat? When climate pushes wheat from South Asia to Siberia, when fisheries migrate with ocean currents, when traditional crops fail due to temperature thresholds, our so-called "dietary freedom" is merely the scraps left behind by climate tyranny. This is not merely an agricultural issue—it is the shaking of civilization’s very foundation. As the ancient Greek historian Herodotus said: "Civilization is born of soil, and dies of drought." Today’s climate crisis is, in truth, nature’s reckoning against the arrogance of modernity.

From a cross-civilizational lens, ancient Egypt relied on the Nile’s floods as "divine agriculture," the Maya collapsed due to drought, and the Mongol Empire expanded in tandem with grassland ecological shifts—the rupture of food systems has always been a harbinger of civilizational rise and fall. Today, we face not the crisis of a single civilization, but a systemic reset of global food geography. If we respond only with technological fixes—drought-resistant seeds, vertical farms—we are merely applying bandages to a bursting dam.

Policy Action Framework

We must move beyond "adaptive agriculture" toward "climate-resilient sovereignty":

  • Establish a transnational "climate-crop-population" dynamic early warning system;
  • Launch a "Climate Justice Agriculture Fund" to compensate vulnerable nations;
  • Designate "Global Food Migration Corridors," allowing affected regions to re-plan agriculture within ecological carrying capacity;

This is not just a survival strategy—it is an ethical commitment to civilizational continuity.

II. Abyss Two: "Taste Colonialism" Under Cultural Alienation — Who Defines "Good Food"?

When 90% of processed foods on global supermarket shelves contain palm oil, high-fructose corn syrup, and artificial flavors—when McDonald’s, Coca-Cola, and Starbucks have become visual symbols of "globalization"—we must ask: who is defining our taste?

This is not merely a matter of dietary preference, but a deep-seated "taste colonialism". Since the 19th-century colonial trade, tropical commodities like sugar, coffee, cocoa, and rubber were reshaped by European capital into "modern necessities," while local food traditions were labeled "backward" and "unhygienic." In the late 20th century, multinational food giants embedded the equation "high sugar/fat = happiness," "fast food = efficiency," and "packaging = safety" into global consciousness through advertising, education, and media—taste has become the final battlefield of cultural sovereignty.

In India, traditional fermented foods (like idli and dosa) have been marginalized for "lack of standardization," while sugary yogurts and fried snacks dominate children’s tables. In Mexico, indigenous tortilla culture is eroded by "Americanized corn chips," with obesity rates doubling in a decade. In Japan, despite washoku’s UNESCO heritage status, over 60% of youth consume Western-style meals (hamburgers, pizza). When taste is encoded by capital, food ceases to be "cultural memory" and becomes "consumption data".

Even more alarming is the hegemony of "health discourse": Western nutritionism, with its singular focus on "calories, protein, and vitamins," dismisses fermentation, food-as-medicine, and seasonal eating as alternative wisdoms. Traditional Chinese medicine’s "grains as sustenance," Ayurveda’s "six-taste balance," and Africa’s "root-crop cultures"—centuries of accumulated dietary philosophy—are marginalized under the banner of "science." True food security is not only "non-toxic and measurable," but "culturally sustainable".

History shows that cultural assimilation often begins at the table. After Rome conquered Gaul, wine replaced mead; during the Age of Exploration, potatoes and tomatoes reshaped European diets; after the Meiji Restoration, Japan’s "de-Asianization" began with eating beef. Today’s "global fast-foodification" is a new wave of unipolar cultural imperialism expressed through taste.

Innovation Pathways for Policy

We must advocate for "pluralistic food sovereignty":

  • Create a "Global Register of Traditional Foods," supporting indigenous peoples, smallholders, and heritage chefs in shaping international standards;
  • Add a "Right to Cultural Diet" clause within the WTO framework, limiting monopolistic marketing by multinationals;
  • Launch a "Taste Education Initiative," restoring native food narratives in primary school curricula;

Let food return to "meaning," not "commodity".

III. Abyss Three: "Supply Chain Tyranny" in Systemic Failure — Is Efficiency the Same as Risk?

When a single salmon is farmed in Norway, processed in Chile, packaged in China, and sold in Europe; when a bag of flour comes from Ukrainian wheat, Canadian yeast, Indian packaging, and African distribution—we pride ourselves on the "efficiency of global supply chains," yet overlook: extreme efficiency is precisely the source of systemic fragility.

The 2020 pandemic revealed the truth: 80% of the world’s vitamin C comes from China, 70% of palm oil from Indonesia, 60% of coffee from Brazil. When any node fails, the entire system collapses like a house of cards. The 2022 Ukraine war spiked global wheat prices; the 2023 Red Sea crisis disrupted Middle Eastern grain imports; the 2024 Panama Canal drought imposed shipping restrictions—"globalization" is no longer a safety net, but a risk amplifier.

Deeper still is the "tyranny of the system": multinational corporations, through vertical integration, patent monopolies, and data control, bind farmers, fishers, and artisans into "efficiency-first" chains. Brazilian soy farmers clear forests for export; Southeast Asian fishers overharvest to supply global markets; African smallholders abandon traditional crops for monocultures to enter supermarkets. Beneath the grand narrative of "global supply chains," countless local food systems are crushed.

This echoes the 19th-century British repeal of the Corn Laws: efficiency rose, but famines ravaged India and Ireland. History never left us—when systems pursue only "lowest cost, highest turnover," they lose sensitivity to life, ecology, and justice.

Philosophically, this is the extreme manifestation of modernity’s "instrumental rationality". Habermas warned: when the "system" (economy, technology) consumes the "lifeworld" (culture, ethics), society falls into a vacuum of meaning. Today’s food system is living proof of this prophecy.

Paradigm Breakthrough in Governance

We must shift toward "distributed resilient systems":

  • Promote "regional food circles" (e.g., EU "short supply chains," Africa’s "local staple program");
  • Develop "open-source agriculture," sharing seeds, technology, and data to break patent barriers;
  • Establish a "Global Food Reserve Alliance," led by the UN, allocating emergency resources based on population, ecology, and cultural needs;

From "efficiency hegemony" to "resilience and justice".

IV. Paradigm Breakthrough: From "Sovereignty" to "Symbiosis" — Rebuilding Food Civilization Through Triple Covenants

Facing these three abysses, we can no longer "patch" the ruptures—we need a paradigm revolution. True breakthrough lies not in technology, but in value reconstruction.

First: Rebuild the "Climate Covenant"

The relationship between humanity and Earth must shift from "conquest" to "symbiosis." The food system must lead the carbon-neutral transition: promote "regenerative agriculture" to sequester carbon in soil; develop "climate-smart fisheries" to restore marine ecosystems; impose a "global food carbon tax," making polluters pay and protectors benefit.

Second: Rebuild the "Cultural Covenant"

Food is not a "standardized product"—it is "civilizational expression." We must integrate a "Food Diversity Index" into national development metrics; support "Community-Supported Agriculture" (CSA), enabling direct dialogue between producers and consumers; elevate "cultural dietary rights" as non-economic priorities in international climate, trade, and health negotiations.

Third: Rebuild the "Systemic Covenant"

Break the "center-periphery" supply chain model and build a "multi-center food network". Drawing from China’s Belt and Road agricultural cooperation, Africa’s "Great Green Wall," and Latin America’s "Andean Food Alliance," we can form a cross-civilizational food governance community. This is not utopia—it is the inevitable choice for civilizational survival.

Taste has never been merely sensory. It is an extension of sovereignty, a code of culture, a mirror of ecology. When a person cannot decide what to eat, they lose their human dignity. When a civilization cannot preserve its food traditions, it begins to dissolve itself.

From Sumerian clay tablets with recipes, to the Silk Road’s spice trade, from Zheng He’s grain-laden voyages, to today’s global cold chains—humanity has never ceased its quest for "safe food." But this time, the challenge is not geographical or technological—it is the self-redemption of civilization.

Let us no longer see food as a commodity, but as a covenant—with the land, with our ancestors, with the future. Only then can taste reclaim sovereignty, and food become a messenger of peace, not a spark of war.

The paradigm has reached its tipping point: either we continue to sink into the abyss of efficiency and colonialism, or we leap into a new civilization of symbiosis and plurality. This is not merely a choice of governance—it is a test of humanity.
