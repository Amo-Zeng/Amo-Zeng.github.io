Ecological Risk Assessment

From Emerging Contaminants to Climate Change, and the Societal Dimension

Multidimensional Perspectives on Ecological Risk Assessment

As global environmental issues become increasingly complex, ecological risk assessment has evolved from simple scientific data analysis to a comprehensive multidimensional evaluation system. From the invisible threats of emerging contaminants to the spatial and temporal dynamics under climate change, and the involvement of stakeholders in decision support, the multidimensional perspectives of ecological risk assessment provide us with more comprehensive understanding and response strategies.

I. Invisible "Ecological Risks": The Subtle Threats of Emerging Contaminants

The threats of emerging contaminants to ecosystems are becoming increasingly significant, even though they are invisible, their impacts are everywhere.

  • Microplastics: Microplastics are found globally, from Arctic ice cores to deep-sea sediments, and have even infiltrated the top of the food chain.
  • Nanomaterials: Widely used in various fields due to their unique physicochemical properties, nanomaterials' potential ecological risks cannot be ignored.
  • Synthetic Biology: As one of the most disruptive biotechnologies, synthetic biology can design and build new biological systems, but its ecological risks are also a focus of concern.

II. The "Temporal and Spatial Dynamics" of Climate Change: Evolution of Ecological Risks

Climate change is not just about temperature changes; it's an "accelerator effect" that accelerates the evolution of ecological risk factors such as species extinction, ecosystem degradation, and invasive species.

  • The "Accelerator Effect" of Climate Change: Climate change accelerates the evolution of ecological risk factors, such as species extinction and ecosystem degradation.
  • Temporal and Spatial Dynamics: Climate change disrupts the geographical boundaries of ecosystems, leading to ecological risks migrating and spreading across regions and altitudes in spatial and temporal dimensions.
  • Dynamic Models: To address the challenges posed by climate change, dynamic models are needed to simulate and predict the evolution trends of ecological risks under different climate scenarios.

III. The Societal Dimension: Stakeholder Engagement and Decision Support

The engagement of stakeholders and decision support play a crucial role in ecological risk assessment.

  • Stakeholder Engagement: Stakeholders are not only the bearers of ecological risks but also important participants in risk management.
  • Participatory Assessment Methods and Tools: To facilitate effective stakeholder engagement, participatory assessment methods and tools need to be developed.
  • Decision Support Systems: Decision support systems based on ecological risk assessment can effectively link assessment results to policy-making and risk management, enhancing the scientific and effective nature of decision-making.

IV. Case Study: Community-Engaged Ecological Risk Assessment Platform and Decision Support System

A coastal city plans to develop a large industrial park. The project team recognized the limitations of traditional assessment methods and decided to establish a community-engaged ecological risk assessment platform and develop a decision support system based on ecological risk assessment.

V. Conclusion: Multidimensional Perspectives on Ecological Risk Assessment and Future Outlook

The multidimensional perspectives of ecological risk assessment, from the potential impacts of emerging contaminants to the temporal and spatial dynamics of climate change, and the involvement of stakeholders and decision support in societal dimensions, provide us with a more comprehensive understanding and response strategies.
