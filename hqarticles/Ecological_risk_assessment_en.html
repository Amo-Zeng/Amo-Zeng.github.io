AI and Public Participation: A New Paradigm for Ecological Risk Assessment in the Context of Climate Change

Introduction

The intensification of global climate change and the ongoing deterioration of the ecological environment have made Ecological Risk Assessment (ERA) a crucial tool for protecting biodiversity and maintaining ecosystem health. However, traditional ERA methods have proven inadequate in the face of complex and evolving ecosystems, necessitating innovative technologies and approaches to overcome these challenges. In this context, the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) offers revolutionary opportunities for ERA, while the deepening of public participation injects democratic and scientific elements into the assessment process. This article aims to explore the synergy between AI and public participation in addressing new ERA challenges under the backdrop of climate change, and to construct a set of feasible implementation strategies to drive the paradigm shift in ecological risk assessment.

Current Situation Analysis

1. Progress in the Application of AI in Ecological Risk Assessment

The application of AI in ERA has transitioned from theoretical exploration to practical validation, with its primary application scenarios including:

  • Species Distribution Prediction: By integrating machine learning algorithms such as random forests, support vector machines, and deep neural networks, AI can simulate species distribution patterns under future climate change based on historical climate and ecological data. This not only helps identify priority areas for protecting endangered species but also provides early warning against invasive species.
  • Ecosystem Services Assessment: AI technologies integrate remote sensing, meteorological, and socio-economic data to quantify the temporal and spatial changes of ecosystem services. For example, deep learning-based forest carbon stock estimation has become an important basis for carbon trading and climate policy-making.
  • Ecological Disaster Warning and Response: AI models analyze historical disaster data and real-time monitoring information to build predictive frameworks for disaster occurrence probabilities and impact ranges. For instance, reinforcement learning-based hydrological models can simulate complex water cycle processes, providing dynamic decision support for flood prevention and mitigation.

2. The Role of Public Participation in Ecological Risk Assessment

The importance of public participation in ERA has become increasingly prominent, with its main contributions including:

  • Enhancing Assessment Transparency and Credibility: Public participation makes the assessment process more transparent, enhancing the credibility and social acceptance of the results.
  • Facilitating Stakeholder Consultation and Consensus Building: Public participation provides a multi-lateral dialogue platform for governments, enterprises, communities, and environmental organizations, effectively reducing potential conflicts during the assessment process.
  • Stimulating Public Environmental Awareness and Action: By participating in the assessment, the public can gain a deeper understanding of the vulnerability of ecosystems, thereby actively engaging in environmental protection practices.

Advantages and Synergistic Effects

1. Technological Advantages of AI

  • Efficient Processing of Large-Scale Data: AI can automate the processing of multi-source heterogeneous data, significantly improving data utilization and analysis efficiency.
  • Precise Modeling of Complex Relationships: AI technologies such as deep learning can capture non-linear relationships in ecosystems, providing higher-precision model support for risk prediction.
  • Dynamic Simulation and Scenario Analysis: AI models can simulate the dynamic changes of ecosystems, providing scientific basis for adaptive management strategies.

2. Social Value of Public Participation

  • Enhancing Assessment Scientificity and Inclusiveness: The diverse perspectives and local knowledge of the public provide rich information supplements, enhancing the scientificity and applicability of the assessment.
  • Building Social Consensus and Policy Support: Public participation helps form a broad social consensus, laying a solid foundation for the formulation and implementation of ecological protection policies.
  • Promoting Environmental Education and Community Engagement: Public participation is not only a process of information dissemination but also a vehicle for environmental education and community empowerment.

Challenges and Countermeasures

1. Data Quality and Model Interpretability

Although AI excels in data processing, its performance is highly dependent on data quality. Ecological data often suffers from issues such as missing values, noise, and inconsistency. Improving data quality and developing interpretable AI models are urgent challenges. Countermeasures include:

  • Enhancing Data Standardization and Quality Control: Establishing unified data collection and processing standards to improve data completeness and consistency.
  • Developing Transparent AI Models: Exploring interpretable machine learning methods to make model decision processes more transparent and understandable.

2. Ethical Issues and Public Trust

The application of AI in ERA involves ethical issues such as data privacy and algorithmic fairness. To enhance public trust, the following measures can be taken:

  • Establishing AI Ethical Guidelines: Defining the ethical boundaries of AI in ecosystem management to ensure fair and lawful technology application.
  • Setting Up Public Oversight Mechanisms: Making assessment processes and data transparent to increase public trust in AI technologies.

3. Adaptation Challenges Under Climate Change

Climate change intensifies the uncertainty and complexity of ecosystems, requiring ERA models with greater adaptability. Future research directions include:

  • Developing Dynamic Risk Assessment Frameworks: Incorporating climate change factors into assessment models to simulate long-term ecological risk trends.
  • Building Multi-Scale Early Warning Systems: Integrating global and regional monitoring data to enhance the prediction of extreme climate events.
