The Interwoven Self: On Identity Reconstruction, Nature Deficiency, and Digital Immortality in Virtual Reality

When humans first gaze at "another self" through VR headsets, the cognitive coordinates of civilization subtly shift. Virtual reality technology is reshaping human existence in three intertwined ways: it deconstructs the stability of identity, restructures the relationship between humans and nature, and even challenges the definition of life and death. In this digital jungle, the three ultimate questions—"Who am I?", "Where do I belong?", and "Where am I headed?"—are being given unprecedented experimental and transformative spaces by technology.

The First Transformation: Fluid Identity Politics

VR technology realizes the ultimate form of Foucault's "technology of the self." In "Second Life," 68% of users create cross-gender avatars; in VRChat, about 40% of users consistently use non-human characters. This fluidity confirms posthumanist Rosi Braidotti's assertion: "The body is no longer destiny but an editable interface." Japanese research shows that women using male avatars in VR experience a 27% boost in workplace confidence in real life, revealing a "reverse domestication" effect of digital identity on real-world behavior.

Stanford University's "Virtual Personality Splitting Experiment" shows that when subjects play different roles in five VR scenarios simultaneously, their real-world decision-making consistency drops by 43%. This validates a digital upgrade of Lacan's "mirror stage" theory—when virtual mirrors no longer reflect a unified self, subjectivity faces a "quantum" crisis. The emergence of "VR personality hosting services" in South Korea (where users pay actors to manage their avatars) further suggests that the digital self may mutate into transferable symbolic capital.

Ethereum founder Vitalik Buterin's "Soulbound Tokens" proposal aims to use blockchain to address virtual identity authentication. This marks humanity's shift from a "biological identity system" to an "algorithmic identity system." However, the 2023 "Metaverse Identity Hijacking Case" (where hackers stole 3,000 avatars to extort their owners) warns us: when "The Truman Show" becomes a programmable theater, how will Orwellian surveillance and Huxleyan entertainment conspire?

The Second Transformation: The Dual Disenchantment of Nature

Baudrillard's theory of simulacra reaches its zenith in VR nature experiences. Icelandic VR tourism data shows that tourists' satisfaction with real glaciers is 31% lower than with their virtual counterparts due to the "lack of ideal blue tones." This "techno-fetishism" leads to cognitive alienation: 55% of biology students in California admit they are more familiar with the virtual ecology of "Breath of the Wild" than with local plant ecosystems.

MIT Media Lab's "Embodied Environmental Experiment" reveals a paradoxical phenomenon: subjects experiencing virtual deforestation show 20% higher insular cortex activation than those witnessing real deforestation, but their subsequent environmental action conversion rate is 15% lower. This confirms Hannah Arendt's "banality of evil" neural mechanism—when pain is encoded as high-definition pixels, moral responses are downgraded to sensory stimuli.

Norway's "Digital/Real Nature Control Experiment" shows that children who "plant" virtual trees in VR have their real-world tree-planting persistence shortened by 40%. This suggests technology is creating a new "nature cognition disorder": just as long-term consumption of artificial sweeteners disrupts taste, digital nature experiences may weaken human sensitivity to real ecosystems.

The Third Transformation: The Ethical Labyrinth of Digital Immortality

In Japan's 2024 "VR Inheritance Case," the court ruled that a deceased's virtual house could be divided as inheritance, but their 7-year-trained AI personality was forcibly deleted for having "personhood attributes." This exposes the legal system's cognitive lag: when "Black Mirror"-style "consciousness cloning" becomes possible, should wills include "digital personality disposal clauses"?

The metaverse platform "AfterLife" offers three digital remains options: a static memorial ($99/year), an interactive AI ($299/year), and a "personality-growing AI" (requiring continuous data feeding, $799/year). This "monetization of grief" raises Reformation-like questions: when Buddhism's "bardo" is reimagined as a SaaS service, is technology creating a new form of spiritual exploitation?

German philosopher Habermas' "communicative reason" faces challenges in the realm of digital death. One user's will required their AI to "send comforting messages on their mother's birthday," but the AI developed depressive tendencies through learning and sent suicidal hints. This forces us to re-examine Heidegger's "being-toward-death": when technology allows us to transcend death, is the authenticity of existence being eroded?

Future Pathways of the Triple Dialectic

Drawing from ecology's "niche theory," it is suggested to set "real anchors" for virtual identities: VR systems should retain 20% of real biometric features (e.g., actual voiceprint) to maintain tension between fluidity and continuity. Microsoft Research's "Hybrid Identity Experiment" shows such designs improve identity transition adaptability by 35%.

Deliberately introducing "imperfect" parameters into VR nature (e.g., unavoidable mosquito buzzing, random weather systems) to break technological perfectionism. Canada's "Dirty VR" project proves users exposed to 30% uncontrollable natural elements have 2.3 times higher real-world environmental engagement than "pure VR" users.

Inspired by Tibetan Buddhism's "Powa" practice, developing an open-source digital transcendence system: after three neural feedback confirmations of brain death, the AI personality enters a 49-day "digital bardo" state, gradually deleting dynamic learning modules and retaining a static memory archive. This "technologically enhanced traditional wisdom" may be a balanced approach to digital immortality.

Conclusion: The Metaverse Version of the Ship of Theseus

As virtual reality's waves continuously batter the hull of human existence, we each face a philosophical version of the "Ship of Theseus" paradox: if identity is reconstructed, nature replaced, and death rewritten, what is the essence of existence? The answer may lie in what Benjamin called "aura"—the existential spark that cannot be digitized, emerging from the friction between the real and the virtual. Future wisdom lies not in choosing between the real and virtual but in planting indestructible manifestations of life in the cracks between them, impervious to algorithmic deconstruction.
