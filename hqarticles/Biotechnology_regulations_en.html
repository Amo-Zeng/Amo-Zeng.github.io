Global Governance of Biotechnology: Promoting Regulatory Coordination, Public Participation, and Ethical Balance

With the rapid development of biotechnology, its applications have expanded from healthcare to agriculture, environmental protection, energy, and many other fields. However, the rapid advancement of biotechnology also brings challenges in ethics, safety, and the environment, which often have cross-border implications and are difficult for a single country to address independently. Therefore, promoting regulatory coordination among countries through international agreements and cooperation mechanisms is a key approach to addressing global biotechnology challenges. Additionally, public participation and ethical balance are crucial factors in ensuring the healthy development of biotechnology. This article will explore how to achieve global governance of biotechnology from these three perspectives.

1. Cross-Border Challenges of Biotechnology and the Importance of International Agreements

The cross-border challenges of biotechnology are mainly reflected in the following areas:

  • Biosafety: The cross-border movement of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) can have unforeseen impacts on local ecosystems, such as gene pollution and loss of biodiversity.
  • Ethical Issues: The application of gene editing technologies like CRISPR-Cas9 has sparked ethical debates about human embryo editing and gene enhancement.
  • Intellectual Property: Innovations in the biotechnology sector often involve complex intellectual property issues, and the patent protection standards vary across countries, affecting the global dissemination and application of technology.
  • Data Sharing: Biotechnology research relies on the collection and analysis of large amounts of data, but data privacy and security issues pose obstacles to international cooperation.

In the face of these challenges, the establishment of international agreements and cooperation mechanisms is particularly important:

  • Unified Standards: Through international agreements, unified biotechnology standards and regulations can be established, reducing the differences in national regulations and lowering compliance costs for multinational companies.
  • Information Sharing: Establishing international information sharing platforms can promote the exchange of research and regulatory experiences among countries, enhancing the transparency and efficiency of global biotechnology governance.
  • Joint Research: Through international cooperation projects, joint biotechnology research can be conducted, especially in addressing global issues such as climate change and disease control, concentrating resources to accelerate technological breakthroughs.
  • Ethical Consensus: International agreements can promote consensus on ethical issues in biotechnology among countries, establishing a global ethical framework to guide the healthy development of biotechnology.

2. Existing International Agreements and Cooperation Mechanisms

Currently, several international agreements and cooperation mechanisms have played important roles in promoting global coordination of biotechnology regulations:

  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD): Signed in 1992, it aims to protect biodiversity and ensure the sustainable use of biological resources, including the regulation of the cross-border movement of GMOs.
  • Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety: Adopted in 2000, it supplements the CBD and specifically addresses the cross-border transfer, handling, and use of GMOs.
  • International Convention for the Protection of New Varieties of Plants (UPOV): Established in 1961, it aims to protect the intellectual property rights of new plant varieties and promote international cooperation in plant breeding.
  • World Health Organization (WHO): In the field of biotechnology, especially gene editing technologies, the WHO has issued multiple guidance documents to promote the formation of global consensus on ethical and safety issues.

3. Public Participation in the Formulation of Biotechnology Regulations

Public participation in the formulation of biotechnology regulations not only enhances the democracy and social acceptance of the regulations but also improves their scientific and practical feasibility. Specifically:

  • Enhancing Democracy: Public participation ensures the transparency and openness of the regulatory process, making the regulations more in line with public wishes and needs, thereby enhancing their democratic nature.
  • Improving Social Acceptance: Through public participation, public concerns and expectations regarding biotechnology can be timely understood and addressed, reducing public misunderstanding and fear of biotechnology and increasing social acceptance of biotechnology regulations.
  • Promoting Scientific Decision-Making: Public participation provides diverse perspectives and information for the formulation of regulations, helping decision-makers to more comprehensively assess the risks and benefits of biotechnology, thus making more scientifically sound and reasonable decisions.

Ways of public participation include:

  • Public Consultations: Governments and relevant institutions can widely solicit public opinions and suggestions on biotechnology regulations through public hearings, online surveys, community discussions, and other forms.
  • Citizen Science Projects: Citizen science projects involve ordinary citizens in the scientific research process. By participating in actual research activities, citizens can gain a deeper understanding of the principles and applications of biotechnology, thereby providing more professional and specific suggestions in the formulation of regulations.
  • Education and Training: Conducting education and training activities related to biotechnology can improve the scientific literacy and legal awareness of the public, enabling them to participate more rationally in the regulatory process.
