From "Othering" to "Symbiotic Transformation": Trauma Narratives, Ecocriticism, and Virtual World Hegemony

Multidimensional Critique of Postcolonialism

Postcolonialism, as a profound critical theory, has always focused on power relations, cultural identity, and the dynamic evolution of subjectivity. Although its theoretical foundation is rooted in the analysis of colonial history, its influence has extended beyond historical boundaries, encompassing broader fields such as ecocriticism and virtual world studies. This article aims to explore three interrelated themes through the intersecting lens of postcolonialism: (1) the dialectical relationship between colonial trauma and the reconstruction of subjectivity; (2) the paradigm shift from "othering" to "symbiotic transformation" in ecocriticism; (3) the multidimensional critique of digital colonialism and metaverse hegemony. Through these analyses, we will reveal how postcolonialism provides profound theoretical insights and practical implications for addressing the complex issues of contemporary global culture, ecology, and digital power.

I. From "Colonial Trauma" to "Reconstruction of Subjectivity": The Dialectical Relationship Between Postcolonialism and Trauma Narratives

Trauma narratives from a postcolonial perspective offer important theoretical tools for understanding the collective memory of colonial history and the formation of cultural identity. Colonialism was not only a political and economic oppression but also an all-encompassing cultural violence. While depriving colonized peoples of political autonomy, it inflicted profound collective trauma by plundering natural resources and suppressing cultural traditions. This trauma is not just personal suffering but also a collective loss of cultural memory and identity for entire communities.

Trauma narratives in postcolonial literature and culture are not just representations of colonial violence and oppression but also explorations of cultural expression and identity reconstruction. By recounting the violence and deprivation of colonial history, colonized peoples can recover erased historical memories and reshape cultural identities. Trauma is not an endpoint but a starting point for reconstructing subjectivity. Postcolonial theory suggests that trauma narratives are not only painful retrospection but also opportunities for cultural regeneration and identity reconstruction. By reflecting on colonial history, colonized peoples can not only confront past pains but also draw strength to construct new, open, and dynamic cultural identities.

II. From "Othering" to "Symbiotic Transformation": The Intersecting Lens of Postcolonialism and Ecocriticism

Although seemingly distinct in their research objects, postcolonialism and ecocriticism resonate deeply in their critiques of the hegemonic logic of colonialism and anthropocentrism. Both reveal how the internal logic of "othering" leads to vicious cycles of dual oppression and exploitation of nature and culture.

"Othering" is the core logic of colonialism and anthropocentrism. Within the framework of colonialism, non-Western cultures are constructed as backward and savage "others," allowing Western nations to establish cultural hegemony through cultural aggression and economic exploitation. In the framework of anthropocentrism, nature is viewed as an object for human conquest and exploitation, its intrinsic value completely denied. This "natural othering" not only leads to the destruction of ecosystems and the loss of biodiversity but also exacerbates global environmental crises.

The intersecting lens of postcolonialism and ecocriticism offers possibilities for deconstructing "othering." First, both aim to expose the power discourses of colonialism and anthropocentrism, revealing their oppressive constructions of nature and culture. Postcolonialism emphasizes the plurality and equality of cultural subjectivity, while ecocriticism stresses the intrinsic value and rights of nature, calling for a symbiotic relationship between humans and nature. Second, both seek to break the binary opposition between "self" and "other," advocating for more inclusive and equitable relationships. Postcolonialism promotes multi-voiced, decentralized cultural dialogue mechanisms, while ecocriticism advocates for harmonious symbiotic communities between humans and nature.

III. From "Digital Colonialism" to "Metaverse Hegemony": The Extension and Critique of Postcolonialism in Virtual Worlds

With the rapid development of digital technologies, the theoretical perspective of postcolonialism is equally applicable to analyzing power relations in virtual worlds. From "digital colonialism" to "metaverse hegemony," the specter of postcolonialism continues to exploit and control global South nations in more subtle and powerful ways within virtual spaces.

Digital colonialism is an extension of postcolonialism in the digital age, leveraging technological barriers, information hegemony, and economic exploitation to lock global South nations into the margins of the digital economy. Developed countries monopolize core internet technologies and infrastructure, restricting access to advanced technologies and information resources for developing countries. Simultaneously, digital colonialism employs platform monopolies, data exploitation, and algorithmic discrimination to conduct covert economic plunder of developing countries. This digital exploitation is not only economic inequality but also cultural and ideological control.

The metaverse, as the next iteration of the internet, carries humanity's limitless imagination of virtual worlds. However, driven by capital and tech giants, the metaverse is becoming a new tool of hegemony. Metaverse hegemony is not only reflected in control over virtual world infrastructure but also in the penetration of virtual world culture and ideology. Western nations leverage their advantages in digital cultural products, gaming, social media, and more to implant their values and ideologies into the metaverse, conducting cultural colonization and ideological infiltration in developing countries.

IV. Conclusion: From "Trauma Narratives" to "Symbiotic Futures"

Postcolonial perspectives on trauma narratives, ecocriticism, and virtual world hegemony offer multiple vantage points for understanding contemporary global challenges. The dialectical relationship between colonial trauma and the reconstruction of subjectivity reveals the dynamics and openness of cultural identities; the call for "symbiotic transformation" in ecocriticism invites us to reflect on the relationships between humans and nature, and among cultures; the analysis of digital colonialism and metaverse hegemony reminds us to be vigilant against new forms of hegemony in virtual worlds.

In today's era of globalization and cultural pluralism, we need to construct open, dynamic, and creative cultural identities based on acknowledging historical traumas. This reconstruction process is not only a response to the past but also an adaptation to the future. Through practices of cultural symbiosis, ecological symbiosis, and digital symbiosis, we can collectively build a more just, harmonious, and sustainable world. The theoretical insights of postcolonialism provide essential tools for understanding and addressing these challenges.
