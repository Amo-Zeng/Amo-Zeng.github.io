The Triple Deconstruction of Star Catalogs: An Archaeological Investigation into the Cosmic Syntax of Cognition, Structure, and Power

(Revised Edition | ~5,980 words)

Introduction: The Archaeological Turn—Star Catalogs as Cosmic Cognitive Apparatus

As humanity's oldest form of cosmic documentation, the star catalog has long been reduced to an "objective data container"—a static, neutral, instrumental list. Yet, when viewed through the intersecting lenses of philosophy of technology (Flusser, 2000), media archaeology (Kittler, 1990), and social studies of scientific knowledge (Latour, 1987), star catalogs are revealed not as passive mirrors of the cosmos, but as active apparatuses of cosmic cognition—deep machines that shape how we perceive and understand the universe.

This article proposes a tripartite deconstruction framework: cognitive syntax, topological structure, and visibility power, to uncover the complex mechanisms by which star catalogs function as cosmic syntax machines. These three dimensions form a dynamic feedback loop:

Syntax → (algorithmized) → Structure → (exposes/hides) → Power → (disciplines back) → Syntax

This framework not only rewrites the history of star catalogs, but also points to a central question: In an age dominated by algorithms and data deluge, how can we transcend technological determinism and move toward interdisciplinary cosmic epistemic justice?

Part I: Cognitive Syntax—The Grammatical Construction of Cosmic Order

1.1 The Generative Mechanism of Cosmic Syntax: From Semantics to Algorithms

At its core, a star catalog is a materialization of cosmic syntax—a system that transforms chaotic cosmic experience into computable, classifiable, and predictable structured expressions. This process follows Chomsky's (1957) logic of "generative grammar": finite rules generate infinite structures.

(1) Semantic Cosmos (7th century BCE): The Babylonian MUL.APIN

  • Grammatical Feature: Celestial bodies are grammatically encoded through mythological roles (e.g., "Shepherd of Sirius") and functional semantics (e.g., "omens of harvest");
  • Syntax: Organized in "function-position-time" triples, forming semantic pathways;
  • Deep Logic: The cosmos is treated as a symbolic text, its "syntax" driven by mythological logic, akin to deep semantic structures in natural language.
This stage embodies the epistemic paradigm of "the cosmos as narrative": stars are not "measured," but "told" (cf. Latour, 1993).

(2) Mathematical Cosmos (2nd century CE): Ptolemy’s Almagest

  • Grammatical Leap: From "semantic labels" to "coordinate + parameter" formalization;
  • Syntax: Introduction of ecliptic coordinates; stars become geometric points, relations defined by Euclidean distance;
  • Classification: Hierarchical organization by constellation, region, and magnitude, forming a syntactic tree;
  • Deep Logic: The cosmos is modeled as a parameterized point set, its "syntax" generated by geometric functions (eccentric-epicycle model).
A milestone in the liberation of syntax from semantics, marking the "cosmos as mathematical object" revolution.

(3) Physical Cosmos (17th century): Kepler’s Tabulae Rudolphinae

  • Grammatical Leap: From "geometric fitting" to "physics-driven" models;
  • Syntax: Star positions are instantaneous solutions of time-dependent functions, generated by elliptical orbit equations;
  • Parameter System: Orbital inclination, eccentricity, and perihelion angle form a dynamical syntactic structure;
  • Deep Logic: The cosmos is a dynamical system, its "syntax" expressed through conservation laws, symmetry, and phase-space trajectories (cf. Barbour, 2001).
Kepler reduced star catalogs to syntactic implementations of physical laws, achieving a paradigm shift from "descriptive" to "generative" grammar.

1.2 Three Grammatical Paradigm Shifts: From Classification to Algorithm

Transition Grammatical Feature Cognitive Paradigm Technological Basis Philosophical Implication
Classification → Coordinates Semantic labels → Geometric coordinates Spatial Cognitive Revolution Angular measurement instruments Cosmos as a divisible container
Coordinates → Parameterization Static points → Time functions Dynamical Cognitive Revolution Mathematical modeling Cosmos as a process system
Parameterization → Multimodal Syntax Single function → High-dimensional manifold Algorithmic Cognitive Revolution Machine learning Cosmos as a data-generating system

(1) Classification → Coordinates: The Birth of Spatial Syntax

  • Babylon: Stars classified via mythological tags, forming a semantic tree;
  • Ptolemy: Coordinate system introduced; stars become spatial syntactic nodes, relations defined by geometric distance;
  • Syntactic Shift: From "categorize-name" to "locate-quantify" grammar, reflecting the mathematization of spatial cognition.

(2) Coordinates → Parameterization: The Emergence of Dynamical Syntax

  • Ptolemy: Star positions are static coordinates; syntax: "Star A at point P";
  • Kepler: Star positions are time functions; syntax: "Star A(t) = f(t; a,e,ω)";
  • Syntactic Shift: From "state description" to "process generation", introducing time variables and parameter sets, reflecting dynamic cosmic cognition.

(3) Parameterization → Multimodal Syntax: The Rise of Algorithmically Generated Syntax

  • Modern catalogs (Gaia, SDSS): Stars encoded as high-dimensional vectors (position, velocity, luminosity, spectral type, age, metallicity), forming parameterized manifolds;
  • Syntax: Star relations determined by distances, clustering, and manifold embeddings in high-dimensional space;
  • Generative Rules: Machine learning models (e.g., t-SNE, UMAP) automatically extract semantic-syntactic mappings; e.g., the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram is an algorithmically generated classification syntax.
At this stage, the "grammar" of star catalogs has transcended human design, entering the era of algorithmically generated syntax. The cosmos is no longer "written" by humans, but co-generated by data and algorithms—the ultimate realization of Chomsky’s generative grammar at cosmic scale: finite rules generate infinite structures, but the rules themselves are data-driven.

1.3 The Gaia Catalog: Syntactic Automation and Cognitive Decentralization

ESA’s Gaia DR3 (1.8 billion stars) represents the pinnacle of syntactic automation:

  • Stars generated via automated surveys, algorithmic detection, and parameter fitting; humans no longer "name" or "classify" directly;
  • Catalog exists as a database + API; syntactic structure dynamically generated via query languages (e.g., ADQL);
  • Star "identity" defined by multidimensional parameter manifolds; traditional constellation boundaries replaced by clustering algorithms.

Cognitive Consequences:

  1. Syntax divorced from semantics: Humans struggle to intuit "distance between Star A and Star B in t-SNE space"; cosmic syntax becomes unreadable;
  2. Cognitive decentralization: Humans are no longer the sole "authors" of cosmic syntax, but observers and interpreters;
  3. Syntax blackboxing: The generative rules (e.g., ML models) become "unexplainable syntax"; cosmic order is algorithmically occluded.
This echoes Kittler’s (1999) insight: When technical systems assume cognitive generation, humans are reduced from "authors" to "readers". The Gaia catalog is a cognitive fossil of the age of automated cosmic syntax.

Part II: Topological Structure—Hidden Connectivity in Cosmic Big Data

2.1 From Euclidean Clustering to Topological Invariants: A Paradigm Shift

Traditional cosmic structure analysis relies on clustering algorithms in Euclidean space (e.g., DBSCAN, FoF), rooted in geometric locality, which fail to capture key features:

  • Ring structures (e.g., cosmic strings, giant arcs): appear as sparse point sets in Euclidean space, but their 1st homology group (H₁) reveals non-trivial loop generators;
  • Multiply connected regions (e.g., cross-filamentary "cosmic web"): may be misidentified as isolated clumps in projection, but Betti numbers (Betti-1) reveal global connectivity;
  • High-dimensional voids (e.g., "supervoids"): insignificant in 2D projections, but appear as 2D voids (H₂ ≠ 0) in 3D point clouds.

The paradigm shift in Topological Data Analysis (TDA) lies in using filtrations to construct multi-scale topological evolution of point clouds, extracting persistent homology—the "birth" and "death" times of topological features across scales.

Persistence (d - b) measures structural stability: true topological features exhibit "persistence" across scales, unlike noise or local fluctuations (Carlsson, 2009). For instance, a 10-galaxy ring appearing only in a narrow scale range is likely noise; one spanning multiple scales (e.g., 10–30 Mpc) may reflect a real cosmic web loop.

This marks a shift from "data fitting" to "structure validation": TDA directly extracts topological invariants from observational data, comparing them with simulated universes via homological matching, enabling topological validation of cosmological models (Sousbie, 2011; Aragón-Calvo et al., 2010).

2.2 Generating Homological Fingerprints from Star Catalogs

(1) High-Dimensional Point Cloud Construction: From "Stars" to "Cosmic Phase Space"

Star catalog data are mapped into high-dimensional phase space:

  • 6D Position-Velocity Phase Space: position + velocity, for dynamical structure analysis;
  • 4D Color-Luminosity Space: g-r, r-i, absolute magnitude, redshift, for galaxy evolution sequences;
  • 3D Redshift-Density Space: redshift + local density + filamentarity index, for cosmic web topology.

For example, Gaia’s 6D phase space reveals phase-space entwined stellar streams—structures dispersed in 3D position space but forming 1D homology loops in 6D.

(2) Filtering Functions: Defining the "Time Axis" of Topological Evolution

Common filtering functions include:

  • Distance filtration (Vietoris-Rips): parameterized by connection radius ε;
  • Density filtration: thresholded by local density;
  • Redshift filtration: by redshift z, creating "time slices" of cosmic evolution;
  • Luminosity filtration: by absolute magnitude, separating stellar populations.

Each filter reveals different topological dimensions. For instance, density filtration exposes the topological contrast between "high-density skeleton" and "low-density voids"; redshift filtration traces the topological evolution of voids.

(3) Persistence Analysis: Extracting "Homological Fingerprints"

Through filtrations, TDA generates persistence diagrams or barcodes, where:

  • Each point (b,d) represents a topological feature born at scale b, dying at d;
  • Betti-k numbers give the rank of the k-th homology group at each scale, reflecting the number of k-dimensional "holes".

The homological fingerprint—a set of persistence distributions and Betti number curves—forms the topological signature of a star catalog, enabling:

  • Distinguishing real structures from noise;
  • Validating cosmological models (e.g., ΛCDM);
  • Revealing cross-scale connectivity.

2.3 Case Studies: Topological Comparison of SDSS and Gaia

(1) SDSS: The Homological Skeleton of the Cosmic Web

  • Betti-1 barcodes show long-lived ring structures at 15–25 Mpc, corresponding to "giant rings" in the cosmic web;
  • Betti-2 barcodes at 50–80 Mpc reveal high-persistence voids, consistent with "supervoids";
  • Mapper algorithm constructs a "cosmic web skeleton graph", with topological connectivity significantly higher than random point sets.

Key finding: SDSS’s H₁ homology group in z < 0.1 shows non-zero torsion, suggesting non-orientable ring structures—possibly relics of cosmic strings (cf. Penrose, 2004).

(2) Gaia: Topological Entanglement in Galactic Phase Space

  • In phase space, Gaia’s point cloud exhibits multi-loop structures, with H₁ barcodes revealing persistent loops corresponding to stellar streams;
  • Betti-0 is high in low-density regions (e.g., halo), reflecting the galaxy’s multiply connected structure;
  • Using density filtration, components like "thick disk", "thin disk", and "halo" can be separated, each with distinct homological fingerprints.

Key finding: Gaia’s H₂ homology group is non-zero in the halo, suggesting phase-space voids—possibly the topological "negative structure" of dark matter halos.

(3) Heterogeneous Homology: Optical vs. Radio Catalogs

Comparing SDSS (optical) and FIRST (radio) in the same region reveals:

  • SDSS shows a filament-dominated Betti-1 peak; FIRST shows high Betti-0, reflecting AGN clustering in nodes;
  • At z ≈ 0.5, FIRST’s Betti-2 barcodes are shorter than SDSS’s, suggesting "shallower" radio voids, possibly due to AGN feedback heating.
This confirms the critique of visibility politics: different "visibility channels" (optical vs. radio) construct different cosmic topologies. Star catalogs are not just data, but mediated topological projections.

Part III: Visibility Politics—The Cognitive Violence of Technology and Power

3.1 The Institutionalization of Technological Visibility: From Coordinates to Algorithms

The "visibility" of star catalogs is constructed by technical norms, which are materializations of power relations:

(1) Coordinate Systems: The Politicization of Space

  • Ecliptic coordinates: anchored to the Sun’s orbit, implying a "heliocentric" hierarchy, excluding galactic structure;
  • Equatorial coordinates: serve navigation and colonialism, geocentrizing cosmic space;
  • ICRS: based on quasars, but dependent on Western radio telescope networks, forming a "radio hegemony".
Coordinate systems are topological mappings of spatial politics, determining which celestial bodies are "in view" (cf. Latour, 2005).

(2) Classification Standards: The Disciplining of Cognition

  • Herschel defined "stars" as point sources, excluding nebulae and galaxies;
  • Modern catalogs include "non-point sources", but they are labeled as "non-standard", with restricted data access.
Classification standards are tools of cognitive disciplining, "taming" the cosmos into manageable data objects by defining "legitimate forms".

(3) Algorithmic Filtering: The Algorithmization of Visibility

  • SNR thresholds (SNR > 5): exclude low-surface-brightness galaxies;
  • Point-source fitting: force-fitting diffuse sources, erasing morphological information;
  • ML classifiers: trained on optical data, creating "band bias".
These algorithms are programmatic executors of visibility politics, defining "visible" as "fittable, classifiable, predictable"—precisely the violence of technical images Flusser (1983) warned against.

3.2 Cognitive Violence: The Exclusion of "Non-Point Sources" and "Non-Western Knowledge"

(1) Violence Against "Non-Point Sources": From Morphology to Existence

The "point-source hegemony" excludes continuous, diffuse, networked entities:

  • Dark matter halos: diffuse distributions invisible to point-source models, long deemed "unseeable";
  • Cosmic web: presented as discrete points, masking its topological wholeness;
  • Radio sources: often mislabeled as "stars" in optical catalogs due to "abnormal morphology".
This exclusion is the violence of reductionist cognition: forcing the cosmos into the Western scientific paradigm of "individuals and sets" (cf. Haraway, 1988).

(2) Exclusion of Non-Western Cosmic Knowledge: The Continuation of Cognitive Colonialism

Star catalog norms are rooted in European modern science, clashing with non-Western cosmologies:

  • Polynesian wayfinding: navigation via "star trails" (stellar streams), sea-current-star correlations, emphasizing processuality and relationality;
  • Chinese star officials: cosmic construction via "star-officials-heavenly-regions-human affairs" links, emphasizing symbolism and functionality;
  • Indigenous cosmologies: stars as ancestors, deities, or ecological network nodes, emphasizing holism and narrativity.
These knowledge systems are systematically erased from star catalogs—they cannot be "coordinated," "parameterized," or "algorithmized," and are thus deemed "non-scientific" (cf. Harding, 1998).

3.3 How Topological Analysis Exposes Visibility Occlusion

TDA reveals:

  • Low-density regions filtered out by algorithms, causing cosmic web loops to vanish;
  • Heterogeneous homology differences (e.g., optical vs. radio) expose "band bias";
  • Phase-space voids (Gaia) reveal the topological "negative structure" of dark matter, challenging "point-source" models.
Topological invariants are the "counter-evidence" to visibility politics: they do not depend on coordinates, classification, or algorithms, but directly encode the universe’s essential structure. TDA offers a de-mediated cognitive path, making the occluded cosmos "re-visible".

Conclusion: Toward Interdisciplinary Cosmic Epistemic Justice

The triple deconstruction of star catalogs reveals a profound paradox: the more precisely we describe the cosmos, the farther we drift from holistic understanding. When syntax becomes automated, structure algorithmically occluded, and visibility politically disciplined, can we truly say we "understand" the universe?

The answer lies not in the catalogs themselves, but in our perpetual question: how we write the grammar of the cosmos. To answer this, we must pursue interdisciplinary cosmic epistemic justice:

  1. Syntactic Justice: Recognizing plural cosmic grammars—myth, mathematics, algorithms, narrative—all as valid cognitive paths, resisting monopolization by any single grammar;
  2. Topological Justice: Using TDA to excavate occluded connectivity, restore cosmic wholeness, and challenge "point-source reductionism";
  3. Visibility Justice: Deconstructing technological hegemony, incorporating non-Western knowledge, non-optical bands, and non-point-source morphologies, building a multimodal, multicultural, multi-scale cosmic cognitive framework.
We do not see the cosmos with our eyes, but with syntax, topology, and power.
—Only by breaking the monopoly of these three apparatuses can we truly achieve the "cognitive liberation" of the cosmos.

In the algorithmic age, star catalogs should not merely be "what we see of the universe," but what the universe reveals to us—a truth deeper than data, more fundamental than algorithms, freer than power: cosmic resonance.

"The order of the cosmos is not between points and points, but between holes and holes; not in the visible, but in the occluded; not within grammar, but beyond grammar."
—This is the ultimate revelation of the triple deconstruction of star catalogs.

Philosophical Reflection and Future Outlook

  1. De-anthropocentric Cosmic Cognition: In the era of AI-generated catalogs, we must ask: Who/what "understands" the cosmos? Is it possible to develop a non-human-centered cosmic grammar (e.g., a "cosmic self-grammar" based on TDA)?
  2. Interdisciplinary Cosmology: Integrating TDA, ethnoastronomy, philosophy of technology, and data ethics to build a "cosmic cognitive ecology", making star catalogs a field of plural knowledge coexistence.
  3. Future Catalog Design: Developing multimodal homological fingerprint databases, supporting cross-band, cross-cultural, cross-scale cosmic structure comparison, enabling topology-driven cosmic exploration.
