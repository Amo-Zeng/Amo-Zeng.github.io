The Technics of Existence in Judaism: A Tripartite Structure of Time, Body, and Diaspora

Abstract

Through constructing the theoretical framework of "Technics of Existence," this study deciphers the survival mechanisms of Judaism throughout diasporic history. Focusing on the tripartite structure of chronopolitics, somatechnics, and diaspora aesthetics, it reveals the philosophical mechanisms by which Judaism constructs collective memory through calendrical apparatuses, resists homogenization through embodied practices, and reconstructs subjectivity through the poetics of incompleteness.

Introduction: The Techno-existential Paradigm Shift

Under the theoretical frameworks of Agamben's "apparatus" (dispositif) and Stiegler's "technological system," Judaism reveals a unique system of survival technics: "It is neither a pure belief system nor mere cultural customs, but rather a sophisticated techno-existential apparatus in continuous operation" (Homo Sacer III, 45).
