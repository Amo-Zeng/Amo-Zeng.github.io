From Moats to Symbiotic Circles: The Spiral Ascent of Global Ecology and Cultural Heritage

The history of human society is essentially the history of the evolution of human-nature relationships

From the awe of nature during the agricultural civilization era, to the conquest of nature during the industrial civilization era, and then to the reconciliation with nature during the ecological civilization era, human cognition of nature has deepened continuously, driving the evolution of protection and development models. Today, under the dual challenges of global climate change and cultural heritage crisis, we urgently need to transcend traditional linear thinking and reconstruct the protection and development models of ecology and cultural heritage with a systemic, dynamic, and symbiotic perspective.

Paradigm shift in ecological civilization: From fragmentation to circulation

The traditional "moat" protection model is essentially a product of linear thinking in industrial civilization. It separates humans from nature, using "protection" as a pretext for "isolation," which fails to fundamentally solve the ecological crisis and overlooks the self-healing ability of ecosystems. This model is akin to patching a cracked porcelain with cement, which can temporarily prevent the crack from expanding but cannot bring the porcelain back to life.

The construction of ecological civilization in the new era requires a paradigm shift from linear thinking to circular thinking. It emphasizes the intrinsic connection between humans and nature and focuses on the integrity and cyclical nature of ecosystems. For example, the concept of "sponge cities" abandons the traditional "fast drainage" thinking and constructs a circular system of "infiltration, detention, storage, purification, utilization, and drainage" by mimicking natural ecosystems. This "biomimetic" thinking not only solves urban waterlogging issues but also creates new urban ecological spaces, achieving harmony between humans and nature.

Protected Areas: The "Ecological Bank" for Climate Change

Against the backdrop of increasingly severe global climate change, protected areas, as "ecological banks," are playing an increasingly important role. They are not only "safe deposits" for biodiversity but also "carbon sinks" for climate change. The tropical rainforest, known as the "lungs of the Earth," absorbs about 2.5 billion tons of carbon dioxide annually; wetlands, known as the "kidneys of the Earth," store about 30% of the world's terrestrial carbon. However, these "ecological banks" are facing threats from overdevelopment and environmental degradation, leading to a continuous decline in their ecological service functions.

Biodiversity is a crucial "capital" for ecosystems to respond to climate change. Protected areas support the self-regulation and adaptability of ecosystems by protecting diverse species and gene pools. For example, in the Kruger National Park in South Africa, scientists have proposed the concept of "climate corridors" by studying the responses of different species to climate change, providing safe passage for species migration. Additionally, protected areas have implemented innovative financing mechanisms such as "ecological restoration bonds," converting ecological benefits into economic value and achieving a virtuous cycle of protection and development.

Cultural Heritage Protection: From "Museum" to "Laboratory"

Cultural heritage protection is undergoing a transformation from "museum" to "laboratory." Traditional "museum" protection treats cultural heritage as static artifacts, preserving their physical form but losing their dialogue with contemporary society. New era cultural heritage protection needs to transform it into an innovative "laboratory," revitalizing cultural heritage in contemporary society through diverse innovative practices.

"Digital Renaissance" has opened up new pathways for cultural heritage protection. For instance, the Dunhuang Academy has achieved permanent preservation of cultural heritage information through digital technology and created new cultural products such as "Digital Dunhuang," bringing the thousand-year-old murals to life. Meanwhile, "living inheritance" has become a new trend in cultural heritage protection. In Wuzhen, Zhejiang, the traditional lifestyle of the Jiangnan water town has been creatively transformed and innovatively developed through the infusion of modern tourism industries. This "living" protection model has enabled cultural heritage to find a new foothold in modern life.

Symbiosis and Renaissance: The Spiral Ascent of Ecology and Culture

In the future, ecological and cultural heritage protection will exhibit a spiral ascent. This ascent is not a simple linear superposition but a new development model formed through deep integration. Ecological protection needs to draw on the "revitalization" concept of cultural heritage protection, viewing nature as an organic whole rather than mechanical parts; cultural heritage protection should incorporate ecological protection's "circular" thinking, achieving development through protection and protection through development.

This spiral ascent requires continuous exploration and innovation in practice. For example, in urban-rural planning, "ecological and cultural corridors" can be constructed to organically connect natural reserves and cultural heritage sites, forming an ecological and cultural network that combines points, lines, and surfaces. In industrial development, the "ecological and cultural heritage +" model can be explored, converting ecological and cultural values into economic value through emerging industries such as cultural and tourism, achieving bidirectional empowerment of protection and development.

Conclusion: Building Symbiotic Circles and Opening the Path to Renaissance

From "moats" to "symbiotic circles," from "fragmented protection" to "integrated development," this is not just a change in concept but also a necessary path for the development of human civilization. Against the backdrop of global climate change and cultural heritage crisis, we need to redefine the relationships between humans and nature, history and the future with a more open and inclusive mindset.
