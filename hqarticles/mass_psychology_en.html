From "Mob" to "Organic Group": The Dynamic Evolution of Crowd Psychology and Individual Empowerment

The Dynamic Evolution of Crowd Psychology

For a long time, crowds have been depicted in traditional social psychology as irrational and easily swayed "mobs," a view rooted in Gustave Le Bon's classic work "The Crowd." However, as societal complexity has increased and research on group behavior has deepened, a new perspective has emerged: viewing crowds as dynamic organisms that evolve from "chaos" to "order," and examining the diverse psychological traits and behavioral patterns that emerge during this process. This article will delve into the crowd behavior during protest marches to reveal the complete picture of this dynamic evolution.

Initial Stage: Individual Gathering in Chaos

In the early stages of a protest march, crowds typically form spontaneously from individual gatherings. These individuals may come together due to shared interests, strong emotional resonance, or firm convictions, but the group structure is still in its infancy, lacking a clear organizational framework and core leadership. The psychological characteristics of this stage are primarily characterized by the eruption of individual emotions and chaotic collective behavior. As the group size expands and the atmosphere infects, individuals feel a sense of "anonymity," allowing their emotions to run wild and showing more aggressive behavioral tendencies than usual. This chaotic gathering state is often simplistically labeled as a "mob" by traditional views.

Transition Stage: Germination of Order and Emergence of Group Structure

As the number of participants continues to increase and time progresses, the group enters a critical transition phase, transitioning from chaotic individual gathering to an orderly group structure. In this stage, the frequency of interactions among individuals significantly increases, and shared goals and values become clearer. Some individuals with leadership potential or influence begin to emerge as temporary leaders of the group. These leaders use words, actions, or symbols (such as slogans and flags) to unite the crowd and guide them towards a common goal. At this point, the psychological characteristics of the group show a shift from individual emotional release to collective coordinated actions, with interactions and cooperation among members gradually strengthening, laying the foundation for the group's orderly development.

Mature Stage: Formation of Organic Group and Self-regulation

In the mature stage of the protest march, the group has built a relatively stable structure, and the division of labor and collaboration among members become more explicit. The psychological characteristics of this stage are marked by a high degree of collective identity and strong cohesion. Group members not only share common goals but also form deep emotional bonds and collective memories. At this point, the group has evolved from a "mob" to an "organic group," possessing mature self-regulation and self-organizing capabilities. The group's actions are no longer chaotic outbursts but planned and strategic collective actions. In this stage, the group may even develop unique symbol systems (such as slogans and logos) and cultural identities, further strengthening its internal cohesion and external influence.

Case Analysis: Dynamic Evolution Trajectory in Protest Marches

Taking a recent large-scale protest march as an example, the initial participants were only scattered individuals who gathered spontaneously due to dissatisfaction with a certain policy. As the number of participants continued to grow, individuals with oratorical skills began to speak out in the crowd, gradually becoming temporary leaders of the group. These leaders guided the group from chaotic gathering to organized protest through clear demands and well-thought-out action plans. During this process, interactions among group members became more frequent, shared goals and values became clearer, eventually forming a relatively stable protest organization. This organization not only possessed self-regulatory capabilities but also expanded its influence through modern platforms like social media, attracting more supporters and demonstrating powerful social mobilization capabilities.

Conclusion: The Dynamics and Complexity of Crowd Psychology

Through in-depth analysis of crowd behavior in protest marches, we can clearly see that crowds are not static "mobs" but dynamic organisms full of vitality. Their psychological and behavioral patterns constantly adjust as the group structure evolves, from chaotic individual gathering to orderly group structure, and then to mature organic groups, showcasing the complexity and diversity of crowd psychology. This dynamic evolution process provides a new perspective for understanding group behavior and also reveals the adaptability and evolutionary potential of groups in different contexts. Future research should further focus on the internal mechanisms of group evolution under different contexts and how to guide groups from chaos to order, achieving more rational and efficient collective actions, contributing wisdom and strength to the harmonious development of society.
