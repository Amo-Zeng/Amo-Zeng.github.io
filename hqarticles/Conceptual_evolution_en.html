From Chaos to Symbiosis: The Three Transformations of Human Cognition

In the evolution of human civilization, the transformation of cognition unfolds like a grand painting: from the chaotic cognition of the physical world, to the reshaping of thought in the digital era, and finally to the profound reflection on the ethical relationship between humans and nature. These transformations, layer by layer, reveal how humanity responds to the challenges of a complex world by understanding order, reconstructing spirituality, and reshaping ethics. These three dimensions not only represent the three major transformations of human cognition but also showcase our profound insight and redefinition of the essence of the world.

I. Entropy and the Reconstruction of Order: From the Physical World to Social Evolution

Entropy, initially a core concept in thermodynamics, describes the relationship between energy dissipation and system disorder. The Second Law of Thermodynamics tells us that in an isolated system, entropy always tends to increase, leading the system from order to disorder. However, this seemingly pessimistic law actually reveals the dynamic nature of order: order is not a static endpoint but a new state of balance achieved through entropy increase. This characteristic of physical entropy provides important clues for understanding the evolution of complex systems.

With the extension of the entropy concept, the emergence of information entropy introduced this theory into the field of information theory. Shannon's information entropy quantifies the uncertainty of information, while information negentropy describes the degree of information organization. This dynamic view of order breaks traditional static understanding and reveals how information systems achieve higher levels of order through structuring during entropy increase.

When the concept of entropy is introduced into sociology, the concept of social entropy emerges. An increase in social entropy means a strengthening of social complexity, but this complexity does not necessarily lead to disorder. Modern society's networked and flattened structures, which appear to be manifestations of disorder, are actually indicative of enhanced social adaptability. The social entropy theory provides a new perspective for understanding the reconstruction of order: in a complex social system, the reconstruction of order is a dynamic and continuously optimizing process.

II. Spiritual Transformation in the Digital Mirror: From Ritual to Multidimensional Perception

The digital era has profoundly reshaped human reading and thinking patterns. In the print era, reading was a culturally significant behavior with strong ritualistic elements. The sound of flipping pages, the feel of paper, and the scent of inkâ€”these physical attributes constructed a complete reading field. However, with the proliferation of electronic devices, this sense of ritual has been reduced to a dispensable ornament. The portability, immediacy, and interactivity offered by electronic devices break the spatial limitations of reading, turning it into a tool for filling idle time.

Digital reading's multimedia presentation breaks the traditional linear reading model. The fusion of images, videos, audio, and other media forms provides a richer sensory experience but also challenges traditional logical thinking modes. Readers need to rapidly switch attention between jumping information nodes, cultivating a divergent thinking rather than a deep thinking habit.

The rise of AR/VR technologies and generative AI redefines the boundaries of reading. Immersive reading experiences provide unprecedented sensory stimulation but may also lead to the degradation of perception of the real world. Generative AI makes personalized reading experiences possible but also brings issues of content production dependent on algorithms. Digital reading is undergoing a transformation from instrumentality to existential significance, requiring us to reconsider: in this era of information explosion, what constitutes true reading? How can we rebuild a complete cognitive system amid fragmented reading?

III. From "Master" to "Citizen": The Ethical Reconstruction Between Humans and Nature

The relationship between humans and nature has undergone a profound transformation from conquest to symbiosis. Western civilization, founded on Descartes' "dualism" philosophy, placed humans and nature in opposition, leading to the exploitative development of nature and triggering environmental crises such as climate change and species extinction. In contrast, the "unity of heaven and human" thought in Eastern civilization emphasizes harmony and symbiosis between humans and nature, offering an alternative inspiration.

The rise of ecocentrism marks a significant shift in human cognition of nature. It advocates the intrinsic value of ecosystems and emphasizes that humans need to fundamentally change their cognitive approach to nature, establishing new ecological ethics. The environmental justice movement further pushes ecocentrism into the practical realm, focusing on social inequality in environmental issues, making environmental protection an integral part of social justice.

The shift from "master" to "citizen" in humans' role marks the core essence of ecological awakening. We are no longer rulers above nature but equal members of the ecosystem. This identity shift requires us to redefine our relationship with nature and establish new responsibilities. Cultivating ecological citizenship requires interventions from multiple levels, including education, institutions, and culture, to build a comprehensive ecological citizenship cultivation system.

Building an inclusive ecological community requires cross-cultural dialogue and cooperation. The ecological wisdom embedded in different cultural traditions is a shared human treasure, requiring integration and innovation on a global scale.

Conclusion: From Chaos to Symbiosis

From the reconstruction of order in physical entropy to the revolution in thinking in the digital era, and then to the ethical reshaping between humans and nature, human cognition has undergone a profound transformation. This transformation is not merely an expansion of concepts but a re-understanding of the essence of the world. In this process, we gradually realize that the reconstruction of order is a dynamic and continuously optimizing process, the reshaping of spirit requires a balance between sensory stimulation and deep thinking, and the ethical relationship between humans and nature needs to shift from conquest to symbiosis. Future challenges lie in how to find new order amidst chaos, rebuild a complete cognitive system in the fragmented information flow, and achieve harmonious symbiosis between humans and nature in a global context. These questions have no standard answers, but it is the exploration and resolution of these questions that shape the future of human civilization.
