A Non-Human Contract: Posthuman Gardens as Participatory Networks of Ecological Governance

Introduction: From Landscape to Agent — The Paradigm Shift of Gardens

In traditional garden history, gardens have always been projections of human will: symbols of royal power (e.g., Versailles), spiritual retreats for literati (e.g., the Humble Administrator’s Garden), or urban leisure spaces (e.g., city parks). At their core, they are anthropocentric landscape apparatuses—nature disciplined, time compressed, bodies visualized, society symbolized. However, under the triple crisis of the Anthropocene—climate acceleration, cognitive colonization, and geopolitical conflict—the garden is undergoing a fundamental paradigm shift: it is no longer a passive "object of viewing," but an active "ecological agent."

At the heart of this shift is the transformation of gardens from "human monologue" to "multispecies dialogue." This article proposes the philosophical vision of the posthuman garden, defined as: a dynamic, responsive network jointly constituted by human and non-human actors (trees, microbes, water, wind, soil) that actively participates in ecological governance. This vision not only redefines the function of gardens but also challenges the deeply entrenched human exceptionalism of modernity, opening a new de-anthropocentric ethics of governance.

Analytical Framework: Three Dimensions of Crisis and Three Forms of Response

To systematically articulate the transformative logic of posthuman gardens, this article proposes a tripartite analytical framework—“ecological—bodily—social”—corresponding to the deep structure of contemporary crises and their garden-based responses:

Crisis Dimension Nature of Crisis Garden Response Mechanism Core Metaphor
Ecological Dimension Time acceleration & ecological rupture Ecological Time Capsule Anchoring slow time, redefining intergenerational contracts
Bodily Dimension Perception colonized by technology Cognitive Antidote Rebuilding embodied contemplation
Social Dimension Geopolitical conflict & cultural fragmentation Soil Diplomacy Activating underground biopolitics

These dimensions are not parallel but dynamically coupled through the agency of non-human actors:

  • Tree rings are not only climatic archives but material carriers of "time capsules," with their carbon sequestration directly participating in ecological governance;
  • Mycorrhizal networks not only regulate soil perception but serve as physiological foundations for "cognitive detox"—microbial metabolites (e.g., tryptophan-serotonin pathways) influence human neural activity;
  • Microbial cross-border migration transcends national boundaries and mediates social trust through "non-linguistic communication," enabling "diplomacy without a foreign ministry."

This framework is grounded in Latour’s Actor-Network Theory (ANT): nature and society, human and non-human, are symmetrical actors co-constructing networks. It also responds to Habermas’s theory of the colonization of the lifeworld—as technological systems datafy perception, memory, and relationships, gardens become reterritorialized spaces of the lifeworld, resisting systemic abstraction through embodied practice.

I. Ecological Dimension: Time Capsules and Ecological Entanglement

Traditional gardens rely on "instant aesthetics"—cherry blossoms lasting seven days, peonies blooming for ten—where beauty lies in transience, yet obscures the deep temporality of ecosystems. Contemporary gardens transform into ecological time capsules, incorporating non-human temporal scales: tree rings, soil deposition, groundwater cycles. For example, the Dutch "Garden of Time" centers on century-old oaks, whose root systems form cross-century carbon sinks, creating "living carbon vaults"; Shenzhen’s "Climate Memory Garden" uses tree-ring slices and glacial sedimentary layers to build "climate memory strata," allowing visitors to perceive century-long temperature fluctuations through walking.

This shift is rooted in Timothy Morton’s theory of ecological entanglement: nature is not an "external landscape" but a non-human actor sharing time, matter, and fate with humans. Gardens thus become "temporal infrastructures," anchoring "slow rhythms" against Stiegler’s "temporal poverty caused by technological acceleration." Crucially, they reconstruct the intergenerational contract between humans and nature through material archives—each ring a promise to the future.

II. Bodily Dimension: Perceptual Correction and Embodied Resistance

In algorithmic societies, human perception is what Habermas calls the colonization of the lifeworld—technological systems datafy, visualize, and fragment perception, replacing lived experience with the "attention economy." Gardens become cognitive antidotes, rebuilding contemplative capacity through embodied spatial design.

Japan’s "Garden of Wind" activates auditory and tactile senses through wind chimes, airflow paths, and textured paving, breaking the visual monopoly; Berlin’s "Sensory Maze" uses scent trails (pine resin, soil, humus) and temperature gradients to restore multisensory memory, awakening the algorithmically suppressed "environmental body." This practice resonates with Merleau-Ponty’s phenomenology of the body: perception is the co-constitution of body and world, not passive reception. Gardens do not offer "information" but perceptual interstices, allowing the body to rebuild a "non-instrumental relationship" with the environment during slow movement—a pre-reflective co-presence.

III. Social Dimension: Underground Diplomacy and Microbial Mediation

While traditional gardens serve as "cultural symbols," contemporary gardens become media for soil diplomacy. Anna Tsing’s theory of "multispecies survival" reveals how fungal hyphae, rhizobia, and other microbes build "ecological communication networks" beneath the soil, crossing borders and species. Examples include the China-Mongolia "Grassland Mycelium Corridor," which activates mycorrhizal networks via leguminous plants to enhance carbon sequestration; and the Israel-Palestine "Shared Olive Grove," where joint care of olive trees builds "ecological trust mechanisms" in conflict zones, enabling "non-verbal reconciliation."

Gardens thus shift from "cultural signifiers" to underground diplomatic platforms, establishing cross-border empathy through microbial "non-human mediation." This "microbial diplomacy" transcends anthropocentric governance, forming a "post-geopolitical governance model"—governance not through state treaties, but via chemical signals in soil (e.g., volatile organic compounds) and rhizosphere dialogue.

Practical Pathways: Three Design Principles

To translate theory into actionable practice, this article proposes three design principles ensuring the replicability, equity, and decentralization of posthuman gardens:

  1. Multispecies Participatory Design
    Invite botanists, microbiologists, indigenous communities, and citizens to co-design, using tools like "non-human agency assessment" (e.g., root growth simulation, microbial community modeling) to ensure plants and microbes retain "design rights" not unilaterally determined by humans.
  2. Sensory Democratization
    Use low-cost materials (tactile paving, scent installations, wind sculptures) to make "cognitive detox" spaces accessible, avoiding exclusivity to the middle class. Example: "Sound Gardens" made from recycled plastic, enabling low-income communities to experience multisensory healing.
  3. Ecological Data Commons
    Employ blockchain + DNA barcoding to create open databases tracking soil microbial diversity and tree-ring carbon sequestration. Use smart contracts to prevent corporate monopolization, ensuring data functions as an "ecological commons" for public research and governance.
Critical Reflection on Technological Mediation: While blockchain enhances transparency, it risks reducing ecological relations to "verifiable nodes"; DNA barcoding increases microbial visibility but may slide into "genetic colonization"—reducing life to sequences. Technology must serve decentralized visibility, not re-centralized control.

Conclusion: The Non-Human Contract and the Ethics of Governance

The vision of the "posthuman garden" ultimately points to a non-human contract: Are we willing to recognize that trees, microbes, and soil are not merely "resources," but "co-inhabitants"? This contract demands a theoretical clarification of "non-human actors":

  • Non-human actors are not anthropomorphized "conscious subjects." Their agency manifests through material practices:
    • Mycorrhizal networks exchange nutrients and warnings via chemical signals;
    • Trees regulate local climate through transpiration;
    • Soil stores water and carbon via pore structures.
    These actions require no "intention" yet effectively participate in governance, forming "distributed agency."
  • Their agency arises from relational existence within networks, as Latour notes: "An actor only becomes an actor within a network."

We must also remain alert to the duality of technological mediation:

  • Positive: DNA barcoding makes microbes "traceable"; biofeedback systems allow plants to "express" health;
  • Risk: Technology may enable the datafied colonization of non-humans, extracting them from ecological wholes and reducing them to governance tools.

True governance must use technology to serve decentralization, not re-centralization. Gardens should ultimately become experimental sites for non-human empowerment. Here, wind speaks, soil remembers, microbes engage in diplomacy—and humans are merely one among many actors. Only then can gardens evolve from "vessels of beauty" into "sites of ecological contract-writing"—at the intersection of time, body, and society—redefining what it means to "co-exist," and what "the future" might be.
