I. The Silence of Mountains: The Misunderstood "Inorganic Life"

This suggests: mountains "learn," "remember," and may even possess a form of "cognitive latency."

The geological disasters we face today may be delayed echoes of ancient events.

This mechanism strikingly parallels psychology's intergenerational trauma. Geointelligence's "memory" and psychological shadows' "trauma" share the same dynamic model in time delay, cross-generational transmission, and nonlinear eruption.

II. Ecological Debt: The Underestimated "Geological-Psychological" Feedback Loop

Traditional ecological economics defines "ecological debt" as human overexploitation of natural systems, but this concept neglects the reverse impact of ecological debt on geological systems. Counterintuitive Insight #2: Humans are "domesticating" faults—only to be "reverse-domesticated" by them.

Geological systems have "resilience thresholds"; once crossed, responses shift from linear to chaotic.

More profoundly, ecological debt self-reinforces through a "psychological-geological" feedback loop. As resource depletion induces human anxiety, it intensifies exploitation; exploitation accelerates geological instability, triggering more disasters, deepening psychological shadows. A 2024 UNEP report revealed that 68% of people with "eco-anxiety" live in high geological-risk zones, and their anxiety levels correlate positively with local earthquake frequency (UNEP, 2024). Perceived geological risk → psychological stress → exploitative behavior → geological instability → heightened risk—a closed loop.

This mechanism is further amplified in the "digital surveillance" era.
