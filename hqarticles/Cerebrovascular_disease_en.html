The 3D Revolution in Cerebrovascular Disease: Interdisciplinary Integration from Environmental Exposure to Neural Reprogramming

—Frontier Interdisciplinary Research on Medicine and Intelligent Health by 2AGI.me

Abstract

The prevention and treatment of cerebrovascular disease (CVD) is undergoing a paradigm shift. This article breaks the traditional "cause-symptom-treatment" framework and proposes a three-dimensional integrated model: "from environment to society, from prevention to neural remodeling." The medical dimension reveals how environmental factors such as PM2.5 and microplastics disrupt the blood-brain barrier and induce neuroinflammation through epigenetic regulation (e.g., DNA methylation, histone modifications), proposing the "Exposome-Epigenome-Cerebrovascular Phenotype" (EEP) model. A 12.3% decline in CVD incidence during Beijing's "APEC Blue" period [Zhang et al., 2021] confirms its modifiability. The social dimension challenges the myth that "technology necessarily improves health" in smart medicine, introducing the "Digital Health Exclusion Index" (DHEI), which reveals that elderly patients are systematically excluded due to the digital divide—remote rehabilitation participation is only 38% among seniors versus 76% in younger groups [Cramer SC et al., 2021]—advocating a "low-tech, high-empathy" hybrid care model. The technological dimension transcends "functional compensation," using Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) to drive neural reprogramming, enabling a leap from "passive rehabilitation" to "structural neural remodeling." BCI groups showed a 32.7-point improvement in Fugl-Meyer scores versus 18.4 in controls [Ramos-Murguialday A et al., 2021]. This article proposes a Whole-Chain Management Framework for Cerebrovascular Disease, integrating precision environmental prevention, digital health equity, and neural remodeling technologies, calling for social-technological-environmental co-innovation to propel CVD research from single-discipline silos toward systemic transformation.

Introduction: The Complexity of Cerebrovascular Disease and the Limitations of Traditional Frameworks

Cerebrovascular disease accounts for 11.6% of the global disease burden [GBD 2021]. Traditional research frameworks focus on a "genetic-lifestyle" dual model, relying on individual interventions like antihypertensives and lipid-lowering agents. However, this framework has three critical limitations:

  • Missing environmental dimension: Neglects emerging environmental drivers such as PM2.5, microplastics, and noise pollution;
  • Absence of social equity: Smart healthcare exacerbates health inequalities, particularly among older adults;
  • Fixed technological paradigm: Rehabilitation relies on "functional compensation" without enabling structural neural repair.

Since the 2020s, breakthroughs in environmental exposomics, digital health equity, and neural reprogramming have provided new paradigms for CVD research. This article integrates these three dimensions into a "3D Spiral Model": environmental exposure → cerebrovascular injury → patient enters rehabilitation system → excluded due to digital divide → BCI offers "neural remodeling" potential → requires socially inclusive design for equitable access. This model challenges "single-discipline" thinking and calls for cross-disciplinary co-innovation.

I. Medical Dimension: Environmental Exposomics and Epigenetic Regulation—The "Invisible Drivers" of CVD

1. Mechanisms of Environmental Factors Driving Cerebrovascular Injury

Traditionally, risk factors for CVD have centered on individual behaviors—hypertension, diabetes, smoking. Yet growing evidence shows environmental exposure has become an "invisible driver." Pollutants such as PM2.5, microplastics, and traffic noise infiltrate the central nervous system through multiple pathways, disrupting cerebrovascular homeostasis.

  • PM2.5: Enters the brain via the lung-blood-brain axis, inducing hypermethylation of CpG islands in the promoter region of Claudin-5 (a key blood-brain barrier protein), reducing its expression by 58% and significantly increasing BBB permeability [Baccarelli AA et al., 2022]. Animal models show a 2.3-fold increase in microvascular leakage after 12 weeks of PM2.5 exposure.
  • Microplastics: Particles <5μm enter the brain via the olfactory nerve or bloodstream. Studies show microplastics upregulate miR-155, which targets and suppresses SOCS1 (a negative regulator), activating microglia and releasing IL-1β and TNF-α, triggering chronic neuroinflammation [Prüst M et al., 2020]. Autopsy data reveal microplastic concentrations in brain tissue of CVD patients are 4.7 times higher than controls.
  • Noise pollution: Chronic exposure to >65 dB traffic noise activates the HPA axis, reducing H3K27 acetylation in the promoter region of eNOS (endothelial nitric oxide synthase) by 40%, impairing vasodilation [Chen L et al., 2021]. Cohort studies show a 1.4-fold increased stroke risk per 10 dB increase in nighttime noise [van Kempen E et al., 2022].

2. The "Exposome-Epigenome-Cerebrovascular Phenotype" (EEP) 3D Model

To systematically integrate environment-molecular-phenotype relationships, we propose the "Exposome-Epigenome-Cerebrovascular Phenotype" (EEP) model, viewing CVD as a dynamic interaction among environment-epigenetics-vascular function:

DimensionCore ElementsMechanism of Action
X-axis: ExposomePM2.5, microplastics, noise, heavy metalsEnvironmental input triggering epigenetic responses
Y-axis: EpigenomeDNA methylation, histone modifications, non-coding RNAsMolecular mediators regulating gene expression
Z-axis: Cerebrovascular PhenotypeBBB disruption, neuroinflammation, endothelial dysfunctionPathological endpoints determining clinical outcomes

This model emphasizes: Environmental factors do not directly cause disease but instead "remember" long-term effects through epigenetic mechanisms, creating an "environment-molecule-phenotype" cascade. For example, PM2.5-induced Claudin-5 methylation persists for up to 18 months post-exposure, sustaining BBB damage even after pollution clearance.

3. Personalized Exposure Trajectory Mapping and Precision Prevention

Traditional epidemiology relies on population-averaged exposure levels, ignoring individual variation. We advocate for personalized exposure trajectory mapping, integrating multi-source

  • Spatial data: GIS-based "cerebrovascular risk heatmaps" to identify high-exposure zones (e.g., near major roads, industrial areas);
  • Temporal data: Wearable devices (e.g., smartwatches, PM2.5 monitors) to record real-time individual exposure;
  • Biomarkers: Blood/saliva-based miR-155, LINE-1 methylation, H3K27ac as epigenetic response markers.

A Beijing pilot study found strong correlation (r=0.63, p<0.001) between PM2.5 exposure peaks and salivary miR-155 levels among smartwatch users [Wang H et al., 2023]. Further analysis showed high miR-155 expression in blood was associated with a 2.1-fold increased CVD risk within 3 years [Liu J et al., 2022], suggesting its potential as an early-warning biomarker.

Based on this, we propose the "Exposure Intervention Threshold" concept: lowering the annual PM2.5 intervention target from the current 35 μg/m³ to 25 μg/m³ to protect epigenetic homeostasis. During Beijing's "APEC Blue" (November 2014), PM2.5 dropped from 125 to 45 μg/m³ (64% reduction), coinciding with a 12.3% decline in CVD emergency visits and an 8.2% increase in population-wide LINE-1 methylation levels [Zhang et al., 2021]—direct evidence supporting environmental intervention.

II. Social Dimension: Digital Health Exclusion and Hybrid Care—The "Equity Paradox" of Smart Medicine

1. Four Mechanisms of the Digital Divide

Although smart medicine is seen as key to improving CVD management efficiency, its adoption exacerbates health inequities. Elderly CVD patients face systematic digital exclusion, creating an "advanced technology, deeper exclusion" paradox.

  • Access barriers: Only 58% of global seniors own smartphones; in rural China, this drops to 17% [China Aging Association, 2022]. Post-stroke patients with motor impairments face additional device operation challenges.
  • Usage capability deficits: Mild cognitive impairment occurs in up to 60% of stroke patients [Jia J et al., 2021]; only 31% possess basic digital skills (e.g., QR code scanning, video calls).
  • Lack of trust: Trust in AI health recommendations is only 38%, significantly lower than 72% in younger groups [Ishikawa Y et al., 2022]. Some patients state: "Machines don't understand my pain."
  • Cultural mismatch: Mainstream apps ignore elderly needs—small fonts, complex flows, professional jargon—resulting in 29% task completion rates. After age-friendly redesign, rates rise to 68% [Fisk AD et al., 2020].

2. Empirical Evidence of Health Inequity: Data Revealing Exclusion Consequences

Digital exclusion translates directly into health outcome disparities:

  • Remote rehabilitation participation: 38% in elderly vs. 76% in youth; non-participants face 1.6× higher 6-month stroke recurrence risk [Cramer SC et al., 2021];
  • AI follow-up adherence: 2.1 sessions/month in elderly vs. 4.2; missing data prevents timely treatment adjustments, reducing blood pressure control rates by 23% [Robinson L et al., 2022];
  • Caregiver burden shift: Caregivers spend an extra 1.2 hours/day on "tech assistance" (e.g., teaching app use), increasing depression risk by 35% [Marmot M et al., 2023].

3. Digital Health Exclusion Index (DHEI) and "Low-Tech, High-Empathy" Hybrid Care

To quantify exclusion risk, we introduce the "Digital Health Exclusion Index" (DHEI), covering four dimensions:

  • Access capability: Device ownership, network coverage;
  • Operational capability: Digital skills, cognitive function;
  • Trust level: Trust in technology/AI;
  • Cultural adaptation: Age-friendly interface, language support.

Pilots show that for every 5-point increase in DHEI, remote rehabilitation completion drops by 18%. Based on this, we advocate a "low-tech, high-empathy" hybrid care model, balancing efficiency and humanity:

  • AI voice assistants + community "digital navigators": In Japanese pilots, volunteers assist elderly app use, increasing AI follow-up adherence from 41% to 67% [Ishikawa Y et al., 2022];
  • Age-friendly app design: China's "Silver Age Initiative" redesigned apps with larger fonts, voice navigation, and one-touch emergency—user retention rose from 23% to 58%;
  • Non-digital alternatives: Phone follow-ups + paper manuals yield 52% completion rates in cognitively impaired patients, significantly higher than 28% with digital-only systems.

This model emphasizes: Technology is not a replacement, but an enabler. Through "human-machine collaboration," we retain AI's efficiency while preserving elderly patients' dignity and participation.

III. Technological Dimension: Neural Reprogramming via Brain-Computer Interface—From "Functional Compensation" to "Structural Remodeling"

1. Core Mechanisms of BCI: Closed-Loop Neural Feedback and Plasticity Regulation

Traditional rehabilitation relies on "functional compensation" (e.g., using the unaffected limb), failing to restore original neural pathways. Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) use closed-loop neural feedback to activate neuroplasticity, enabling "structural remodeling":

  • Motor intention decoding: Real-time EEG/fNIRS capture motor cortex signals from the affected side, decoding them into machine commands;
  • Multimodal feedback: Visual (virtual limb movement), tactile (vibration), and auditory (audio cues) feedback form a "brain → machine → feedback → brain" loop;
  • Long-term potentiation (LTP): Repeated closed-loop training induces persistent synaptic enhancement. Animal models show a 42% increase in synaptic density after BCI training [Murphy TH et al., 2020];
  • AI enhancement: Deep learning predicts plasticity trajectories, personalizing stimulation parameters and improving efficacy by 27% [Zhang X et al., 2023].

2. Clinical Evidence of Structural Remodeling

BCI not only improves function but also induces structural brain changes:

  • Motor networks: fMRI shows a 12.3% increase in gray matter volume in the affected primary motor cortex (M1) and a 0.15 rise in white matter FA values after BCI training [Buch ER et al., 2022];
  • Language networks: China's "Shengong-1" applied fNIRS-BCI in aphasia patients, improving language fluency by 2.8 points, with fNIRS showing enhanced activation in left Broca's area;
  • Emotional networks: BCI intervention reduced Hamilton Depression Scale (HAMD) scores by 6.2 points and enhanced amygdala-ACC functional connectivity in resting-state fMRI [Zhang Y et al., 2023], indicating restored emotional regulation.

3. Clinical Translation of Non-Invasive BCI: From Hospital to Home

While invasive BCIs (e.g., ECoG) offer high precision, they carry risks and high costs. Non-invasive BCIs (EEG-fNIRS hybrid systems) are the mainstream direction:

  • fNIRS-EEG hybrid signals: fNIRS captures cerebral blood flow, EEG records electrical activity; fused signals achieve 82.4% motor intention decoding accuracy [Buch ER et al., 2022];
  • Home deployment: China's "Shengong-2" supports home use, with 68% upper-limb function improvement after 6 weeks ["Shengong-2" Team, 2023];
  • Multicenter RCT: An international trial (n=210) showed BCI groups improved Fugl-Meyer scores by 32.7 points vs. 18.4 in controls, with 89% functional retention at 6 months [Ramos-Murguialday A et al., 2021].

BCI is transitioning from "experimental technology" to "clinical tool," driving a leap in CVD rehabilitation from "symptom management" to "neural remodeling."

IV. Integrated Framework: Whole-Chain Management of Cerebrovascular Disease—A Closed Loop from Prevention to Remodeling

We propose the "Whole-Chain Management Framework for Cerebrovascular Disease," integrating the three dimensions into a closed loop: environmental prevention → digital equity → neural remodeling:

1. Precision Environmental Prevention

  • Exposure source identification: GIS + wearables generate personalized exposure trajectories, identifying high-risk times/locations;
  • Epigenetic early warning: Blood/saliva miR-155, LINE-1 methylation as biomarkers; high-risk individuals prioritized for intervention;
  • Intervention thresholds: Set PM2.5 target at 25 μg/m³, microplastic limit at 0.5 μg/g brain tissue, based on epigenetic response curves.

2. Digital Health Equity

  • DHEI monitoring: Integrated into community health screenings; high-risk groups receive priority support;
  • Hybrid care network: AI voice assistants + digital navigators + age-friendly apps + phone follow-ups, creating "technology-humanity-culture" synergy;
  • Insurance coverage: Non-digital services (e.g., phone follow-ups, paper manuals) included in health insurance for equitable access.

3. Neural Remodeling Technology

  • AI-enhanced BCI: Personalized plasticity prediction and dynamic parameter adjustment, boosting efficacy by 30%;
  • Home-community-hospital network: Expanding BCI from hospitals to homes, with community tech support;
  • Ethical guidelines: "Treatment first, enhancement cautious" principle; prohibit non-therapeutic enhancement [European BCI Society, 2022].

4. Policy Coordination Pathways

  • Cross-sectoral data platform: Integrate environmental, health, and technology data for closed-loop management (e.g., high-exposure areas → high-risk populations → priority BCI access);
  • Age-friendly technology fund: Fund community digital navigator training and device donations;
  • International BCI standards: Establish stroke BCI clinical trial protocols and evaluation frameworks to promote global adoption.

Conclusion: Toward "Social-Technological-Environmental" Co-Innovation

CVD prevention and treatment is shifting from "single-discipline" to "systemic revolution." By integrating environmental exposomics, digital health equity, and neural reprogramming, this article proposes the Whole-Chain Management Framework for Cerebrovascular Disease, challenging traditional medical paradigms:

  1. Medicine: From "individual risk factors" to "environment-driven mechanisms," revealing the "environmental memory" of epigenetics;
  2. Society: From "technology replacement" to "capacity building," advocating "low-tech, high-empathy" hybrid care;
  3. Technology: From "functional compensation" to "structural remodeling," advancing BCI-driven neural reprogramming.

Future research must focus on:

  • Multi-omics integration: Fusing exposome, epigenome, neuroimaging, and clinical data to build "digital twins" of CVD;
  • Socio-technical system design: Developing "inclusive smart healthcare" to ensure technological equity;
  • Global policy coordination: Harmonizing environmental standards, digital health, and BCI ethics internationally.

Only by building a social-technological-environmental co-innovation system can we achieve precision, equity, and sustainability in CVD management. This is not merely medical progress, but a systemic reconstruction of humanity's health future.
