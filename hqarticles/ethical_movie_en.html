From "Taboo" to "Reconciliation" — Reflections on Humanity and the Reconstruction of Cultural Collisions in Ethical Films

As a unique film genre, ethical films have become an indispensable part of contemporary cinema culture through their profound exploration of moral dilemmas and revelation of human complexity. However, the significance of ethical films goes beyond exposing the hidden corners of human nature; it is also a medium for cultural reflection. By deconstructing "taboos," these films seek to explore deep interactions between humanity and society. This article will start with the dialectical relationship between "taboo" and "humanity," combined with the identity dilemma of "the other" and "the self," as well as the cultural tension between "tradition" and "modernity," to discuss how ethical films present the complex interaction between humanity and society through artistic expression, and further reflect on how such films bring profound emotional resonance and cultural reflection through the growth and reconciliation of characters.

Step 1: Redefining the Boundaries of "Taboo" and "Humanity"

"Taboos" are not innate but rather societal constraints imposed on human nature. Topics in ethical films, such as extramarital affairs, homosexuality, and incest, often challenge the established moral framework of society. These topics are considered "taboo" because they touch upon society's binary division of "normal" and "abnormal." However, from a human perspective, these "taboo" behaviors are not symbols of moral decay but natural expressions of human instincts and emotional needs. Ethical films, by examining these "taboos," reveal a more essential aspect of human nature—the purity of emotions and the diversity of individual existence.

Case Study: For example, the homosexual love between two cowboys in "Brokeback Mountain" challenges traditional societal gender norms but actually reveals the power of transcending gender boundaries. Their love is built on profound understanding and resonance, rather than simple sexual orientation labels. Despite being seen as "the other" by society, the film's emotional depiction proves the universality and transcendence of love.

Step 2: Analyzing the Root of Moral Dilemmas and Identity Crises

The moral dilemmas in ethical films are essentially a博弈 between human nature and societal norms. These dilemmas arise not only from human instincts and emotional needs but also from societal cultural discipline and oppression. The crisis of identity is an inevitable product of cultural conflicts, which is not only an opposition between the individual and society but also a suppression of one culture by another.

  1. Conflict between Human Instincts and Social Norms: Freud's "id," "ego," and "superego" theory provides a psychological explanation for moral dilemmas in ethical films. However, modern ethical films go beyond personal inner conflicts and place human instinctual needs under the microscope of social norms. For instance, the extramarital affair in "Fatal Attraction" is not merely moral degradation but a博弈 between human desire for freedom and novelty and societal expectations of loyalty and responsibility.
  2. Cultural Conflict and Identity Disruption: The crisis of identity in ethical films often stems from cultural heterogeneity. The protagonist in "The Wedding Banquet" struggles between Chinese and Western cultures, with his homosexual identity clashing with traditional Chinese family values. This conflict reveals the suppression of individual identity by cultural hegemony. It is not only a confrontation between the individual and family but also a competition between cultural diversity and uniformity in the context of globalization.

Case Study: In "12 Years a Slave," the protagonist's identity crisis is not only a result of racial oppression but also the complete erasure of humanity under slavery culture. His self-redemption is not only a defense of personal dignity but also a rebellion against cultural violence. The film reveals the historical and social roots behind ethical dilemmas through this process.

Step 3: Exploring the Artistic Expression and Cultural Reflection of Ethical Films

Ethical films transform moral dilemmas, identity crises, and cultural conflicts into a visual and emotional experience through unique artistic techniques. They are not merely narrative carriers but also cultural mirrors, reflecting the complex relationship between society and individuals.

  1. Metaphorical Lens Language and Narrative Structure: Ethical films often challenge the audience's thinking through nonlinear narratives and symbolic lens language. For example, the blending of dreams and reality in "Mulholland Drive" not only reveals the protagonist's inner struggle but also symbolizes the individual's cultural identity loss in the context of globalization. This narrative structure breaks traditional linear logic, guiding the audience to re-examine the multiple dimensions of identity and culture.
  2. Interweaving Visual Symbols and Cultural Metaphors: Visual symbols in ethical films are not merely aesthetic expressions but also carriers of cultural metaphors. The use of colors in "The Three Colors Trilogy" showcases the dialogue and conflict between tradition and modernity. Red symbolizes the warmth and constraints of traditional society, while blue represents the freedom and coldness of modern society. The contrast not only reveals cultural tensions but also prompts the audience to think about how individuals can find their position between tradition and modernity.

Case Study: "Parasite" uses spatial symbolism to reveal the oppression of identity by class culture. The protagonist's family infiltrates the upper class through disguise but gradually loses themselves in the class gap. The film's deconstruction of space reveals the vulnerability and complexity of cultural identity in the modernization process.

Step 4: From "Taboo" to "Reconciliation" — The Healing Power and Cultural Insights of Ethical Films

Although ethical films focus on moral dilemmas and identity crises, they do not merely reveal problems but also demonstrate a cultural healing power through the growth and reconciliation of characters. This healing is not only individual self-redemption but also a dialogue and integration between cultures.

  1. Self-Reconciliation and Cultural Inclusiveness: The protagonists in ethical films often achieve a transcendent self-reconciliation after struggling with identity. For example, "Moonlight" traces the protagonist's journey from childhood to adulthood, showing the struggle of homosexual identity under cultural oppression and eventual self-acceptance. This reconciliation is not only a personal liberation but also a manifestation of cultural inclusiveness.
  2. Emotional Resonance and Deep Cultural Reflection: Ethical films evoke emotional resonance and cultural reflection through profound emotional expression. "Blue Valentine" paints a nuanced picture of love and marriage dilemmas, revealing the complexity of emotions and the limitations of social norms. While reflecting on their own marital relationships, the audience also feels the profound influence of culture on personal emotions.

Case Study: "12 Years a Slave" not only showcases the protagonist's self-redemption but also reveals the roots and historical continuity of cultural oppression. By recounting history, the film inspires reflection on racial discrimination and cultural hegemony in modern society.

Conclusion

From "taboo" to "reconciliation," ethical films challenge societal moral boundaries and stimulate deep thinking about humanity, ethics, and culture through the deconstruction of moral dilemmas and the revelation of human complexity. By depicting the growth and reconciliation of characters, these films provide opportunities for emotional resonance and cultural reflection, prompting us to rationally examine our emotions and behaviors when faced with "taboo" topics. This not only represents a re-understanding of human nature but also advocates for cultural diversity and inclusiveness, promoting deeper balance between morality and humanity, tradition and modernity.
