Riemann Zeta Function from an Interdisciplinary Perspective: The Convergence of Quantum Physics, Data Science, and Information Theory

The Riemann zeta function, a central object in number theory, has long been a focal point of mathematical research. However, with the advancements in quantum physics, data science, and information theory, the study of the Riemann zeta function has welcomed multiple new perspectives. This article explores how the Riemann zeta function can be linked with concepts from these disciplines and discusses its applications in quantum physics, data science, and information theory, as well as the potential breakthroughs that this interdisciplinary approach may bring.

From Quantum Physics to the Riemann Hypothesis: Exploring the Quantum Perspective of the Riemann Zeta Function

The basic definition of the Riemann zeta function is:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \]

where \( s \) is a complex number. An important property of the Riemann zeta function is that its non-trivial zeros (i.e., those on the line where the real part of \( s \) is \( \frac{1}{2} \)) are closely related to the distribution of prime numbers. The Riemann Hypothesis asserts that all non-trivial zeros lie on this line, but this conjecture remains unproven.

In quantum physics, there is a striking similarity between the energy level distributions of quantum systems and the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. Specifically, the energy level distributions of quantum chaotic systems can be described by random matrix theory, and the eigenvalue distributions of random matrices have a profound connection with the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

Quantum chaos refers to quantum systems that exhibit chaotic behavior in classical mechanics. The energy level distributions of these systems typically exhibit statistical randomness, which can be described by random matrix theory. The core idea of random matrix theory is to treat the Hamiltonian of a quantum system as a random matrix, and the distribution of its eigenvalues (i.e., energy levels) can be predicted using statistical methods.

In the study of the Riemann zeta function, random matrix theory provides a new tool to predict the distribution of its non-trivial zeros. Specifically, the Riemann zeta function can be regarded as the energy level distribution function of a special quantum state, and the tools and methods from random matrix theory can be used to study its properties. For example, by calculating the eigenvalue distribution of random matrices, one can predict the distribution pattern of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function, thereby providing new clues for the study of the Riemann Hypothesis.

This interdisciplinary approach may offer new insights for proving the Riemann Hypothesis. By linking the Riemann zeta function with concepts from quantum physics, researchers can use the tools and methods from quantum physics to reveal deeper physical laws hidden behind the distribution of prime numbers. For instance, the similarity between the energy level distributions of quantum chaotic systems and the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function may imply an unknown physical mechanism that may be universally present in broader quantum systems.
