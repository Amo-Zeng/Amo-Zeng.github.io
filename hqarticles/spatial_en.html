1. The Symphony of Space-Time: The Essence of the Universe

The intertwined relationship between time and space is the cornerstone of understanding the essence of the universe. Einstein's theory of general relativity tells us that time and space are not isolated from each other but are closely intertwined, jointly forming the four-dimensional space-time continuum. Space is no longer a static container but a dynamic structure that interacts with matter and energy, constantly changing. The flow of time is manifested through the structure of space, much like waves eroding the beach, leaving behind layers of traces in space. The flow of time also etches eternal marks in space, which are not static but continuously evolving and interacting.

2. The Perceptual Boundary of Space: From Limitation to Freedom

Space is first and foremost a perceptual horizon, defining the boundaries of our cognition. Visual, auditory, and tactile sensory experiences are all constrained by space, limiting our cognitive scope to specific spatiotemporal dimensions. For example, visual perception relies on the propagation of light in space, and auditory perception requires the transmission of sound waves in space. However, space does not completely restrict our perception; it also partially shapes our perceptual methods. The depth perception of space enables us to judge the distance of objects, and spatial orientation helps us locate our relationship with the world.

3. The Container of Space: The Projection of Social Relations

Space is not merely a physical entity but also the container of social relations. Philosopher Alain Badiou points out, "Space does not exist independently; it is the projection of social relations." This statement reveals the profound connection between space and social relations. From ancient Roman public baths to modern shopping malls, from the alleyways of Beijing to the lanes of Shanghai, space always reflects and shapes social relations in its unique way.
