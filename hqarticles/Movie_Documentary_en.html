A Journey of Sound Weaving Reality and Fiction: Exploring Multi-Dimensional Narratives in Documentary Films

In the world of cinema, documentary films have long been regarded as faithful witnesses to reality, capturing historical moments and societal changes. However, with technological advancements and innovative narrative techniques, documentaries are subtly crossing the boundaries between fiction and reality, creating a unique narrative charm. This charm is not only reflected in the profound revelations of reality but also in the weaving of reality and fiction, the power of sound, and individual storytelling strategies, offering audiences richer and more diverse viewing experiences. This article will explore how documentary films navigate between true and fictional identities, constructing a multi-dimensional narrative world, from these three perspectives.

I. Blurring the Boundaries of Reality: The Dual Identities of Documentary Films

In traditional cinematic concepts, documentaries and feature films were clearly distinct: the former sought truth, while the latter created fiction. However, with the advancement of film technology and narrative techniques, the boundaries between the two are gradually blurring. Documentary films, originally tasked with "recording reality," are quietly crossing these boundaries, navigating between true and fictional identities, and creating a unique narrative charm.

Fictional techniques in documentary films are no longer novel. The use of reenactments, actor portrayals, and plot designs allows documentaries to move beyond simple recording. Take *Searching for Sugar Man*, for example, where director Malik Bendjelloul, unable to find sufficient historical footage, cleverly employed reenactments, making the audience feel as if they were on the journey to find the legendary singer Rodriguez. This fictional technique not only filled the gaps in footage but also enhanced the film's suspense and dramatic tension.

The use of these fictional techniques allows documentary films to maintain authenticity while gaining stronger narrative tension and emotional appeal. *Citizenfour*, a documentary about the Edward Snowden incident, achieves its heart-wrenching impact not only from the drama of the event itself but also from director Laura Poitras's carefully designed narrative structure. By recreating key scenes and arranging character interviews, the film successfully creates a suspenseful and thrilling viewing experience while maintaining journalistic authenticity, making the audience deeply aware of the critical issue of information security.

This dual identity of documentary films raises profound questions about authenticity. Viewers often face confusion when watching these films: is this a true record or a meticulously designed plot? This confusion actually confirms the relativity of documentary authenticity. In fact, all truths are selected truths, and all facts are edited facts. By using fictional techniques, documentary films reveal this truth more profoundly: what we call reality is always a joint construction of subjectivity and objectivity.

The audience's acceptance of this blurred boundary also reflects the changes in contemporary viewing culture. In the post-truth era, audiences no longer seek absolute truth but are more focused on the emotional resonance and depth of thought in the story. By combining reality and fiction, documentary films can maintain the seriousness of journalism while gaining dramatic appeal, catering to the aesthetic needs of contemporary audiences.

II. The Power of Sound: Exploring Soundscapes in Documentary Films

In the world of cinema, sound is often regarded as a secondary element to visuals. However, for documentary films, sound is an indispensable core element. Known for their record and representation of the real world, soundscapes—the sonic environments of a film, including ambient sounds, background music, and character dialogue—are key factors in shaping this authenticity. This article will delve into the unique role of soundscapes in documentary films, analyze their differences from traditional film sound effects, and explore how sound influences the viewer's experience, as well as the future trends in soundscapes.

Unlike traditional films, the core of documentary films lies in true recording rather than fictional narrative. Therefore, soundscapes in documentaries are not just about enhancing dramatic effects but are essential in providing audiences with a sense of being there. Traditional film sound effects often exaggerate or fictionalize sound designs to intensify emotions and storytelling, while documentary soundscapes focus more on recreating the soundscapes of the real world, helping viewers immerse themselves in the film's presented situation.

This design of soundscapes is not for dramatic effect but to maximize the reconstruction of real emotions and atmospheres.

Different types of soundscapes can greatly influence the audience's emotional and cognitive experiences. Grandiose scores can imbue historical events or grand themes in documentaries with a sense of historical weight, such as *Cinemania*, which uses classic film scores and sound effects to transport viewers to an era of passion and creativity. Subtle ambient sounds can reveal the inner world of characters; in *Searching for Sugar Man*, the hustle and bustle of the street and the background music of the café showcase the lonely yet resilient heart of musician Rodriguez.

With the continuous development of virtual reality (VR) technology, the creation of soundscapes in documentary films will usher in new possibilities. VR technology allows viewers to immerse themselves in the film's scenes, and the design of soundscapes will become the core of this immersive experience. Future documentaries may use 360-degree surround sound technology to make viewers feel more authentic ambient sounds, and even enhance their participation and interactivity through the directionality and distance of sound.

III. The Grand Narrative through a Micro Lens: Individual Storytelling Strategies in Documentary Films

As an important form of art, documentary films have long been known for their grand narratives of historical events and social issues. However, in recent years, an increasing number of documentaries have distanced themselves from grand historical and social backgrounds, focusing instead on individual lives, telling individual stories through a micro lens, and reflecting broader social issues. This shift not only changes the narrative approach of documentaries but also provides audiences with a deeper and more authentic emotional experience.

The advantage of individual storytelling strategies lies in its ability to create vivid character images, evoke emotional resonance, and flexibly present complex social issues. By focusing on individuals' emotions, thoughts, and behaviors, individual storytelling can create more vivid and three-dimensional character images, allowing audiences to deeply understand the inner world of the characters and feel their joys and sorrows. For example, *Searching for Sugar Man* tells the legendary life of a forgotten singer, Rodriguez, evoking reflection on dreams, persistence, and fate.

Another advantage of individual storytelling is that it can evoke emotional resonance by showing the daily lives, struggles, and endeavors of individuals. When watching individual stories, audiences often relate to their own life experiences, making it easier to empathize. For example, *The Cove* tells the story of a group of environmental activists fighting to reveal the truth about dolphin hunting by Japanese fishermen, reflecting the global issue of marine ecosystem protection.

However, individual storytelling strategies also have certain limitations. It can easily fall into subjective emotional expression, neglecting the objective presentation of facts. As a medium for recording reality, the core value of documentaries lies in presenting the truth. Therefore, documentary creators need to balance individual emotions and social realities when adopting individual storytelling strategies to ensure the authenticity and depth of the film.

Conclusion

Documentary films are creating a multi-dimensional narrative approach through the blurring of reality and fiction, the power of sound, and individual storytelling strategies. This narrative approach not only challenges traditional concepts of truth but also provides audiences with a deeper emotional experience. In future film creation, the intertwining of reality and fiction, immersive soundscapes, and micro perspectives on individual stories will continue to bring new possibilities to documentary films, opening a window for audiences to understand society, history, and culture. As documentary filmmaker Michael Moore said, "Sound is the soul of cinema, and the sound of documentaries is the witness to truth."
