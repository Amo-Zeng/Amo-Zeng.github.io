Reevaluating the Multifaceted Value of Agricultural Resources and Sustainable Revitalization Pathways

Against the backdrop of global climate change, resource scarcity, and escalating environmental pollution, the reevaluation of agricultural resources and their sustainable utilization have become crucial topics for rural revitalization and global sustainable development. This article will explore innovative applications and synergistic development pathways of agricultural resources under the new era framework from three perspectives: multifaceted value reevaluation of agricultural resources, sustainable utilization and ecological restoration, and humanistic value and rural cultural revitalization.

1. Multifaceted Value Reevaluation of Agricultural Resources and New Business Models

Traditional agricultural resources are often viewed solely as production materials, neglecting their multifaceted potential values. Through resource utilization, industrial integration, and big data technologies, the value of agricultural resources can be rediscovered and reevaluated, thereby driving the formation of new business models.

1.1 Resource Utilization of Waste Materials

Waste materials from traditional agricultural production, such as straw and livestock manure, can be converted into high-value products like biomass energy, organic fertilizers, and bioplastics through resource utilization. For instance, straw can be used to produce biocarbon for soil improvement and carbon offset trading, while livestock manure can be used to generate biogas for electricity generation that is fed into the grid. This resource utilization not only reduces environmental pollution but also brings new economic benefits to agricultural production.

1.2 Integrated Development of "Agriculture + Cultural Tourism"

The cultural and experiential values of agricultural resources can be fully tapped through the integrated development of "Agriculture + Cultural Tourism." By promoting leisure agriculture, sightseeing agriculture, and agri-tourism, distinctive towns and rural complexes can be created, transforming the multiple values of agricultural resources. For example, leveraging agricultural heritage sites like terraced fields, combined with cultural experiences and eco-tourism, can create unique agri-tourism destinations, enhancing both economic benefits and cultural attractiveness in rural areas.

1.3 Value Mining in Agricultural Big Data

The application of IoT and artificial intelligence technologies enables efficient collection and analysis of agricultural data. These data not only support decision-making for precision agriculture but also form new business models through data trading and sharing. For instance, using drone remote sensing to monitor crop growth, predict yields, and detect pest and disease outbreaks provides data support for agricultural insurance and futures trading, driving the digital transformation of the agricultural value chain.

2. Sustainable Utilization of Agricultural Resources and Ecological Restoration: Pathways for Synergistic Development

The sustainable utilization of agricultural resources and ecological restoration are key to achieving high-quality agricultural development. Through nature-based solutions (NbS), agricultural carbon sequestration, and non-point source pollution control, agricultural resources can achieve synergistic development between ecological protection and economic benefits.

2.1 Nature-Based Solutions (NbS)

NbS designs sustainable agricultural production models by mimicking the functioning mechanisms of natural ecosystems, achieving a win-win for agricultural production and ecological protection. For example, the rice-fish farming model reduces the use of pesticides and fertilizers, improves water quality through rice-fish symbiosis; under-forest economies combine forestry with agriculture, enhancing land use efficiency and preserving forest ecosystems. Additionally, restoring wetlands, riparian vegetation, and farmland shelterbelts can rehabilitate degraded agricultural ecosystems, increasing their stability and productivity.

2.2 Agricultural Carbon Sequestration and Climate Change Mitigation

Agricultural carbon sequestration, which absorbs and fixes atmospheric carbon dioxide, is a crucial pathway for addressing climate change. By developing low-carbon and circular agriculture, such as promoting conservation tillage and carbon-fixing crop cultivation, the carbon sequestration capacity of farmland can be enhanced. Additionally, developing agricultural carbon sequestration projects to participate in global carbon markets can provide additional economic benefits for farmers and enterprises, promoting sustainable agricultural development.

2.3 Non-Point Source Pollution Control and Ecological Compensation

Non-point source pollution control in agriculture requires establishing scientific monitoring and evaluation systems, promoting clean production technologies like precise fertilization and integrated water-fertilizer management to reduce nitrogen and phosphorus loss and pesticide residues. Furthermore, market-based and diversified ecological compensation mechanisms, such as government subsidies, carbon trading, and ecological banking, can incentivize farmers and enterprises to participate in ecological protection, achieving clean and sustainable agricultural production.

3. Humanistic Value of Agricultural Resources and Rural Cultural Revitalization

Agricultural resources are not only the foundation of economic development but also a significant carrier of rural cultural revitalization. By protecting agricultural heritage, tapping into the modern value of local knowledge, and reshaping rural aesthetics, agricultural resources can inject new vitality into rural cultural revitalization.

3.1 Protection and Inheritance of Agricultural Heritage

Agricultural heritage, such as terraced fields and mulberry-fish ponds, is not only a crystallization of agricultural wisdom but also a unique expression of rural landscapes. By developing agricultural heritage tourism and creating cultural experience and educational bases, the socio-economic value of agricultural resources can be enhanced, cultural identity strengthened, and rural cultural revitalization promoted.

3.2 Modern Value and Innovative Application of Local Knowledge

Local knowledge, accumulated by rural residents through long-term production and life, is highly practical and adaptive. By integrating local knowledge with modern technologies, such as precision agriculture and integrated water-fertilizer management, innovative eco-agriculture and smart agriculture can be developed, improving agricultural productivity, reducing resource waste, and promoting sustainable agricultural development.

3.3 Agricultural Resources and Rural Aesthetic Reshaping

The integration of agricultural resources with rural landscapes, architecture, and art can create beautiful, regionally distinctive rural areas, enhancing the aesthetic value and cultural significance of rural areas. For example, through land art and rural art industries, attracting talent to return to rural areas can promote integrated urban-rural development, strengthening rural cultural appeal and economic vitality.

Conclusion

The multifaceted value reevaluation and sustainable utilization of agricultural resources are not only critical to rural revitalization but also a vital component of global sustainable development. Through resource utilization, industrial integration, ecological restoration, and cultural revitalization, agricultural resources can transition from traditional production materials to multifaceted value carriers, providing new pathways and directions for the sustainable development of rural economy, ecology, and culture. In the future, we should place greater emphasis on the multifaceted value of agricultural resources, promoting high-quality agricultural development and contributing to global ecological security and sustainable development.
