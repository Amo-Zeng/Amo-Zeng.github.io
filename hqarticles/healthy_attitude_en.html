Existence·Evolution·Practice: Three Dimensions of Reconstructing Modern Values

In the long river of human civilization, values have always been the guiding light that leads us forward. However, faced with the complex realities of contemporary society, traditional value systems are encountering unprecedented challenges. How can we inherit traditional wisdom while adapting to modern needs? This article will explore the path of reconstructing modern values from the dimensions of ontology, evolution, and praxis.

I. Ontology: Exploring the Essential Pillars of Value

Ontology concerns the essence of existence, providing a foundational framework for understanding the world. From the ancient Greek philosophers' pursuit of "being" to the Eastern sages' contemplation of "Dao," ontology and values have always been intertwined. Plato's ideal world and Laozi’s "Dao follows nature" both provide ultimate justifications for human value pursuits.

In contemporary society, faced with phenomena such as information explosion and virtual reality, the explanatory power of traditional values is gradually waning. We need deep ontological thinking to redefine "reality" and "essence." For example, "information ontology" offers a new perspective on understanding the relationship between the virtual and the real, prompting us to re-examine the value foundations behind digital life.

Ontology not only provides a basis for value judgments but also helps us maintain clarity amidst conflicting values. Understanding the essence of "existence" clearly helps us rationally choose value orientations in line with this essence, avoiding getting lost in the complex realities.

II. Evolution: Understanding the Diversity and Dynamic Nature of Values

Evolution provides a biological perspective for understanding the diversity of values. From the perspective of natural selection, human values may be products of survival adaptation. Values such as cooperation and fairness, which are beneficial for group survival, are strengthened through evolution, while individualism and competition have survival advantages in specific environments.

This perspective allows us to adopt a more inclusive attitude toward the diversity of values. Differences in values across different cultural backgrounds can be seen as results of adapting to different survival environments. For example, competitive values in resource-scarce environments and cooperative values in resource-rich environments are both evolutionary products and are reasonable.

Evolution also reminds us that values need to keep pace with the times. Just as species need to evolve continuously to adapt to environmental changes, human values also need to adjust and update through social practice. This understanding provides important insights for addressing modern value conflicts: while respecting traditional cultural values, we should embrace new ideas with an open mind.

III. Praxis: Reconstructing the Value System Through Action

Praxis emphasizes that values are not just theoretical constructs but also practical guides. Marxist praxis theory asserts that only through practice can the rationality of values be tested and established. This reflection is particularly important in contemporary society.

Globalization and informatization have deeply transformed the ways of practice. Traditional agricultural society practices are insufficient to explain complex realities, and we need to focus on new forms such as virtual and digital practices. These practices profoundly influence the formation and evolution of values. For example, moral judgments in cyberspace significantly differ from traditional social evaluation standards, requiring us to re-examine our value systems.

Praxis also reminds us that in the face of value conflicts, we should take specific issues as our guide and seek solutions through practice. For instance, in balancing individual freedom and social order, we can explore successful practices from different societies to find a way to respect individual rights while maintaining social stability.

IV. Integration of Three Perspectives: Building a Modern Value System

By combining the depth of ontology, the breadth of evolution, and the effectiveness of praxis, we may be able to construct a new value system that roots itself in traditional civilization while adapting to modern society.

Firstly, based on ontology, we can find reliable foundations for values. Inheriting the philosophical thinking on the essence of existence from traditional philosophy and absorbing new discoveries from modern sciences such as quantum mechanics and information philosophy can provide theoretical support for value judgments.

Secondly, by drawing on the evolutionary perspective, we can adopt an inclusive attitude toward value diversity. This understanding is based on a deep recognition of human nature and history, acknowledging the rationality of value differences and seeking dialogue and integration among different values.

Finally, adhering to the principles of praxis, we can test and establish values through action. When faced with specific issues, we should use theoretical thinking and practical effects, reflecting and adjusting to make the value system truly effective in guiding us.

In an era full of uncertainties, reconstructing the value system is not about establishing new dogmas but creating a value generation mechanism capable of continuously adapting to environmental changes. This mechanism must be open, inclusive, and dynamic, finding direction in deep reflection, seeking balance in diverse perspectives, and achieving innovation through action and reflection.
