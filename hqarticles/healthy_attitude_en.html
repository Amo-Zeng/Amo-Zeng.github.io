Digitalization of Values and Intergenerational Conflict: The Dance of Genes, Culture, and the Virtual World

With the rapid advancement of technology and the swift societal changes, the value system of human society is undergoing unprecedented reconstruction. This transformation stems not only from superficial technological changes but also profoundly impacts people's thinking patterns, social structures, and cultural transmission. This article will delve into this complex process from three dimensions: the digitalization and virtualization of values, the conflict and integration of intergenerational values, and the biological basis and social construction of values, revealing how genes, culture, and the virtual world collectively shape contemporary values.

1. Digitalization and Virtualization of Values: Reconstruction in the Virtual World

As internet and virtual reality technologies advance rapidly, human values are shifting from the physical world to the digital world. This transformation reshapes social, economic, and cultural structures not just on a technical level but also on a deeper level.

1.1 Value Expression in Social Media and Virtual Communities

In social media and virtual communities, individual behaviors and expressions often directly reflect their values. Virtual spaces provide a freer and more anonymous environment, allowing individuals to showcase themselves and express their views more boldly. However, this freedom is not without boundaries. On one hand, social media grants individuals unprecedented expression rights, making the spread of values faster and more extensive; on the other hand, value expression in virtual spaces is often influenced by algorithm recommendations and group pressures, shaping individual behavior by invisible forces.

Case Analysis: On Instagram, users showcase their lifestyles and values by posting photos and videos. Research shows that Instagram users tend to post content that receives more "likes" and comments, a behavior termed "social media performance." Psychologists believe this behavior affects users' self-perception and may lead to "social comparison," where users evaluate their values and happiness by comparing their lives to others.

Empirical Study: A study by the University of Oxford indicates that the "like" culture on social media is closely related to individual self-worth. The study found that frequent reliance on "likes" to measure self-worth is associated with higher anxiety and depression, reflecting the homogenization of values in the virtual world and how algorithm recommendations influence behavioral choices.

1.2 Impact of Virtual Currencies and Digital Assets on Wealth and Consumption

The rise of virtual currencies and digital assets is profoundly changing people's wealth and consumption perspectives. Traditionally, wealth was seen as a tangible asset, such as gold, real estate, or currency. However, with the development of blockchain technology, virtual currencies (e.g., Bitcoin, Ethereum) and non-fungible tokens (NFTs) are gradually entering the mainstream, becoming a new form of wealth.

Bitcoin's price volatility is over ten times that of the S&P 500. This high volatility affects individual wealth perceptions and poses new challenges to global financial stability.

1.3 Impact of Digital Identities and Virtual Social Relations on Traditional Values

Digital identities and virtual social relationships are another important dimension of the digitalization and virtualization of values. In the virtual world, individuals can separate their digital identities from their physical identities and even have multiple virtual identities. This phenomenon provides more freedom and choice but also challenges traditional values.

Case Analysis: In the virtual game "Second Life," players can create virtual characters and engage in various social activities in the virtual world. Research shows that many players exhibit behaviors and values in the virtual world that differ dramatically from their real lives. This phenomenon, known as "virtual identity separation," reflects the complexity and diversity of digital identities.

2. Intergenerational Value Conflict and Integration: The Evolution of Ages and the Future of Society

With rapid societal changes and technological advancements, the value differences between generations are becoming increasingly apparent. These differences arise from the unique environments in which each generation grew up, covering career views, family views, consumption views, and more.

2.1 Analysis of Intergenerational Value Differences

The differences in values among generations are most evident in career, family, and consumption views. The Baby Boomer generation (born 1946-1964) experienced post-WWII economic recovery, valuing high professionalism, stability, and long-term career development. Generation X (born 1965-1980) grew up during inflation and economic downturns, having more realistic career views and prioritizing work-life balance. Millennials (born 1981-1996) were influenced by globalization and technological innovation, having more personalized and diverse career views. Generation Z (born after 1997) grew up during the rise of the internet and social media, having more flexible career views, emphasizing the combination of work and interests.

Empirical Study: A Pew Research Center survey found that Gen Z is more flexible in career choices, with over 60% of respondents willing to try freelance or remote work. This trend contrasts sharply with the stability-focused Baby Boomer generation, reflecting profound intergenerational value differences.

2.2 Integration of Intergenerational Values: Bridges of Education and Communication

Education is a crucial channel for integrating intergenerational values. Intergenerational communication courses, cross-generational learning projects, and media literacy education can help different generations understand and respect each other.

Solutions:

  1. Intergenerational Communication Courses: Schools can offer specialized courses to help students from different generations understand each other's values, encouraging interaction and dialogue to enhance understanding.
  2. Cross-Generational Learning Projects: Through collaborative projects like volunteer services and community activities, different generations can complete tasks together, enhancing cooperation and understanding.
  3. Media Literacy Education: Older generations can better understand how new technologies and media shape younger generations' values through media literacy education, reducing misunderstandings.

3. Biological Basis and Social Construction of Values: A Dance of Genes and Culture

The formation of values results from the intertwined influences of genes and culture, biology, and social construction.

3.1 Genes and Brain: The Biological Foundation of Values

From a biological perspective, values are deeply rooted in our genes and brain structure. Certain gene variations are associated with specific value tendencies, such as dopamine-related genes influencing risk preference and oxytocin-related genes linked to altruistic behavior. Brain structure and function also play crucial roles in value formation; the prefrontal cortex is closely related to decision-making and moral judgment, while the amygdala is involved in emotional responses and fear learning.

Empirical Study: MIT research indicates that dopamine-related gene variations may influence individual risk preferences. Individuals with specific gene variations tend to be more inclined to take risks and explore new things, affecting their career choices and lifestyles.

3.2 Social Culture and Education: Shapers of Values

Social constructs such as social culture and education systems also play a vital role in value formation. Different cultural backgrounds and education systems convey different value perspectives; for example, collectivist cultures emphasize cooperation and community interests, while individualist cultures prioritize personal freedom and independence.

Case Analysis: In Silicon Valley's innovative culture, individualism and adventurous spirit are highly valued, with many tech companies encouraging employees to pursue innovation and personal achievements. This culture contrasts sharply with Asia's emphasis on stability and collective benefits, reflecting the role of different cultures in shaping values.

Conclusion

In this era of rapid technological advancement and societal change, the digitalization of values, intergenerational conflict and integration, and the interplay of genes and culture collectively shape contemporary value systems. The rise of the virtual world changes how people express and interact, while generational differences reflect value conflict and integration across different growth environments. The interaction between genes and culture reveals the diversity and complexity of human behavior. Facing this complex situation, we need to find balance between the virtual and the real, seek understanding amidst generational conflicts, and maintain inclusiveness and diversity in the dance of genes and culture. Only by continuously reflecting and adjusting can we find our value orientation in this rapidly changing era and collectively shape a more inclusive and harmonious society.
