The Dance of Time Between Reality and Fiction: Morality, Language, and Existence in Screenwriting

Screenwriting is an art form that balances between reality and fiction, morality and language, time and existence. It is not only an exploration of the depths of human nature but also a reflection of social reality; it is both a deconstruction of linguistic violence and a weaving of temporal paradoxes. In this creative journey, the writer dances on a tightrope, maintaining balance and grace, ultimately illuminating the stage with eternal brilliance.

I. The Moral Maze Between Reality and Fiction: Finding the Golden Mean of Humanity

The essence of screenwriting lies in finding a subtle balance between fiction and reality. Fiction grants creators boundless imagination, allowing stories to unfold in impossible worlds; reality imbues the script with vitality, enabling audiences to find emotional resonance in the magic of drama. Shakespeare's "Othello" is a classic example, showcasing the multifaceted nature of human morality through intricate dramatic conflicts, revealing both dramatic tension and the fragility and paranoia of human nature.

Akira Kurosawa's "Rashomon" offers another perspective, illustrating the complexity of human nature through multiple perspectives. This narrative technique not only maintains character authenticity but also avoids simplistic dichotomies of good and evil, allowing audiences to feel the ambiguity of morality and the complexity of reality through layered conflicts. In screenwriting, creators must delve deeply into human nature, as Kurosawa did, and present its complexity through layered narratives, rather than distorting authenticity for dramatic tension.

Avoiding stereotypes of specific groups is also a significant ethical consideration in screenwriting. "Moonlight" respects the authenticity of its characters while exploring the protagonist's growth and identity crisis, avoiding the pitfalls of stereotypes. This approach reminds us that screenwriting is not only an art but also a social responsibility, requiring a moral balance between fiction and reality.

II. The Power Struggle Beneath the Lines: The Deep Mirror of Linguistic Violence

Dialogue is not merely a tool of drama but a precise mechanism of power struggles. In Shakespeare's "The Taming of the Shrew," Petruchio's roar, "I will make you compliant," oppresses not only Katherine but also metaphorically critiques patriarchal culture. Linguistic violence in dialogue often stems from deep cultural structures, trapping women within established social frameworks through stereotypical gender language. In Ibsen's "A Doll's House," Helmer's shifting address to Nora exposes the dual standards of patriarchal culture, revealing the underlying mechanisms of linguistic violence.

Class symbols in dialogue are equally striking. In Chekhov's "The Cherry Orchard," Trofimov's disdain for the aristocracy subtly critiques social hierarchy. Linguistic violence transforms class conflicts into linguistic hierarchies through dialogue deconstruction, subtly aligning the audience with certain ideologies. Linguistic violence is not inevitable; it depends on how creators wield the power of language. Wilde's "The Importance of Being Earnest" successfully deconstructs hypocrisy through satirical wordplay, showcasing an alternative side of linguistic violence.

Screenwriting should not merely present linguistic violence but also serve as a witness to social critique. Beckett's "Waiting for Godot" exposes modern society's alienation and alienation through absurd dialogue, itself a form of critique. Dialogue is a tool for deconstruction; by dismantling existing power relations, new possibilities are created. Creators must confront the power dynamics behind dialogue to truly liberate dramatic language, transforming it into a force for social progress.

III. The Dance of Time: The Artistic Puzzle in Screenwriting

Time in screenwriting is a mysterious tapestry, weaving past, present, and future together. The multiplicity of time is not just a narrative technique but also a profound existential metaphor. "Memento" and "Inception" intertwine temporal paradoxes, blending fragmented memories with continuities, and prophecies with uncertainties, creating dizzying narrative mazes. This temporal multiplicity is crucial in character development, making choices more complex and profound.

Narrative art of time is expressed through linear and nonlinear conversations. Traditional linear narratives like "The Godfather" showcase the heaviness of time through rigorous structures, while nonlinear narratives like "Pulp Fiction" break the single dimension of time, creating new possibilities through fragmented storytelling. Time-loop narratives, such as "Triangle" and "Happy Death Day," interpret time differently, exploring the relationship between fate and free will through infinite loops.

The use of temporal paradoxes is not merely a narrative technique but also a philosophical inquiry. "Tenet" explores the nature of time through reverse time concepts. Through the use of temporal paradoxes, writers can create more complex character arcs, as seen in "The Butterfly Effect," where the protagonist repeatedly returns to the past to alter fate but cannot escape its grip. This struggle reveals the depth and breadth of human nature, deepening the thematic expression of the script.

Conclusion: The Eternal Inquiry of Time, Language, and Morality

In the artistic temple of screenwriting, time, language, and morality intertwine throughout the creative process. Temporal paradoxes are not just explorations of narrative techniques but also profound inquiries into the meaning of human existence; linguistic violence not only reveals power relations but also deconstructs social reality; moral dilemmas not only explore human nature but also uphold social responsibility. Creators challenge the boundaries of time, deconstruct linguistic violence, and seek the golden mean of morality in this creative journey, ultimately producing awe-inspiring artistic masterpieces.

Screenwriting is not just an artistic exploration but also a profound reflection on human nature, society, and existence. In this creative journey, time ceases to be a shackle for creators and becomes wings of imagination. Through the artistic puzzle of time, the deconstruction of language, and the exploration of morality, screenwriting illuminates the stage with eternal brilliance, guiding audiences to contemplate the essence of humanity, morality, and life.
