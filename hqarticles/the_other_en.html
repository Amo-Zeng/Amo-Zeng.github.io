"The Other": Reflections on Existence in the Metaverse and Geopolitics

"The Other" is a complex and profound concept that permeates human thinking about self, society, and existence. From philosophy to sociology, from intimate relationships to globalization, it serves as both a mirror for self-awareness and a product of social construction, and it is at the core of existential reflections. In today's rapidly changing era, the meaning of "the other" continues to expand, and its influence becomes increasingly profound. Through multi-dimensional perspectives, we gain a more comprehensive understanding of the essence of "the other" and its significance in human life.

"The Other" as a Mirror of the Self

Breaking the "Consensus of Silence" in Self-Awareness

Self-awareness is the bedrock of human exploration of the world. However, traditional self-reflection based on "consensus of silence" often falls into subjective traps. Our self-awareness is based on existing experiences and beliefs, and while this introspective approach is profound, it is easily influenced by entrenched thinking and biases. When we attempt to understand ourselves and others through self-reflection, we often uncover unsettling truths. Selfish genes, cultural mutations, and technological advancements all contribute to unprecedented anxiety when confronting "the other." Here, "the other" serves as a mirror, allowing us to step out of ourselves and examine ourselves from an external perspective.

Through interaction with "the other," we can discover desires, fears, and aspirations that have been overlooked. For example, encountering a highly confident individual may reveal our own insecurities and unease. Conversely, meeting someone with a simple yet fulfilling lifestyle may prompt us to reflect on our excessive pursuit of material success. This comparison is not about superiority or inferiority but about using others as a reflection to uncover traits we have not previously recognized within ourselves. Through this mirroring, we transcend self-centered limitations and form a more comprehensive and objective self-awareness.

Digital Identities and Virtual Selves

In the metaverse, digital identities and virtual selves serve as important media for interaction with others. As a virtual reality technology, the metaverse provides a new social platform. On this platform, we can interact through digital identities and virtual selves, thereby gaining a better understanding of ourselves and others.

The emergence of digital identities and virtual selves not only changes our social interactions but also our self-awareness. In the metaverse, we can interact through digital identities and virtual selves, achieving a clearer understanding of cultural limitations and superiority. For instance, the conflict between individualism in Western culture and collectivism in Eastern culture prompts us to reconsider the relationship between the individual and the collective, leading to a more balanced and inclusive set of values. In this process, we gradually move beyond the limitations of a single cultural perspective, forming a more open and diverse cultural identity.

Self-Projection in Intimate Relationships

In intimate relationships, we often unconsciously project ourselves onto "the other," viewing them as an idealized "other half." This projection is both a means of seeking understanding and connection and a manifestation of self-escaping and suppression. For example, someone who lacked care in childhood may seek endless nurturing and protection from a partner. While this projection may appear to be an expression of love, it is often an overcompensation for unmet internal needs.

By becoming aware of and reflecting on this projection, we can establish more authentic and healthy intimate relationships. When we recognize that what we seek in a partner is actually the fulfillment of our own inner deficiencies, we can more rationally address these needs and attempt to satisfy them through self-growth. Additionally, a partner's reactions can help us gain deeper self-awareness. For instance, a partner's discomfort with certain behaviors may reflect self-deficiencies we are unwilling to confront. Through this interaction, we not only deepen our connection with our partner but also achieve self-growth and self-improvement.

"The Other" as Social Construction

Power Relations and the Shaping of "The Other"

"The other" is not an objective existence but a product of social power relations. Throughout history, factors such as race, gender, and class have been used to construct "the other" in order to consolidate the interests and power of specific groups. Exploring how "the other" is constructed not only helps us understand the roots of social inequality but also provides a pathway toward breaking down "other" boundaries and moving toward a more equal and inclusive society.

In sociology and philosophy, "the other" has long been considered a key concept for understanding social structures and individual identities. However, the complexity of this concept lies in its non-natural generation; it is carefully shaped by power relations within society. Historically, phenomena such as racism, sexism, and class division have been perpetuated by positioning certain groups as "the other," thereby enabling control and oppression. For example, during the colonial era, racial categorization depicted "non-my-kind" as barbaric and uncivilized, justifying colonial exploitation.
