Prefabricated Buildings Driven by Digital Twin Technology: Whole Life Cycle Cost Optimization and Sustainable Economic Models

In the context of global climate change and accelerated urbanization, the construction industry is facing unprecedented challenges and opportunities. How to achieve a win-win situation for economic and environmental benefits during this transformation process has become a focus of the industry. This article explores how, driven by digital twin technology, prefabricated and modular construction (PMC) can promote the green transformation of the construction industry through whole life cycle cost optimization and the construction of sustainable economic models.

I. Limitations of Traditional Building Economic Analysis and the Introduction of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms

Traditional building economic analysis often focuses on explicit financial costs, ignoring implicit environmental costs, leading to the prevalence of high-energy-consumption and high-pollution construction schemes. To change this situation, introducing carbon pricing mechanisms, incorporating carbon emissions costs into various stages such as building materials, construction, and operation and maintenance, not only quantifies implicit environmental costs but also guides investors and developers to choose more environmentally friendly and sustainable construction schemes.

  • High Energy Consumption and High Pollution: Due to the lack of carbon pricing, carbon emissions generated during the production of building materials, energy consumption in construction, and energy use during building operation are not included in cost accounting. As a result, high-energy-consumption and high-pollution construction schemes, which have lower initial costs, are favored, further exacerbating global climate change issues.
  • Resource Waste and Ecological Damage: The mining, processing, and transportation of building materials often involve excessive resource consumption and ecological damage. The lack of carbon pricing mechanisms means these implicit costs cannot be reflected in the economic analysis of buildings, leading to a vicious cycle of resource waste and ecological damage.
  • Neglect of Social Costs: Environmental pollution from construction activities not only directly affects ecosystems but also indirectly impacts human health and social welfare. Social costs related to air pollution, water pollution, and noise pollution are also overlooked by traditional building economic analysis.

II. Economic Benefit Assessment of Prefabricated and Modular Buildings

Prefabricated buildings involve pre-manufacturing building components in factory environments and then assembling them on-site; modular buildings involve pre-manufacturing entire building units (modules) in factories and then transporting them to the site for rapid installation. PMC has been widely applied worldwide, but its economic benefits still lack systematic evaluation and analysis. This article constructs a comprehensive assessment model to compare PMC with traditional buildings in terms of cost, project duration, quality, environmental impact, and other dimensions, and explores the applicability of different types of projects (residential, commercial, public buildings) to provide a scientific basis for policy formulation and market promotion.

  • Cost Analysis: Compare the cost differences between PMC and traditional buildings in material procurement, processing, and transportation.
  • Labor Cost: Analyze the changes in labor costs during factory production and on-site assembly of PMC.
  • Equipment and Tool Costs: Evaluate the investment costs of specialized equipment and tools required for PMC.
  • Project Duration Evaluation: Compare the pre-manufacturing cycle of PMC in factories with the design and construction cycle of traditional buildings.
  • On-Site Construction Duration: Analyze the time difference between on-site assembly of PMC and construction time of traditional buildings.
  • Quality Control: Discuss how standardized production in factories improves building quality.
  • On-Site Quality Control: Evaluate potential quality issues and solutions during the on-site assembly of PMC.
  • Environmental Impact: Compare the carbon emissions of PMC and traditional buildings throughout their life cycles.
  • Resource Utilization Efficiency: Analyze the environmental benefits of PMC in material utilization and waste management.

III. Application of Digital Twin Technology in Building Asset Management

Digital twin technology creates a virtual copy of physical entities, simulating the operational status of buildings in real-time, providing owners and operators with unprecedented insights and control capabilities. This article explores the impact of digital twin technology on the whole life cycle costs of building assets from an economic perspective, analyzes its application potential in reducing maintenance costs, extending building life, and increasing asset yields, and explores corresponding business models.

  • Reducing Maintenance Costs: Through real-time monitoring and data analysis, the digital twin system can identify potential failure points and predict equipment aging, enabling preventive maintenance. This proactive maintenance strategy significantly reduces the frequency of sudden failures and maintenance costs.
  • Process Optimization: By simulating the operational status of equipment, the system can automatically generate the best maintenance plan, ensuring that each maintenance is necessary and efficient. Research shows that building maintenance costs can be reduced by 10% to 20% with the adoption of digital twin technology.
  • Extending Building Life: By real-time monitoring of the structural health of buildings, the system can promptly identify and address potential structural issues, preventing small problems from escalating into major failures, thereby extending the life of buildings.
  • Adapting to Future Needs: By simulating building performance under different usage scenarios, owners and operators can plan and implement corresponding upgrades and renovations in advance, ensuring that buildings remain efficient and safe over the long term.
  • Increasing Asset Yields: By optimizing energy use and equipment operation, the digital twin system can significantly reduce building energy consumption and energy costs.
  • Improving Tenant Satisfaction: By enhancing building safety and comfort, digital twin technology can improve tenant satisfaction and increase rental income.
  • Supporting Investment Decisions: By simulating building performance and economic benefits under different investment schemes, the system can provide the best investment advice, thereby increasing the overall asset yield.

IV. Exploration of Business Models

As the application of digital twin technology in building asset management becomes more widespread, corresponding business models are also emerging. The following are several potential business models:

  • Subscription Service Model: Building management companies can offer subscription services based on digital twin technology, with owners and operators paying a fixed monthly or annual fee to enjoy comprehensive building management services.
  • Performance Guarantee Model: Building management companies can sign contracts with owners, promising specific levels of performance optimization through digital twin technology, such as reducing energy consumption, reducing maintenance costs, etc. If the promised performance indicators are not met, the management company must bear corresponding economic responsibilities.
  • Data-Driven Value-Added Services: Building management companies can leverage the vast amounts of data generated by the digital twin system to provide additional value-added services, such as market analysis, investment advice, etc. These data-driven services can help owners and operators make more informed decisions and increase the overall value of assets.

V. Conclusion

Driven by digital twin technology, prefabricated and modular buildings, through whole life cycle cost optimization and the construction of sustainable economic models, can not only maximize economic benefits but also effectively reduce environmental impacts, promoting the green transformation of the construction industry. Although challenges such as the implementation difficulty of carbon pricing mechanisms, data and technological limitations, cost transfer risks, and policy and market uncertainties still exist during implementation, through the joint efforts of governments, enterprises, investors, and the public, we have reason to believe that the combination of digital twin technology and prefabricated buildings will open up new avenues for the sustainable development of the construction industry and make a positive contribution to addressing global climate change.
