The Revolution in Narrative: From Screen to Interaction

The Revolution in Narrative: From Screen to Interaction, Reshaping Creation and Reception

Throughout the course of human civilization, narrative has always been a core method of conveying thoughts, expressing emotions, and exploring human nature. From the myths passed down orally to literary works written in text, and then to films on the silver screen, the forms of narrative have continuously evolved, yet its essence—the artistic expression centered around storytelling—has remained unchanged. However, with the rapid development of media technology, particularly the advent of the digital age, narrative is undergoing an unprecedented revolution. This revolution is not only changing the way we tell stories but also profoundly reshaping the relationship between creation and reception, opening up new dimensions in artistic expression.

I. The Shattering of the Screen: The Rise of Media Convergence and Multidimensional Narrative

The "screen" was once a sacred metaphor for the art of film, symbolizing a fixed space and a single perspective for narrative. Through this glowing rectangular glass, audiences peered into a world meticulously woven by the director. However, as the wave of media convergence sweeps across, the traditional "mirror of the screen" is shattering, fragmenting into countless glittering shards that reflect the possibilities of multidimensional narrative.

Media convergence is not merely a technological replacement; it is a narrative revolution akin to a renaissance. In this process, film art is no longer confined to a fixed screen space but extends into a multidimensional and multi-layered format. Interactive films, VR technology, augmented reality (AR), and other new media break traditional linear narrative modes, transforming audiences from passive receivers into active participants. In the interactive film "Black Mirror: Bandersnatch," audience choices determine the story's direction; in the VR experience "Google Earth VR," audiences are no longer mere observers of the world but explorers who immerse themselves in the narrative space.

The essence of this multidimensional narrative is the breaking of traditional linear structures and the construction of an open and diverse narrative network. Within this network, audiences can freely choose narrative paths, creating personalized narrative experiences. Just as Cubist painting broke the constraints of the frame, media convergence extends narrative from a single dimension to multiple dimensions, opening up a new artistic expression space.

II. Deconstructing Narrative: A Carnival of Images, Sounds, and Symbols

Against the backdrop of media convergence, the form of narrative is being deconstructed, replaced by a carnival orchestrated by images, sounds, and symbols. Traditional narrative modes rely on linear logic, with stories progressing from beginning to end, plot points interconnected, and meaning clearly defined. However, in an age dominated by visual culture, narrative has become increasingly fragmented, diverse, and nonlinear.

The Carnival of Images

Images are no longer mere accessories to text but have become an independent discourse system. In the "age of images," visual impact and emotional expression make images the core element of narrative. Films, advertisements, and short videos use visual language such as montage, color, and composition to construct a visually charged carnival. For example, the black monolith symbol in the film "2001: A Space Odyssey" is a classic case of deconstructed narrative, with its simple yet enigmatic visual image.

The Carnival of Sounds

The role of sound in narrative has undergone a revolutionary change. Elements such as music, sound effects, and dialogue no longer depend on the visual, but have become independent sound narrative texts. In the film "Dunkirk" by composer Hans Zimmer, the use of sound effects breaks traditional narrative pacing, creating a novel auditory experience. The power of sound narrative lies in its ability to express emotions through silence, create atmospheres through sound effects, and even hint at the direction of the plot through music.

The Carnival of Symbols

The meaning of symbols has become ambiguous and mutable in deconstructed narrative. In traditional symbol systems, the connection between signifier and signified is fixed, but in contemporary culture, the meaning of symbols is infinitely extended. Popular culture, internet slang, and emojis are new symbol systems that deconstruct traditional symbols, creating a space of infinite possibilities for meaning. For instance, the spinning top symbol in the film "Inception" not only hints at the boundary between dreams and reality but also triggers endless interpretations of narrative meaning.

III. The Reconstruction of the Creative Subject: Audience as Co-Creator of Narrative

The revolution of media convergence and deconstructed narrative not only changes the form of narrative but also profoundly reshapes the relationship between creation and reception. In traditional narrative modes, the author is the absolute ruler of the text, while the audience is a passive receiver. However, in the era of new media, audiences are no longer content with passive reception but actively participate in the creative process, becoming co-creators of narrative.

Audience Participation: From Observer to Co-Creator

In modern experimental theater and new media art, the role of the audience has undergone a fundamental shift. In the immersive theater piece "Sleep No More," audiences can freely explore the performance space, with different viewing paths leading to unique narrative experiences; in the interactive game "Detroit: Become Human," player choices determine the story's outcome. This participatory narrative makes the meaning of the text no longer fixed but fluid and continuously reconstructed through audience participation.

Collective Creation: Reconstructing the Creative Subject

When the audience becomes part of the creative subject, the concept of the author also changes. In interactive web novels, reader comments and votes influence the novel's direction; in "relay creation" on short video platforms, everyone is both a creator and an audience. The phenomenon of collective creation indicates that the act of creation is no longer the exclusive domain of a single genius but an open and fluid process.

Democratization of Creation: Rebuilding Creative Ethics

The involvement of audiences in creation brings another layer of transformation: the rebuilding of creative ethics. Traditional creative ethics emphasize the originality and authority of the author, while in the era of new media, creative ethics become more democratic. Everyone has the opportunity to be a creator, and every voice can enter the creative field. This democratization of creation not only breaks the limitations of traditional creation but also promotes the integration of creation and reception, making art a truly participatory cultural practice.

IV. The Future of Narrative Revolution: Infinite Possibilities

The wave of narrative revolution continues, and the future is full of exciting possibilities. The development of quantum computing may bring about true "hyperlinked narrative," allowing stories to unfold simultaneously in countless parallel universes; the advancement of brain-computer interface technology may enable immersive experiences at the level of consciousness. Although these technologies are filled with unknowns, they all point in the same direction: to break the boundaries of physics and psychology and achieve true freedom in narrative.

In this era of narrative revolution, stories are no longer confined to a single mode of expression, and audiences are no longer passive receivers. The new era of multidimensional narrative has arrived, poised to redefine the infinite possibilities of artistic creation with an open and diverse attitude. This revolution not only changes the way we tell stories but more importantly, it is redefining our relationship with art, opening a new epoch in human expression.

The essence of the narrative revolution is a revolution in creation and reception. In this revolution, the shattering of the screen opens up possibilities for multidimensional narrative, the carnival of images, sounds, and symbols deconstructs traditional narrative modes, and the reconstruction of the creative subject, with the audience as a co-creator, marks the arrival of the democratization of creation. The future of narrative will be freer, more open, and more diverse, breaking the boundaries of physics and psychology and creating unprecedented spaces for artistic expression. In this new era of infinite possibilities, everyone can be a weaver of narrative, and every voice deserves to be heard.
