Building a New Pattern of Multi-Stakeholder Natural Resource Governance

Global Challenges and the Necessity of Multi-Stakeholder Governance

In the face of global climate change, biodiversity loss, and resource depletion, sustainable natural resource governance has become a central issue for human society's development. Traditional governance models, overly reliant on government or a few interest groups, have led to issues such as uneven resource distribution, weak ecological protection, and insufficient social participation. To address these challenges, building a collaborative governance system with multiple stakeholders involved is essential for achieving harmonious coexistence between humans and nature.

I. Co-construction and Sharing: Conceptual Reconstruction and Multi-Stakeholder Participation in Natural Resource Governance

The "co-construction and sharing" concept emphasizes that all stakeholders participate equally, make collective decisions, and take coordinated actions to achieve sustainable resource utilization and maximize ecological value. This concept breaks away from the traditional one-way management mindset, viewing the governance process as a dynamic system of multi-party collaboration, aiming to form a synergy for resource protection and utilization through the joint participation of multiple stakeholders.

  • Diverse Stakeholders: Moving from single-actor management to collaborative governance, involving not only the government but also enterprises, civil society organizations, research institutions, and the public.
  • Fair Distribution of Outcomes: Establishing fair benefit-sharing mechanisms to balance interests, enhance the sustainability and social acceptance of the governance system.

II. Climate Change and Biodiversity: Dual Crises of Collaborative Governance

Climate change and biodiversity loss are the most severe challenges facing humanity today. The two are intertwined and exacerbate each other, forming a complex systemic crisis. The traditional single-issue governance model is no longer viable, necessitating the establishment of a new collaborative governance mechanism.

  • The Necessity of Collaborative Governance: Integrating rather than fragmenting, climate change and biodiversity loss are highly interrelated.
  • Practical Exploration and Success Stories: For example, the integration of the Biodiversity Convention and the Paris Agreement, the water catchment protection alliance in Costa Rica, and the comprehensive governance project in China's Sanjiangyuan region.
  • Building Resilient Ecosystems: Protecting and restoring key ecosystems, promoting multifunctional utilization of ecosystems, and enhancing their service values.

III. Natural Resource Protection and Rural Revitalization: A Practical Pathway for Organic Integration

Against the backdrop of rural revitalization, the organic integration of natural resource protection and economic development is not only a crucial path to sustainable development but also provides new ideas and practical directions for creating a new model of a beautiful China.

  • The Intrinsic Connection Between Natural Resources and Rural Revitalization: Natural resources provide rural areas with abundant ecosystem services, while rural revitalization promotes the rational development and protection of natural resources.
  • Policy Guidance and Institutional Innovation: Establishing ecological compensation mechanisms, improving land use planning, and ensuring the sustainable use of natural resources.
  • Development of Eco-Agriculture and Green Industries: Promoting eco-agriculture and green industries, reducing negative environmental impacts, and increasing farmers' incomes.
  • Social Participation and Public Education: Strengthening public education, enhancing environmental awareness, and encouraging social capital and non-governmental organizations to participate.

IV. Conclusion: From Concept to Practice - Comprehensive Collaboration

The "co-construction and sharing" concept, collaborative governance of climate change and biodiversity, and the organic integration of natural resource protection and rural revitalization together form a triad for natural resource governance. Through the joint participation of multiple stakeholders, addressing dual crises through collaborative governance, and deeply integrating rural revitalization with natural resources, we can not only achieve sustainable resource utilization but also build a more equitable, inclusive, and sustainable society.
