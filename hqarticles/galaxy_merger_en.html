The Cosmic Dance of Dark Matter: The Core Force of Galaxy Mergers

Galaxy mergers are a significant mechanism of cosmic evolution, not just involving the collision of stars, gas, and dust, but also a cosmic "dance" led by dark matter. As the mysterious substance accounting for about 85% of the universe's total mass, dark matter plays a central role in galaxy mergers. Each galaxy is surrounded by a massive halo of dark matter, whose mass far exceeds the visible parts of the galaxy. When two galaxies merge, their dark matter halos also collide and fuse, forming complex dynamical processes.

During the merger, the collision of dark matter halos is not merely a simple superposition, but also induces phenomena such as tidal stripping and superposition oscillations. Tidal stripping tears apart smaller dark matter halos, eventually merging them into larger ones; superposition oscillations cause the dark matter distribution to become temporarily uneven, forming density waves. These density waves, like ripples, propagate through the universe, affecting the distribution and motion of surrounding matter, and even shaping the form and dynamical characteristics of new galaxies.

Gravitational Waves: The Notes of the Cosmic Symphony

When galaxy mergers occur, the massive concentration of mass and energy release cause severe distortions in spacetime, generating intense gravitational waves. These gravitational waves propagate through the universe in wave form, carrying the "sound" of galaxy mergers, making them the most moving "notes" in the cosmic symphony.

The frequency, intensity, and pattern of gravitational waves are closely related to factors such as mass, speed, and distance during the merger process.
