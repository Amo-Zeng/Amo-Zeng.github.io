Ethics and Future in the Digital Age: From Platform Capitalism to Post-Human Economy

In the era of the digital economy, the rapid development of data, algorithms, platforms, and artificial intelligence (AI) has profoundly reshaped economic structures and social relationships, triggering extensive discussions on the nature of technology, ethical responsibilities, and the future societal form. This article attempts to propose a multi-dimensional analytical framework that transcends the current economic, ethical, and technological boundaries by exploring the controversy between "digital essentialism" and "digital fetishism," the ethical dilemmas of platform capitalism, and the future forms of the post-human economy, in order to provide deeper insights into the ethics and social development in the digital age.

Digital Essentialism and Digital Fetishism: The Dual Nature of Technology

Data and algorithms play increasingly important roles in modern society, but are they independent entities with intrinsic value, or merely over-glorified tools? This question can be answered by contrasting the views of "digital essentialism" and "digital fetishism."

  • Digital Essentialism: Supporters argue that data and algorithms are not just tools for specific functions but also entities with independent significance. They create unprecedented economic value and social benefits, becoming the core productive forces of the new era.
  • Digital Fetishism: Critics point out that the "value" of data is often artificially assigned, similar to Marx's description of "commodity fetishism." The widespread application of data and algorithms may exacerbate social inequality and highlight ethical issues.

Ethical Dilemmas of Platform Capitalism: From Kant to Rawls

Platform capitalism, as the core form of the digital economy, brings efficiency improvements and economic prosperity, but it also triggers profound ethical dilemmas. Social media, e-commerce, and sharing economy platforms have become the infrastructure of modern society, yet their operational models often conflict with ethical principles.

  1. Kant's Moral Duty Theory: Platform enterprises should respect users' privacy and data rights when handling user data, rather than merely maximizing profits.
  2. Rawls' Theory of Justice: Platform enterprises should consider how to reduce social inequality and ensure the rights of all users and participants when designing their business models.

Post-Human Economy: Technological Singularity and New Economic Forms

With the deep integration of artificial intelligence and biotechnology, human society is accelerating towards the technological singularity, and traditional economic theories and ethical frameworks will face fundamental challenges. In the post-human economy, economic entities will no longer be limited to humans but will consist of multi-dimensional subjects such as AI, bio-enhanced humans, and cyborgs. This transformation not only redefines the concept of "human" but also overturns traditional production relations and distribution methods.

  • Post-Humanism: Technology is not only an extension of humans but also a force that redefines human existence.
  • AI Value Distribution Mechanism: Establishing an "AI value distribution mechanism," such as through an "AI tax" to allocate part of the value created by AI for public welfare and public services.

Conclusion: Balancing Technological Progress and Social Ethics

From the controversy between digital essentialism and digital fetishism, to the ethical dilemmas of platform capitalism, and then to the future forms of the post-human economy, we can see that technological progress is both a powerful driving force for social development and brings profound ethical challenges. How to ensure social fairness and justice while enjoying the convenience brought by the digital economy is a common issue we face.
