Symbiosis in Fracture: Reconstructing "Relational Ecology" in Agricultural History

—Decentralized Practices of Body, Species, and Institutions

Introduction: The Historization of Ecological Fracture

The crises of modern agriculture—soil degradation, groundwater depletion, genetic homogenization of crops, and waves of farmer suicides—are often attributed to "technological overreach" or "market failure." Yet such explanations obscure a deeper structural fracture: the systematic dismantling of long-standing relational networks between human bodies, non-human species, and institutional arrangements. This fracture is not a natural evolution, but a historical product of ecological disembedding under modern governance. This article proposes "relational ecology" as an analytical framework to reveal the dynamic, asymmetrical, and embodied co-constitution among body, species, and institution in agricultural transformation, asking: when these relations are institutionally fractured, how are ecological consequences historically produced and reproduced?

This framework does not nostalgically return to a romanticized "harmony between heaven and humanity," but engages deeply with Foucault's "biopolitics", Haraway's "Cyborg Manifesto", Hardt and Negri's "the multitude", and Anna Tsing's "mushrooms growing in ruins". It challenges the traditional environmental history binary of nature/culture, human/non-human, and critiques political ecology's institutional determinism, instead focusing on the multi-agentivity (multi-agentivity) that emerges and collapses within specific historical contexts.

I. The Body: The "Ecological Interface" Under Discipline

The "body" in agricultural history is not merely a passive tool of labor, but an embodied node of ecological relations. In traditional paddy farming societies (e.g., Jiangnan in China, Kansai in Japan), farmers' feet and the muddy soil layers formed a symbiotic adaptation: long-term barefoot cultivation thickened the foot's keratin layer, creating a natural barrier against pathogens; simultaneously, farmers used tactile sensation to assess soil moisture and fertility, achieving a somatic coupling (somatic coupling) between body and soil. This coupling already existed in Foucault's sense of the "disciplined body," but unlike the factory assembly line, it manifested as non-institutionalized, context-specific knowledge production.

However, the Green Revolution of the mid-20th century de-agentialized the farmer's body. In Punjab, India, groundwater extraction increased by 470% from 1970 to 2020(CGWB, 2021), while farmers were institutionally reduced to "technology adopters": the state promoted high-water-demand wheat varieties (e.g., Kalyan Sona), provided chemical fertilizers and irrigation subsidies, yet shifted water management responsibility to individuals. The body transformed from a "sensing-regulating" system into an "input-output" machine, severing the symbiotic perception between foot and soil, replaced by anxiety over electricity meters, water gauges, and loan cycles. This disembodiment is the agricultural version of Foucault's "biopower": the state indirectly controls the body through biological regulation (e.g., seed distribution, credit access).

More profoundly, this control operates through "ecological debt." Farmers over-extract groundwater to repay loans, creating a vicious cycle of "borrow more, extract more, become poorer." In 2018, the loan default rate among Punjabi farmers reached 23.6%(NSSO 77th Round), embedding the body within the reproductive chain of financialized ecology. Here, Haraway's "cyborg" theory is inverted: farmers are not merging with machines, but being consumed by machine logic, becoming the "interface" of a capital-technology-ecology complex.

II. Species: The Institutionalized "Non-Human Agency"

In traditional agriculture, species (crops, livestock, microbes) possess institutional agency, where their very existence constitutes governance structures. For example, the "rice-fish-duck" system in Hani terraces of Yunnan is not "natural symbiosis," but institutionally embedded through village rotation, water allocation, and ritual ceremonies. Fish eat pests, duck droppings fertilize the soil, and rice roots stabilize it—forming a institutionally sustained ecological cycle, whose stability depends on the symbolic empowerment of non-human species within local institutions (e.g., ducks as "messengers of water deities").

The Green Revolution shattered this institutional agency. High-yield wheat and hybrid corn were symbolically elevated to "national food security," while local varieties (e.g., Indian Aus rice, Chinese red rice) were stigmatized as "low-yielding" and "backward." Species selection shifted from ecological embeddedness to standardization dominated by state-capital complexes. In this process, species' "agency" was redefined: no longer as "actors" in local governance, but as managed resources.

Anna Tsing, in The Mushroom at the End of the World, reveals how non-human life (e.g., matsutake mushrooms) rebuilds relational networks in ruins. But in agriculture, species fracture is marked by institutional violence. Take U.S. corn: in 2020, 92% was Monsanto's GMO varieties(USDA, 2021). Seed patent laws prevent farmers from saving seeds, legally stripping species of their "reproductive agency." Crucially, this disempowerment is enacted through agricultural extension systems: technicians stigmatize local varieties as "pest-prone" or "herbicide-sensitive," transforming biodiversity into a symbol of "technological backwardness." Species thus shift from participants in relational ecology to victims of institutional fracture.

Notably, this fracture is not unidirectional. Some species "reverse-agency" in institutional interstices. For example, "heirloom" rice varieties in Southwest China regain market value in ecological farms and CSAs (Community-Supported Agriculture), as farmers rebuild "human-rice" relations through consumer alliances. This echoes Hardt and Negri's "the multitude": non-human life and marginalized groups form resistant ecological networks, forging "alternative agricultural practices" outside dominant institutions.

III. Institutions: The Production and Reproduction of Fracture

Institutions serve as the structural mediators of body-species relations, yet they too undergo fracture. Traditional agricultural institutions (e.g., village councils, irrigation associations, crop rotation cycles) possess ecological feedback mechanisms: declining yields trigger institutional adjustments (e.g., fallow periods, water reallocation). In contrast, modern institutions (e.g., credit, insurance, futures markets) are built on linear growth assumptions, externalizing ecological risks.

In the U.S. Corn Belt, federal subsidies and futures markets "hedge" price volatility, masking the ecological cost of soil organic matter declining from 3.5% to 1.8%(NRCS, 2019). With "insurance backstops," farmers expand monoculture, as institutions encourage ecological risk accumulation. This embodies Hardt's concept of "imperial governance": institutions do not resolve contradictions, but delay and displace them via financial instruments.

The mechanism of institutional fracture reproduction lies in triple disembedding:

  • Economic disembedding: agriculture shifts from subsistence to commodity economy, where price signals replace ecological signals;
  • Epistemic disembedding: extension systems exclude local knowledge, monopolizing "scientific" authority by expert systems;
  • Temporal disembedding: short-term market cycles replace long-term ecological cycles (e.g., groundwater recharge takes decades).

This disembedding was exposed during the 2008 financial crisis: U.S. farm loan defaults peaked at 14.3%(USDA, 2009), forcing farmers to sell land and abandon ecological management. Institutions did not repair fractures, but financialized them—land became speculative asset, and ecological relations were further commodified.

Conclusion: The Analytical Significance of Relational Ecology

"Relational ecology" is not a description of an "ideal state," but an analytical tool for revealing the dynamic co-constitution and structural fractures among body, species, and institution in agricultural history. Its theoretical boundaries are clear:

  • It rejects reductionism (does not attribute ecological problems to a single cause);
  • It rejects romanticization (does not idealize traditional agriculture as "perfect balance");
  • It rejects anthropocentrism (emphasizes non-human agency, but not "animism");
  • It rejects institutional determinism (institutions are mediators, not root causes).

This framework has potential implications for three fields:

  1. Environmental History: shifts focus from "natural history" to "relational history," emphasizing embodied practices (e.g., barefoot farming), non-human actors (e.g., rice-fish systems), and institutional fractures (e.g., subsidy policies);
  2. Agricultural Anthropology: moves beyond "cultural adaptation," proposing the concept of ecological agency to analyze how farmers rebuild relational networks in institutional interstices (e.g., CSAs, seed banks);
  3. Political Ecology: challenges the "state-capital" binary critique, introducing a multi-agentivity lens to reveal non-human participation in ecological resistance (e.g., species' reverse selection, microbial community shifts).

Ultimately, relational ecology reminds us: the agricultural crisis is not a technical question of "how to farm," but a deeper one of how to reconnect—between body and soil, species and institution, past and future. As Anna Tsing writes, "symbiosis always begins in ruins." In the fractures, we may find the starting point of relational rebuilding: not a return to pre-modernity, but through decentralized practices, allowing bodies to re-sense the soil, species to reclaim agency, and institutions to learn to listen to the silence of ecology.
