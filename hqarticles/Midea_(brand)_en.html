Exploring Beauty in Multiple Dimensions: From Cultural Differences to Philosophical Contemplation

Beauty, a seemingly simple term, carries profound reflections of human civilization and rich emotions. It is not only a visual pleasure but also a vivid reflection of cultural differences, and a central issue of philosophical speculation. From the Eiffel Tower to the Forbidden City, from the scientific explanation of the visual nerve to Plato's metaphysics, the exploration of beauty spans across cultures, science, and philosophy, revealing its complex nature and multidimensional essence.

Cultural Differences: Diverse Expressions of Beauty

In 1889, the birth of the Eiffel Tower sparked strong opposition among the elite in Paris, who viewed it as a "barbaric" symbol of industry. Meanwhile, in the East during the same period, the architectural aesthetics of the Forbidden City garnered widespread admiration. These starkly different aesthetic reactions deeply reflect the shaping power of culture on beauty. The aesthetic ideas in the West following the Industrial Revolution emphasize functionality, standardization, and rational thinking, with the simple lines and precise structure of the Eiffel Tower embodying these principles. In contrast, the beauty in traditional Chinese culture is closely linked to the unity of man and nature, and the harmony of yin and yang. The central axis symmetry and color layout of the Forbidden City reflect the cosmological view of "ritual."

These cultural differences are not limited to the architectural field but permeate various art forms such as painting, music, and literature. Western classical music seeks perfection in harmony, emphasizing rational structures and balance, while traditional Chinese music emphasizes "vivid rhythm," striving to create意境 and emotional resonance. Renaissance Western painting focuses on perspective and the precision of anatomy, aiming to reproduce nature, whereas Chinese landscape painting stresses "writing spirit through form," focusing on the charm of brushwork and the expression of the mind. Each culture has its unique aesthetic code, and these differences constitute the rich and colorful aesthetic spectrum of human civilization.

Scientific Explanation: The Neural Basis of Beauty

Perceiving beauty does not solely rely on cultural background; it also involves complex neuroscientific processes. How do visual nerves transform the stimuli of color, lines, and shapes into a feeling of beauty when we appreciate a piece of art? Research shows that the perception of beauty is a multi-layered process involving the collaboration of the visual system, emotional system, and cognitive system.

Neurons in the visual cortex are particularly sensitive to specific visual features, such as symmetry, color contrast, and movement patterns. These features are considered fundamental elements of beauty. When we see an object with high symmetry, the brain releases dopamine, a chemical closely associated with pleasure. However, the perception of beauty does not stop at the level of external stimuli; emotional brain regions are also activated, indicating that the experience of beauty is a comprehensive emotional response.

Cultural background and personal experiences play significant roles in the perception of beauty. The same piece of art may be interpreted differently by people from different cultural backgrounds. Even the same individual may have vastly different feelings towards the same artwork under different moods. Evolutionary psychology provides an intriguing perspective: certain aesthetic preferences may stem from our survival instincts. Symmetrical faces are often considered beautiful because symmetry is generally associated with health and superior genetic traits. The harmonious color combinations may originate from our ancient adaptation to natural environments.

Philosophical Discussion: Quest for the Essence of Beauty

What is the essence of beauty? This question has sparked philosophical reflections from Plato to Wittgenstein. Plato believed that beauty is an eternal idea, and beautiful objects are merely imperfect reflections of this idea. This viewpoint initiated the Western metaphysical tradition of aesthetics. Wittgenstein, through linguistic analysis, challenged the way we talk about beauty, arguing that "beautiful" cannot be defined as an essential property of things.

Kant, in "Critique of Judgment," proposed the concept of "purposiveness without a purpose," suggesting that aesthetic judgments are free from interests and concepts yet can be universally communicated. This idea reveals the tension between the freedom and universality of beauty. Hegel viewed beauty as the "sensuous appearance of the idea," believing that the development of art is the process of the absolute spirit recognizing itself. Heidegger, through his critical reflection on technology, rethought the relationship between art and existence, arguing that artworks reveal "the thing itself" rather than merely serving as objects for humans. Derrida's deconstructionism challenges traditional binary thinking, suggesting that beauty should not be fixed within specific categories.

In contemporary society, debates about beauty abound. From cosmetic surgery to AI-generated art, from internet celebrity aesthetics to the preservation of traditional crafts, each topic involves complex philosophical considerations. Aesthetics is no longer confined to ivory tower musings but is closely linked to real-life dimensions of thought.

Conclusion: Universality and Diversity of Beauty

As a universal human experience, beauty manifests in diverse expressions and profound meanings through different cultural, scientific, and philosophical perspectives. From the Eiffel Tower to the Forbidden City, from the scientific explanation of the visual nerve to Plato's metaphysics, the exploration of beauty reveals its universality across cultural differences, while also showcasing its diversity due to individual and cultural backgrounds.

Renowned architect I.M. Pei once said, "The most beautiful architecture should be timeless and transcultural; it should possess eternal artistic charm." This kind of beauty that transcends cultural differences is truly the art of universal value. In today's globalized era, understanding different aesthetic standards becomes尤为 important. Only on the basis of mutual understanding and respect can different civilizations truly achieve dialogue and exchange.

The exploration of beauty will never cease; it will continue to inspire deep reflections on ourselves and the world. Whether through cultural differences, scientific explanations, or philosophical discussions, the multidimensional exploration of beauty provides a unique perspective for understanding human civilization and individual existence.
