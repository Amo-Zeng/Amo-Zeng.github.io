Philosophy of Art Analysis

Cubism Through Analytical Aesthetics

Application of Frege's "Context Principle" in Visual Semiotic Systems

Cubism's revolutionary nature lies not only in formal fragmentation but also in its structural reconstruction of visual semiotic systems. While traditional art sought to establish stable correspondences between symbols and objects through realistic imitation, Cubism reveals a more fundamental proposition by deconstructing the coherence of forms: the meaning of visual symbols does not inherently reside in individual elements but emerges through dynamic structural networks.

I. Frege's Philosophical Revolution: Primacy of Context and Generative Meaning

1.1 The Collapse of Traditional Semantics

In 1884, Frege proposed the groundbreaking "Context Principle" in The Foundations of Arithmetic: "A word has meaning only in the context of a sentence." For Frege, any isolated name (e.g., "cat") remains a potential symbol whose true meaning activates only within specific propositional structures. This meaning-generation mechanism shows that a symbol's essential function lies not in "referring to an object" but in "performing a role within a system".

1.2 "Use" Transcending "Reference"

Wittgenstein deepened this approach in Philosophical Investigations, proposing "meaning as use." This conceptual shift finds mirroring in Cubist collage: when Picasso pasted cane patterns directly onto canvas, his intention was not to depict an actual chair but to use the material as a tool for constructing spatial relationships.

II. Goodman's Stratified Symbol Theory: Reconstructing Cubism's Non-Referential Debate

2.1 Self-Referential vs. Hetero-Referential Signifiers

Nelson Goodman distinguishes between "hetero-reference" and "self-reference" in art semiotic systems in Languages of Art. Juan Gris's Breakfast (1914) exemplifies this tension: the coffee pot in the painting is neither fully abstract color blocks nor direct representation of reality.

2.2 The Translation Dilemma of Label Systems

Goodman further notes that all symbolic systems require labels to connect with referents. Cubism's innovation lies in dissolving univocal labels: a single symbol may simultaneously perform multiple labeling functions – form reference, spatial positioning, and rhythmic control.

III. Visual Syntactic Analysis of Breakfast: Strategies of Semiotic Network Reconstruction

3.1 Topological Transformation of Perspective System

Breakfast achieves topological deformation of traditional perspective through multi-perspective superimposition. This operation parallels Frege's "function-variable" concept – the meaning of fragmented forms shifts according to their positions.

3.2 Structuralization of Color Coding

Warm tones create dynamic forward movement while cool tones form a static base. Each color's value depends on its relative position in the chromatic network – perfectly aligning with Frege's view that "a symbol's semantic value is determined by its systemic coordinates."

V. Philosophical Debate: Is Cubism a Visual Prelude to the "Linguistic Turn"?

5.1 Radical Propositions

Proponents argue that Cubism completed art's "Copernican Revolution": every symbol in Breakfast functions like a "syntagm" in language systems, its value entirely defined by differential relations between signs.

5.2 Conservative Counterarguments

Critics note Cubism never completely abandoned reality: fragmented forms still suggest three-dimensional existence, the semiotic system retaining traces of the original world.
