Insights into Agricultural Technology

Building a Sustainable Future in Agriculture

Synergistic Effects of Environmentally Friendly Pesticides, Microbiome, and Smart Delivery Systems

With the continuous growth of the global population, the demand for food is also rising. To meet this demand, agricultural production must continuously improve efficiency and yield. However, traditional agricultural practices, especially the extensive use of chemical pesticides, have had severe negative impacts on the environment, including soil degradation, water pollution, and loss of biodiversity. Therefore, the development and application of environmentally friendly pesticides, combined with the protection and utilization of the microbiome, and the use of smart pesticide delivery systems, are crucial for achieving sustainable agricultural development.

1. Development and Application of Environmentally Friendly Pesticides

Environmentally friendly pesticides, also known as biopesticides or green pesticides, are those that have minimal impact on the environment, low toxicity to non-target organisms, are easily degradable, and do not persist in the environment. These pesticides are primarily derived from natural sources, such as plant extracts, microorganisms, and animal toxins, and are developed and utilized through biotechnological means. Compared to traditional chemical pesticides, environmentally friendly pesticides have the following characteristics:

  • Low Toxicity: Lower toxicity to humans and non-target organisms.
  • High Selectivity: Target specific pests or pathogens, reducing impacts on other organisms.
  • Easy Degradation: Easily decomposed in the natural environment, avoiding long-term pollution.
  • Ecosystem Balance: Help maintain ecosystem balance and promote biodiversity.

The development of environmentally friendly pesticides involves multiple disciplines, including biology, chemistry, and agricultural science. Key development technologies include:

  • Biotechnology: Using genetic engineering, cell engineering, and other techniques to develop microorganisms or plant extracts with specific functions. For example, gene editing technology can enhance plant disease resistance, reducing reliance on pesticides.
  • Natural Product Extraction: Extracting biologically active compounds from plants, animals, and microorganisms, such as azadirachtin from the neem tree, which has excellent insecticidal properties.
  • Microbial Formulations: Using beneficial microorganisms (such as bacteria, fungi, and viruses) to control pests and pathogens. For example, Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) is a widely used biopesticide that is highly effective against various pests.
  • Nanotechnology: Using nanomaterials to improve the stability and targeting of pesticides, reducing usage and enhancing effectiveness.

Although environmentally friendly pesticides show great potential in theoretical and laboratory studies, they still face challenges in practical applications, such as cost issues, stability of effects, market acceptance, and policy support. Future research directions may include interdisciplinary collaboration, intelligent applications, and the establishment of policies and market mechanisms.

2. Interactions Between Pesticides and the Microbiome

The microbiome, the community of microorganisms living in a specific environment, plays a crucial role in maintaining ecosystem balance and promoting plant growth. The widespread use of pesticides not only has profound impacts on the environment and human health but also has complex effects on soil and plant microbiomes.

  • Reduction in Microbial Diversity: The use of pesticides can significantly reduce the diversity of soil and rhizosphere microorganisms, altering microbial community structures. This reduction in diversity can weaken the soil's self-repair capabilities and the plant's disease resistance.
  • Changes in Microbial Function: The microbiome plays a key role in soil nutrient cycling, plant growth promotion, and disease control. Pesticide use can interfere with these functions, such as inhibiting the activity of nitrogen-fixing bacteria, affecting the nitrogen cycle in the soil.
  • Emergence of Resistant Microorganisms: Long-term pesticide use can lead to the emergence of resistant microorganisms that can tolerate high concentrations of pesticides, gaining a dominant position in the environment. This reduces the effectiveness of pesticides and poses threats to ecosystems and human health.

The impacts of the microbiome on pesticides include:

  • Degradation: Some microorganisms in the microbiome have the ability to degrade pesticides, converting them into harmless or less toxic substances through biotransformation processes, reducing pesticide residues in the environment.
  • Bioremediation: The microbiome plays a significant role in bioremediation, accelerating the recovery of contaminated soils and water bodies by introducing specific microorganisms or enhancing the activity of existing microorganisms.
  • Synergistic Effects: There are complex interactions between the microbiome and plants, with some beneficial microorganisms enhancing plant tolerance to pesticides, reducing the negative impacts of pesticides on plants.

Reasonable use of pesticides, protection, and utilization of the microbiome's functions are essential for maintaining ecological balance, promoting sustainable agricultural development, and protecting human health. Management strategies include reducing pesticide use, developing selective pesticides, microbial remediation technologies, and establishing monitoring and evaluation systems.

3. Smart Pesticide Delivery Systems

Smart pesticide delivery systems are a new type of pesticide application technology that integrates modern information technology, biotechnology, and materials science. The core of these systems lies in achieving precise application of pesticides through accurate positioning, intelligent control, and efficient transmission, thereby reducing pesticide usage, improving control effectiveness, and minimizing environmental impact.

  • Precision Positioning: Using GPS, remote sensing, and drones to collect high-precision geographic information of farmland, determining the specific locations and extent of pest and disease infestations.
  • Intelligent Control: Connecting sensors, controllers, and actuators through the Internet of Things (IoT) to achieve real-time monitoring and intelligent control of the pesticide application process.
