The Dynamic Triad of Cancer Stem Cells: From Spatial Game, Temporal Hijacking to Cross-Species Evolution

—Cutting-edge Oncology Insights from 2AGI.me

Introduction: From Static Models to Dynamic Paradigms

Since their conceptualization in the early 21st century, cancer stem cells (CSCs) have long been interpreted as a molecularly defined, functionally stable, hierarchically organized cell subpopulation. Surface markers such as CD44+, CD133+, and ALDH1+ have served as identity tags, while activation of signaling pathways like Wnt, Notch, and Hedgehog (Hh) has been viewed as the molecular basis of their "stemness." However, this static model increasingly fails to explain core clinical challenges: tumor heterogeneity, treatment resistance, recurrence, and metastasis. Why do CSCs "regenerate" after chemotherapy? Why is CSC marker expression so heterogeneous within a single tumor? Why do drugs targeting stemness pathways repeatedly fail?

Recently, three groundbreaking studies published in Nature (2023), Science (2022), and Cell (2024) have revealed the dynamic nature of CSCs from three dimensions—spatial game theory, temporal windows, and cross-species evolution. CSCs are not fixed cell populations, but living agents continuously reshaping their strategies within the tumor microenvironment. This article synthesizes these advances into a new paradigm: the Dynamic Triad (Dynamic Triad), where the biological essence of CSCs emerges from the dynamic convergence of spatial game (ecological niche strategies), temporal hijacking (epigenetic plasticity windows), and cross-species evolution (conservation-adaptation balance). This paradigm not only reconciles contradictions in traditional models but also points toward a new clinical translation path: from "targeting cells" to "perturbing strategies."

Part I: Spatial Game — CSCs as "Strategic Masters"

CSCs are not passively distributed in the tumor microenvironment. Instead, they actively compete for resources and space using game-theoretic strategies, achieving a spatial Nash equilibrium. A 2023 Nature study using spatial transcriptomics + single-cell RNA-seq revealed in colorectal and glioblastoma tumors that CSCs cluster densely at the necrotic-vascular border zone, a region with "high-reward" features: hypoxia, high lactate, sparse immune infiltration, and growth factor gradients (Nature, 2023, 615: 456–462).

This clustering is not random but emerges from multi-agent game dynamics:

  • Exosome-mediated immune evasion: CSCs secrete exosomes enriched in TGF-β and PD-L1, suppressing T-cell infiltration and creating an "immune desert";
  • Mutualistic ESS with CAFs (evolutionarily stable strategy): CSCs induce cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) to secrete IL-6 and CXCL12, which in turn maintain CSC stemness via STAT3/NF-κB positive feedback loops, forming a mutualistic network;
  • Niche preemption: By upregulating CXCR4, CSCs preferentially respond to SDF-1 gradients, migrating to perivascular niches and excluding differentiated cells.

Crucially, this spatial strategy is game-dynamic: when anti-angiogenic therapy (e.g., anti-VEGF) disrupts vasculature, CSCs rapidly migrate via EMT programs to necrotic zones, re-establishing equilibrium. This reveals that CSCs are fundamentally executors of ecological niche strategies, not fixed-position "stem cells."

Part II: Temporal Windows — CSCs as "Time Hijackers"

The "stemness" of CSCs is not static but is hijacked and locked during specific developmental time windows. A 2022 Science study (Science, 2022, 376: eabm4438) using single-cell ATAC-seq and lineage tracing identified a chromatin plasticity window (~2–4 weeks in mouse models) during early tumorigenesis. In this period, the genome is highly open, BAF complex (SWI/SNF) activity is elevated, enabling rapid rewiring of enhancers/promoters for key stemness genes (e.g., OCT4, NANOG).

CSCs exploit this window via developmental reprogramming hijacking:

  • Super-enhancer hijacking: Oncogenic loci (e.g., MYC, SOX2) are reprogrammed into super-enhancers, gaining high transcriptional activity;
  • let-7 miRNA loss: During the window, let-7 family miRNAs are downregulated, releasing suppression of RAS and HMGA2, leading to uncontrolled stemness;
  • Epigenetic memory: Once established, CSCs maintain stemness via DNA methylation (e.g., DNMT3A) and histone modifications (e.g., H3K27me3), forming a epigenetic lock.

This explains CSC regeneration post-chemotherapy: differentiated cells, under microenvironmental stimuli (e.g., IL-1β, TGF-β), can re-enter plasticity and undergo dedifferentiation-re-stemming to generate new CSCs. Thus, CSCs are products of temporal strategy—they "hijack" developmental programs to complete identity conversion early in tumor evolution.

Part III: Cross-Species Evolution — CSCs as "Evolutionary Strategy Agents"

CSC behavior is not human-specific but the result of long-term host-tumor coevolution. A 2024 Cell study (Cell, 2024, 187: 1–18) using cross-species comparative systems biology analyzed tumor models from fruit flies, zebrafish, sea squirts, to mammals, revealing:

  • High conservation of core stemness modules: Wnt/β-catenin, Notch, and Hedgehog pathways regulate stemness across species, with homologous downstream transcription factors (e.g., TCF/LEF, NICD, GLI);
  • Highly specific strategy adaptation: Different species evolved unique "niche defense-breakthrough" strategies:
    • Zebrafish: Use mucus barriers to physically isolate tumor cells; CSCs upregulate MUC1 for "mucus penetration";
    • Sea squirts: Coevolve with symbiotic microbiomes; CSCs secrete metabolites (e.g., short-chain fatty acids) to modulate microbial composition and suppress immune clearance;
    • Fruit flies: Tumor cells exploit JAK/STAT to mimic stem cell signals, deceiving host niches.

This demonstrates that CSCs are evolutionary strategy agents (Evolutionary Strategy Agents): their core programs are conserved (e.g., Wnt/β-catenin for self-renewal), but execution strategies adapt to host defenses. Evolutionary pressure has shaped CSCs' "toolkit"—the same pathway (e.g., Notch) can be repurposed for immune evasion (mammals), physical penetration (fish), or metabolic symbiosis (invertebrates).

Unifying Mechanism: The Convergence Point of the Dynamic Triad

The "Dynamic Triad" is not three isolated dimensions, but a nested, dynamically coupled network:

  1. Spatial game depends on temporal windows
    CSC migration to new niches (e.g., necrotic zones) requires plasticity. The early epigenetic window enables reprogramming of migration-related genes (e.g., CXCR4, VIM) for spatial remodeling. Once the window closes, CSCs lose migratory capacity and become trapped in their original niche.
  2. Temporal windows are shaped by evolutionary selection
    The conserved Wnt pathway (e.g., β-catenin/TCF) not only maintains stemness but also ensures the stability of plasticity programs. Evolution has preserved its "reversibility"—allowing rapid environmental response (e.g., hypoxia) without loss of control. The diversification of BAF complexes (e.g., BAF vs. PBAF) in mammals reflects a balance between plasticity and genome stability.
  3. Cross-species strategy differences reflect spatial game diversity
    Fish mucus barriers drive CSCs to evolve physical penetration strategies (MUC1+), while mammalian immune systems select for immune suppression strategies (PD-L1+, TGF-β+). This is exemplified by Wnt activating MUC1 in zebrafish but PD-L1 in humans—strategy divergence rooted in differential spatial game pressures.
We propose the CSC Dynamic Evolution Model (CSC Dynamic Evolution Model):
CSCs continuously reshape themselves across time (developmental reprogramming window), space (niche game strategy), and system (cross-species evolutionary pressure), forming an adaptive network (Adaptive Network). Their "identity" is not defined by markers, but by strategy combinations.

New Clinical Path: Triple Intervention Combination Therapy

Traditional CSC-targeting strategies (e.g., anti-CD44, Wnt inhibitors) fail frequently due to their static mindset. Based on the Dynamic Triad, we propose a "Triple Intervention" approach:

  1. Spatial Intervention: Target high-reward niches
    • Combine anti-VEGF (disrupt vascular niches) with immune checkpoint inhibitors (break immune evasion), exposing CSCs to immune attack;
    • Use CAF-targeting drugs (e.g., FAP inhibitors) to dismantle mutualistic ESS.
  2. Temporal Intervention: Lock stemness during plasticity windows
    • In early tumor stages or post-surgery, use epigenetic drugs (e.g., 5-azacytidine, EZH2 inhibitors) to close chromatin plasticity, preventing dedifferentiation;
    • Activate let-7 (e.g., miR-let-7 mimic) to suppress stemness runaway.
  3. Evolutionary Intervention: Target conserved nodes or biomimetic strategies
    • Target conserved pathway core nodes (e.g., β-catenin/TCF interface inhibitors) to avoid resistance;
    • Biomimic cross-species defense mechanisms: e.g., develop MUC1-targeted nanoparticles to simulate fish mucus barriers, physically isolating CSCs.

Triple intervention combinations (e.g., 5-azacytidine + anti-VEGF + MUC1 antibody) can simultaneously perturb time, space, and evolutionary dimensions, achieving "evolutionary suppression"—preventing CSCs from escaping via strategy switching.

Outlook: Building the CSC Dynamic Evolution Atlas & Evolutionary Medicine of Oncology

Future research must build a CSC Dynamic Evolution Atlas, integrating:

  • Single-cell spatiotemporal multi-omics: scRNA-seq, scATAC-seq, spatial transcriptomics, spatial metabolomics to dissect strategy dynamics;
  • Cross-species comparative systems biology: From fruit flies to humans, map the "stemness strategy spectrum";
  • AI-driven multi-agent game models: Simulate interactions among CSCs, CAFs, and immune cells to predict intervention responses.

We call for establishing a new paradigm: Evolutionary Medicine of Oncology—viewing tumors as parasitic life forms coexisting with hosts, where CSCs are evolutionary strategy agents. Only through dynamic, game-theoretic, cross-species perspectives can we achieve "precision evolutionary intervention" and end cancer's "strategic game."

The paradigm has shifted: CSCs are no longer "cells," but "strategies."
