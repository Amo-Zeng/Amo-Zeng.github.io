From the Basement to the Digital Square: The Socialization of Psychoanalysis, the Other, and the Technological Turn

From the Basement to the Digital Square: The Socialization of Psychoanalysis

Psychoanalysis, a discipline born at the end of the 19th century that delves into the mysteries of the human mind, has undergone a century of transformation. From Freud's dimly lit "basement" office in Vienna to the digital "squares" of the 21st century, psychoanalysis has not only extended its theories and become socialized but also faced deep challenges related to the other and a new turn in its dialogue with technology. This historical process is both a natural outcome of the development of psychoanalytic theory itself and a profound reflection of social and cultural changes since the 20th century.

I. From the "Basement" to the "Square": The Socialization of Psychoanalysis

Psychoanalysis was born with a strong "basement" atmosphere. Freud's office in Vienna was like a private secret space where he listened to the unspeakable secrets of his patients' inner depths, revealing repressed desires and the roots of their pain. Early psychoanalysis was a private conversation behind closed doors. Patients lay on a couch while the analyst, using techniques such as free association and dream interpretation, explored their inner worlds. This form itself symbolized the origin of psychoanalysis from the "basement," a private healing art focused on deep personal secrets.

However, with the passage of time, psychoanalysis moved from the "basement" to the "square." It no longer remained confined to the therapist's office but began to influence various aspects of social life. This process was driven by profound social and cultural backgrounds:

  1. Theory Expansion: Psychoanalytic theories expanded from explaining individual psychopathology to exploring broader human psychological phenomena such as the Oedipus complex, superego, and personality structures. These theories provided new perspectives for understanding human behavior and social and cultural phenomena.
  2. Mass Communication: The advent of mass media in the early 20th century allowed psychoanalytic theories to be disseminated on a wider scale. Freud's works were translated into multiple languages, influencing literature, art, film, and other fields. Psychoanalysis ceased to be the exclusive domain of a few professionals and became part of popular culture.
  3. Social Demand: Social changes such as the two World Wars, industrialization, and the women's liberation movement led people to focus on their mental health and psycho-spiritual needs. Psychoanalysis, as a method for understanding and solving psychological issues, gradually gained acceptance and application in society.

However, the "squareization" of psychoanalysis also brought some reflections: the simplification of theories, the generalization of treatments, and the risks of commercialization. Nevertheless, the journey from the "basement" to the "square" reflects the deepening and broadening of human understanding of their own mental worlds.

II. Beyond the Mirror: The Other in the Psychoanalytic Movement

In the psychoanalytic theoretical system, the "mirror" concept is not only a stage of psychological development but also the starting point for understanding self-other relationships. However, beyond the "mirror," the psychoanalytic movement has always faced a more complex issue: the other (Autre). The other is not just the "others" in our daily lives but a more elusive, heterogeneous existence. It can be external and specific, or internal and a manifestation of symbolic order.

1. Freud: The Other as the Source of Repression

In Freud's theory, the process of individual socialization is one of continuous repression of the id and desires, and the source of this repression is the external social norms and moral codes, i.e., the other. Freud believed that humans are inherently driven by the "id," filled with primal desires and impulses. However, the symbolic order of society, through the "superego," suppresses these desires, forcing individuals to act according to social norms. In this process, the other, as a concrete embodiment of social norms, becomes an alien power suppressing individual freedom.

2. Lacan: The Other as the Presence of Symbolic Order

Lacan further developed Freud's theory, emphasizing the intersubjective dimension of the other. He argued that individuals do not have a stable self by birth but gradually construct their identity through interaction with the "other." Here, the "other" is not just specific individuals but also a manifestation of the symbolic order. Language and social norms form a vast network, the symbolic order. Individuals from birth are entangled in this network, and in this process, they are given certain identities and statuses. In other words, individuals are "spoken" by the symbolic order rather than speaking for themselves. In this sense, the other is not external to the individual but internalized within the individual, becoming part of the individual's unconscious.

3. Contemporary Implications of the Other

In contemporary society, the issue of the other remains a pressing challenge. The acceleration of globalization has facilitated exchanges and collisions between different cultures, but it has also intensified anxieties about cultural identities and crises of recognition. How should we face the differences with the other? How can we respect other cultures while maintaining the independence of our own culture? These questions require in-depth discussions from a psychoanalytic perspective. Additionally, technological developments, especially emerging technologies such as artificial intelligence and virtual reality, have triggered a series of reflections on the other. As machines increasingly acquire "anthropomorphic" characteristics, can they be considered a kind of other in a certain sense? How should the relationship between us and machines be defined?

III. The Interpretation of Dreams and the Machine of Desire: The Technological Turn in Psychoanalysis

With the rapid development of technology, psychoanalysis is facing new challenges and opportunities. In this era where technology increasingly permeates human life, how psychoanalysis dialogues with technology and how it leverages technological power to expand its theoretical boundaries become issues worthy of in-depth discussion.

1. The Interpretation of Dreams: From Text to Data

Freud's "The Interpretation of Dreams" revealed the logic of unconscious desires. However, the traditional method of dream interpretation relies on the analyst's interpretation of the dream text, limiting the universality and efficiency of psychoanalysis due to its subjectivity and individual differences. With the development of artificial intelligence and big data technology, dream interpretation is transitioning from "text analysis" to "data analysis." By collecting, organizing, and analyzing large amounts of dream data, a more objective and precise dream database can be established, and deeper unconscious patterns can be discovered. For example, using natural language processing technology, emotional analysis and theme extraction can be performed on dream texts to identify common dream themes and symbolic elements; using machine learning algorithms, cluster analysis can be conducted on dream data to discover patterns of dreams and latent psychological characteristics in different groups.

2. The Machine of Desire: From Instinct to Algorithm

Lacan's concept of the "machine of desire" reveals that desire is not a simple instinctual drive but a complex mechanism mediated by language and social structures. In the digital era, the operational logic of the machine of desire is being reshaped by algorithms. Social media, recommendation algorithms, and virtual reality technologies are profoundly influencing the structure and fulfillment of human desires. Algorithms, by analyzing user data, can accurately predict users' interests and preferences and provide personalized content and services, continuously stimulating and guiding users' desires. In this process, algorithms are no longer mere tools for fulfilling desires but have become producers and manipulators of desires.

3. The Technological Turn in Psychoanalysis: Challenges and Opportunities

The technological turn in psychoanalysis presents both new challenges and opportunities:

  • Challenges: Ethical risks (e.g., privacy protection), dissolution of subjectivity (e.g., over-reliance on technology), theoretical dilemmas (e.g., the essence of the unconscious).
  • Opportunities: Expanding research areas (e.g., neuroscience, cognitive science), enhancing diagnostic efficiency (e.g., virtual reality therapy), promoting theoretical innovation (e.g., data-driven psychoanalysis).

Conclusion

From Freud's "basement" to Lacan's "mirror," and then to the "machine of desire" in the digital age, the psychoanalytic movement, over more than a century, has continuously engaged in dialogue with society, the other, and technology, expanding its theoretical boundaries. In this era where technology and humanities intersect, the technological turn in psychoanalysis is both an inevitable trend of the times and an intrinsic requirement of its own development. While embracing technology, we must maintain profound reflections on human subjectivity and ethical values, thus constructing a more comprehensive and humanistic psychoanalytic theoretical system to guide the mental health of humanity.
