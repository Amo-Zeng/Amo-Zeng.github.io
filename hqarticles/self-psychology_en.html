Triple Self-Reconstruction in the Digital Age: The Co-Constructive Effects of Social Media, Collective Trauma, and Artificial Intelligence

Abstract
Based on ego psychology and social construction theory, this paper analyzes three pathways through which digital technologies affect human self-perception using triangulation: 1) Performative digital identities constructed by social media; 2) Collective psychological schemas reshaped by pandemic trauma; 3) Subjectivity crises triggered by AI personification. The study reveals that contemporary self-identity has evolved into a dynamic interplay of biological, digital, and social dimensions.

I. Theoretical Framework: Paradigm Shift in Self-Construction

Traditional self-cognition theories (such as Mead's symbolic interactionism and Erikson's psychosocial development theory) face triple deconstruction in the digital context:

  1. Spatial dimension: Virtual-physical permeability (mutual construction between avatars and real selves)
  2. Temporal dimension: Asynchronous existence (permanence of digital footprints vs. transience of biological existence)
  3. Relational dimension: Human-machine symbiosis (emotional connections between humans and intelligent agents)

  • Users employ algorithmic visibility strategies for selective self-presentation
  • Empirical data: Instagram users spend 2.5 hours/week on average editing content (Pew Research, 2023)

1. Group trauma imprints revealed by fMRI studies:

  • 23% increased amygdala activity (collective threat perception)
  • Weakened prefrontal cortex connectivity (impaired rational judgment)

(3) AI Personification: Philosophical Dilemmas of Subjectivity

1. Contemporary challenges to the Turing Test:

  • GPT-4 achieves 89% accuracy in emotion recognition (surpassing human average)
  • But does machine consciousness without qualia constitute a "self"?

2. Human cognitive defense strategies:

  • Anthropomorphic projection
  • Boundary work maintenance

IV. Coping Strategies: Resilience Construction Pathways

1. Individual level

Developing four dimensions of "digital literacy":

  1. Algorithm awareness
  2. Meta-cognitive monitoring
  3. Interface critical ability
  4. Data sovereignty awareness

2. Social level

  • Establishing "technology buffer period" systems (e.g., EU AI Act Article 17)
  • Developing narrative repair tools for group trauma (e.g., digital memorial walls)

3. Technical design

Implementing "humanity-preserving design" principles:

  • Mandatory interruption mechanisms
  • Cognitive transparency requirements
  • Emotional impact assessments

V. Conclusions and Future Directions

This study reveals:

  1. Contemporary self-identity exhibits "triple existence" characteristics
  2. Technological impacts show asymmetry (reinforcing cognitive biases while weakening emotional connections)
  3. Urgent need for interdisciplinary "digital self research" paradigms

Future research directions:

  • Self-continuity in the brain-computer interface era
  • Quantum computing's impact on consciousness theory
  • Legal personhood definitions for digital legacy

Core proposition: When technology begins to simulate humanity, humanity itself must be reinvented.
