From "Metaverse" to "Meta-Diffusion": Information and Cultural Dissemination in Virtual Spaces

The "metaverse," as a buzzword in the tech field in recent years, paints a picture of a virtual space parallel to the real world, carrying unlimited imagination about the future form of the internet. However, technological progress often transcends the boundaries of concepts. As we attempt to build the metaverse as a closed, immersive virtual world, the flow of information and culture is breaking this imagination, trending towards a more open and fragmented "meta-diffusion." This article will explore the new paradigms of information and cultural dissemination in virtual spaces from three dimensions: virtual identities and community building, cross-platform diffusion and content variation, and the bidirectional influence of virtual and real worlds.

I. Virtual Identities and Community Building: Decentralized Self-Expression and Cultural Identity

In the metaverse, users can create virtual identities to engage in socializing, entertainment, creation, and other activities with a new appearance. This virtual identity gives users greater freedom to break through the limitations of real life and explore diverse self-expression. However, unlike traditional social networks, virtual identities in the metaverse tend to be more decentralized. Users no longer rely on a single platform but can freely switch between different platforms, shaping different virtual images and participating in different communities.

This decentralized model of virtual identity construction has given rise to a new type of community culture. Unlike real-life communities based on geography, kinship, profession, etc., metaverse communities are more often formed around common interests, values, and virtual experiences. For example, gaming communities, AI art creation communities, and virtual currency investor communities. These communities transcend geographical boundaries in virtual spaces, forming a new way of cultural identity and dissemination.

II. Cross-Platform Diffusion and Content Variation: Fission Dissemination of Information and Culture

The openness and interoperability of the metaverse provide the possibility for cross-platform diffusion of information and culture. Different virtual platforms are not isolated but connected and interacted through various technical means. Users can migrate virtual assets, creative content, social relationships, etc., from one platform to another, thereby achieving the sharing and dissemination of information and culture.

However, cross-platform diffusion is not a simple copy-paste but accompanied by a complex process of variation. Different platforms have different user groups, technical architectures, and cultural atmospheres, which cause information and culture to undergo adaptive changes during dissemination, generating new meanings and values. For example, a meme culture popular on one gaming platform, when propagated to another social platform, may be given new interpretations and expressions, forming a new cultural phenomenon.

III. The Bidirectional Influence of Virtual and Real: A Virtual-Real Integrated Cultural Landscape

The metaverse is not a utopia completely detached from reality but maintains close interaction and connection with the real world. The dissemination of information and culture in virtual spaces not only affects the virtual world itself but also has profound implications for the real world, forming a virtual-real integrated cultural landscape.

On one hand, information and culture in virtual spaces provide new inspiration and creativity for real life. For instance, virtual idols, virtual exhibitions, and virtual concerts have become important cultural consumption forms in real life, enriching people's cultural experiences. On the other hand, social issues, values, and cultural traditions in the real world are also reflected and spread in virtual spaces, influencing the cultural construction of the virtual world.

IV. Social Media Algorithms: Accelerator or Filter for Dissemination?

The rise of social media platforms marks a revolutionary transformation in human communication patterns. In this process, algorithms are no longer just technical tools for content distribution; they actually have a revolutionary impact on information flow and social communication patterns. Social media algorithms, as the nervous system of the digital age, play a complex and crucial role, serving as both accelerators for information dissemination and effective filters. Exploring this dual identity not only helps to deeply understand the manipulation mechanism of algorithms on information dissemination but also reveals their profound impact on social structures and human cognition.

V. Algorithmic Bias and Dissemination Inequality

The core purpose of social media algorithms is to ensure that users receive information matching their interests and behaviors through automated analysis and push. However, behind this seemingly neutral technical operation lies profound algorithmic bias. First, algorithm design is rooted in specific technical cultures and value orientations. For example, some platforms may be more inclined to push high-click-rate content while ignoring important but less attention-grabbing information. This bias leads to a "Matthew effect" in the information ecosystem: popular content becomes even more prevalent, while marginal and niche content is further marginalized.

Additionally, the reliance of algorithms on user profiles exacerbates this bias. Users' historical behavior, interest preferences, and social relationships are interpreted by algorithms as decisive factors, thus forming "information cocoons" or "echo chamber effects." In this environment, users continuously receive information consistent with their established viewpoints, further deepening the rigidity of their existing cognition. More seriously, this bias is not just a technical issue; it actually exacerbates social information inequality. Institutions and individuals with more resources can further expand their influence through algorithm optimization strategies, while ordinary users increasingly rely on algorithm pushes, gradually losing the ability to actively explore information.

VI. User Attention Competition and the Tipping Point of Information Dissemination

Social media algorithms continuously attract user attention through precise recommendations, personalized pushes, and dynamic content updates. This design is essentially based on a deep understanding of human psychology and behavior, using emotional resonance, short-term stimulation, and instant feedback mechanisms to牢牢抓住用户的关注. This design model seems to improve information dissemination efficiency, but it may actually bring long-term negative effects.

The first is the tipping point issue of information dissemination. Although social media algorithms can drive information dissemination rapidly, the underlying dissemination mechanism is often nonlinear. Once information breaks through a certain tipping point, it can explode within a short time, forming so-called "viral dissemination." However, this dissemination pattern does not necessarily reflect the actual value and importance of the information. Instead, it is more dependent on the algorithm's push strategy and user interaction behavior. This uncertainty and randomness of information dissemination make it difficult to effectively ensure content quality on social media platforms.

Moreover, the manipulation of user attention actually weakens individuals' information processing capabilities. In the face of massive information, users often rely on algorithm recommendations and give up opportunities for independent selection and in-depth thinking. This dependency not only weakens individuals' judgment but may also further exacerbate the loss of information authenticity and reliability. Algorithm-pushed information may be biased, misleading, or even false, and users in such an environment are prone to forming a passive, one-sided cognitive pattern.

VII. Algorithm Governance and Information Ecosystem

Facing the challenges brought by algorithms, how to effectively govern and reconstruct a healthy, diverse, and balanced information ecosystem has become an urgent issue. The core of algorithm governance lies in ensuring that the design, deployment, and use of algorithms conform to ethical and social value standards. First, algorithms should be transparent, enabling users to understand the push rules and the underlying logic and decision-making basis. This transparency is not just a technical issue but also a respect for users' right to know and participate.

Secondly, the fairness of algorithms should be ensured. This means that algorithms should not serve solely commercial interests but should consider public interests and social values. For example, in news push, algorithms should avoid excessive bias towards certain viewpoints and strive to provide diverse information sources. Additionally, in response to the "information cocoon" phenomenon caused by algorithms, platforms should design corresponding mechanisms to encourage users to encounter information different from their established cognition, thereby promoting the formation of social consensus and balanced cognitive development.

The construction of an information ecosystem also requires the joint participation of platforms, users, and regulatory bodies. Platforms should shoulder social responsibilities, not only providing information services to users but also maintaining information diversity and authenticity through technical means and mechanism design. Users need to enhance media literacy and strengthen critical thinking skills regarding algorithm pushes, avoiding complete reliance on a single platform or algorithm. Regulatory bodies should formulate relevant regulations and policies to ensure the fairness and transparency of algorithms and prevent their abuse.

VIII. From Virus to Meme: Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue on Biological and Cultural Dissemination

The dissemination mechanisms of biological viruses and cultural memes show striking similarities, providing a new perspective for understanding information dissemination in complex systems. In the field of biology, the transmission of viruses depends on host contact and infection, with its speed and breadth influenced by various factors such as the virus's infectivity, the host's immunity, and environmental conditions. Similarly, the spread of cultural memes also relies on human contact and communication, with its speed and scope similarly affected by the meme's appeal, the recipient's cultural background, and the transmission channel.

The common features of these two dissemination mechanisms mainly include the following aspects: Firstly, both have high infectivity or appeal, capable of rapidly spreading in a short time. Secondly, the dissemination process exhibits exponential growth characteristics, with slow initial growth but explosive growth once a certain tipping point is broken. Thirdly, both are influenced by group characteristics, such as population density and social network structure. Additionally, the dissemination of viruses and memes has the possibility of mutation and evolution, making their dissemination paths and final impacts more complex and diverse.

By comparing the dissemination mechanisms in-depth, we can find many similarities in the dissemination dynamics of biological viruses and cultural memes. For example, both exhibit the concept of "critical mass," where dissemination reaching a certain scale triggers broader diffusion. At the same time, both are influenced by "super-spreaders," individuals or nodes playing key roles in the dissemination process, significantly accelerating the speed of dissemination. However, despite these similarities, we cannot ignore the fundamental differences between the two. The dissemination of biological viruses involves complex processes within biological organisms, while the spread of cultural memes relies more on human cognition and behavior patterns. This difference reminds us to be cautious when applying analogical research but also provides rich materials and ideas for interdisciplinary research.

IX. Group Immunity and Cultural Immunity

Group immunity and cultural immunity are two seemingly different yet inherently related concepts, and their comparative study provides a new theoretical framework for addressing viral dissemination. In biology, group immunity refers to a sufficient proportion of individuals in a population being immune to a certain pathogen, making it difficult for the pathogen to spread within the population. This concept was first applied in vaccination strategies, aiming to control the spread of infectious diseases by enhancing group immunity. Similarly, cultural immunity can be understood as a population's resistance or adaptability to certain cultural memes. This immunity may stem from various factors such as the population's cultural background, values, and education level.

In terms of countermeasures, enhancing group immunity and cultural immunity share many similarities. First, both emphasize wide dissemination and coverage. In biomedical fields, this is reflected in the widespread vaccination; in cultural fields, it may be manifested in diverse cultural education and socialization processes. Second, both strategies focus on the relationship between individuals and the group. In biology, an individual's immunity directly affects the overall immunity level of the group; in cultural dissemination, an individual's cultural literacy and critical thinking ability impact the group's resistance to harmful memes.

However, enhancing cultural immunity might be more complex than enhancing group immunity. This is because the diversity and variability of cultural memes far exceed those of biological viruses. An effective strategy to enhance cultural immunity needs to consider this diversity, thereby developing more flexible and comprehensive countermeasures.
