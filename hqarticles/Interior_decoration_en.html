From Human Monologue to Ecological Dialogue: Paradigm Shift and Resilience Reconstruction in Interior Design

When rooms begin to read minds, when pets, plants, and AI become empathic subjects, when redundant design constructs resilient barriers—interior design is undergoing a systemic revolution from human-centric to ecological symbiosis.

Introduction: The Triad of Paradigm Shift

Traditional interior design has long been confined to a human-centric paradigm: space is a container for human activity, beauty is an expression of human aesthetics, and function is the fulfillment of human needs. However, three transformative forces are dismantling this paradigm: emotional intelligence technology enables spaces to sense and respond to human emotions; posthumanist ethics incorporates non-human subjects into design considerations; resilience design philosophy addresses uncertainty through strategic redundancy. These are not isolated phenomena but different manifestations of the same paradigm shift—from a static, monologic, control-oriented view of space to a dynamic, dialogic, symbiosis-oriented ecological interface.

This article argues that the core of this transformation is a paradigm shift from human monologue to ecological dialogue, with its deep logic rooted in the integration of cybernetics, ecological psychology, and posthumanist philosophy. We will explore how this shift reshapes the fundamental assumptions, practical methods, and value systems of interior design through three dimensions: technological integration, ethical reconstruction, and resilience enhancement.

I. Technological Integration: Intelligent Systems from Response to Dialogue

Emotionally intelligent spaces and non-human-centric design share the same technological foundation: a multimodal perception-analysis-response loop. However, current implementations have key limitations: they remain within the response paradigm rather than the dialogue paradigm.

Emotionally intelligent spaces use biosensors to capture human emotional data and trigger environmental adjustments. Non-human design expands the objects of perception: cat behavioral patterns, plant bioelectrical signals, microbial activity data. Yet both face the risk of data colonialism—systems collect data and intervene unilaterally rather than establishing genuine dialogue.

The breakthrough lies in the introduction of embodied cognition and cybernetics. Embodied cognition theory emphasizes that cognition occurs not only in the brain but also through the interaction between the body and the environment. This means intelligent systems should not merely "read" emotions or behaviors but facilitate cross-species embodied dialogue.

For example, Tokyo's "Human-Cat Coexistence House" not only provides cat walkways but also uses pressure sensors to record feline movement patterns and dynamically adjust spatial layouts (e.g., movable resting platforms), achieving spatial negotiation between humans and cats. Berlin's "Mycelium Wall Kitchen" uses a visual interface to display microbial activity, making "dialogue" between humans and microorganisms possible.

Emotionally intelligent systems can adopt this approach: when anxiety is detected, instead of automatically initiating a "calming program," they offer multiple choices via an AR interface ("adjust lighting," "suggest meditation," "maintain status quo"), transforming unidirectional response into bidirectional dialogue. This requires AI to evolve from black-box algorithms to embodied agents (e.g., Samsung's Ballie), participating in household interactions through physical presence.

The vision of technological integration is to build a multi-species dialogue platform: humans, animals, plants, AI, and microorganisms negotiate through data sharing and environmental regulation. This is not only a technical challenge but also a shift in design philosophy—from control to dialogue.

II. Ethical Reconstruction: Power Order from Centricity to Symbiosis

When technology enables multi-species dialogue, ethical issues emerge: Who is qualified to participate in the dialogue? How are resources allocated? How are conflicts resolved?

The ethical dilemmas of emotionally intelligent spaces (privacy erosion, emotional dependency) and the ethical challenges of non-human design (ceding control, value measurement) are essentially different manifestations of the same problem: how to define the agency and rights of different subjects.

Traditional design implies a human sovereignty model: humans are the only agents, while non-human entities are either tools (pets, plants), threats (microorganisms), or servants (AI). The new paradigm requires a multi-agency model—acknowledging that different subjects possess varying degrees of agency.

Posthumanist philosophers like Bruno Latour's "Actor-Network Theory" provide a framework: humans and non-humans are both "actants," collectively forming a network. In design, this means:

  • Pets are not only emotional companions but also co-users of space, their behavioral patterns shaping spatial forms;
  • Plants are not decorations but living beings, their phototropism and water needs driving environmental adjustments;
  • Microorganisms should not be eradicated but managed as "ecological partners";
  • AI should not be invisible servants but embodied mediators.

However, the multi-agency model faces a practical paradox: when system resources are limited, should human or non-human needs be prioritized? For example, an emotionally intelligent system might reduce brightness to alleviate human anxiety, but this could affect plant photosynthesis.

Resilience design provides an ethical trade-off framework: by increasing the resource pool through redundant systems, zero-sum games are reduced. For instance, energy redundancy (solar薄膜 + fuel cells) can simultaneously meet human lighting needs and plant grow-light requirements without sacrificing either.

The core of ethical reconstruction is the shift from sovereign ethics to symbiotic ethics: no longer asking "who dominates," but exploring "how to coexist." This requires designers to relinquish some control and become coordinators of the ecosystem rather than masters.

III. Resilience Enhancement: Value Shift from Efficiency to Adaptability

Resilience design (the beauty of redundancy) may seem technical, but it is essentially a value revolution: from pursuing short-term efficiency to long-term adaptability, from rejecting uncertainty to accepting and utilizing it.

Both emotionally intelligent spaces and non-human design imply a resilience诉求: emotional intelligence enhances psychological resilience through dynamic responses; non-human design enhances ecological resilience through multi-species cohabitation. However, both face system fragility: emotional intelligence relies on continuous data streams, non-human design relies on complex sensor networks. Once energy is interrupted or systems fail, these "intelligent" spaces may instantly become无效.

Therefore, resilience design is not a supplement but a foundation. It provides fault-tolerant space for emotional intelligence and non-human design:

  • Energy redundancy ensures biosensors and AI systems continue operating during extreme weather;
  • Spatial redundancy allows environments to be reconfigured according to multi-species needs;
  • Functional redundancy provides backup systems to prevent single points of failure.

But resilience design also needs guidance from emotional intelligence and non-human ethics, otherwise it risks becoming techno-fascism—e.g., sacrificing all comfort and beauty for the sake of resilience. The Dutch concept of "visible reliability" attempts to address this: transforming emergency facilities into daily aesthetic elements, such as turning water purification systems into indoor water features.

On a deeper level, ecological psychology shows that human perception of security comes not only from physical protection but also from environmental legibility and a sense of participation. Therefore, resilience systems should provide transparent feedback: when switching to backup power, a visual interface displays remaining duration; when spaces change function, all subjects (including pets) are informed in advance.

The ultimate goal of resilience enhancement is to build a learning adaptive system: using AI to analyze past disturbances (from human emotional breakdowns to extreme weather), optimize response strategies, and help all subjects develop adaptive capacities. Integrative Reflection: Cross-Cutting Issues and Core Challenges

At the intersection of the three transformative forces, common challenges emerge:

  1. Data Politics and Privacy Paradox: Emotional data, pet health data, plant physiological data, energy usage data—who owns this data? How to prevent misuse? The EU's "right to delete emotional data" is just a starting point; a multi-species data ethics framework is needed.
  2. Technological Inclusivity: Emotionally intelligent systems, multi-species interfaces, and resilience modules are costly, potentially exacerbating the "resilience gap." Open-source designs (e.g., MIT's "Open Resilience Platform") and community共享 systems (community energy banks) are potential solutions.
  3. Cultural Adaptability and Pluralistic Knowledge: Western AI models may misinterpret non-Western emotional expressions; urban design may ignore indigenous ecological knowledge. Pluralistic epistemology must be引入, allowing traditional knowledge (e.g., passive cooling techniques) to dialogue with high-tech solutions.
  4. New Aesthetic Paradigm: From "sterile minimalism" to "ecological abundance," from "hidden technology" to "visible reliability"—a symbiotic aesthetics needs to be developed, accommodating complexity, visibility, and adaptability.

Future Vision: The Ecological Symbiosis Home Interface

The future home will no longer be a human-exclusive fortress but a multi-species symbiotic interface: a resilient ecosystem that regulates emotions, allocates resources, and responds to disturbances through continuous dialogue.

Technologically, we will see resilience modules embedded with emotional intelligence: participating in multi-species dialogue during normal times and switching to life-support mode during emergencies. Ethically, multi-species participatory design will become standard: designers collaborating with human clients, veterinarians, botanists, and AI engineers. Aesthetically, ecological visibility will become the new aesthetic: displaying internal system operations (e.g., water recycling, energy flow), enhancing the sense of security and participation for all subjects.

Ultimately, the goal of this paradigm shift is not perfect control but dignified adaptability: in an uncertain world, through the integration of technology, ethics, and aesthetics, enabling all life forms to flourish their potential.

When a room begins to read minds, it should read not only the human heart but the heartbeat of the ecological network; when a home is full of redundancy, it offers not redundancy itself but the rich possibilities of life; when design transcends the human, it achieves not technological miracles but the dawn of symbiotic civilization.
