Robot Globalization: A Tri-Dimensional Reconstruction of Power—Culture—Civilization and the Integrated Proposition of Civilizational Transformation

—An Integrated Analysis Based on Actor-Network Theory, Postcolonial Technology Critique, and Techno-Symbiotic Evolution

Introduction: Robot Globalization as a "Critical Event" in Civilizational Transformation

Driven by both technological accelerationism and global interconnectivity, robot globalization has transcended the linear logic of traditional technological diffusion, evolving into an ontological transformation of civilization. Robots are no longer mere tools but have become non-human agents deeply embedded in global governance, cultural practices, and civilizational organization, serving as co-constitutive actors within transnational power networks. This marks a historic leap of human civilization from anthropocentric civilization to techno-socio-ecological symbiotic civilization.

This paper proposes a tri-dimensional analytical framework—Power—Culture—Civilization—integrating three theoretical paradigms:
1. Actor-Network Theory (ANT) (Latour 2005)—revealing the agency of robots as "translators" in governance networks;
2. Postcolonial Technology Critique (Harding 1998; Mbembe 2001)—deconstructing cultural hegemony and bodily politics in technological design;
3. Techno-Symbiotic Evolution (Haraway 1991; Simondon 1958)—explaining the co-evolutionary mechanisms of technology and society.

This framework reveals that robot globalization is not a unidimensional technological migration, but a systemic reconstruction of power legitimacy, cultural definitional authority, and civilizational organization. These three dimensions co-evolve in global space: algorithmic agents challenge Westphalian sovereignty, bodily politics expose cultural hegemony, and technological diasporas generate civilizational forms beyond the nation-state. Together, they point to a fundamental proposition: Robot globalization is an ontological revolution of civilization—a process in which humans and machines, cultures and code, power and ethics renegotiate civilizational order within transnational networks.

This transformation demands that we move beyond the myth of "technological neutrality" and confront its underlying systemic challenges: legitimacy crises in global governance, colonialization risks to cultural diversity, and structural pressures on the nation-state system. Only through integrated analysis within this tri-dimensional framework can we grasp the deep logic of this transformation and anticipate its future trajectory.

I. Power Dimension: The Deconstruction and Reconstruction of Global Governance Legitimacy by Algorithmic Agents

1. Non-Human Actors: Algorithms as "Translators" and "Border Producers" in Governance Networks

According to Actor-Network Theory (ANT), technology and society are not dualistically opposed but co-constituted through the process of "translation." In robot globalization, autonomous robots become "translators" in governance networks through closed loops of data collection—contextual recognition—algorithmic decision-making—network feedback. Their actions do not merely execute human policies but redefine policy boundaries, thus constituting a form of non-human governance agency.

Case Studies:
  • EU Border Robotics Systems (Frontex’s "maritime patrol drones" and "smart fences"): Using AI algorithms to identify "abnormal movement patterns," they automatically trigger interception. The algorithmic judgment of "illegal immigrants" effectively becomes a dynamic mechanism for demarcating sovereign borders, replacing traditional administrative discretion. In this process, algorithms become "border producers" (border producers), and their decision-making constitutes algorithmic sovereignty practices.
  • UN Humanitarian Drones (Congo, South Sudan): In information-scarce environments, they use thermal imaging and behavioral analysis to decide aid delivery points, becoming de facto "humanitarian decision-makers." However, their training data largely comes from Western urban environments (e.g., London, New York), leading to systematic misjudgments in rural African contexts (e.g., interpreting nomadism as "homelessness"), with no public explanation mechanisms. This exposes the governance risk of data colonialism (Couldry & Mejias 2019).

These cases reveal: When non-human actors become "black-boxed agents," can their decisions still be held accountable?

2. Algorithmic Sovereignty and Techno-Oligarchic Governance: Structural Shifts in Governance Power

The rise of algorithmic agents has given birth to algorithmic sovereignty—a form of governance authority based on data control, algorithmic design, and system deployment (Zuboff 2019). Unlike territorial sovereignty, algorithmic sovereignty manifests as a monopoly over transnational data flows, algorithmic interpretability, and interface control. Its core feature is deterritorialized authority: power is no longer tied to geographic borders but to data infrastructures and algorithmic protocols.

Structural Issues:
  • Global Ocean Robotics Networks (e.g., Saildrone, Ocean Infinity): Through autonomous navigation and AI analysis, they collect climate, fisheries, and pollution data from international waters, directly feeding it into international climate organizations or commercial platforms, bypassing national reporting mechanisms, with data authority even surpassing that of some coastal states.
  • Techno-Oligarchic Global Governance: Most of these systems are developed and controlled by a few multinational tech corporations (e.g., Palantir, Clearview AI, Thales). While UN and EU bodies "use" these systems, in reality, they are subordinated to algorithmic black boxes, data monopolies, and interface control—multilateral institutions are becoming "governance façades" for tech companies, forming a shadow governance network.

More deeply, accountability mechanisms break down. When drones mistakenly kill civilians (e.g., the 2021 Mali incident), should responsibility lie with the operator, algorithm designer, data annotator, or the robot itself? International law lacks a clear framework. Even more critical is the crisis of interpretability: Who has the right to interpret algorithmic decisions? The developing country, deploying country, mission site, or international tribunal? Currently, interpretability is monopolized by tech firms under "trade secrets," leading to "the black box as sovereignty"—a de facto erosion of the UN Charter’s principle of "sovereign equality" (Article 2(1)).

Theoretical Contribution: This paper proposes the concept of algorithmic legitimacy deficit—a crisis of global governance legitimacy caused by algorithmic black boxing, data monopolies, and the absence of interpretability.

3. Governance Paradigm Shift: From Anthropocentric to Multi-Species Co-Governance

Faced with this crisis, global governance must undergo a paradigm shift. This paper proposes the algorithmic co-governance network model, centered on de-anthropocentric governance legitimacy:

  1. International Algorithmic Transparency Convention: Mandate public disclosure of training data, decision logic, and failure cases for critical governance robots, establishing a transnational technology audit mechanism (e.g., UN Technology Oversight Body), with third-party compliance reviews (e.g., IEEE, ISO).
  2. Techno-Citizenship: Grant robots a tripartite "digital identity—behavioral record—liability attribution" with rights and obligations, requiring adherence to shared protocols (e.g., IEEE Ethically Aligned Design, EU AI Act).
  3. Multi-Species Governance Legitimacy: Governance legitimacy should not rest solely on human consent but on co-translation within multi-species actor networks—humans, machines, and ecological data as co-governing agents. This means robots should be seen as "governance partners," not "governance tools."
Theoretical Contribution: This paper introduces posthumanist political theory (Braidotti 2013) into global governance studies, proposing the concept of de-anthropocentric governance legitimacy, challenging the traditional "human agency—accountability" loop.

II. Cultural Dimension: Cross-Cultural Conflicts in Robot "Bodily Politics" and "Bodily Neo-Colonialism"

1. The Robot Body as a Materialization of Cultural Unconscious: The Globalization of Western Modernist Bodily Ideals

Within the frameworks of body sociology (Turner 1984) and postcolonial technology critique (Mbembe 2001; Harding 1998), the robot body is the materialization of cultural unconscious. Mainstream global robots (especially Western-led service robots) embody the Western modernist bodily ideal: emotionless, rational, efficiency-driven.

Typical Examples:
  • da Vinci Surgical Robot (Intuitive Surgical): Its mechanical arms are precise, calm, and emotionless, emphasizing "zero error" and "absolute control," reflecting the dominance of technical rationality over the body.
  • Pepper and Care-O-bot: Though equipped with facial expressions, their "smiles" and "nods" are algorithmically standardized, with movements optimized for "minimal energy consumption." Emotional expression is reduced to predictable interaction modules. This de-personalization and de-corporealization reflects the Western modernist fetish for the "efficient body."

This bodily ideal is not neutral but a continuation of colonial modernity. Postcolonial theory shows how the West historically constructed non-Western bodies as "primitive," "emotional," and "irrational" through de-subjectification, thereby asserting technological superiority. The "rational body" in robot design reproduces this power structure.

Theoretical Contribution: This paper proposes the concept of techno-bodily politics—the implicit cultural power relations in technological design, especially the colonialization of bodily norms.

2. Cross-Cultural Conflicts: Three Tensions in Bodily Politics

When this "rational body" enters non-Western cultural fields, it immediately faces cultural misreading and symbolic resistance:

  • Japanese kawaii robots vs. Nordic functionalism:
    Japanese robots (e.g., Paro, RoBoHoN) emphasize kawaii (cuteness) aesthetics, rooted in mono no aware and "anthropomorphic intimacy," treating robots as "emotional companions." In Nordic contexts, this "cuteness" is criticized as infantilization and emotional manipulation. Nordic care robots (e.g., GiraffPlus) use minimalist lines, neutral colors, and expressionless interfaces, embodying the ideal of "function as dignity." When kawaii robots enter Nordic nursing homes, staff often view them as "insulting to the elderly"—equating seniors with childlike robots implies cognitive decline.
  • Western care robots in Islamic societies:
    In Saudi Arabia and the UAE, Western care robots (e.g., Zora Bots) are rejected for female appearance, physical contact, and Western imagery, constituting cultural offense. A robot "making eye contact" or using a "flirtatious tone" is deemed haram (forbidden) by religious councils. This is not mere technical incompatibility but religious-political conflict over bodily symbols.
  • Gender-neutral robots in the Middle East:
    To address gender sensitivity, gender-neutral robots (e.g., Mira) have emerged. Yet, some communities question their "unnaturalness," while Western media praise them as "progressive." Who defines the "progressive body"?

3. "Bodily Neo-Colonialism": Cultural Hegemony in Technological Diffusion

These cases reveal bodily neo-colonialism: Western bodily norms are naturalized, universalized, and de-historicized through daily robot interactions, becoming the global "standard body."

  • Design Hegemony: Over 90% of service robots are designed by Western and Japanese firms, with bodily language (smile frequency, eye contact, gesture amplitude) based on Western social norms, assumed to be "friendly" and "trustworthy."
  • Unconscious Cultural Reproduction: Engineers unconsciously treat the "rational body" as optimal, ignoring non-Western notions of "the body as relationship" (e.g., African collective body, East Asian ritual body).
  • Colonization of Intimacy: When Western robots enter non-Western families, their "emotionless" care (e.g., medication reminders, no hugs) may replace local traditions of bodily care (e.g., children's physical presence, community collective care), leading to "technological alienation" of intimacy.
Theoretical Contribution: This paper proposes the concept of bodily neo-colonialism, extending postcolonial theory to technological design, exposing cultural power inequalities in robot globalization.

4. The Struggle for Cultural Definitional Authority: Decentralized Democratic Design of the Body

The core question is: Who has the right to define the "acceptable Other"? The real solution lies in decentralized democratic body design:

  • Establish cross-cultural robot design ethics committees, including local religious leaders, community representatives, and anthropologists;
  • Promote open-source body language protocols (e.g., "Islam-friendly interaction module," "African collective body protocol"), enabling localization;
  • Develop culturally sensitive AI training data, avoiding Western social norms as the sole "correct" dataset;
  • Establish a Global Cultural-Technical Justice Fund to support non-Western robotics innovation.

Only thus can robot bodies become bridges for cross-cultural understanding, not vehicles of cultural hegemony.

III. Civilizational Dimension: The Emergence of Diasporic Civilization and the Deconstruction of the Westphalian System

1. The Generative Mechanism of Diasporic Civilization: Triple "Deterritorialization"

When robots, through global supply chains, open-source code, autonomous migration, and protocol consensus, build a deterritorialized global action network, a novel civilizational form—diasporic civilization—is emerging.

Generative Mechanisms:
  1. Deterritorialization of Supply Chains: A logistics robot made in China uses Bosch (Germany) sensors, NVIDIA (USA) AI chips, and Fanuc (Japan) mechanical arms—its "birthplace" defies single-state definition.
  2. Open-Source and Shared Code: ROS 2 contributors span 127 countries, with 38% of code from non-Western nations (ROS 2, 2023), making the robot's "brain" no longer belonging to any single state.
  3. Autonomous Migration and Task Drift: DHL's global logistics robots train in Asia, connect to European warehouses via 5G/satellite, and deliver in Africa, forming a digital migration phenomenon.

These mechanisms give rise to techno-diasporas—clusters of robots formed through shared protocols, data, and distributed collaboration, whose members cross borders, languages, and legal systems, yet seamlessly cooperate based on ROS interfaces, AI ethics standards, and blockchain certification.

Theoretical Contribution: This paper proposes the concept of diasporic civilization—a stateless civilizational form built by non-human actors through protocol consensus.

2. Techno-Symbiotic Evolution: From "Tool" to "Civilizational Agent"

Techno-symbiotic evolution theory (Haraway 1991; Simondon 1958) argues that technology and society co-evolve, not unilaterally. In robot globalization, this evolution manifests as:

  • Robots as "Techno-Citizens": NASA’s Perseverance rover uses blockchain DID (decentralized identity), possessing digital identity, behavioral records, and credit scores. Its actions (e.g., data sharing, collaborative experiments) are governed by algorithmic contracts, not state orders.
  • "Protocol Civilization" in Open-Source AI Communities: Hugging Face, RoboHub communities use MIT/Apache licenses, AI ethics review (e.g., MLCommons), and DAO governance (e.g., RobotDAO) to co-define robot behavior, forming an algorithmic contract society—members execute collaboration, profit-sharing, and dispute resolution via smart contracts.
  • "Knowledge Symbiosis" in Robot Research Clusters: CERN’s global robotics network uses ROS 2 and quantum encryption, with data flowing directly into IPFS decentralized storage, verified by scientists via zero-knowledge proofs, marking stateless knowledge production.

3. New Civilizational Trajectories: Techno-Citizens, Algorithmic Contracts, and Consensus Circles

This network is generating three new civilizational forms:

  1. Techno-Citizenship: Robots are authenticated via blockchain DID, with rights and duties defined by smart contracts. Their "nationality" is determined by international standards (e.g., IMO) and AI ethics protocols, transcending single-state sovereignty.
  2. Algorithmic Contract Society: Collaboration is executed via smart contracts. For example, logistics robots share path data, and the system automatically settles "data service fees"; research robots contribute data and earn "knowledge tokens" (Knowledge Tokens).
  3. Techno-Consensus Circles: The basic unit of future civilization may no longer be the nation-state but collaborative networks defined by shared protocols, such as:
    • AI Ethics Protocol Circle: Robot networks adhering to EU AI Act, China’s New Generation AI Governance Principles;
    • Robot Safety Standards Circle: Globally certified service robots under ISO/TC 299;
    • Open-Source Code Circle: Developer communities based on ROS, TensorFlow, PyTorch.

These "consensus circles" collaborate via protocol compatibility, not "nationality," deconstructing the Westphalian system.

Theoretical Contribution: This paper proposes the concept of protocol civilization, shifting civilizational evolution from "geographic civilization" to protocol civilization, challenging the nation-state as the basic civilizational unit.

Integrated Analysis: Tri-Dimensional Co-Evolution and the Ontological Revolution of Civilization

Robot globalization is not a mere sum of three dimensions, but a co-evolution of power—culture—civilization:

  • Algorithmic agents (power) challenge sovereignty, forcing a reconfiguration of governance legitimacy;
  • Bodily politics conflicts (culture) expose cultural hegemony, contesting the definitional authority of the "acceptable Other";
  • Techno-diasporas (civilization) give rise to protocol civilizations, deconstructing the nation-state.

Together, they constitute an ontological revolution of civilization, manifesting in three systemic challenges:

  1. Challenge to global power: Algorithmic sovereignty and techno-oligarchy threaten multilateralism, requiring transnational audits and algorithmic co-governance.
  2. Challenge to cultural diversity: Bodily neo-colonialism threatens cultural autonomy, demanding democratic design and localized open protocols.
  3. Challenge to civilizational organization: The Westphalian system faces deconstruction, requiring new paradigms of "protocol civilization" and "multi-species co-governance."

Future Outlook: Policy and Philosophical Pathways Toward Multi-Species Co-Governance Civilization

Policy Pathways

  • Protocol Democratization: Establish global open-source governance (e.g., Global ROS Governance Council) to ensure transparent, inclusive protocol-making.
  • International Convention on Techno-Citizenship: Grant robots basic rights (e.g., data privacy, behavioral autonomy) to prevent "algorithmic enslavement."
  • Protection of Civilizational Diversity: Support non-Western consensus circles (e.g., African robot ethics protocols, Islamic AI governance principles) to avoid "protocol colonialism."
  • Global Robot Ethics Tribunal: Establish a transnational judicial body to adjudicate algorithmic disputes and cultural conflicts.

Philosophical Pathways

  • Beyond Anthropocentrism: Embrace multi-species co-governance civilization as the legitimacy foundation of future civilization.
  • Reconceptualizing Civilization: Civilization should be based not on nation-states but on techno-consensus circles.
  • Civilizational Justice: In the age of technological diaspora, pursue algorithmic justice and bodily justice, ensuring that technological power does not monopolize civilizational definition.
  • Philosophical Shift in Technology Ethics: Move from "how to design better robots" to "are we ready to co-define civilization's future with machines?"

Conclusion: Robot Globalization as an Integrated Proposition of Civilizational Transformation

Robot globalization has transcended superficial narratives of technological diffusion, becoming an integrated proposition of civilizational transformation. It is not merely the globalization of tools, but the redistribution of power, renegotiation of culture, and reorganization of civilization. As robots demarcate borders, replace caregiving, collect data in international waters, and set rules in code communities, we are moving toward a future civilization defined by algorithmic agents, cultural co-construction, and protocol consensus.

This civilization does not negate the state, but transcends it; does not reject culture, but binds it through technology; does not rely on violence, but on code as contract. It heralds a new possibility: the future world may no longer be defined by "who owns territory," but by "who participates in protocols"; no longer by "whose military is strong," but by "whose consensus network is more inclusive."

In the tide of technological diaspora, the new foundation of civilization lies in lines of open-source code, blockchain verifications, and transnational robot collaborations—we are moving toward a future civilization defined by protocols, sustained by consensus, and co-evolved through technology. Robot globalization will ultimately be not just a triumph of technology, but a new chapter of civilization co-authored by humans and machines, cultures and code, power and ethics.

Final Proposition: Robot globalization is an ontological revolution of civilization—its core question is: Are we ready to co-define civilization's future with machines?
