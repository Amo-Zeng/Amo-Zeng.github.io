II. A Life Legend in Chinese Civilization

Throughout the millennia of Chinese civilization, Cordyceps has been not only a natural miracle but also a cultural symbol. As early as the Ming Dynasty, Li Shizhen listed Cordyceps as a "superior herb" in the "Compendium of Materia Medica," alongside ginseng and ganoderma lucidum as the "three treasures." Due to its unique nourishing properties and rarity, Cordyceps has become an indispensable gem in traditional Chinese medicine.

In the context of Chinese civilization, Cordyceps is not just a medical symbol but also a cultural emblem. It represents humanity's reverence for nature and respect for life. In the Tibetan medical system, Cordyceps is considered "heaven-bestowed medicine," possessing sacred powers to purify the body and mind and enhance wisdom. The excavation and use of this rare medicinal herb are not merely medical acts but also cultural rituals for dialogue with nature and communication with the divine.

Along the ancient Tea-Horse Road, the tinkling of camel bells weaves a trade legend spanning millennia. Legend has it that during the Tang Dynasty, a Tibetan merchant introduced Cordyceps to the Central Plains, initiating its journey of dissemination. On this ancient route connecting Tibet and the Central Plains, Cordyceps became a bridge for cultural exchange between different peoples and a witness to the inclusiveness and fusion of Chinese civilization.
