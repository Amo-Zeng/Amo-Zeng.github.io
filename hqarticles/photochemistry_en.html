A Light-Driven Future: From Molecular Machines to Clean Energy

As the name suggests, light-driven technology refers to the use of light energy as a driving force to achieve specific functions or purposes. Compared to traditional energy-driven methods, light-driven technology has significant advantages such as being clean, renewable, and precisely controllable. In recent years, breakthroughs in photoreactive materials, photocatalysts, and photonic devices have enabled light-driven technology to show great potential in various fields.

Molecular Machines: Precise Manipulation at the Microscopic Level

Molecular machines are an important application area of light-driven technology. By using light as a trigger signal, scientists can precisely control the movement of molecular machines, thereby achieving precise manipulation at the microscopic level. Imagine using light-driven molecular machines to detect and remove cancer cells within the human body, or using molecular machines for drug delivery and chemical synthesis in industrial production—these scenarios, once found only in science fiction, are gradually becoming reality.

In recent years, research on light-controlled molecular machines has made breakthrough progress. For example, scientists have developed light-responsive DNA nanomachines that can use specific wavelengths of light to control the folding and unfolding of DNA molecules, enabling the recognition and manipulation of target molecules. Additionally, light-controlled protein machines have shown great potential, such as using light to regulate enzyme activity to achieve precise control over biocatalytic reactions.

Clean Energy: Reshaping the Future Energy Landscape

The energy problem is one of the major challenges facing human society, and light-driven technology offers a new approach to solving this issue. Using solar energy for photocatalytic water splitting can produce clean and renewable energy—hydrogen. Compared to traditional fossil fuels, hydrogen energy has significant advantages such as high energy density, combustion products being only water, and no pollution, making it one of the ideal clean energies for the future.

In recent years, photocatalytic water splitting technology has made rapid progress. Scientists have developed various efficient and stable photocatalysts, such as those based on metal oxides, metal sulfides, and perovskite materials, which continuously improve the efficiency of converting solar energy into hydrogen. Moreover, researchers are also developing new photocatalytic reaction systems, such as reducing carbon dioxide to fuel, providing new pathways to achieve carbon neutrality.

  • Light-Driven Medicine: Using light-driven technology for disease diagnosis, drug delivery, and tissue engineering can achieve more precise and efficient treatments.
  • Light-Driven Manufacturing: Using lithography techniques for micro-nano fabrication can produce more precise and powerful devices and materials.

The Dance of Light and Matter: From Molecular Selectivity to Optogenetics

From the wavelength-selective excitation of specific molecular sites to the precise regulation of biological functions through optogenetics, photochemistry demonstrates its interdisciplinary charm in precision chemistry and life sciences.

The Magic of Light: Molecular Selectivity and Light-Controlled Chemistry

The charm of photochemistry lies in its precision. By selecting specific wavelengths of light, scientists can selectively excite particular chemical bonds or structural units within molecules, thereby achieving precise control over chemical reactions. This "molecular selectivity" magic is vividly demonstrated in light-controlled click chemistry and photoisomerization reactions.

Light-controlled click chemistry (Phototriggerable Click Chemistry) uses photosensitive groups as triggers to precisely initiate chemical reactions under light exposure. For example, the light-controlled alkyne-thioether click reaction, when excited by UV light, can efficiently form covalent bonds at specific sites on biomolecules. This technology has been used to construct biosensors and targeted drug delivery systems. Light-controlled polymerization, by regulating the photoreactivity of monomers, has achieved precise shaping of material structures at the micrometer and even nanometer scales.

Photoisomerization is another fascinating molecular dance. Under the influence of light, molecules can transform from one configuration to another, often accompanied by significant changes in physical and chemical properties. The E/Z photoisomerization reaction in liquid crystal materials and photoresponsive drugs showcases the potential of photochemistry in designing smart materials. Light-controlled protein isomerization reveals the importance of photochemistry in studying cell signaling.

The Wisdom of Light: The Art of Biological Regulation in Optogenetics

When the precision of photochemistry meets the complexity of life sciences, optogenetics emerges. This technology uses light-sensitive proteins to precisely regulate the functions of specific cells or tissues, opening up new avenues for understanding complex biological processes and developing precision medical technologies.

Optogenetics involves expressing light-sensitive proteins in target cells through genetic engineering. These proteins can change their conformation or function under stimulation by specific wavelengths of light. For example, the light-sensitive ion channel channelrhodopsin opens under blue light, allowing ions to flow into neurons, thus precisely controlling the excitation state of neurons. This technology has been used to study neural circuit functions and develop new methods for treating neurological diseases.

The applications of optogenetics extend beyond neuroscience. In cell biology, through light-controlled gene expression (photogenetics), scientists can real-time regulate various aspects of cellular activities. Light-controlled gene switches can precisely turn genes on or off in response to specific light signals, showing great potential in studying gene functions and developing gene therapies.

Photochromism: Capturing the Magic Pen of Light

Photochromic materials, as the name suggests, are a class of magical materials that can respond to light and change color according to the intensity or wavelength of the light. This phenomenon is fundamentally due to reversible changes in the molecular structure of the material under the influence of photon energy.

Take spiropyran, one of the most common photochromic materials, as an example. Its molecules are in a closed-ring form when not exposed to light, appearing colorless or pale. When exposed to specific wavelengths of light (such as UV light), the molecules open to form an open-ring structure, changing color to a darker shade. This color change is not irreversible; when the light exposure stops, the molecules return to the closed-ring form, and the color becomes pale again.

The characteristics of photochromic materials make them highly promising in applications such as information storage, optical anti-counterfeiting, and smart windows. For instance, anti-counterfeiting marks printed with photochromic ink can display different colors under different wavelengths of light, significantly enhancing the reliability of anti-counterfeiting technology. Applying photochromic materials to building glass can automatically adjust the transmittance according to the intensity of sunlight, achieving energy-saving and environmental protection.

Photonic Crystals: Sculptors of Light

If photochromic materials are like using a magic pen to sketch the shape of light, then photonic crystals are using periodic structures to precisely sculpt light.

Photonic crystals are artificially synthesized periodic structure materials with regularly arranged regions of different dielectric constants, similar to natural crystals. Just as electrons move in semiconductors, photons in photonic crystals are modulated by the periodic structure.

This modulation effect allows photonic crystals to strongly reflect or refract specific wavelengths of light, thereby achieving precise control over the propagation of light waves. Using this property, scientists have developed various optical devices based on photonic crystals, such as photonic crystal fibers, photonic crystal filters, and photonic crystal sensors.

More impressively, photonic crystals can dynamically regulate optical properties such as refractive index, reflectivity, and dispersion by adjusting their structural parameters. This means that in the future, we can use photonic crystals to manufacture smarter and more efficient optical devices, injecting new vitality into the development of information technology, energy technology, and other fields.
