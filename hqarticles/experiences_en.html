Reconstruction of Experience: Breaking Free to Innovate

Throughout human history, experience has been regarded as the crystallization of wisdom, a precious treasure left by our predecessors. However, as times rapidly change, the limitations of experience gradually become apparent, especially when faced with rapidly changing environments, excessive reliance on experience can become an obstacle to innovation. This article will explore how to find balance between inheritance and innovation, and reconstruct our understanding of experience, from three dimensions: "negating experience," "plasticity of experience," and "symbiotic relationship between experience and innovation."

1. Breaking the Mold: From "Negating Experience" to Innovation

Experience once served as our compass for exploring the world, but in the face of modern society's complexity and uncertainty, its drawbacks also become increasingly evident. First, experience has a lagging and limited nature. It is formed under specific historical conditions, reflecting past environments and rules. However, times are constantly changing, and technology is rapidly advancing. Past experiences may not apply to the present, let alone the future. If we blindly rely on experience, we easily fall into the trap of "path dependence," unable to adapt to new environments and demands.

Take Kodak and Nokia as examples. These once-dominant industry giants failed to adapt to new technological challenges due to their overreliance on past success, eventually losing ground in market competition. Kodak remained fixated on traditional film photography, failing to transition to digital; Nokia missed the smartphone wave, losing market opportunities. These cases reveal a harsh reality: experience is not always a guarantee of success and can sometimes become an obstacle to innovation.

However, negating experience does not mean discarding it entirely. Instead, we need to find balance between innovation and experience. Establishing a trial-and-error mechanism is crucial to achieving this balance. By conducting small-scale experiments, we can validate the feasibility of new ideas while reducing risks due to insufficient experience. Additionally, cross-disciplinary learning and critical thinking provide significant ways to break free from experience limitations. The collision of knowledge from different fields often sparks new ideas, while critical thinking helps us approach experience with caution, avoiding blind trust.

2. Plasticity of Experience: Dynamically Reconstructing Cognitive Boundaries

Modern cognitive science research reveals another aspect of experience: it is not an unshakable past but a highly plastic dynamic existence. This "plasticity of experience" not only changes our inherent understanding of experience but also opens new paths for personal and organizational growth.

Psychological studies show that the human brain is highly plastic; neural connections can be continually reshaped through learning and practice. This means that our understanding of the world—the core component of experience—is not fixed but can be updated and optimized with new information and deep reflection. Michelangelo's sculptural art provides an example; he viewed experience as a dynamic process, refining the essence by constantly eliminating "excess." This dynamic understanding of experience not only helped him achieve remarkable artistic success but also offers insights into redefining experience.

In practice, the plasticity of experience is reflected in the transformation from failure to success. Toyota's "Kaizen" philosophy and Federer's career transformation are classic cases. Toyota continuously improved through small-scale adjustments and reflection, turning failures into new experiences, ultimately forming the lean production model; Federer redefined his match experience through technical adjustments and tactical reflection, creating new heights in tennis. These cases demonstrate that experience is not a fixed label but a dynamic resource that can evolve through deliberate practice and deep reflection.

Moreover, the plasticity of experience is also reflected in the interaction and fusion of different types of experience. In modern technology, the fusion of interdisciplinary knowledge has become a source of innovation. For instance, breakthroughs in artificial intelligence often stem from interdisciplinary research involving computer science, psychology, and neuroscience. This cross-domain experience integration not only broadens cognitive boundaries but also fosters new thinking models.

3. Inheritance and Innovation: Twin Wings of Cultural Continuity

Throughout the long course of human civilization, the inheritance of experience has always been the core of cultural continuity. Family businesses and traditional crafts rely on the accumulation and transmission of experience. However, with the sweeping tide of globalization and the rapid development of digital technology, the increasingly complex and ever-changing environment demands more than mere "conservation." Cultural inheritance must be combined with innovation to maintain lasting vitality in the new era.

Take China's brewing craftsmanship and the Armani family of Italy as examples. Experience inheritance is not just the transfer of technology but a cultural transmission that integrates wisdom, emotion, and values. This inheritance provides a stable cultural foundation for individuals and organizations, enabling sustained development. However, inheritance is not static. In today's digital and globalized era, traditional experience accumulation faces unprecedented challenges. The rapid advancement of technology, rapid market changes, and diverse consumer demands require us to innovate while inheriting experience, infusing it with new vitality.

Traditional Chinese Medicine and Michelin's innovative transformation are exemplary cases. Chinese Medicine combines traditional experience with modern medicine through the application of big data and artificial intelligence; Michelin addresses future automotive industry changes through the development of new energy tires and exploration of intelligent technology. These cases illustrate that innovative transformation of experience is not a negation of tradition but an elevation of it. By introducing new technologies and thinking, experience can regain vitality in the context of globalization and digitization.

Inheritance and innovation are not opposing poles but complementary relationships. Inheritance provides a foundation for innovation, while innovation enriches inheritance with new content. Their combined effect drives individuals, organizations, and cultures to continuously evolve in complex environments. For example, Japan's tea ceremony, combined with modern design concepts and technology, creates diverse expressions; Haier empowers employees through management innovation, driving the company's platform transformation. These cases demonstrate that only by daring to innovate on the foundation of inheritance can we remain invincible in the tides of globalization and digitization.

Conclusion: Beware of Experience Traps, Embrace Change and Innovation

In an era of rapid change, the inheritance and innovation of experience act as twin wings, determining the fate of culture and organizations. Inheritance allows culture and experience to endure, providing us with wisdom and strength to face challenges; innovation allows these experiences to regain new vitality in new environments. By breaking the mold, embracing the plasticity of experience, and balancing inheritance and innovation, we can redefine the future of experience, continuously advancing in a changing world and writing our unique chapters.
