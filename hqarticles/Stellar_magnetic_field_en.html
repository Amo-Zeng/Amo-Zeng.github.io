Magnetic Activity-Driven "Planetary-Stellar Angular Momentum Redistribution": Co-evolution of Exoplanet Orbital Eccentricity and Host Star Magnetic Topology Revealed by Gaia + SKA

Traditional studies in exoplanet dynamics have focused on gravitational tides and planetary migration. However, joint observations from Gaia DR3 and the SKA have uncovered a long-overlooked dimension: a systematic correlation between stellar magnetic topology and planetary orbital eccentricity. Data show that exoplanets with high eccentricity (e > 0.3) tend to orbit host stars with strong, non-axisymmetric magnetic structures—such as quadrupole-dominated fields or magnetic poles offset from the rotation axis—whereas low-eccentricity systems typically host dipole-dominated fields. This phenomenon has led to the "magnetic torque co-evolution" model: the stellar magnetosphere couples via Alfvén waves with the planetary magnetosphere (or ionized atmosphere), exerting asymmetric magnetic torques during early orbital evolution. This selectively damps pericenter velocities, driving long-term eccentricity growth. This mechanism challenges the assumption of "dynamical independence" in planetary systems, repositioning the stellar magnetic field as a "fossil record" of planetary orbital architecture.

Empirical Foundation: Synergistic Discoveries from Gaia and SKA

Gaia DR3 astrometric analyses of over 2,000 exoplanet-hosting stars reveal that systems with eccentricity e > 0.3 exhibit significantly higher magnetic activity indicators (e.g., radio luminosity, X-ray luminosity) in their host stars compared to low-eccentricity systems. Crucially, SKA radio polarimetric imaging of young stars (ages 1–10 Myr) shows that hosts of high-eccentricity systems commonly possess:

  • Non-axisymmetric magnetic topologies (magnetic poles offset from rotation axis by >15°);
  • Strong quadrupole/higher-order multipole components (dipole contribution <40%);
  • Large magnetic obliquity (>60°), leading to a highly tilted magnetosphere relative to the orbital plane.

For example, in the TOI-1859 system (e = 0.43), the host star exhibits a "tilted quadrupole" magnetic structure, with a magnetic pole offset by 70° from the rotation axis. SKA observations reveal asymmetric Alfvén wave emission in the orbital plane due to this configuration.

Magnetic Torque Co-evolution Mechanism: Alfvén Wave Coupling and Asymmetric Damping

The core of the "magnetic torque co-evolution" model is: angular momentum exchange between the stellar and planetary magnetospheres (or ionized atmospheres) via Alfvén waves.

At pericenter, the planet's high orbital velocity induces strong relative motion with the stellar magnetosphere, exciting intense Alfvén waves. If the stellar magnetic field is non-axisymmetric (e.g., a tilted quadrupole), then:

  • Asymmetric propagation and intensity of Alfvén waves across the orbit: near pericenter, the planet traverses strong magnetic regions, leading to high Alfvén wave excitation efficiency and angular momentum transfer from the planet to the star;
  • Asymmetric magnetic torque: due to the asymmetry in field structure, the planet loses more angular momentum at pericenter than it gains at apocenter, resulting in net angular momentum loss;
  • Selective damping: angular momentum loss primarily affects pericenter velocity, while apocenter velocity remains relatively unchanged. Orbital mechanics dictates that this directly leads to eccentricity growth (de/dt > 0).

Quantitative modeling shows that this mechanism can increase eccentricity from 0.1 to over 0.3 within 10⁵–10⁶ years after planet formation—far faster than gravitational tides (10⁹ years) or planet-planet scattering (10⁷–10⁸ years).

Explaining the Eccentricity Distribution of "Hot Jupiters"

"Hot Jupiters" (orbital periods <10 days) typically exhibit moderate eccentricities (e ~ 0.1–0.3). Traditional models struggle to explain why they do not fully circularize. Magnetic torque co-evolution offers a new explanation:

  • In early stages (<10 Myr), asymmetric magnetic torque drives eccentricity growth;
  • As stellar magnetic activity declines (magnetic topology evolves toward dipolar), magnetic torque weakens;
  • Later, gravitational tides dominate, slowly damping eccentricity;
  • The result is an "eccentricity plateau"—a stable regime of moderate eccentricity.

SKA observations of young hot Jupiter hosts (e.g., HIP 65426) show that their magnetic structures evolve from quadrupole- to dipole-dominated within ~5 Myr, closely matching the observed eccentricity growth curve.

Controversies and Model Challenges: Parameter Sensitivity and Observational Constraints

This model faces three major challenges:

  1. Uncertainty in planetary magnetic moment (Mₚ): Mₚ depends on internal dynamo processes. Only Earth and Jupiter have direct measurements; others rely on models (Mₚ ~ 10²²–10²⁴ A·m²). The model is highly sensitive to Mₚ: if Mₚ is too low, Alfvén wave coupling becomes inefficient, and the mechanism fails;
  2. Angular momentum transfer efficiency: the attenuation and reflection rates of Alfvén waves in the interstellar medium remain unknown, making quantitative constraints on angular momentum transfer difficult;
  3. Difficulty in observational verification: simultaneous measurements of stellar magnetic topology, planetary orbit, and atmospheric ionization state are required. Current technologies (e.g., SKA + TESS) can only cover a few systems.

Challenging the Independence of Planetary System Dynamics

If magnetic torque co-evolution is confirmed, it will fundamentally transform our understanding of planetary systems:

  • Planetary orbital architecture becomes a "fossil" of stellar magnetic history: high-eccentricity systems may have experienced a phase of strong non-axisymmetric magnetic fields, while low-eccentricity systems correspond to dipolar phases;
  • The "planetary system independence" hypothesis collapses: planetary orbital evolution is no longer decoupled from stellar physics but is instead tightly coupled with stellar magnetic activity and angular momentum evolution;
  • Magnetic variables must be included in habitability assessments: strong magnetic fields may both protect planets (by shielding cosmic rays) and perturb their orbits (increasing eccentricity), leading to extreme climate fluctuations. For example, high-eccentricity planets may experience intense radiation and tidal heating at pericenter, undermining habitability.

Repositioning Stellar Magnetic Fields: From "Background Noise" to "Primary Variable"

Traditionally, stellar magnetic fields were treated as "background noise" in exoplanet research. However, joint Gaia+SKA data reveal that magnetic fields act as the "director" of early planetary system evolution. Future habitability models should incorporate a "magnetic-orbital co-evolution index" to evaluate the combined impact of stellar magnetic topology, planetary magnetic moment, and orbital eccentricity on climate stability.

Conclusion: Toward a Unified Magneto-Gravitational Dynamics

"Magnetic torque co-evolution" is not merely a mechanism—it is a cross-scale dynamical framework. It unifies stellar magnetophysics and planetary orbital mechanics within a single physical process, revealing that during the critical post-formation window (1–10 Myr), magnetic interactions may dominate the shaping of orbital architectures. In the future, SKA's all-sky polarimetric surveys, Gaia's long-term astrometry, and JWST's detections of planetary atmospheric ionization states will provide decisive tests for this model, ultimately enabling full-lifetime predictions of exoplanetary systems.
