The Intersection of Root Perspective and Postcolonial Ecocriticism: Reimagining Symbiosis in Ecological Networks

In the expansive field of contemporary ecocriticism, the intersection of root perspective and postcolonial ecocriticism offers an opportunity to re-examine the relationships between humans and non-human entities. This theoretical convergence challenges traditional anthropocentrism and Western centrism, calling for a more equitable and inclusive approach to natural and cultural diversity. By integrating the ecological network metaphor of the root perspective with the Southern perspective of postcolonial ecocriticism, we can construct a more symbiotic ecological future.

Root Perspective: From Ecological Symbiosis to Cultural Integration

The root perspective uses the metaphor of plant root networks, particularly mycorrhizal networks, to situate humans within a vast and complex ecological web. Mycorrhizal networks, formed by underground fungal filaments interwoven with each other, connect different organisms and non-organisms, forming a vast system for information exchange and resource sharing. In this system, there is no center or hierarchy; all nodes are equal, interconnected and interdependent through the flow of matter, energy, and information.

1. Literary Depiction of Symbiotic Relationships

Traditional literary works often simplify the relationship between humans and nature into one of conquest and subjugation. The root perspective calls for a re-examination of the portrayal of symbiotic relationships in literature, uncovering the ecological wisdom embedded within. For example, "Silent Spring" reflects on the relationship between humans and nature, and "The Little Prince" establishes a deep connection between the protagonist and the fox, both exemplifying an ecological view beyond dualistic opposition.

2. Plant Wisdom and Literary Influence on Ecological Networks

Concepts of plant wisdom and ecological networks provide new inspiration for literary creation. Writers begin to tell stories from the perspective of plants or integrate metaphors of ecological networks into their works. For instance, "The Wisdom of Plants" describes the sensory and communicative abilities of plants, influencing contemporary ecological literature; while "Cloud Atlas" uses metaphors of cross-temporal ecological networks to express deep connections between humans and the non-human world.

3. The Role and Responsibility of Humans in Ecological Networks

The root perspective reminds us that humans are not independent of the ecological network but are a node within it. Our actions affect the entire network, necessitating a reconsideration of our roles and responsibilities. Postcolonial Ecocriticism: Southern Perspective and Environmental Justice

Postcolonial ecocriticism combines ecological issues with colonial history and power relations, exposing the deep-seated injustices behind environmental problems. This theoretical paradigm not only focuses on the global impact of ecological crises but also re-examines environmental issues from the perspective of the Global South, exploring pathways to environmental justice.

1. Ecological Experiences and Indigenous Ecological Wisdom of the Global South

Historically, countries in the Global South have suffered from colonial exploitation, and their ecosystems have borne tremendous pressure. However, the literature, culture, and social practices of these countries contain rich indigenous ecological wisdom, which is of significant importance for addressing contemporary ecological crises. For instance, writers in the Caribbean depict the close connection between tropical rainforests and local residents, criticizing the destruction of natural resources by Western industrialization.

2. Southern Perspective on Climate Change and Species Invasion

Climate change and species invasion are representative of contemporary global environmental issues, but the impact of these problems on countries in the Global South is often overlooked. Climate injustice exposes the power imbalances in global ecological governance, calling for an increase in the voice of developing countries. Meanwhile, species invasion reveals the deep-seated connections between environmental issues and colonial history, urging a rethinking of the paradigm of global ecological governance.

3. Enhancing the Voice of the Global South and Achieving Environmental Justice

Postcolonial ecocriticism calls for the construction of a more diverse and inclusive global ecological governance framework, recognizing the subject status of countries in the Global South in ecological governance, respecting their indigenous ecological wisdom and environmental demands. Through the integration of cultural diversity, global ecological governance can become more comprehensive and effective.
