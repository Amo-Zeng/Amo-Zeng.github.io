Interdisciplinary Reconstruction of Psychological Trauma

From Neural Encoding to the Redemption of Existential Time

In the darkest recesses of human experience, psychological trauma acts as a profound incision, etched not only into an individual's nervous system but also reshaping their relationship with the world. Traditionally examined separately within psychology, neuroscience, or philosophy, its essence calls for an interdisciplinary understanding—trauma is physiological, technological, and ontological. Centered around the theme of "Interdisciplinary Reconstruction of Psychological Trauma," this article integrates three dimensions—"Body-Neuroscience-Art," "Technology-Media-Interface," and "Time-Existence-Meaning"—to construct a "Physiology-Technology-Philosophy" trinity analytical framework, aiming to reveal trauma's multifaceted nature and potential pathways to healing.

I. Body-Neuroscience-Art: The Sensory Encoding and Aesthetic Reconstruction of Traumatic Memory

Traumatic memory is not ordinary memory. Neuroscientific research indicates that extreme stress triggers an excessive release of cortisol, inhibiting hippocampal function—the key brain region responsible for integrating fragmented experiences into coherent narratives. Consequently, traumatic memories are often encoded as sensory fragments (images, sounds, smells, bodily sensations) within the amygdala and limbic system, becoming somatic memories that are "known but unspeakable."

This is where art therapy finds its purpose. Art—particularly painting, dance, and music—provides a means to bypass language and directly engage with sensory memory. An fMRI study from Utrecht University in the Netherlands found that painting therapy significantly reduces hyperactivity in the Default Mode Network (DMN) while enhancing functional connectivity between the prefrontal cortex and visual cortex. This means that when trauma survivors depict fear on canvas, they are not merely "expressing" emotion but are engaged in a neural-level re-encoding: through visual-motor participation, they reintegrate fragments stored in the limbic system and imbue them with new context.

Dance therapy activates the insula and cerebellum through rhythm and movement, breaking the common "body freeze" associated with trauma; music, with its temporal structure and emotional tension, helps reorganize fragmented time experiences. The commonality among these art forms is that they are embodied, non-semantic interventions, acting directly on the neural imprints of trauma.

Furthermore, art therapy accomplishes a transformation from "trauma as disease" to "trauma as creative material." When a survivor paints a nightmare scene as "a darkness with starlight within," she is not only processing trauma but also undertaking an aesthetic self-reconstruction. Neuroaesthetics thus reveals: trauma can, through the channel of creativity, transform from an absolute pressure controlling the individual into an object that can be examined, modified, and even transformed.

II. Technology-Media-Interface: The Circulation and Transformation of Trauma in Digital Environments

If art works through the body and brain, technology participates in the construction and transformation of trauma on a broader social and media dimension. In the digital age, technology has become a "symbiont" of trauma—it records, amplifies, and repeats trauma, but also offers new interfaces for healing.

Social media is a typical case. Algorithmic recommendation mechanisms constantly push traumatic content (e.g., violent videos, disaster footage) to users, creating a "digital loop" where indirect exposure can lead to PTSD-like symptoms. Neurological studies confirm that repeated exposure to traumatic visual material strengthens amygdala responses, deepening fear-related neural pathways. Philosopher Bernard Stiegler warned that technology can be both a remedy (pharmakon) and a poison—while preserving memory, it can also alienate trauma into a consumable spectacle.

Yet technology also holds redemptive potential. Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy (VRET) allows patients to "re-experience" traumatic scenes in a safe, controlled environment, achieving memory reconstruction through graduated exposure. VR therapy experiments conducted by the U.S. military showed reduced amygdala activity and enhanced prefrontal regulation post-intervention—paralleling the neural mechanisms of art therapy, both achieving guided neural plasticity through controlled experience.

Artificial intelligence is also expanding the boundaries of healing. AI chatbots like Woebot and Replika, while incapable of deep empathy, can serve as "transitional objects" providing immediate support. Studies show that regular users of such tools experience significantly reduced anxiety levels, indicating that even limited human-machine interaction can alleviate loneliness and re-experiencing to some degree.

Moreover, technology alters the form and dissemination of collective trauma. During the Black Lives Matter movement, the video of George Floyd's ordeal circulated globally, fostering transnational consensus and empathy but also causing a "global re-traumatization." Sociologist Jeffrey Alexander's theory of the "trauma process" becomes crucial here: the social meaning-making of trauma heavily relies on media narratives—and digital technology accelerates, amplifies, and complicates this process.

The ethics of technology are therefore paramount: Should algorithms incorporate "trauma-sensitive design"? How can VR therapy avoid becoming exclusive to the technological elite? The answers to these questions will influence trauma's ultimate trajectory in the digital age—whether it becomes entrenched and amplified or transformed through interaction and intervention.

III. Time-Existence-Meaning: Trauma's Shattering of Temporality and the Philosophical Response

The most profound devastation of trauma extends beyond the brain or societal level, reaching the very foundation of existence: temporality. As Heidegger pointed out, the essence of human existence (Dasein) is temporality—a coherent whole composed of "having-been, present, and future." Trauma violently interrupts this flow, imprisoning the individual in an inescapable "now."

Neuroscience offers an explanation for this "temporal rupture": normally, the insula and anterior cingulate cortex integrate momentary experiences into a continuous temporal flow. Under extreme stress, however, high cortisol concentrations inhibit activity in these regions, leading to fragmented time perception. fMRI scans show that PTSD patients exhibit significantly reduced anterior cingulate activity and highly activated amygdala when recalling trauma—confirming that traumatic memory is not a "memory of the past" but a "persistently present perception."

This "past within the present" consumes the future. A retired soldier described: "I don't live in the past; the past lives in me—it has taken my future." Trauma destroys the capacity to be oriented toward the future, and as philosophy emphasizes, it is precisely this "openness to the future" that gives life meaning and hope.

Paul Ricoeur further argues that personal identity relies on narrative integration—we understand ourselves through stories. Trauma shatters this story; the victim is not "having a traumatic story" but "being possessed by a traumatic fragment." This loss of narrative ability not only causes psychological suffering but also reveals the inherent fragility of human existence itself.

Therefore, from a philosophical perspective, healing is not merely symptom reduction but restoring temporal coherence and narrative wholeness—reconnecting past, present, and future, reclaiming the unbearable "moment" as part of one's life story.

IV. Toward Integration: An Interdisciplinary Healing Paradigm for Trauma

The above three dimensions are not isolated but mutually illuminating and intertwined. Neuroaesthetics reveals how art reconstructs memory, technological interfaces show how media carries trauma, and philosophical temporality interrogates its underlying existential meaning—their convergence points toward an integrated healing paradigm.

We can envision the following interdisciplinary intervention pathways:

  1. Neurofeedback + VR Narrative:
    Use EEG to monitor the patient's brain state (e.g., amygdala activity) in real-time, dynamically adjusting the intensity and content of VR exposure narratives to achieve precise, controlled memory re-contextualization.
  2. Artistic Expression + AI-Assisted Integration:
    Following painting or music creation, use Natural Language Processing (NLP) AI to help patients narratively expand upon their works, connecting imagery, emotion, and language to promote the reconstruction of the narrative self.
  3. Body Time Training + Phenomenological Guidance:
    Enhance insula function and improve time perception through somatic training like dance and breathing exercises; incorporate phenomenological "epoché" or Ricoeurian narrative reconstruction methods during this process to help patients distinguish between the "past self" and the "present self."

This composite therapy is not merely a technological stack but a practice of philosophical理念 (concept): it acknowledges the multidimensionality of trauma, responding to its neural basis, technological environment, and ontological challenges, thereby facilitating repair and transformation across multiple levels.

Conclusion: Rebuilding the Dignity of Time within Fracture

The interdisciplinary reconstruction of psychological trauma not only expands our understanding of trauma but also illuminates new possibilities for healing. It tells us that trauma cannot be fully explained by a single discipline nor彻底 (completely) healed by one method alone. Yet it also reveals that humans possess both fragility and an extraordinary capacity for reshaping—through art to remake memory, through technology to transform experience, through philosophy to restore meaning.

As a Holocaust survivor said in later years: "Time did not heal everything, but it gave me a language to converse with the scars." This dialogue is neural, technological, and profoundly human—it is choosing to weave time amidst acknowledged fracture; it is opening to the future after confronting fear.

From the intersecting perspectives of physiology-technology-philosophy, trauma ceases to be merely an error awaiting repair and becomes a potential契机 (opportunity) to rediscover the self, time, and the world. And this is the greatest hope bestowed upon us by interdisciplinary inquiry.
