I. M. Pei: Architecture and Life, Carving Eternal Time

I. M. Pei, hailed as "the last master of modern architecture," has left an indelible mark on the architectural world. This Chinese-American architect's unique architectural language and profound cultural reflections have found his modernity at the intersection of Eastern and Western cultures. His works are not just the creation of spaces but also dialogues of culture, deconstructing and reconstructing modernity and tradition. Behind his illustrious career, Pei's life is equally a moving story of balance and integration. He used architecture as a pen to carve time, creating eternal art while composing a moving life symphony.

I. Eastern Imagery and Western Deconstruction: Pei's Modernity Code

Pei's architectural works are always suffused with a sense of Eastern poetry, but this poetry is not a simple replication of traditional architectural elements. Instead, it translates Eastern aesthetics into a new form of expression through modern architectural language. The Louvre Pyramid is a prime example. In this modern structure, Pei transformed the techniques of borrowing and revealing scenes from Eastern gardens into geometric compositions of glass and steel, creating a modern yet poetic effect. Pei deconstructed the spatial aesthetics of Eastern gardens with modern architectural vocabulary, creating a space that is both modern and imbued with Eastern charm.

However, Pei's deconstruction is not purely a formal game but a profound cultural reflection. In the design of the New Museum of History in Germany, he employed a spiral structure, reinterpreted the German rationalist传统through modern architectural language. This deconstruction is not destruction but a reconstruction, a modern interpretation of tradition. The design of the Bank of China building reflects his thoughts on the relationship between modernity and national identity. The vertical lines of the building echo the verticality of traditional Chinese architecture while reflecting the characteristics of modern high-rise buildings. This design is neither a simple restoration nor pure modernism but a delicate balance.

Pei's uniqueness lies in his not being a simple modernist nor a purist traditional culture guardian. He found a middle way: reinterpreting traditional culture through modern architectural language, creating works that are both modern yet culturally deep. This creative approach not only responds to the cultural dilemmas of the globalized era but also provides a new possibility for the development of modern architecture.

II. The Return of Rationality and Order: Pei's Spiritual Totem

Pei's architectural works are always permeated with the pursuit of rationality and order. Deeply influenced by modernist masters such as Le Corbusier and Mies van der Rohe, he emphasizes functionality, simplicity, and structural clarity. He excels at using geometric shapes and clear lines to construct the skeletal frame of buildings, simplifying complex architectural languages into basic elements, creating visually stunning effects. The Louvre Pyramid, with its simple triangular structure, breaks the heaviness of the Louvre's existing buildings, creating a light and transparent entrance space that seamlessly integrates with its surroundings, embodying Pei's ultimate pursuit of rationality and order.

Despite being deeply influenced by Western modernism, Pei never forgot his cultural roots. He integrated traditional Chinese garden art, calligraphy, and philosophical thoughts into modern architectural design, creating works that blend Eastern charm with modern temperament. In the Fragrant Hill Hotel in Beijing, he applied the "borrowing scenes" technique of traditional Chinese gardens to architectural design, cleverly arranging spaces and landscapes to bring natural scenes into indoor environments, creating an "although man-made, as if nature-created"意境. Additionally, he paid attention to proportions and details, incorporating the essence of traditional Chinese architecture into modern architectural language, giving the Fragrant Hill Hotel unique cultural appeal.

Pei's architecture is not only functional but also deeply concerned with human emotions and the spiritual world. He excels at using light, materials, and space to create atmospheres, producing poetic architectural spaces where people can find solace and spiritual elevation. The Suzhou Museum, with its simple geometric forms and elegant tones, creates a tranquil and harmonious atmosphere. He skillfully uses light changes to introduce natural light into indoor spaces, creating a soft lighting environment for exhibitions that allows visitors to better appreciate the exhibits. Additionally, he integrated the delicacy and refinement of Suzhou gardens into the museum's design, creating a public space rich in poetry and cultural内涵.

Pei's architectural works transcend temporal limitations, becoming eternal spiritual totems. He consistently adhered to his design philosophy, not swayed by trends nor yielding to commercial interests, using his works to interpret his understanding of architecture and the world. He once said, "Architecture is the frozen music, it should convey an eternal spirit." It is this pursuit of eternal spirit that makes Pei's architectural works remain fresh over time, becoming immortal milestones in the history of modern architecture, injecting profound cultural内涵and spiritual value into modern architectural design concepts.

III. Architecture and Life: A Dual Concerto of Carving Time

Although Pei's architectural works are spread across the globe, from the East Building of the National Gallery of Art in Washington to the Louvre Pyramid in Paris, from the Bank of China Tower in Hong Kong to the Miho Museum in Japan, he created timeless classics with simple geometric lines, clever use of light and shadow, and a deep understanding of traditional culture. Yet, he never forgot his commitment to family. His love story with his wife, Luchia Ching, began at Harvard, and they supported each other for over half a century, raising five children together. Even during his busiest periods, Pei made time for his family, the weekend family dinner being an unbreakable habit. He once said, "Family is the most important pillar of life; only family happiness can support career success."

Pei found balance between personal life and professional achievements, thanks to his positive attitude toward life and rigorous time management. He enjoyed traveling, reading, and music, which not only relaxed his mind but also provided continuous inspiration for his architectural creations. He was adept at drawing sustenance from life, integrating personal experiences and insights into his architectural designs, making his works more humanistic and vibrant.

Pei's life is a perfect fusion of architecture and life. He used architecture as a pen to carve time, creating eternal art while composing a moving life symphony. His experiences tell us that a remarkable life is not just about career success but finding a balance in life while pursuing dreams, allowing one's life to shine more brilliantly.

Between Eastern imagery and Western deconstruction, between rationality and poetry, between architecture and life, Pei found his balance. His architectural works are not just the creation of spaces but also dialogues of culture, deconstructing and reconstructing modernity and tradition. His life story is a moving chapter on balance and integration, explaining how to find meaning in life while pursuing excellence. I. M. Pei is not just a master architect but also a life artist, leaving us eternal spiritual totems with his architectural language and life wisdom.
