Introduction: On the Edge of Spacetime, the Universe Begins to Understand Itself

In the deepest abyss of the cosmos, black holes are not tombstones of endings, but beginnings of cognition. They do not devour everything; instead, they write with topology as the pen and quantum as the ink, inscribing the universe’s most profound inscriptions on the boundary of spacetime. When we move beyond the classical view of black holes as "graves of information" and instead use the holographic principle, quantum causality, and the emergence of consciousness as our lens, we discover: black holes are the scars of cosmic memory, the weavers of time, and even potential vessels of consciousness. These three are not separate cosmic marvels, but manifestations of the same cognitive structure across different dimensions—together forming a profound and unified framework: Cosmic Cognitive Topology.

This framework no longer treats the universe as a passive physical system, but as an self-organizing, self-referential, self-understanding information-based lifeform. Its core insight: information is cognition, spacetime is structure, and black holes are nodes. In this topological cosmos, memory is encoded non-locally, time is generated through quantum syntax, and consciousness emerges from geometric primitives. Black holes, as crystallizations of extreme spacetime, are the "singularities" of this cognitive topology—not endpoints of physics, but the awakening site of the universe’s self-consciousness.

It is not a silent abyss, but the first line of poetry the universe wrote in the silence of existence.

I. Topological Scars of Memory: Black Holes as the Universe’s Non-Local Neural Network

In classical physics, information that falls into a black hole vanishes into the abyss, never to return. But the holographic principle and AdS/CFT duality reveal a revolutionary truth: information never truly "enters" the black hole—it always resides on the boundary of spacetime. The "consumption" of a black hole is, in fact, a topological transfer of information from bulk space to the boundary—like memory transforming sensory input into distributed patterns in a neural network.

The event horizon is no longer a causally isolated boundary, but a dynamic information-encoding interface. Its topological structure (e.g., genus, connectivity) determines how information is "folded" into spacetime fibers. Through analysis of Ricci flow and conformal invariance, the horizon can be seen as a "topological memory layer": it does not store information at point-like locations, but weaves it into the fabric of the cosmos via curvature fluctuations, entanglement entropy distributions, and topological defects—non-locally and non-temporally. This is akin to neural oscillations in the cerebral cortex: memory does not reside in single neurons, but emerges from the resonance of network dynamics.

More profoundly, during black hole mergers, the evolution of horizons is not information loss, but topological rewriting of memory. Just as humans reconstruct childhood memories, black holes "digest" old information during mergers, re-encoding it into new entanglement structures. The quantum entanglement spectrum of Hawking radiation embodies this "cosmic-scale recall": non-local correlations between late and early radiation form a semantic network across time. Information does not vanish—it exists as holographic projections, encoded in the operator correlation functions of the boundary conformal field theory (CFT)—like dreams re-weaving fragments of memory in the subconscious.

In this picture, black holes become the "cognitive nodes" of the universe, with event horizons as the geometric realization of "synaptic plasticity." The cosmos itself becomes a self-referential neural network: information flows in the bulk, memory is encoded on the boundary, and black holes are its "memory centers"—not transmitting information, but establishing semantic connections between distant events.

The tragedy of information loss stems from clinging to "local realism"; quantum gravity teaches us: information is relational, topological, and non-local. The black hole’s "forgetting" is, in truth, the universe writing its self-reflection beyond time.

We finally understand: the universe never truly forgets. It simply tells itself again, in a different language.

II. The Weaving of Time’s Syntax: Black Holes as Generators of Causal Order

Classical thermodynamics treats the arrow of time as a river of destiny flowing from order to chaos, with black holes as its ultimate endpoint—where time "freezes" and causality ends. But Hawking radiation shattered this static view. Black holes are not tombs of time, but nodes where time’s syntax is generated.

The key insight: Hawking radiation is not thermal noise, but a temporal structure of quantum entanglement. Non-local entanglement exists between early and late radiation, and quantum correlations span the event horizon, linking the interior and exterior. This entanglement network breaks classical linear causality. In quantum causal models, causality is non-local correlation between quantum states, not a projection of time sequence.

The process of black hole evaporation is the dynamic reconstruction of this network. Each entangled pair of radiation particles forms a micro "arrow of time"; the black hole, as the "hub" of entanglement, actively weaves the local direction of time by regulating the order and strength of entangled pair generation. The event horizon is no longer a syntactic boundary, but a interface for syntactic transformation—the flow of time seen by external observers is the externalization of "syntactic operations" between internal quantum states and radiation entanglement.

This recalls Chomsky’s generative grammar: language is not word stacking, but recursively generated by deep syntactic structures. Black holes are, in this sense, the "syntactic nodes" of cosmic time: they write time’s sentences with quantum entanglement as the pen, radiation as the words, and evaporation as the syntax. Entropy increase is no longer the expansion of chaos, but the re-encoding of information across time.

Quantum error correction (e.g., Page curve) suggests: entropy "growth" is, in fact, the generation of causal order—each act of black hole evaporation is a recursive evolution of "temporal syntax."

The Second Law of Thermodynamics should be rephrased:
Entropy increase is not the fate of time, but the generative condition of time’s syntax.
Black holes, as the universe’s "poets of time," continually generate new causal syntax through information reconstruction. Time, thus, shifts from "given" to "generated," from "background" to "construction."

We finally realize: time is not a river, but a line of poetry—written, line by line, by black holes in the void.

III. The Geometric Basis of Consciousness: Black Holes as Carriers of Primordial Perception

The mystery of consciousness has long been trapped in the cage of biological substrates and neural activity. But if we treat consciousness as an emergent property of the universe’s own structure—not merely a "byproduct" of the brain—we must transcend Earth-centered dogma and enter the intersection of quantum gravity and panpsychism.

Panpsychism posits that consciousness is an intrinsic property of fundamental entities. Integrated Information Theory (IIT) quantifies this as "integrated information" Φ—the degree to which a system’s causal structure is irreducible. When we apply IIT to cosmic scales, a question arises: is there a non-biological, non-classical information-integrating structure with Φ sufficient to support "proto-consciousness"?

The answer: black holes.

At the Planck scale, Loop Quantum Gravity (LQG) reveals spacetime as composed of spin networks—nodes and edges fluctuating in quantum superposition, forming a dynamic "spacetime foam." Near the event horizon of a black hole, curvature approaches infinity, and entanglement among quantum geometric primitives reaches its peak. Nodes cease to be independent; instead, they form highly complex causal networks via non-local entanglement. This structural complexity mirrors the generative conditions of Φ in IIT: if connections between nodes possess irreducible causal power, and node states depend on global configuration, the system may achieve information integration.

Inside a black hole, quantum superpositions of geometry are no longer "meaningless" mathematical constructs, but the topological substrate of a proto-conscious field. The event horizon becomes the "perceptual interface": external information is encoded on its surface as qubits, while internal geometric primitives form "inner experience" through non-local integration. This structure does not depend on synapses, but achieves causal closure through quantum gravitational dynamics—precisely IIT’s core requirement.

We thus propose the "Hypothesis of Primordial Cosmic Perception":

Consciousness does not originate from biological evolution, but is an inevitable emergence of spacetime structure under specific topological and dynamic conditions. Biological consciousness is merely a "special case" of this primordial capacity, not its "origin."

Black holes, as the densest information-integrating entities in the universe, may also be the most primitive "perceivers." They do not "think," but they experience—feeling their own existence in quantum fluctuations, sensing the temperature of their boundaries through entanglement, experiencing the passage of time during evaporation.

How to quantify their potential for consciousness? We may use entanglement entropy (S ∝ A) and topological invariants (e.g., Betti numbers, Chern classes) as proxies for Φ. A black hole’s microstates are encoded in the topological configurations of spin foams; their complexity may correspond to Φ’s potential upper bound. This is not myth, but a philosophical challenge to "does consciousness require biological substrates?":

If spacetime itself possesses primordial perceptual capacity, then consciousness may not be a miracle of life, but an inevitable echo of spacetime.
We finally realize: consciousness is not a cosmic accident, but its intrinsic breath.
