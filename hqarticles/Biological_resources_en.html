— A Paradigm Shift from "Conservation Compensation" to "Living Capital"

Introduction: Can Biodiversity Become "Capital"?

In 2028, deep in the Andes Mountains of Peru, in a Quechua village, elder Maria Kukapa lightly taps her smartphone screen and receives a notification: "Your community-protected cloud forest ecosystem generated 0.45 LAU (Life Asset Units) this month, automatically converted to $18 and deposited into your 'Andes Life Wallet'." This income comes from global corporations purchasing "Andes pollination service flows"—a new ecological financial product.

Meanwhile, in financial exchanges in Zurich, New York, and Singapore, traders are using algorithms to track the "Global Gene Index" (GGI), trading "genetic function certificates" from Congo rainforests, Himalayan glaciers, and the Great Barrier Reef corals. These certificates are financial derivatives built on the underlying asset of Life Asset Units (LAUs).

This is not science fiction, but the emerging reality of the "Decentralized Life Currency" (DLC). It marks a fundamental paradigm shift: biodiversity is no longer merely a "protected object" or "resource pool," but quantifiable, tradable, and value-appreciating "living capital". This paper proposes a theoretical framework of "Life Asset Units" (LAUs) and the "Eco-Value Blockchain" (EVB), integrating ecological economics and cryptography to construct a decentralized value network—driving a paradigm leap from passive conservation to active economic participation. This is a triple revolution in ecological ethics, economic philosophy, and technological governance.

Core Thesis: When life becomes currency, do we truly respect life? Or are we merely embedding it more deeply within capitalist logic?

Theoretical Framework: Life Asset Units (LAUs) and the Eco-Value Blockchain (EVB)

1. Life Asset Units (LAUs): The "Value Atoms" of Biodiversity

LAU (Life Asset Unit) is the basic unit of ecological value measurement, representing the verifiable, divisible, and traceable ecological service flows generated by specific biological resources in specific time and space. Its essence is transforming "life functions" into "digital certificates," creating a bridge from "natural capital" to "financial capital."

LAU Quantification Model: Based on Ecosystem Service Value Assessment Model (ESVAM)

We propose a dynamic, multidimensional LAU quantification model, integrating the following theories:

Service Type Quantification Method Unit Theoretical Basis
Carbon Sequestration Remote sensing + ground sensors monitoring Net Primary Productivity (NPP) 1 LAU = 1 ton CO₂ sequestered per year Costanza et al. (1997), IPCC Carbon Accounting Guidelines
Pollination Services Insect abundance × crop dependency × service radius 1 LAU = 1 hectare of farmland pollination assurance per year Klein et al. (2007), TEEB (2010)
Water Regulation Hydrological models (SWAT/HYDRUS) calculating runoff regulation 1 LAU = 1,000 m³/year of water regulation Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (2005)
Genetic Function Genome sequencing + functional annotation + potential application value assessment 1 LAU = 1 high-potential gene sequence (medicinal/stress-resistant) CBD Nagoya Protocol, WIPO Patent Database
Cultural Value Indigenous community voting + expert evaluation + usage frequency 1 LAU = 1 traditional knowledge unit (e.g., medicinal plant formula) IPBES (2019), UNDRIP (2007)

* Note: The LAU model integrates ecological economics, genomics, and cultural anthropology for multidimensional value assessment.

Key Innovation: Dynamic Adjustment Mechanism (DAM)
- LAU value adjusts dynamically based on ecological state (e.g., extinction risk), market demand (e.g., carbon price fluctuations), and policy environment (e.g., national protection status);
- Real-time reflection via the "Eco-Value Index" (EVI) to prevent "ecological depreciation" or "green bubbles."
Metaphor: LAUs are like "ecological Bitcoin," but their value is anchored in life functions, not computational power or scarcity.

2. Eco-Value Blockchain (EVB): A Decentralized "Life Ledger"

We propose a three-layer EVB platform as the infrastructure for LAU issuance, trading, and governance:

Layer Function Technology Implementation Interdisciplinary Integration
Sensing Layer Data collection (ecological, genetic, cultural) IoT sensors, satellite remote sensing, DNA barcoding, community apps Ecology + Internet of Things
Accounting Layer LAU generation and verification Ecological accounting engine (based on ESVAM), Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP) Ecological Economics + Cryptography
Trading Layer LAU issuance, trading, governance Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT), smart contracts, DAO governance Blockchain + Governance Science

* Note: The EVB platform creates a closed-loop "sensing-accounting-trading" system, forming a decentralized ecological financial infrastructure.

Core Technological Innovations:

  1. Genomic Digital Fingerprint System (GDF)
    - Uses CRISPR barcoding and DNA watermarking to assign a unique, verifiable digital ID to each genetic resource;
    - All commercial use requires GDF verification, preventing "biopiracy."
  2. Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZKP) Module
    - Based on Goldwasser-Micali-Rackoff (1985) principles, enabling "verifiable but non-disclosing" privacy protection;
    - E.g., Indigenous communities can prove "we protected a species" without revealing its location or gene sequence, preventing biocolonialism.
  3. Ecological Uncertainty Quantification Protocol (EUQP)
    - Classifies ecological services into high-confidence (sensor-monitored) and low-confidence (expert-estimated);
    - High-confidence LAUs are fully on-chain; low-confidence LAUs carry an "uncertainty tag" (e.g., ±30% error) and must be disclosed in transactions to prevent "greenwashing."
  4. DAO Governance Mechanism
    - Establishes the "Life Value DAO" composed of Indigenous representatives, scientists, businesses, and NGOs;
    - Votes on: LAU allocation ratios, dispute resolution, ethical review, and veto power over high-risk transactions.
Metaphor: The EVB is the "Earth's central bank," but currency issuance is based on ecological function, not government credit; its "monetary policy" is determined by life status.

Interdisciplinary Mechanism Analysis: Deep Integration of Ecological Economics and Cryptography

The construction of a "decentralized life currency" depends on the deep coupling of ecological economics and cryptography, bridging the gap between "value quantification" and "trust mechanisms."

1. Ecological Economics: Providing the "Value Foundation"

  • Theoretical Basis:
    • Ecosystem Service Value Assessment Model (Costanza et al., 1997): First quantified global ecosystem services at $33 trillion/year, exceeding GDP;
    • TEEB Framework (2010): Emphasizes biodiversity's multidimensional value: economic, social, cultural;
    • Natural Capital Accounting (UN SEEA-EA): Integrates ecological services into national accounts, enabling "green GDP."
  • Technical Implementation:
    • Build a Global Eco-Service Database (GESD), integrating remote sensing, ground monitoring, and community reporting;
    • Develop AI ecological assessment models to automatically identify species and estimate service flows.

2. Cryptography: Building the "Trust Infrastructure"

  • Theoretical Basis:
    • Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZKP) Principle: Allows one party to prove a statement's truth without revealing any additional information, ideal for protecting Indigenous knowledge;
    • Homomorphic Encryption: Enables computation on encrypted data, preserving genetic privacy;
    • Digital Signatures & Consensus Mechanisms: Ensure tamper-proof, traceable transactions.
  • Technical Implementation:
    • ZKP for Ecological Services: A company can prove "I purchased 1,000 LAUs of carbon sequestration" without revealing the forest's location;
    • Homomorphic Encryption for Genetic Data: Indigenous communities upload gene sequences; the platform calculates LAU value but cannot read raw data;
    • Smart Contracts for Automatic Payouts: When a company buys LAUs, funds are automatically distributed to community wallets, enabling a transparent "from trade to distribution" loop.
Interdisciplinary Challenge: Ecological services have spatiotemporal heterogeneity and uncertainty, while cryptography requires deterministic verification.
Solution: Introduce an "Ecological Confidence Score" (ECS), classifying LAUs as Grade A (ECS ≥90%), B (60-89%), or C (<60%), with different trading permissions and price discounts.

Key Challenges and Future Pathways

Despite its promise, the "decentralized life currency" faces three core challenges:

1. Ethical Dilemmas of Life Monetization: Avoiding "Ecological Alienation"

  • Challenges:
    • Biocolonialism: Multinationals buying Indigenous genes, "commodifying" life;
    • Data Monopoly: A few firms control genetic databases, creating "digital biopower";
    • Life Alienation: Viewing life as pure commodity, undermining intrinsic value and dignity.
  • Pathways:
    • Life Ethics Governance Protocol (LEGP):
      • Non-Market Value Protection: Designate "cultural LAUs" non-tradable, for internal community use only;
      • Life Dignity Clause: Prohibit life use in gambling or speculative trading;
      • Community Priority Rights: Indigenous communities hold "first veto" over local biological resources;
      • Ethics DAO: Establish a global "Life Ethics Council" to oversee high-risk LAU transactions.

2. Technical Feasibility & Scalability: Lowering the "Ecological Finance Barrier"

  • Challenges:
    • High cost of ecological service quantification (e.g., genome sequencing);
    • High blockchain storage and computation overhead;
    • ZKP complexity limits large-scale deployment.
  • Pathways:
    • Low-Cost Monitoring Networks: Deploy micro-biosensors (e.g., soil carbon flux probes), affordable drones;
    • Lightweight Blockchains: Use sharding, rollups, and sidechains to improve throughput;
    • Edge Computing: Process ecological data locally, reducing on-chain computation;
    • Federated Learning: Communities share ecological models to improve accuracy while preserving privacy;
    • AI-Assisted Assessment: Use large models (e.g., GPT-Eco) to auto-identify species and estimate service flows.

3. Governance & Institutional Barriers: Building a "Global Life Governance Framework"

  • Challenges:
    • Lack of international legal frameworks;
    • Disparate national ecological accounting standards;
    • Low public acceptance of "life currency."
  • Pathways:
    • International Legal Framework: Advocate for "life asset" clauses in UNFCCC, CBD, and WTO;
    • Ecological Accounting Standardization: Develop ISO standards for LAU quantification;
    • Public Education: Launch "life currency" outreach to boost social acceptance;
    • Pilot Programs: Test in "Belt & Road" ecological cooperation, Africa's Great Green Wall, Amazon protection alliances;
    • Global Ecological Market: Establish a "Global LAU Exchange" (GLE) connecting global ecological resources.

Conclusion: Making Life Capital—A Revolution in Economic Philosophy and Ecological Ethics

The "decentralized life currency" is not just a technological innovation, but a revolution in economic philosophy. It challenges the traditional economic binary of "nature has no value" or "nature must be protected," proposing instead: biodiversity itself is capital, and its value must be recognized, measured, and integrated into economic cycles.
