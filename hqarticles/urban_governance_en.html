From "City as a Service" to "Diverse Symbiosis": Reconstructing Modern Urban Governance Paradigms

Challenges and Transformation in Urban Governance

Cities, as symbols and gathering places of human civilization, carry the aspirations of people for a better life. However, with the acceleration of globalization and the continuous expansion of urbanization, traditional urban governance models are facing unprecedented challenges: imbalances in public service supply and demand, fragmentation of social governance, and increasing pressure on environmental resources. These issues not only reveal the limitations of the "City as a Service" concept but also expose the deeper dilemmas of single-dimensional governance models. In this context, reconstructing the urban governance paradigm and promoting the transformation from "problem-oriented" to "value co-creation," and then to "diverse values," has become an inevitable choice for achieving sustainable urban development.

From "City as a Service" to "Urban Symbiotic System": The Evolution of Ideas

In the traditional "City as a Service" concept, the city is regarded as a mechanical system, with the government as the dominant party and citizens as the recipients of services, with the provision of public services as its core goal. Although this model has met the basic needs of citizens to some extent, its limitations have become increasingly apparent: overlooking the essence of cities as complex, dynamic ecological systems, leading to fragmented governance and inefficient resource allocation.

In this context, the "Urban Symbiotic System" concept emerged. This concept views the city as a vibrant, diverse, and symbiotic organism, where governments, citizens, businesses, non-profit organizations, and other stakeholders are no longer simple management-managed relationships, but equal system participants, shaping the direction of urban development together. The governance goal has shifted from single public service provision to promoting collaboration, innovation, and shared prosperity among all parties. This change of concept is not only a profound reflection on traditional governance models but also a re-recognition of the essence of cities. A true "Urban Symbiotic System" means that every member of the city can participate in decision-making, share resources, and achieve collective value creation through collaboration.

From "Problem-Oriented" to "Value Co-Creation": Innovation in Governance Models

In the traditional "problem-oriented" governance model, the government is the main problem solver, with the core being to address specific issues through policy formulation and resource investment, such as traffic congestion, environmental pollution, and housing shortages. However, this model often neglects the diverse needs and emotional aspirations of citizens, leading to fragmented governance and reducing citizen participation.

The introduction of the "value co-creation" concept provides a new direction for urban governance. This concept emphasizes that urban governance should focus on the needs and expectations of citizens, by stimulating the creativity and participation of all parties to jointly create a better urban life. The core lies in "co" - co-participation, co-creation, and co-sharing. In this model, the government is no longer just a decision-maker and executor but plays a more coordinating and promoting role; citizens transform from passive recipients to active participants, with their needs and creativity becoming important driving forces for urban governance. For example, by establishing citizen participation platforms and encouraging residents to provide suggestions on urban planning and public facility construction, the efficiency of governance can be improved, and citizens' sense of belonging and responsibility to the city can be enhanced.

From "Single Dimension" to "Diverse Values": Comprehensive Transformation in Urban Governance

For a long time, the core goals of urban governance have often revolved around single dimensions such as economy and environment. Although this governance model has promoted economic growth and environmental improvement to some extent, it has also led to excessive resource consumption, increased social inequality, and the loss of cultural diversity. The single-dimensional governance model is no longer capable of addressing the complex challenges faced by modern cities, nor does it meet the increasingly diverse needs of citizens.

The introduction of the "diverse values" governance concept marks a comprehensive transformation of urban governance from single dimension to multiple dimensions. This concept emphasizes the balanced development of multiple values, such as economy, society, environment, and culture, aiming to build a sustainable urban ecosystem. For example, in the economic dimension, cities should promote the development of green and low-carbon industries; in the social dimension, they should promote social fairness and justice; in the environmental dimension, they should promote green buildings and low-carbon transportation; in the cultural dimension, they should protect and inherit local culture, enhancing the city's cultural soft power. Through the coordinated development of multiple dimensions, cities can not only maintain economic competitiveness but also achieve inclusive social growth, environmental sustainability, and cultural diversity, thereby providing citizens with a more comprehensive life experience.

Building an Urban Symbiotic System: Pathways and Practices

To achieve the transformation from "City as a Service" to "Diverse Symbiosis," urban governance needs to implement a series of specific measures in practice:

  1. Multi-stakeholder Governance Platforms: Build multi-stakeholder governance platforms, establish online and offline participation channels, encourage citizens, businesses, and other entities to actively participate in urban planning, decision-making, and management, forming a governance pattern of co-construction and shared benefits. For example, Hangzhou's "City Brain" project integrates data resources from multiple departments, achieving real-time perception and intelligent regulation of urban operational status.
  2. Cross-Department and Cross-Sector Collaboration Mechanisms: Promote cross-department and cross-sector collaboration, break down information barriers, achieve resource sharing, and realize collaborative governance. For example, Shenzhen's "Traffic Brain" built with big data technology has successfully alleviated urban traffic pressure.
  3. Establish Citizen Needs Feedback Mechanisms: Understand citizens' pain points and demands through various channels, converting citizens' needs into governance action guidelines. For example, a city promoted the construction of community elderly care centers through a citizen feedback mechanism.
  4. Encourage Community Participation and Cultivate Community Leaders: Communities are the basic units of urban governance. By encouraging community participation and cultivating community leaders, community vitality can be stimulated. For example, a community successfully promoted the construction of a community garden by cultivating community leaders, enhancing residents' happiness.
  5. Promote Urban Cultural Innovation: Enrich citizens' cultural life and enhance the city's visibility and attractiveness by hosting cultural festivals, art exhibitions, and other activities.

The Vision of Diverse Symbiosis

From "City as a Service" to "Diverse Symbiosis," urban governance is undergoing a profound transformation. This transformation is not only a reflection on traditional governance models but also an exploration of future urban development. In the urban symbiotic system, the government is no longer an aloof manager but a "servant" serving citizens, businesses, and various organizations; citizens are no longer passive users of services but active "masters" participating in urban governance; businesses are no longer merely profit-driven "economic agents" but "city partners" bearing social responsibilities. On the basis of equality and mutual benefit, all parties will jointly build a future city that is more beautiful, resilient, and sustainable.

Conclusion

The transformation and reconstruction of urban governance is not only an urgent need to address current challenges but also a long-term goal to achieve sustainable urban development. From "City as a Service" to "Diverse Symbiosis," from "problem-oriented" to "value co-creation," and from "single dimension" to "diverse values," urban governance is moving towards a more open, inclusive, and innovative new era. The future city will be a symbiotic system characterized by economic prosperity, social fairness, environmental friendliness, and cultural diversity, providing citizens with a better life experience. Let us join hands and embrace change to jointly write a new chapter in urban development!
