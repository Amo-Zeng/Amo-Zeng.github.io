Gene Editing Clinical Trials as Catalysts for Sociotechnical System Change: The Triangular Dynamics of Trust, Stratification, and Decentralization

Gene editing clinical trials are far more than mere scientific exploration or medical breakthroughs—they are profound catalysts for sociotechnical system transformation deeply embedded within social structures. This article takes "gene editing clinical trials" as its entry point, integrating three critical dimensions: sociotechnical contracts, medicalization diffusion, and decentralized healthcare networks. It constructs a unified theoretical framework to reveal the dynamic co-construction among these elements: how institutional trust shapes the boundaries of medicalization diffusion, how decentralized networks reconfigure sociotechnical contracts, and how medicalization diffusion, in turn, drives the evolution of decentralized governance. Through a progressive analysis of the CLIMB trial, Verve experiment, and CGHN prototype, this article ultimately proposes the "Triangular Model of Gene Editing Governance"—the Trust-Stratification-Decentralization (TSD) model—offering a systemic framework for future policy.

I. Institutional Trust: The "Gatekeeper" and "Accelerator" of Medicalization Diffusion

Gene editing clinical trials are first and foremost a political contest of trust. The first article, "Gene Editing as the 'Litmus Test of Trust,'" argues that public acceptance of the technology does not depend on its scientific validity, but on institutional trust—the collective legitimacy conferred upon the "sociotechnical contract" formed by regulatory bodies, medical institutions, and researchers. This contract is not static; it is continuously negotiated and reconfigured through the dynamic process of clinical trials.

Example: The CLIMB Trial achieved success not only in clinically resolving symptoms in over 90% of patients, but more importantly through transparent data sharing, independent ethics oversight, and mechanisms for patient involvement—building a form of participatory trust. This trust is not granted top-down, but gradually accumulated through openness, verifiability, and shared responsibility throughout the trial process.

However, trust is unevenly distributed. The second article, "The Medicalization Diffusion of Gene Editing," reveals that as gene editing shifts from "treating rare diseases" to "preventing common conditions" and even "health optimization" (e.g., the Verve experiment targeting familial hypercholesterolemia), its logic of medicalization slides from "repairing defects" toward "enhancing capabilities." This blurs the boundary between therapy and enhancement, transforming technology into a new tool of social stratification. While the Verve experiment achieved scientific breakthroughs, its exorbitant cost (~2 million USD per treatment) and early participant pool concentrated in high-income nations make it a symbol of "elite medicine."

Thus, institutional trust serves as the boundary condition for medicalization diffusion: it both prevents misuse and determines whether the technology can cross the ethical threshold from "treatment" to "enhancement," enabling broader societal adoption.

II. Decentralized Networks: The "Reconstructor" of Sociotechnical Contracts

When medicalization diffusion faces a crisis of trust, decentralized healthcare networks emerge as a new pathway to reconstruct sociotechnical contracts. The third article, "Decentralized Healthcare Networks," argues that the traditional hospital-centric model of gene editing is constrained by resource centralization, information asymmetry, and participation barriers, making equity and inclusivity difficult to achieve. In contrast, community-driven clinical trial networks, such as the CGHN prototype (CRISPR Global Health Network), are reshaping the sociotechnical contract—redefining who decides, who participates, and who benefits—through distributed data platforms, local ethics committees, and patient-led trial design.

CGHN in Practice: By establishing regional nodes in Africa, South Asia, and Latin America, CGHN adopts a modular trial design: the core scientific framework is set by global experts, but participant recruitment, informed consent processes, and data usage agreements are determined through local community negotiation. For instance, in India, CGHN partnered with community organizations to embed the informed consent process within traditional healthcare consultations, rather than relying on impersonal digital signatures. This culturally embedded governance prevents the technology from being perceived as "imposed Western science," instead enabling local societies to actively adopt and redefine it.

Decentralized networks do not merely change trial organization—they reconfigure the very sources of trust. In the CLIMB trial, trust relied on international authorities (e.g., FDA, EMA); in CGHN, trust is built on local social networks—community leaders, traditional healers, and patient support groups act as "trust intermediaries." This horizontal trust is more resilient than vertical, top-down trust.

More importantly, decentralized networks break the monopoly of expert-driven governance. In the Verve experiment, decision-making power was centralized within multinational pharmaceutical firms and elite hospitals. In contrast, CGHN employs an open governance platform that enables patients, community representatives, and scientists to co-define trial priorities. For example, the African node once rejected a gene-editing trial for obesity, stating, "Our most urgent needs are malaria and malnutrition"—demonstrating localization of technological agendas.

Thus, decentralized networks are not merely supplements to existing systems, but represent a fundamental reconstruction of traditional sociotechnical contracts: shifting from "expert-led, centralized control" to "multi-stakeholder participation, distributed governance."

III. Medicalization Diffusion: The "Reverse Push" Driving Decentralized Governance

Decentralization does not emerge spontaneously—it is forced by the inequalities inherent in medicalization diffusion. As noted in the second article, when gene editing shifts from "treatment" to "optimization," its market logic inevitably concentrates resources among high-income populations. The Verve experiment, despite claiming to "prevent heart disease," enrolled early participants almost exclusively from wealthy Western countries. This preventive privilege exacerbates health inequity, provoking resistance from the Global South.

The emergence of CGHN is precisely a countermeasure to 'optimization-driven medicalization': through decentralized design, it redirects technological agendas from "global market orientation" back to "local health needs," thereby re-anchoring the public value of gene editing.

More deeply, the inequality of medicalization diffusion exposes the failure of centralized governance. When global regulators fail to coordinate cross-border trials or ensure equitable access, decentralized networks become adaptive responses to governance gaps. For instance, in regions lacking national oversight, CGHN fills institutional voids through soft governance mechanisms (e.g., community ethics guidelines, data trusts). This bottom-up governance innovation challenges the authority of traditional regulatory bodies, forcing institutions like the FDA and WHO to reconsider their global roles.

Therefore, medicalization diffusion is not only a catalyst for decentralized governance but also its sustaining reverse force: every time technology amplifies inequality, it triggers greater demand for decentralization, forming a cycle of "inequality → decentralization → rebalancing."

IV. The Triangular Model of Gene Editing Governance: Trust-Stratification-Decentralization (TSD Model)

Based on the above analysis, this article proposes the Triangular Model of Gene Editing Governance (TSD Model), revealing the dynamic interplay among the three elements:

  1. Trust (Institutional Trust): The foundational legitimacy for medicalization diffusion, determining whether technology can cross ethical and distributive thresholds.
  2. Stratification (Social Stratification): The inequality inherent in medicalization diffusion, exposing the limits of centralized governance and driving decentralization.
  3. Decentralization (Decentralization): Reconstructs sociotechnical contracts, rebuilds trust, and mitigates stratification trends.

These three elements form a dynamic feedback loop:

  • Positive Cycle: High trust → equitable diffusion → low stratification → strengthened decentralization → higher trust;
  • Negative Cycle: Low trust → elite diffusion → high stratification → centralized failure → further erosion of trust.

The core insight of the TSD model is that governance of gene editing cannot rely solely on technology or law, but must be embedded in the dynamic adjustment of social structures. It requires policymakers to move beyond the traditional "risk-benefit" framework and adopt a perspective of sociotechnical system resilience.

V. Policy Implications: Toward Resilient Governance

The TSD model offers three policy pathways for gene editing governance:

  1. Institutionalizing Trust Building: Establish a dual-track global-local trust mechanism, such as harmonizing international ethical standards (global) with community-based informed consent protocols (local);
  2. Pre-emptive Stratification Monitoring: Embed social impact assessments in clinical trial design to evaluate accessibility, cost, and ethical risks for different population groups;
  3. Empowering Decentralization: Support community-led gene editing networks by providing funding, data access, and governance tools, positioning them as "innovation sandboxes" within mainstream healthcare systems.

Building on the CGHN prototype, a Global Decentralized Gene Editing Consortium could be established—integrating CLIMB's rigor, Verve's technological breakthroughs, and CGHN's inclusivity—achieving a triple balance of science, ethics, and society.

Ultimately, gene editing clinical trials test not the precision of CRISPR, but the governance wisdom of human society. Within the triangular tension of trust, stratification, and decentralization, we may yet find a path toward a future that is fair, sustainable, and trustworthy.
