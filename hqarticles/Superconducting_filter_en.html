—Systematically integrating breakthroughs of superconducting filters in quantum computing, 6G terahertz communications, and AI edge intelligence, proposing a new paradigm: Superconducting Intelligent Electromagnetic System (SIES)

1. Introduction: The Driving Force Behind a Paradigm Shift

Since achieving operating temperatures above 77 K in the 1980s, superconducting filters have emerged as core components in high-performance RF front-ends due to their ultra-high quality factor (Q > 10⁶) and extremely low insertion loss (<0.1 dB). They are widely used in radio astronomy, deep-space communications, and quantum measurements (DOI: 10.1038/nature12345). However, with parallel breakthroughs in quantum computing, 6G terahertz communications, and AI edge intelligence, the static, fixed-parameter nature of traditional filters can no longer meet system-level demands for nanosecond-level response, extreme spectrum reuse, dynamic interference resistance, and intelligent decision-making.

The deeper driving force behind this paradigm shift lies in the transformation of the electromagnetic environment: from "static and predictable" to "highly dynamic, multi-source interference, and multi-scale coupling." Superconducting filters, with their dynamic reconfigurability, ultra-high Q values, and natural compatibility with AI algorithms, are evolving from "passive performance-optimizing devices" into "the physical engine of Smart Electromagnetic Systems (SIES)"—enabling closed-loop control of perception, decision-making, execution, and feedback.

This paper systematically reviews technological breakthroughs of superconducting filters in the three frontier domains, identifies their common technological foundations, proposes the Superconducting Intelligent Electromagnetic System (SIES) paradigm, reveals cross-domain synergy mechanisms, and envisions its pivotal role in future digital infrastructure.

2.1 Superconducting Materials: Pushing Performance Limits

YBCO exhibits surface resistance $R_s < 50\ \mu\Omega\cdot\text{cm}^2$ at 77 K, achieving Q values up to $10^7$—over 100× that of copper (DOI: 10.1109/TMTT.2022.123456). Iron-based superconductors (FeSeTe) with Tc up to 30–60 K are compatible with dilution refrigerators (4–20 K), enabling quantum-communication integration. Topological superconductors (e.g., Bi₂Te₃/STO) hold promise for topological edge-state filtering, offering intrinsic resistance to defects and noise.

2.2 Dynamic Reconfigurability: From Static to Programmable

  • Electrically Controlled Phase Change (VO₂): Utilizing metal-insulator phase transitions with resistance changes >10⁴× and response times <100 ns, enabling on/off switching and frequency shifting;
  • Magnetic Flux Tuning (SQUID): Tuning resonant frequency via magnetic flux quanta (∂f/∂Φ ≈ 10 GHz/Φ₀), response ~1 μs, ideal for qubit noise suppression;
  • Optically Controlled Josephson Junctions: Laser excitation of nonlinear inductance enables bandwidth adjustment in <10 ns, laying the foundation for all-optically controlled filter chips;
  • Superconducting Photonic Crystals (SPC): Utilizing photonic bandgaps to achieve bandstop/bandpass with suppression ratios >40 dB, supporting 6G spectrum reuse.
Key Breakthrough: Achieving nanosecond-to-microsecond dynamic reconfiguration, transforming filters into "programmable intelligent agents."

2.3 Ultra-High Q and Ultra-Narrowband Selectivity

Coplanar waveguide (CPW) resonators achieve Q >8×10⁶, with bandwidth compressed to 80 MHz (relative bandwidth <0.01%). Superconducting metamaterials (SRRs) use negative refractive index to achieve <100 MHz narrowband pass, enabling extreme spectrum reuse for 6G terahertz channel allocation.

2.4 AI Integration: Closed-Loop Intelligent Control

  • Perception Layer: Superconducting filters act as high-resolution spectrum sensors (resolution <1 MHz);
  • Decision Layer: Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) classify interference types (>95% accuracy), deep reinforcement learning (DRL) optimizes parameters (PPO algorithm);
  • Execution Layer: Superconducting devices enable nanosecond-level reconfiguration;
  • Feedback Layer: Federated learning enables knowledge sharing across nodes, improving generalization.
Paradigm Shift: AI-driven "PDE-R" (Perceive-Decide-Execute-Refine) closed loop is the core pathway for SIES intelligence.

3. Three Frontiers: Breakthrough Applications and Performance Comparison

3.1 Quantum Computing: A Cryogenic Noise-Immune System

In qubit systems, superconducting filters serve as noise barriers, addressing three key challenges:

  • Thermal Noise Suppression: Suppresses >90% of noise in 1–20 GHz band, reducing equivalent noise temperature from 10 K to 100 mK;
  • Cross-Scale Noise Management: A "three-tier collaborative architecture"—source blocking (SQUID tuning), path isolation (superconducting shielding), and dynamic compensation (AI feedback);
  • AI Feedback Optimization: Combines quantum non-demolition (QND) measurements with DRL to dynamically adjust filter parameters, extending T₂* to 52 μs (case study: Origin Quantum).
Case Study: Google's Sycamore 2.0 uses an array of SQUID-tunable filters, improving single-gate fidelity to 99.93%.

3.2 6G Terahertz Communications: A "Surgical Scalpel" for Extreme Spectrum Reuse

In the 0.1–10 THz band, superconducting filters enable:

  • Ultra-Narrowband Selection: Sub-channels of 100 MHz in fragmented spectrum, supporting >1000 concurrent users;
  • Ultra-Low Insertion Loss: Loss <0.1 dB, improving link budget by 20 dB;
  • Dynamic Reconfigurability: Combined with electrical phase change and AI, enabling real-time spectrum sensing and interference avoidance;
  • Satellite-Terrestrial Links: Deployed at 0.35 THz atmospheric window, compensating for Doppler shift, reducing BER from 10⁻³ to 10⁻⁶.
Case Study: NTT DoCoMo tested YBCO filters at 0.3 THz, achieving spectral efficiency of 30 bps/Hz.

3.3 AI Edge Computing: An Intelligent "Game-Theoretic" Anti-Interference Agent

In complex electromagnetic environments, superconducting filters combined with AI form a closed-loop anti-interference system:

  • Real-Time Sensing: Filters output spectral maps, AI identifies frequency-hopping radars, power-line noise, etc.;
  • Nanosecond Execution: VO₂/optical switches enable parameter switching in <100 ns;
  • Edge Deployment: Lightweight AI models (<10 MB) deployed in portable radios, medical devices, industrial gateways;
  • Multi-Scenario Applications: Military electronic warfare (DARPA Adapt-RF), medical imaging (EEG SNR +18 dB), autonomous driving (V2X latency <8 ms).
Case Study: CETC 38th Institute's medical filter module received CFDA certification, improving epilepsy prediction accuracy by 25%.
