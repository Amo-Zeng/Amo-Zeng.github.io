Agroecology 3.0: Systemic Reconfiguration of Ecological Capitalization and Farmer Participatory Governance

Transforming Smallholders' Green Behaviors into Tradable Assets for Rural Revitalization and Green Transformation Paradigm Shift

Introduction: From External Compensation to Incentive-Driven Participation — The Paradigm Shift in Agroecology

Under the dual drivers of China’s "dual carbon" goals and rural revitalization strategy, agroecology has evolved beyond its role in environmental protection to become a core engine for sustainable income growth among smallholder farmers. However, traditional agroecology (1.0–2.0) faces a fundamental dilemma: the "externality" of ecological practices cannot be internalized as economic returns, leading to insufficient farmer motivation.

According to China’s Ministry of Agriculture (2023), only 12% of agroecology projects achieved “farmer income growth exceeding 15%,” with most relying on subsidies for survival—a fiscal dependency rate as high as 68% (World Bank, 2023)—trapped in a vicious cycle of “green investment → fiscal transfusion → unsustainability.”

In this context, we propose the concept of Agroecology 3.0—centered on ecological capitalization (Ecological Capitalization). Through institutional innovation, market mechanisms, and cultural empowerment, it transforms smallholders’ “green behaviors” into measurable, tradable, and inheritable “ecological assets,” enabling a paradigm shift from “passive response” to “active participation” in governance.

Its essence lies in integrating natural capital into modern economic accounting systems, turning ecological behavior into a productive factor that can be accumulated, appreciated, and passed on.

Problem Statement: The Three Structural Contradictions Agroecology 3.0 Aims to Solve

The proposal of Agroecology 3.0 arises from a systemic diagnosis of smallholder farming realities. Its core mission is to resolve the following three deep-seated contradictions:

  1. “High Cost–Low Return” Contradiction: Ecological farming increases average costs by 20%–30% (FAO, 2022), yet market premiums are less than 15%, and only 30%–40% of that premium reaches farmers (OECD, 2023), creating a widespread problem of “growing green, earning little”;
  2. “Fragmented Production–Centralized Market” Contradiction: Smallholders operate on fragmented land (average <5 mu per household), unable to bear transaction costs such as carbon accounting (>2,000 CNY/household), brand registration, or third-party certification, thus excluded from green markets;
  3. “Traditional Wisdom–Modern Market” Disconnection: Local ecological knowledge (e.g., crop rotation, composting, biological pest control) lacks mechanisms for property rights. Over 90% remains non-assetized (UNESCO, 2022), preventing its conversion into brand value.
The goal of Agroecology 3.0 is to reconfigure the system through “institution + market + culture” to transform ecological behavior from a “public good” into a “private right,” and from “externality” into a “tradable asset,” restoring smallholders’ agency in the ecological economy.

Institutional Innovation: Capitalization Pathways for Ecological Behavior

1. Ecological Credit Points — A Digital Tool for Participatory Governance

The Ecological Credit Point system is the digital embodiment of participatory governance at the grassroots level. Its core lies in quantifying farmers’ green behaviors (e.g., fertilizer reduction, straw incorporation, biodiversity protection) into points and integrating them into village-level credit systems.

Core Mechanism: Points not only redeem agricultural inputs but also link to social resources like “green loans,” “extra points for children’s school admission,” and “collective dividends,” forming a social recognition that “green = capital.” Its essence is to frame ecological behavior as part of “social capital” (Social Capital), creating a closed-loop incentive: “behavior → credit → resource.”

2. Agricultural Carbon Tickets — A Market Channel for Ecological Capital

Agricultural carbon tickets convert farmland carbon sequestration into tradable assets, breaking the fiscal dependency of traditional ecological compensation. Data from Sanming, Fujian shows:

  • 0.2–0.3 tons of carbon sequestered per mu of rice annually
  • Carbon ticket price: 50–80 CNY/ton
  • Farmers earn an extra 10–24 CNY per mu
  • If 10% of China’s farmland (~350 million mu) enters carbon trading, annual transaction volume could exceed 30 billion CNY
  • Carbon conversion rate reaches 50%–60%, approaching EU levels (60%–70%)
Key Breakthrough: Carbon tickets represent not only carbon credits but also ecological capital, enabling collateralization and securitization, forming a green financial closed loop. The institutional logic transforms the “negative externality” (pollution) penalty mechanism into an “positive externality” (sequestration) incentive system.

Cultural Empowerment: IP-ization and Assetization of Local Knowledge

IP-ization of Ecological Knowledge: From “Local Knowledge” to “Cultural Capital”

Local ecological knowledge (e.g., Hani Rice Terraces, rice-fish co-culture, Dong rice-duck systems) has long been treated as “non-marketable.” Agroecology 3.0 IP-izes such knowledge into cultural capital (Cultural Capital), achieving knowledge assetization.

Three Transformation Pathways:
  1. Knowledge Explicitization: Systematize tacit knowledge via oral history, digital archives, and farming method diagrams;
  2. Brand Personification: Assign IP personas like “Dong Rice-Fish Guardian” or “Hani Terrace Keeper” to farming methods;
  3. Consumption Scenarization: Embed IP into urban life via “cloud adoption,” “seasonal food calendars,” and “eco-study tours,” creating emotional connections “from field to table.”

Synergy with Institutions: Building the “Ecological Asset Triangle”

  • Points + IP: Farmers practicing ecology earn titles like “Ecological Heritage Keeper,” enhancing pride and identity;
  • Carbon Tickets + Culture: Carbon transactions include “ecological stories,” attracting ESG investors to pay premiums—up to 20%–30% above carbon value;
  • Cultural Feedback: IP revenue can fund “ecological funds” to support carbon development and point rewards, forming a “culture-finance-governance” closed loop.
Key Synergy Effect The three pathways are not isolated but form a “institution-market-culture” triangular support model, jointly enabling smallholders’ ecological capitalization. Their synergy can increase overall returns by 30%–50%.

Challenges: Institutional Risks and Practical Barriers

Despite the promise, the three pathways face multiple challenges:

  • Credit Point Abuse Risk: “Point gaming” occurs in some regions. Requires blockchain + remote sensing (e.g., satellite + drone) to ensure data integrity—data tampering risk can be reduced by >90%;
  • Carbon Ticket Bubble Risk: Inconsistent carbon accounting standards may lead to “greenwashing.” Requires a national MRV system (Monitoring-Reporting-Verification) with unified methods—carbon ticket credibility must exceed 95%;
  • Cultural IP Homogenization: Many “rice-fish” or “eco-farming” IPs are similar. Requires geographical indication + digital copyright composite protection and an “IP originality assessment system”;
  • Organizational Cost Barrier: Low farmer organization raises carbon ticket development costs (>2,000 CNY/household). Requires fiscal subsidies + financial support. Propose an “Ecological Asset Development Fund” covering 70% of initial costs.

Systemic Solution: Building the Agroecology 3.0 Support Ecosystem

1. Policy Synergy: Breaking Down Departmental Silos

  • Led by agriculture departments, coordinate with ecology, culture, finance, and regulatory agencies to establish a “Cross-Departmental Coordination Mechanism for Agroecology 3.0”;
  • Incorporate points, carbon tickets, and IP into rural revitalization KPIs, with eco-return share weighted at ≥20%.

2. Technological Support: Digital Twin + Blockchain

  • Build an “Ecological Asset Digital Twin System” to record green behavior, carbon data, and IP impact in real time—achieving “one field, one code; one farmer, one ledger”;
  • Use blockchain to ensure points are tamper-proof, carbon tickets traceable, and IP copyrights tradable—target ≥90% data on-chain.

3. Market Platform: National Ecological Asset Trading Platform

  • Propose a “National Ecological Asset Trading Platform” integrating:
    • Green Points Exchange Zone (linked to inputs, finance, public services);
    • Agricultural Carbon Ticket Trading Zone (linked to national carbon market, green finance);
    • Cultural IP Licensing Zone (linked to e-commerce, tourism, ESG funds);
  • Enable “one platform, multiple assets” integrated trading—transaction costs reduced by 40%–60%.

4. Pilot Selection Criteria

Prioritize cities with the following characteristics to form “Ecological Asset Pioneering Zones”:

  • Strong ecological foundation: Located in key ecological zones (e.g., water conservation, biodiversity hotspots);
  • High organizational capacity: Cooperative coverage ≥50%, village collective income ≥1 million CNY/year;
  • Robust digital infrastructure: 5G coverage ≥90%, digital rural platform coverage ≥80%;
  • Unique cultural resources: Possess national/global agricultural heritage or IP-ready traditional farming systems.

Conclusion: Toward a New Paradigm of Ecological Capitalization

The essence of Agroecology 3.0 is a paradigm revolution from “environmental justice” to “economic justice.” It capitalizes ecological behavior through institutional innovation, monetizes ecological value through market mechanisms, and assetizes local knowledge through cultural empowerment—ultimately achieving “green prosperity.”

The competitiveness of future agriculture will not lie in yield alone, but in the capacity to accumulate ecological assets. Smallholders are no longer cost-bearers of green transition but creators and sharers of ecological capital.

We advocate: Adopt Agroecology 3.0 as a strategic direction, building a four-in-one support system of institution, technology, market, and culture—transforming every field into a “green bank” and every farmer into an “ecological asset holder.” Only then can rural revitalization shift from “blood transfusion” to “blood-making,” and from “green” to “golden.”
