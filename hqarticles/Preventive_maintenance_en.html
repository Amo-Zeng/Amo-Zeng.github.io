Theoretical Framework: Temporal Preferences and Risk Desensitization

1. Hyperbolic Discounting: The Tyranny of Now

Behavioral economics experiments show: When asked to choose between "$100 now" or "$150 after one year", 75% of subjects chose the former despite the 50% discount rate. This non-linear time preference is magnified in engineering management:

  • Economic Discount Rate Imbalance: A utility company calculated that delaying transformer replacement boosted current profit margins by 1.2%, but increased 10-year failure probability by 40%
  • Decision Hierarchy Mismatch: Boards prioritize quarterly reports over infrastructure lifecycle, resulting in 32% lower maintenance budget approval rates compared to equipment deterioration speeds

2. Normalization Bias: The Numbing Slope

Prior to the 1979 Three Mile Island accident, control rooms ignored 18 consecutive cooling system alarms. Disaster psychology reveals three cognitive distortions:

  1. Adaptive threshold drift: Maintenance standards gradually relax based on "no-accident" experience
  2. Collective rationalization: A chip fab extended exhaust fan maintenance intervals to 1 year citing "German industry practice"
  3. Responsibility dilution effect: 85% of engineers admitted downgrading maintenance recommendations to "observation items"
