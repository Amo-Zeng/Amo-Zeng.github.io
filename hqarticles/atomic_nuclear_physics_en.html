I. Quantum Entanglement: The Quantum Code of Strong Interaction

Quantum entanglement, once referred to by Einstein as "spooky action at a distance," is now not only the core pillar of quantum computing and information fields but also shows profound potential in nucleon physics. Scientists are gradually extending this concept to the nucleon scale, attempting to decode the essence of the strong interaction between nucleons through quantum entanglement.

In traditional cognition, quantum entanglement mainly occurs between microscopic particles such as electrons and photons. However, when we expand our vision to the nucleon scale of protons and neutrons, the complexity of quantum entanglement suddenly escalates. Inside the atomic nucleus, protons and neutrons are tightly bound by the strong interaction, which far exceeds electromagnetic interaction, making the nucleus stable while forming a complex quantum state. Studies show that quantum entanglement between nucleons may be the core manifestation of strong interaction. Unlike simple entanglement between microscopic particles, entanglement in the nucleon system exhibits multibody characteristics, forming complex entanglement networks among multiple nucleons. This collective behavior not only determines the motion state of nucleons but also profoundly affects the overall properties of the nucleus.

The introduction of quantum entanglement provides a new perspective for understanding strong interaction. The entangled state between nucleons shows significant non-locality characteristics, not only in spatial correlation but also in the mutual correlation of intrinsic properties such as nucleon spin and isospin. By deeply studying these entanglement characteristics, scientists can more accurately describe the interaction mechanism between nucleons, thereby opening up new avenues for nuclear structure research. This research not only helps reveal the essence of strong interaction but may also provide a theoretical foundation for future nuclear energy applications.

II. The Mirror of Time: Symmetry Breaking and the Deep Laws of the Universe

Time, this seemingly invisible mirror, seems to maintain perfect symmetry in the macroscopic world, but in the depths of the microcosm, subtle cracks appear. The time-reversal symmetry breaking observed in nuclear decay is like a key gradually opening the door to the mysteries of time essence.

The key to this phenomenon lies in the weak interaction. As one of the four fundamental interactions in nature, weak interaction plays a crucial role in nuclear decay. Unlike strong interaction, electromagnetic interaction, and gravity, weak interaction does not maintain time-reversal symmetry but exhibits a property called "parity violation." This means that in nuclear decay processes dominated by weak interaction, some physical quantities will undergo subtle changes under time reversal, resulting in overall symmetry breaking.

However, weak interaction is not the only explanation. Researchers are exploring other possibilities, including potential unknown quantum effects. These effects may arise from more subtle symmetry-breaking mechanisms in quantum systems, possibly hinting at even more fundamental physical laws. For example, some theoretical models suggest that spacetime itself may not be perfectly symmetric at the most fundamental level but may have some slight "distortions." These distortions may be amplified in nuclear decay processes, leading to observed symmetry breaking.

The significance of this research goes far beyond revealing the basic mechanism of nuclear decay. It may also provide important clues for understanding the origin of time asymmetry in the universe. From the Big Bang to life evolution, time seems to flow in a single direction, a phenomenon known as the "arrow of time." Scientists have long sought the origin of this arrow of time, and the symmetry breaking observed in nuclear decay may be one of the keys to solving this puzzle. By deeply studying this phenomenon, we can not only understand the essence of time more profoundly but also reveal deeper symmetry-breaking mechanisms in the universe.

III. Nucleon Spin: The Quantum Compass for Dark Matter Detection

In the vast field of physics, the seemingly microscopic phenomenon of nucleon spin is gradually becoming a key link in exploring the mysteries of dark matter in the universe. Dark matter, accounting for about 27% of the total mass-energy in the universe, cannot be fully captured through current electromagnetic radiation and gravitational observations, but its impact on the formation of cosmic structures cannot be ignored. Today, the study of nucleon spin provides us with a new way to detect dark matter.

Nucleon spin, as one of the cornerstone properties of the nucleus, is far more complex than its apparent rotational behavior. The internal quark and gluon structures of nucleons—protons and neutrons—determine the distribution and dynamics of spin. By precisely measuring nucleon spin under different conditions, such as high-energy collisions or deep inelastic scattering, scientists can gain profound insights into these internal structures. These insights align with the interaction forms between dark matter particles and nucleons, as predicted by theoretical models.

Research has found that certain specific nucleon spin characteristics, such as abnormal changes in spin polarization under specific magnetic fields or temperatures, seem to match the weak interaction between dark matter particles and nucleons predicted by theoretical models. These signals not only provide potential pathways for dark matter detection but also suggest more subtle interaction mechanisms between nucleons and dark matter. By further studying these interactions, scientists can not only improve the sensitivity of dark matter detection but also reveal the essential characteristics of dark matter, providing crucial clues for answering one of the biggest mysteries in the universe.
