The Cage and Liberation of Perception: Triple Colonization in the AR Era and a Fourth Possibility

When technology not only transforms the world but reshapes how we see it—can we still say that it is “our” seeing?

Augmented Reality (AR) is infiltrating daily life at an unprecedented pace: from navigation arrows floating above city streets, to social filters distorting faces, to virtual real estate transactions in the metaverse. AR is not merely “overlaying” reality—it is reconstructing perception itself—ushering in a new form of perceptual colonization. What we are experiencing is not a neutral technological extension, but a systematic expropriation of perceptual sovereignty. This article reveals the triple logic of colonization embedded in AR technology and asks: Is it possible to open up a fourth possibility—a liberatory practice grounded in the perceptual commons, enabled by shared perception protocols, and rooted in cognitive sovereignty?

I. First Colonization: Spatial Colonization — From Geography to the Privatization of Perception

AR transforms physical space into a programmable interface, turning cities into battlegrounds for “augmented layers.” Google Maps AR navigation, Apple’s LiDAR modeling, and Meta’s “Horizon Worlds” all convert public streets into platform-owned data layers. Every step you take is recorded, tagged, and commodified. Space is no longer merely “occupied”—it is defined by how it is perceived.

This is not simple surveillance, but the privatization of perception. Agamben’s concept of “bare life” (zōē) becomes manifest here: when your walking path is algorithmically predicted, your gaze directed by ads, and your body marked in the digital layer as a unit of traffic—you are no longer the subject of space, but a carrier of perceptual data.

Quote 1: We no longer “see” space—we are “seen” by it. In AR, the reversal of the gaze is the inversion of sovereignty.

Platforms, under the guise of “optimizing user experience,” transform public space into a field of perceptual monopoly. The street you see is the platform’s customized “reality filter.” This colonization is not violent occupation, but gentle discipline: you willingly put on the glasses—yet surrender your eyes.

II. Second Colonization: Temporal Colonization — From the Present to the Algorithmization of Memory

AR does not only reshape space—it restructures time. Filters that beautify faces “aestheticize the past”; AR try-ons “rehearse the future”; and social platforms’ “memory recall” features perform algorithmic reconstruction of memory. Childhood photos are AI-colorized, friends’ birthdays are “reminded” by the system, and your “significant moments” are marked by the platform as consumable content.

Here, Stiegler’s concept of “technological pharmakon” (a cure and a poison) becomes evident: AR, as an external organ of memory, could compensate for forgetting—but is instead privatizing memory itself. The platform controls when you remember, how you remember, and what you remember. Memory is no longer internal, private, and fluid—but categorized, archived, and commodified data streams.

More dangerously, the authenticity of memory is being redefined. When AI generates “your smile at age five,” when filters “restore” your youthful face, you begin to wonder: which version is the “real” me? When memory is “optimized” by the platform, are we losing our capacity for self-narration?

Quote 2: When an algorithm remembers you better than you do, who is the legitimate subject of memory?

III. Third Colonization: Subjective Colonization — From Cognition to the Algorithmization of Perception

The deepest colonization is the colonization of cognitive structures. AR uses “context-awareness” to anticipate your needs: you look at a building, and a price tag appears; you gaze at food, and calorie information pops up. Superficially convenient, this is actually the preemption of cognitive pathways.

Your attention is directed, your judgment simplified, your curiosity “fed” by algorithms. You no longer explore actively—you passively receive an “optimized reality.” This is not merely “information overload,” but the surrender of cognitive sovereignty—you no longer decide “what to see,” but the system decides “what to show you.”

When AR glasses become a “cognitive interface,” we are sliding toward automation of perception: we no longer “see,” but are “seen”; we no longer “think,” but are “thought for.” Foucault’s “disciplinary society” is upgraded into a “perceptual discipline”: power no longer controls through confinement, but through the comfort and convenience of perception.

Quote 3: The freest tools are often the most efficient cages—when convenience becomes habit, resistance becomes discomfort.

IV. Intersectionality: The Complicity and Reinforcement of the Three Colonizations

These three forms of colonization are not isolated—they reinforce each other. The privatization of space (First) provides data for algorithms (Third), which optimize memory presentation (Second), which in turn strengthens spatial control. Platforms create a “perceptual feedback loop”: collect—analyze—feedback—recollect, constructing a self-reinforcing system of colonization.

Case Study: An AR city tourism project. Tourists wear AR glasses, and the system recommends “personalized routes” based on past behavior. Seemingly thoughtful, this traps tourists in an algorithmically constructed perceptual cage: the sights you see are what the platform wants you to see; the stories you hear are what it wants you to believe; the history you “discover” is the history the algorithm “generates” for you.

This is not liberation, but perceptual re-colonization. What you believe is “exploration” is actually “being guided”; what you believe is “choice” is actually “being chosen for.”

V. The Fourth Possibility: Perceptual Commons, Shared Protocols, and Cognitive Sovereignty

Yet technology is not destined for control. Stiegler reminds us: technology is “tertiary retention”—it is both poison and antidote to memory. Can we transform AR from a “cage of perception” into a “liberation of perception”?

The key lies in reconstructing the logic of perceptual ownership: moving from “private perception” to the perceptual commons—treating perceptual data, memory records, and cognitive pathways as shared cognitive resources of humanity, not as monopolized digital assets of platforms.

This requires establishing shared perception protocols:

  • Data Sovereignty: Users own and control their perceptual data;
  • Open Protocols: AR systems adopt open, interoperable protocols to avoid platform lock-in;
  • Perceptual Diversity: Allow multiple realities to coexist, rejecting a single “optimized reality”;
  • Collective Memory Mechanisms: Build decentralized archives of memory to resist algorithmic monopolization of history.
Quote 4: True augmented reality is not about making reality “more beautiful”—but about making it “more contestable.” For only what is contestable is free.

This vision is not a technological utopia, but a political practice. It demands that we redefine “cognitive sovereignty”—the individual and collective autonomy over perception, memory, and cognition. It means:

  • Rejecting “default consent” for data collection;
  • Demanding explainability and exitability from AR systems;
  • Advocating for the inclusion of “the right to perception” in constitutions as a fundamental human right in the digital age.

VI. The Dialectics of Liberation: Hope Born from Colonization

Colonization and liberation are never binary opposites. The colonizing nature of AR precisely reveals the vulnerability of perception—precisely because perception is so important, it is contested. Precisely because memory is so intimate, it is commodified. Precisely because cognition is so central, it is algorithmized.

But it is this very contestation that opens the possibility of liberation. When platforms privatize perception, we realize: perception should be public. When memory is manipulated by algorithms, we ask: who has the right to define my past? When cognition is predetermined, we awaken: do I still possess the ability to “see”?

Quote 5: Liberation begins with suspicion toward “convenience,” and is realized through the reclamation of “seeing.”

Conclusion: Rewriting the Eyes in Code

AR is not an endpoint, but a threshold. It forces us to rethink: What is perception? What is reality? What is subjectivity? In what Agamben calls the “state of exception”—when technology becomes the norm—can we still distinguish “natural” from “augmented”?

What we need is not to reject technology, but to rewrite its political grammar. Not to escape AR, but to rebuild the perceptual commons within AR—so that every pair of eyes becomes autonomous, critical, and shared.

When children on the street see the scars of historical wars through AR, when elders upload their memories via open protocols, when cities democratically decide “what information may be overlaid”—then AR will truly become a tool of liberation, not a complicit agent of colonization.

The cage of perception will ultimately lead to liberation.
But only if we are willing to see again.
