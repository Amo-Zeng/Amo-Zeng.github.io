The Stage: A Confluence of Desire, Power, and Trauma

The stage, a small space illuminated by lights, carries infinite spiritual depth. It is a confluence of desire, power, and trauma, a hidden emotional laboratory. Here, every performance is a projection of collective psychology, and every viewing is a journey of self-exploration. From the mirror of desire to the game of power, from the reproduction of trauma to the possibility of healing, the stage becomes an excellent field for dissecting the psychological dilemmas of modern humans.

The Mirror of Desire: The Eternal Entanglement of Self and Other

The stage is a giant spiritual mirror, reflecting the deep-seated anxieties and desires of modern humans. In this world dominated by "watching," everyone is both an audience and an actor, performing the eternal entanglement of self and other in an invisible web of gazes. The actors under the spotlight endure the scrutiny of countless eyes from the audience. This experience of being watched is not only a test of their acting skills but also a severe challenge to their self-identity.

During the performance, actors deeply analyze themselves through their roles. This identification is not just a technical means of performance but also a psychoanalytic self-exploration. Actors project their subconscious desires and unmet emotions into their roles, allowing those suppressed emotions to be released and vented on stage. This identification is both psychological healing and a breakthrough and reconstruction of self-boundaries.

At the same time, the audience also watches themselves invisibly while viewing the performance. The plot on stage touches the unacknowledged emotions and desires deep within them, awakening these feelings through empathy and resonance. The audience projects themselves onto the actors, viewing them as objects of desire. In this projection, they can face their inner fears and desires at a safe distance. The charm of drama lies in providing a safe emotional container for the audience, allowing them to deeply explore their inner selves without exposing their true selves.

The Game of Power: The Invisible Arena on Stage

On the stage of drama, power relations unfold in a subtle and hidden way. This is not the traditional tension of swords drawn but a silent game in the depths of the soul. Every physical movement, every line, and every pause are subtle representations of power relations. The stage, this meticulously designed spiritual arena, becomes an excellent window to observe the workings of power deep within human nature.

From ancient Greek tragedies to modern experimental theater, power has always been one of the core themes of drama. However, from a psychoanalytic perspective, power is no longer confined to political possession but transforms into a more essential psychological existence. The tragedy of Oedipus stems from his unconscious pursuit of power and the conflict with social taboos, and this power not only points to the throne but also to the possession and control of the mother. In Shakespeare's "Macbeth," desire and power catalyze each other, pushing the protagonist towards the abyss of destruction.

In contemporary drama, the game of gender power presents a more complex appearance. Female roles gradually transform from passive objects to active subjects of desire. Blanche in "A Streetcar Named Desire" constantly seduces men to confirm her charm, which is essentially a silent resistance to patriarchal society; in "A Doll's House," Nora overturns the traditional gender power structure by leaving home. These roles are not only the protagonists of the drama but also symbols of the game of gender power.

The Reconstruction of Trauma: Healing Memories on Stage

On the stage of drama, trauma is no longer cold fragments of memory but a process of reconstruction and healing through performance. Every movement is a tremor of the soul, and every line is a hidden emotional clue. When we talk about "the reproduction of trauma on stage," we are actually exploring a journey through the labyrinth of memory: playwrights weave stories within it, actors seek the souls of their characters, and the audience finds themselves in it.

The process of dramatic performance itself is a ritualized healing of trauma. Actors repeatedly practice a certain movement or line during rehearsals. This is not only a refinement of the role but also a deconstruction and reconstruction of traumatic memories. Each repetition is like peeling an onion of memory, gradually revealing the emotions hidden deep within. This process is full of pain but also contains the possibility of healing.

When the audience watches trauma-themed dramas, they often experience a profound emotional experience. The psychological mechanism of "projection" plays a key role here: the audience projects their traumatic memories onto the characters and plot on stage. Through this projection, they can re-experience those suppressed emotions. The "identification" mechanism helps the audience internalize the healing process on stage, thereby realizing the healing of their own trauma.

Conclusion: The Stage, a Magic Mirror of the Spirit

The stage, as a confluence of desire, power, and trauma, is not just a performance space but also a magic mirror reflecting the psychological dilemmas of modern humans. It reminds us that true self-awareness requires both the courage to face desire and power and the wisdom to maintain emotional distance. In the game of watching and being watched, we need to face others and ourselves with both openness and caution. This is perhaps the ultimate revelation that drama offers us: between gazing and being gazed upon, we find another possibility for understanding ourselves and the world.
