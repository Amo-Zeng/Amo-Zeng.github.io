Starlight Enlightenment: From Ignorance to Rationality

When ancient humans first gazed at the starry sky beside the campfire, the mysterious night sky cast the seeds of enlightenment in their primitive minds. The ancient Egyptians determined the seasons of the Nile floods by observing the position of Sirius, while the Mayans recorded the trajectories of planets with precise astronomical calendars. These initial astronomical observations were not only the beginning of practical technology but also humanity's first attempt to interpret natural laws. Humboldt once said, "Only those who gaze at the stars are qualified to talk about the laws of nature." This sense of awe and exploration of cosmic laws opened the first chapter of human rational thinking.

With the invention of the telescope and advances in astronomical observation technology, humanity began to truly knock on the door of the universe. The moment Galileo pointed his telescope at the night sky, he not only discovered Jupiter's moons but also shattered the shackles of geocentrism. Kepler summarized planetary motion laws from observational data, and Newton unified celestial and terrestrial phenomena under the law of universal gravitation. These explorations by scientific giants were no longer simple empirical summaries but a cognitive revolution based on rigorous logical reasoning. As Einstein said, "The most incomprehensible thing about the universe is that it is comprehensible."
