Paradigm Shift in Environmental Governance: From Equilibrium Optimization to Dynamic Adaptive Governance
—A Triple Evolution Model of Behavior, System, and Institutions

Abstract
Traditional environmental economics has long relied on the "rational economic agent" and "static equilibrium" paradigms, which struggle to address the complexity, nonlinearity, and evolutionary nature of real-world environmental governance. This paper proposes a triple-evolution model framework that integrates behavioral science, complex systems theory, and institutional evolution theory to systematically construct the Dynamic Adaptive Governance (DAG) paradigm for environmental governance. Through the integration of three models—the Behavior-Network Coupling Model (BNCM), the AI-driven Emergent Governance Model (AI-EGM), and the Institutional Coevolution Model (ICE-MLG)—this framework reveals the interactive mechanisms of behavioral heterogeneity, system emergence, and institutional synergy in environmental governance. Empirical cases—including urban waste sorting, green transportation transition, and carbon market development—validate the model’s policy foresight. The study finds that the DAG paradigm can identify "social trigger points," "transition tipping points," and "institutional complementarities," enabling a shift from "reactive governance" to "anticipatory governance." The paper further extracts cross-cutting insights and proposes a "three-stage adaptive governance pathway," offering policy tools with both theoretical innovation and practical value for tackling complex challenges such as climate crisis and biodiversity loss.

1. Introduction: The Paradigm Crisis in Environmental Governance and the Triple-Evolution Pathway

Environmental policy is facing an unprecedented paradigm crisis. Traditional environmental economics, centered on cost-benefit analysis (CBA) and internalization of externalities (e.g., Pigouvian taxes, Coasian property rights), implicitly relies on three assumptions:

  1. Rational economic agents: Individual behavior is predictable and stable;
  2. Static equilibrium: Systems converge toward a single optimal state;
  3. Exogenous institutions: Policy tools are independent of social structures.

However, real-world environmental governance often suffers from "policy failure": carbon taxes sparking social unrest, subsidies leading to rent-seeking, and stalled technology diffusion. The root cause lies in the fact that environmental systems are fundamentally complex adaptive systems (CAS) shaped by the triple evolution of behavior, system, and institutions.

This paper proposes the Dynamic Adaptive Governance (DAG) paradigm, arguing that environmental governance should be grounded in a triple-evolution logic:

  • Behavioral evolution: Individual behavior is driven by heterogeneous preferences and social norms, enabling "soft internalization" through social incentives;
  • Systemic evolution: Environmental-economic-social systems exhibit nonlinearity, path dependence, and emergence, requiring AI-driven dynamic simulations for "anticipatory governance";
  • Institutional evolution: Institutions are endogenous outcomes of multi-level, multi-agent games, necessitating the identification of "institutional complementarities" and "institutional traps" for "coevolution."

These three evolutions are not isolated but are coupled through feedback loops and cross-scale interactions into a unified governance system. By integrating three cutting-edge models—the Behavior-Network Coupling Model (BNCM), the AI-driven Emergent Governance Model (AI-EGM), and the Institutional Coevolution Model (ICE-MLG)—this paper constructs the theoretical framework and policy toolkit of DAG, driving a paradigm shift in environmental economics from "equilibrium optimization" to "evolutionary adaptation."

Overarching Theoretical Proposition:
"In complex social-ecological systems, effective environmental governance must shift from 'static optimality' to 'dynamic adaptability,' with its core being the identification and guidance of the triple coevolutionary pathways of behavior, system, and institutions."

2. Theoretical Integration of the Triple-Evolution Model

2.1 Unified Framework: Dynamic Adaptive Governance (DAG)

The core of the DAG model is a three-layer coupled system (see Figure 1), enabling multi-scale dynamic feedback from micro-behavior to macro-institutions:

[ Behavior Layer: Individual Decisions ]
    ↓ (Social networks, information diffusion)
[ System Layer: Macro-emergence ]
    ↓ (Performance feedback, policy response)
[ Institution Layer: Multi-level Games ]
    ↑ (Institutional adjustment, policy intervention)
[ Behavior Layer: Adaptive Learning ]

(Diagram: Three-layer feedback loop; arrows indicate information flow, behavioral responses, institutional adjustments, and AI learning; dashed arrows indicate cross-layer feedback; node size represents influence weight)
  • Behavior Layer: Based on behavioral heterogeneity and social norm dynamics, individuals adjust environmental behavior (e.g., waste sorting, low-carbon travel) through mechanisms like imitation, reputation, and social comparison within social networks (BNCM);
  • System Layer: Micro-level interactions generate macro-emergence (e.g., green technology diffusion, carbon emission tipping points, ecological resilience shifts). AI models, using real-time data and complex system simulations, identify "transition tipping points" and "resilience trajectories" (AI-EGM);
  • Institution Layer: System performance (e.g., emission reductions, economic volatility) feeds back into institutional evolution. Multi-level actors (central/local government, firms, NGOs) adjust policy rules through games, forming institutional coevolution or institutional friction (ICE-MLG);
  • Feedback Loops: Institutional changes reshape behavioral incentives and system structures, forming a "policy-system-institution-behavior" coevolution cycle—the dynamic adaptive core of DAG.

2.2 Core Mechanisms and Mathematical Formulations

(1) Behavioral Evolution: Social Incentives and Norm Cascades (BNCM)

The probability that individual $ i $ adopts an environmentally friendly behavior (e.g., waste sorting, low-carbon travel) at time $ t $:

Equation 1 $$ P_i(t) = \sigma\left( \beta_1 U_i^{\text{eco}}(t) + \beta_2 S_i^{\text{norm}}(t) + \beta_3 L_i^{\text{loss}}(t) + \beta_4 C_i^{\text{cost}}(t) + \gamma \cdot \text{Network}(i) \right) $$
Where:
  • $ U_i^{\text{eco}} $: Economic utility (e.g., subsidies, cost savings);
  • $ S_i^{\text{norm}}(t) = \alpha \cdot \frac{1}{k_i} \sum_{j \in N(i)} \mathbb{I}(a_j = 1) $: Social norm pressure, $ k_i $ = number of neighbors, $ \mathbb{I} $ = behavior indicator;
  • $ L_i^{\text{loss}} $: Loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979), sensitivity to policy changes;
  • $ C_i^{\text{cost}} $: Behavioral execution cost;
  • $ \text{Network}(i) $: Network position effects (e.g., centrality, bridging);
  • $ \sigma(\cdot) $: Sigmoid function; $ \beta_k $: behavioral heterogeneity parameters reflecting individual differences.
Core Mechanism: Social norms propagate via cascading diffusion in social networks. Key nodes (e.g., community leaders, influencers) can trigger "social trigger points" (Social Trigger Points), enabling low-cost behavioral change.

(2) Systemic Evolution: AI-Driven Adaptive Learning (AI-EGM)

Policy agents learn optimal strategies via deep reinforcement learning (DRL) in complex systems:

Equation 2 $$ \pi^* = \arg\max_{\pi} \mathbb{E}\left[ \sum_{t=0}^T \gamma^t R_t \mid s_0, \pi \right], \quad R_t = w_1 \cdot \Delta E_t + w_2 \cdot G_t + w_3 \cdot S_t + w_4 \cdot R_t^{\text{climate}} $$
Where:
  • $ \pi $: Policy mix (e.g., carbon tax, subsidies, infrastructure investment);
  • $ R_t $: Multi-objective reward function, covering emissions $ \Delta E $, economic growth $ G $, social equity $ S $, and climate resilience $ R^{\text{climate}} $;
  • $ \gamma $: Discount factor;
  • $ w_k $: Objective weights, determined via participatory weighting.
Core Mechanism: AI models simulate policy paths via digital twins, identifying "transition tipping points" (e.g., 25% charging network coverage, ¥80/ton carbon price) and generating "resilient policy portfolios" to handle uncertainty.

(3) Institutional Evolution: Multi-Level Games and Coupled Networks (ICE-MLG)

Institutions $ s_i $ (e.g., carbon market rules, local environmental assessments) evolve via evolutionary game theory:

Equation 3 $$ \frac{ds_i}{dt} = s_i \left[ f_i(s_i, s_{-i}) - \bar{f}_i \right] + \epsilon \cdot \mathcal{L}(s_i, \text{learning}) $$
Where:
  • $ f_i $: Fitness of institution $ i $ (e.g., emission reduction efficiency, implementation cost);
  • $ \bar{f}_i $: Average fitness;
  • $ \mathcal{L} $: Learning mechanism (e.g., policy pilots, international experience);
  • $ \epsilon $: Learning intensity.

Institutional complementarity $ IC(i,j) $ and institutional friction $ IF(i,j) $ are quantified via multi-layer network coupling strength:

Equation 4 $$ IC(i,j) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\rho \cdot (c_{ij} - c_0)}}, \quad IF(i,j) = \max(0, -IC(i,j)) $$
Where $ c_{ij} $ is the synergy effect between institutions $ i,j $ (e.g., carbon market + green finance), and $ \rho $ is a sensitivity parameter.
Core Mechanism: Institutional evolution is influenced by the structure of the multi-level governance network. Central institutions (e.g., central environmental inspections) have greater evolutionary influence.

(4) Cross-Layer Coupling: The Unified Mechanism of DAG

The three models are dynamically linked via coupling functions $ \Phi, \Psi, \Theta $:

  • $ \Phi: \text{Behavior Layer} \to \text{System Layer} $: Behavioral density $ \rho_a(t) $ drives system state $ S(t) $ evolution;
  • $ \Psi: \text{System Layer} \to \text{Institution Layer} $: System performance $ P(t) $ feeds back to institutional fitness $ f_i $;
  • $ \Theta: \text{Institution Layer} \to \text{Behavior Layer} $: Institution $ s_i $ alters behavioral utility functions $ U_i^{\text{eco}} $ and $ S_i^{\text{norm}} $.
Unified Mechanism: DAG achieves "adaptive learning" through cross-layer feedback loops—policies are not static designs but "intelligent agents" coevolving with the system.

3. Empirical Integration: Synergistic Application of the Triple Model

3.1 Case 1: Urban Waste Sorting—Coevolution of Behavior, System, and Institutions

  • Behavior Layer (BNCM): A Beijing community implemented "green role models + social comparison," increasing waste sorting rate from 42% to 83% in 6 months (Scenario S4), with key nodes (e.g., building managers) contributing 40%;
  • System Layer (AI-EGM): AI simulation showed that once sorting exceeds 60%, waste collection can shift to "scheduled collection + smart sorting," reducing operational costs by 30% and emissions by 25%;
  • Institution Layer (ICE-MLG): The model identified institutional complementarity: waste sorting + community co-management + green credits ($ IC = +0.35 $), and an institutional trap: sorting policy + local fiscal assessment ($ IF = +0.28 $), leading to superficial compliance;
  • Synergy: A "Community Green Governance Committee" integrated behavioral incentives and institutional design, achieving a stable 85% sorting rate and 50% lower policy costs.

3.2 Case 2: Green Transportation Transition—Tipping Points and Resilience Pathways

  • System Layer (AI-EGM): AI identified 25% charging network coverage and ¥80/ton carbon tax as "transition tipping points"; beyond these, EV adoption surged (15% → 40%);
  • Behavior Layer (BNCM): Social comparison feedback made the public perceive "most people choose EVs," reducing "status quo bias" and accelerating behavioral change;
  • Institution Layer (ICE-MLG): The model diagnosed institutional friction: central carbon tax + local industrial protection ($ IF = +0.41 $), suggesting a "green transition fund" to align incentives;
  • Synergy: AI recommended a "gradual policy mix"—first invest in charging infrastructure (behavior + system), then gradually raise carbon taxes (institution), avoiding social backlash from abrupt policies.

3.3 Case 3: Carbon Market Development—Institutional Synergy and Trap Avoidance

  • Institution Layer (ICE-MLG): The model revealed the "institutional bundle" effect: carbon market + green finance + industrial fund ($ IC = +0.38 $), enhancing overall effectiveness;
  • Behavior Layer (BNCM): Firms used "carbon allowance pledging + green loans" to reduce financing costs, increasing emission reduction willingness (high heterogeneity: SMEs responded 60% more strongly than large firms);
  • System Layer (AI-EGM): AI simulations showed that without green finance, the carbon market would fall into a "low-liquidity trap"; with it, carbon price stability could be achieved 3 years earlier;
  • Synergy Diagnosis: The model identified "local protectionism" as a key institutional trap, recommending "cross-regional carbon allowance auctions" and "fiscal transfer mechanisms" to resolve it.

4.2 Paradigm Shift: From Static Equilibrium to Dynamic Adaptation

The DAG paradigm enables three fundamental shifts:

  1. From "price incentives" to "social-system-institutional synergy"
    - Traditional: Carbon taxes, subsidies—rely on "rational agent" responses;
    - DAG: Social comparison + AI dynamic adjustment + institutional bundles—achieving "multi-dimensional internalization."
  2. From "reactive governance" to "anticipatory governance"
    - Traditional: Policies based on historical data and linear extrapolation;
    - DAG: AI simulates future scenarios, identifies "tipping points" and "resilience pathways," enabling anticipatory intervention.
  3. From "top-down design" to "coevolution"
    - Traditional: Centralized institutions, passive local implementation;
    - DAG: Policies as "adaptive agents" coevolving with behavior, system, and institutions—enabling self-organizing governance.

5. Operationalization Pathway: The Three-Stage Adaptive Governance Approach

To operationalize DAG, we propose a "three-stage adaptive governance pathway":

(1) Diagnosis Stage: Evolutionary State Assessment

  • Construct a Behavior-System-Institution (B-S-I) Coupling Map to identify:
    • Behavioral heterogeneity: norm-sensitive groups, status quo bias groups;
    • System tipping points: technology diffusion thresholds, ecological limits;
    • Institutional complementarity/friction: IC/IF indices, network centrality.
  • Output: Environmental Governance Evolution Health Report (EGEHR).

(2) Simulation Stage: Dynamic Policy Scenario Testing

  • Use AI-EGM to simulate policy combinations over 5–10 years;
  • Evaluate emission reduction, economic cost, social acceptance, and institutional resilience;
  • Identify "high-performance, low-friction, high-acceptance (HPLH)" policy bundles.

(3) Adaptation Stage: Real-Time Feedback and Iteration

  • Establish a Digital Twin Governance Platform to monitor behavior, system, and institution states in real time;
  • Trigger "policy adaptation mechanisms" when deviations occur (e.g., declining sorting rate, carbon price volatility):
    • Behavior Layer: Adjust social incentives (e.g., role model rotation, community competitions);
    • System Layer: AI suggests parameter adjustments (e.g., dynamic subsidies);
    • Institution Layer: Launch institutional learning forums to optimize bundles.
  • Enable "policy self-evolution."
Example: The EU’s "Digital Twin Cities" project already integrates BNCM and AI-EGM for real-time optimization of transport and energy policies.

6. Ethical Challenges and Governance Boundaries

While DAG holds promise, it must address three risks:

  1. Algorithmic bias: AI may perpetuate historical inequalities (e.g., fewer charging stations in low-income areas);
  2. Accountability gap: When AI-driven policies fail, responsibility is unclear;
  3. Power concentration: Technology platforms may become "shadow governments."

Mitigation Strategies:

  • Transparency: Publish model logic and data sources; adopt explainable AI (XAI);
  • Participation: Allow public "experience" of policy paths via digital platforms (Participatory Simulation);
  • Accountability: Establish "AI Governance Ethics Committees" and Guidelines for Dynamic Governance Use.

7. Conclusion: Toward the Future of Evolutionary Adaptive Governance

By integrating behavioral, systemic, and institutional triple-evolution models, this paper constructs the theoretical and practical framework of Dynamic Adaptive Governance (DAG). Key conclusions:

  1. Environmental governance is fundamentally a complex adaptive system shaped by triple evolution, requiring a departure from the "rational agent-equilibrium-exogenous institution" paradigm;
  2. The DAG model identifies "evolutionary junctures"—social triggers, tipping points, institutional complementarities—enabling anticipatory intervention;
  3. Policies should shift from "optimal design" to "coevolution", achieved via the "three-stage governance pathway";
  4. Future directions: Promote "digital twin governance," "AI policy sandboxes," and "institutional learning platforms" to build a resilient global environmental governance network.
Final Proposition: In the age of climate crisis, the success of environmental policy lies not in "heavier taxes" but in "smarter evolutionary design." The DAG paradigm is the theoretical engine and governance compass toward this future.
