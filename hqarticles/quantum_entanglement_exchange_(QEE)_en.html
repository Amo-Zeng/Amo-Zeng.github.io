Non-Locality of Quantum Entanglement and the Link Transcending Space and Time

The core feature of quantum entanglement is that once two particles become entangled, their states become instantaneously correlated, regardless of the distance between them. This instantaneous correlation seems to defy traditional causality, as it cannot be explained by local interactions. Einstein famously referred to this phenomenon as "spooky action at a distance," believing it to be a flaw in the quantum mechanics theory. However, a series of experiments (such as the Aspect experiment) have irrefutably proven the non-locality of quantum entanglement, which is a fundamental characteristic of the quantum world.

Quantum entanglement swapping further reveals the essence of this non-locality by entangling two originally unentangled particle systems, demonstrating the profound connections within the quantum world. For example, suppose we have two particles, A and B, entangled respectively with particles C and D. By measuring the states of C and D, we can entangle A and B, even though A and B have never directly interacted. This process is known as "entanglement swapping." From a physical perspective, entanglement swapping is a link that transcends space and time. It does not rely on the propagation speed of signals, as classical communication does, but occurs instantaneously. This means that quantum entanglement swapping can be considered a form of "instantaneous information transmission," although it is not the traditional kind of classical information transfer.

Quantum Entanglement Swapping: The Cornerstone of Quantum Communication Networks

Quantum communication is one of the most promising applications of quantum information technology, and quantum entanglement swapping is the core technology for building quantum communication networks. Unlike classical communication, quantum communication relies on the transmission of quantum states and the sharing of quantum entanglement, with quantum entanglement swapping providing crucial support for this process.

The core idea of quantum communication is to use the non-clonability of quantum states and the non-locality of quantum entanglement to achieve secure communication. The most famous quantum communication protocol is Quantum Key Distribution (QKD), which generates shared keys through the transmission of quantum states, enabling unconditionally secure communication. However, the practical application of QKD is limited by distance, as quantum states are susceptible to environmental noise and attenuation during long-distance transmission, leading to the destruction of entangled states.

To overcome this limitation, the concept of Quantum Repeater was proposed. Quantum Repeaters generate and store quantum entanglement at intermediate nodes, then "splice" these entangled states together to enable long-distance quantum communication. Quantum entanglement swapping is the key step in achieving this process.

The basic idea of quantum entanglement swapping is: by measuring part of the state of two originally unentangled particle systems, they can become entangled without direct interaction. Specifically, suppose we have two particles, A and B, entangled respectively with particles C and D. By measuring the states of C and D, we can entangle A and B, even though A and B have never directly interacted. This process is known as "entanglement swapping."

In quantum communication networks, the role of quantum entanglement swapping is mainly reflected in the following aspects:

  1. Long-Distance Quantum Communication: Quantum entanglement swapping enables remote nodes to establish entanglement relationships through intermediate nodes, thereby achieving long-distance quantum communication. This is crucial for building a global quantum internet.
  2. Core Technology of Quantum Repeaters: Quantum Repeaters generate and store quantum entanglement at intermediate nodes, then "splice" these entangled states together to achieve long-distance quantum communication. Quantum entanglement swapping is the key technology for this "splicing" process.
  3. Improving Communication Fault Tolerance: Quantum entanglement swapping can resist environmental noise and quantum state attenuation to some extent, thereby improving the fault tolerance and stability of quantum communication.

Quantum entanglement swapping dynamically generates and breaks entanglement relationships between multiple qubits, providing an efficient parallel acceleration mechanism for quantum computing.
